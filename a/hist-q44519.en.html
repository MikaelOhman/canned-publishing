Selma Ottilia Lovisa Lagerlöf ([ˈsɛ̂lːma ˈlɑ̂ːɡɛˌɭøːv]), born on November 20, 1858, at Mårbacka in the parish of Östra Ämtervik in the county of Värmland, and died on March 16, 1940, also at Mårbacka in the same parish and county, was a Swedish author.

Lagerlöf's debut novel, *Gösta Berlings saga*, was published in 1891, followed by the children's book *The Wonderful Adventures of Nils Holgersson* in 1906–1907. Her writing draws on a Värmland literary tradition rooted in oral storytelling.

Lagerlöf was the first woman to be awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature, in 1909. She was also the first woman elected to the Swedish Academy, and one of the founding members of the *Samfundet De Nio*, as well as a member of the Royal Society of Sciences and Letters in Gothenburg.

Although Lagerlöf is associated with Mårbacka, she lived and worked in Falun for many years, where she wrote some of her most important works. For several years, she also lived in Landskrona, where she worked as a teacher.

Lagerlöf appeared on the Swedish twenty-kronor banknote from 1991 to 2016. At the Dalarna Museum in Falun, there is a preserved library and study belonging to Selma Lagerlöf, complete with furnishings, known as the "Selma Lagerlöf Room." The Mårbacka farm near Sunne, which she herself ran as a farm, has become a museum according to her will after her death. It preserves her memory and offers guided tours of the main house. Furthermore, she has been portrayed in various works, such as the drama series *Selma* (2008) and the documentary series *Selma Lagerlöf* (2022).

== Early Life ==

=== Family and Relationships ===

Selma Lagerlöf was born in 1858 at the Mårbacka estate as the fifth of six children to Lieutenant Erik Gustaf Lagerlöf (1819–1885) and Elisabet Lovisa Wallroth (1827–1915), daughter of a wealthy industrialist. Among her siblings was the physician Daniel.

Lagerlöf belonged to the clerical family Lagerlöf from Värmland. Her grandfather, Daniel Lagerlöf (1776–1852), was a cousin of Esaias Tegnér, and her grandmother, Elisabet Maria Wennervik (1784–1864), was the daughter of a parson. Lagerlöf was also related to Erik Gustaf Geijer, and she descended from the Geijer family in more ways than one.

=== Health === Lagerlöf was born with a hip injury caused by an improperly attached femoral head. When she was three and a half years old, in the summer of 1862, she became ill and completely paralyzed in both legs. This disappeared as suddenly as it had appeared, but during her childhood, she did not have as easy a time playing as other children. At the age of fourteen, Lagerlöf spent some time in Stockholm to receive physiotherapy for her hip injury. She recounts her journey to the capital and events there in "Diary for Selma Ottilia Lovisa Lagerlöf." According to that book, she stayed in Stockholm for several months for physiotherapy when she was only nine years old.

Teachers came to Mårbacka and provided instruction, and Lagerlöf received lessons in both English and French. When Selma Lagerlöf was ten years old, in 1868, she read the entire Bible. Her father was very ill at the time, and she hoped that God would heal him if she read the whole Bible from cover to cover. Her father lived for another 17 years. In this way, Selma Lagerlöf became familiar with the language of the Bible early on, which she likely benefited from in her later writing career. When she was twelve years old, she wrote a longer poem about Mårbacka and continued to write poetry throughout her childhood. It wasn't until one of her benefactors, Sophie Adlersparre, encouraged her to write prose that she did so – and became an author.

Lagerlöf was confirmed in Karlskoga during the summer of 1875 by Erik Tullius Hammargren, her aunt's husband. It was during this period, when she was staying in Karlskoga, that she drew inspiration for the novel Charlotte Löwensköld from 1925, where she refers to Karlskoga as Korskyrka.

After completing her education, Lagerlöf spent a few years working as a governess before, in the autumn of 1882, she began attending the Higher Teachers' Seminary in Stockholm, against her father's wishes. During Lagerlöf's time at the seminary, her family fell into financial difficulties, and Mårbacka had to be sold. Her father died in 1885, the same year that Lagerlöf completed her studies to become a teacher.
