TL;DRSweden aims to make it easier for the police to obtain digital evidence, such as emails or chat logs, from companies located in other EU countries. This is intended to help solve crimes more quickly when the evidence is stored abroad.
In plain language

Digital evidence is extremely important when investigating crimes. Since a great deal of information is stored by companies in countries other than Sweden, Swedish authorities need better ways to request access to this data. To address this, the EU has created new rules that Sweden is now proposing to implement as law.

The government proposes that prosecutors should be able to send requests to other EU countries to obtain electronic evidence. This also means that the prosecution authority may have the right to determine penalties if the rules are not followed. The purpose is to make cooperation between countries smoother when evidence is needed.

Another proposal is that certain companies that provide digital services must have a person or a location responsible for making decisions about evidence. An authority will monitor whether the companies comply with these rules and may also impose sanctions, which is another term for penalties or fines, if they do not.

The new laws are intended to come into effect during the summer and August 202kommandot 26. This is part of a larger effort to comply with the new rules established within the EU.

Full text

Electronic data is increasingly playing a crucial role as evidence in criminal investigations. Since such data is often stored with service providers outside of Sweden, law enforcement agencies need better and more effective cross-border tools to access this information. Against this backdrop, the EU Regulation on European Production Orders and European Preservation Orders for electronic evidence ([EU] 2023/1543) and the EU Directive on designated points of contact and legal representatives for the collection of electronic evidence in criminal proceedings ([EU] 2023/1544) were adopted.

The government proposes the introduction of a new law with supplementary provisions to the EU Regulation. This proposal includes, among other things, that prosecutors, and in certain cases other law enforcement agencies, should be able to issue a European Production Order or a European Preservation Order. Prosecutors should be able to decide on the enforcement of an order issued by an authority in another Member State. The Public Prosecutor's Office should be able to decide on sanctions for violations of the EU Regulation.

The government also proposes a law to implement the EU Directive, which, among other things, means that certain service providers must designate a recipient for decisions and requests concerning the collection of electronic evidence. A central authority is proposed to have supervisory responsibility and the power to decide on sanctions.

The law with supplementary provisions to the EU Regulation is proposed to enter into force on August 18, 2026. The law implementing the provisions of the EU Directive is proposed to enter into force partially on July 1, 2026, and partially on August 19, 2026. Certain consequential amendments are proposed to enter into force on August 18, 2026.

Proposed Resolution of the Riksdag (Parliament)

Prop. 2025/26:147

Government Proposal:

  1. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposal for a law containing supplementary provisions to the EU regulation on European production orders and preservation orders for electronic evidence.
  2. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposal for a law on designated operational points or legal representatives for the collection of electronic evidence.
  3. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2007:980) on supervision of certain law enforcement activities.
