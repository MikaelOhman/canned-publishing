In plain language

Christine Lagarde, President of the European Central Bank (ECB), discusses the economic situation in Europe. Following the outbreak of a war in the Middle East, energy prices have risen, leading to an increase in inflation – that is, the rate at which prices are increasing – to 3.2 percent in May.

To counter this, the ECB has decided to raise interest rates by 0.25 percentage points. An interest rate increase makes it more expensive to borrow money, which helps to keep price increases in check. The ECB aims for a stable inflation rate of 2 percent in the long term.

The European economy is still growing, but at a slower pace than initially hoped. The war has increased uncertainty about the future and made it more expensive to live. At the same time, businesses and households are still in a relatively stable financial position.

The future remains uncertain due to the war and the risk of high energy prices. Therefore, the ECB will continue to closely monitor new information and make new decisions at each meeting. They have not yet decided whether to raise or lower interest rates further in the future, but will make adjustments gradually based on how the economy develops.

Brussels, June 22, 2026

It is a pleasure to return to this committee for our regular dialogue.

When we last met in February, inflation had been close to the ECB's target of 2% for more than a year, and economic activity in the Eurozone showed solid growth.

Just a few days later, a war broke out in the Middle East, reminding us of how quickly external factors can change the economic situation.

The topic you have chosen for today's discussion – how monetary policy should be conducted in an environment characterized by increased geopolitical tensions and frequent disruptions to supply chains – is therefore particularly relevant.

In my speech, I will first review the latest data on the Eurozone economy and explain our recent decisions regarding monetary policy. I will then explain in more detail how our monetary policy strategy guides us in managing crises such as the one we are facing today.

The Eurozone economy was on a positive trajectory when the war in the Middle East broke out. Real GDP increased by 0.3%, per quarter, in the first quarter of 2026, after adjusting for exceptional volatility in Ireland.

The war is now negatively impacting activity, and the latest information suggests a slowdown, particularly in the service sector. The manufacturing sector has so far fared relatively well. This is partly due to increased inventories in response to problems in supply chains, but also to increased defense budgets.

According to the Eurosystem's June 2026 forecasts, real GDP growth is expected to be 0.8% in 2026, 1.2% in 2027, and 1.5% in 2028.

The staff now expects domestic demand to be weaker than they predicted in March, as the war has eroded consumer confidence and higher energy costs are affecting real incomes. At the same time, household finances remain largely stable, and consumption should continue to be the main driver of growth. Investments should also be supported by companies' investments in new digital technologies, and by increased government spending on defense and infrastructure.

The war is also contributing to the increased inflation, which rose to 3.2% in May, compared to 3.0% in April. The main driver of inflation since February has been rising energy prices, which were over 10% in April and May. In May, inflation excluding energy and food also increased to 2.6%, partly reflecting the initial indirect effects of higher energy prices.

As a result of this energy surge, inflation expectations over the short term have risen well above the levels observed before the outbreak of the war in the Middle East. However, the public currently does not expect the high inflation to be long-lasting: most measures of long-term inflation expectations are around 2%, which supports a stabilization of inflation around the medium-term target.

We are therefore confident that inflation will return to the target, provided that appropriate measures are taken in monetary policy. The Eurosystem's June forecasts predict an inflation rate of 3.0% in 2026, 2.3% in 2027, and 2.0% in 2028.

However, the outlook remains uncertain, with upward risks for inflation and downward risks for economic growth. The peace agreement in the Middle East is welcome, but the situation remains fragile, with a risk of setbacks or escalation. The full consequences of the war for medium-term inflation and growth depend on the intensity and duration of the decline in energy prices, as well as the extent of its indirect and other effects.

This uncertainty is reflected in the wide range of possible outcomes for inflation and growth in the alternative scenarios that our staff have developed as part of their forecasts, and which are published on our website.

In line with our commitment to ensuring that inflation stabilizes at our target level of 2% in the medium term, we decided at our meeting in June to raise the three key interest rates of the ECB by 25 basis points.

This decision to raise interest rates is robust across the scenarios developed by our staff, meaning that an interest rate increase is justified in all scenarios. Recent developments have remained within the range of scenarios considered.

With this decision, we are well-positioned to manage the uncertainty caused by the war. We will carefully monitor developments and use a data-driven approach, where each decision on monetary policy is made at each meeting. Our decisions on interest rates will, in particular, be based on our assessment of the development of inflation and the risks associated with it, taking into account the current economic and financial information, as well as the underlying dynamics of inflation and how effectively monetary policy is transmitted to the economy. We do not commit ourselves in advance to a specific interest rate path.

Let me now explain in more detail how our monetary policy strategy guides us in an environment characterized by frequent supply shocks – and how it supported the decision we made in June.

The latest shock emerged suddenly and developed rapidly, but it did not entirely surprise us.

Our strategic review for 2025 specifically focused on how monetary policy should be adapted in an environment characterized by higher uncertainty and more frequent supply shocks. Our strategy is designed to ensure that we can continue to maintain price stability under such conditions. [1]

It is based on three principles: [2]

First, we must carefully assess the nature of the supply shock – its magnitude, duration, and scope.

Monetary policy cannot directly lower energy prices. However, it must assess to what extent higher energy costs spread to other prices – what we call "indirect effects" – and risk triggering further effects through wages and pricing.

Both the magnitude and duration of the shock are crucial. Small, short-lived shocks typically have limited effects beyond the energy component. However, as shocks become larger and more prolonged, their impact can increase in a non-linear way, leading to stronger and more widespread effects on prices and wages. [3]
