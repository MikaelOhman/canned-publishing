Anders Chydenius, born on February 26, 1729, in Sotkamo, Kajanaland, and died on February 1, 1803, in Gamlakarleby, Middle Ostrobothnia, was a priest, physician, member of parliament, and an author writing about economics and politics. As a liberal, he played a key role in Sweden obtaining the world's first freedom of the press law in 1766.

== Biography == Anders Chydenius was the son of Jakob Chydenius, a chaplain who later became the vicar of Gamlakarleby, and Hedvig Hornæus. His brother, Samuel (1727–1757), was a renowned naturalist and inventor. After studying at the Royal Academy in Åbo and Uppsala University (where Pehr Kalm was one of his teachers), Chydenius earned a candidate's degree in philosophy in Åbo in 1753 and a master's degree in 1754. He had been ordained as a priest the year before and served as a chaplain in Nedervetil, from which he was transferred to Gamlakarleby in 1770, where he remained until his death.

The Chydenius family name was adopted by Anders Chydenius's grandfather (also named Anders, who died in 1711) when he entered school in Åbo. The name refers to the ancestral estate of Kytyniemi in Nykyrko.

During his studies, he devoted himself to natural sciences. At his chaplain's residence, he set up a laboratory for producing medicines, wrote prescriptions, performed operations (primarily on patients with eye conditions), invented an eyewash, and was a strong advocate of inoculation. He also proved to be a diligent pastor and a skilled practical farmer.

He used his free time to study economics, primarily the works of Nordencrantz and the French physiocrats, and he held a strong distrust of the state's coercive measures that were then believed to promote economic growth. He soon began a career as a writer and received the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences' silver medal for his essays "On the best way to cultivate moss-covered meadows" (1763) and "On improving pastures" (1764). However, he caused considerable controversy with his competitive essay submitted in 1763, "What may be the cause that such a large number of Swedish people annually emigrate from the country? And by what regulations can it best be prevented?", in which he expressed the view that all laws restricting freedom of work should be abolished. In particular, he had learned firsthand in his hometown about the harmful effects of the Stockholm staple monopoly on Åbo and Gävle, and his 1765 publication, "Refutation of the reasons by which one seeks to dispute the free navigation of the eastern and western Bothnian cities," was printed at the expense of the city of Gamlakarleby. In 1765, he sharply and concisely criticized the 1724 product proclamation in "The Source of the Kingdom's Weakness," which prohibited foreign ships from transporting anything other than their own country's products to Sweden. This work provoked counter-arguments, and Chydenius responded with rebuttals.

Chydenius's most important work is "Den nationnale winsten" (1765), which deals with economic liberalism and its social effects. In the book, Chydenius proposes a theory that resembles Adam Smith's "invisible hand," although Smith did not describe this concept until 1776 in "The Wealth of Nations."

These writings brought him widespread attention, and in 1765–1766, the chaplains of Österbotten elected him as their representative to the Diet. His economic views led him to join the "Hats," where he played a significant role in resolving issues related to staple city obligations and peasant navigation. The Hats had learned to value freedom of the press during their long period in opposition and were determined to establish it, but the 1766 Freedom of the Press Ordinance would not have been enacted as broadly if it were not for Chydenius. He was instrumental in the "Great Secret Committee" drafting committee, with the help of citizens and peasants, in successfully pushing through a proposal to the Estates to abolish censorship (except for theological writings), along with two important principles that were later adopted by the 1809 Constitution: public access to documents and the principle that freedom of the press is a fundamental right. Despite the opposition from the nobility, the committee's request became a decision of the Riksdag.

However, before this regulation came into effect, Chydenius himself experienced that freedom of expression was not yet fully guaranteed. He had written a pamphlet titled "The Kingdom's Help Through a Natural Financial System" (1766), in which he highlighted what he believed to be harmful measures proposed by the secret committee for restoring banknotes to their nominal value – measures that, as experience later showed, were far from unfounded. The censors initially granted permission for the publication, but revoked it after a warning from the marshal of the realm, Thure Gustaf Rudbeck. Nevertheless, Chydenius received permission from his estate to publish the work, which caused immense attention and simmering resentment among the leaders of the "Möss" party. The secret committee summoned Chydenius for questioning and, under the pretext of protecting him from harsher penalties, they stripped him of his eligibility to serve as a member of parliament for the current and immediately following parliamentary sessions in July 1766.
