Clarifications and additions regarding sanctions for tax evasion and fraud.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the rules to make it easier to punish tax crimes and fraud. The proposal means that even incorrect oral statements can lead to fines or penalties.
In plain language

The government has proposed a measure that will make it more difficult to avoid punishment for tax crimes. Currently, there are exceptions that mean oral statements are not always treated the same way as written ones, but the government wants to remove this exception. This means that if someone provides incorrect information verbally, they can now be held liable for violations of tax law.

The proposal also concerns tax surcharges, which is a type of fee that must be paid to the Swedish Tax Agency. The government also wants oral statements to be admissible as evidence for collecting these fees. The purpose is to make the rules clearer and close loopholes that are exploited in tax crimes.

Another part of the proposal concerns VAT fraud. The government wants to change the definition of what constitutes an incorrect statement regarding value-added tax, which is commonly known as VAT. If a person provides information about a transaction that is actually part of a fraud, it will be considered incorrect if the person knew about or should have understood that it was a fraud.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on April 1, 2026. The proposal is included in a document submitted to the Parliament, called proposition 2025/26:43.

Full text

The government proposes to remove the exception for oral statements regarding tax offenses, negligent tax reporting, and tax return fraud. This means that oral statements can also lead to criminal liability under the tax code. The government also proposes that oral statements can serve as a basis for tax surcharges.

Furthermore, the definition of the term "incorrect information" is proposed to be amended. This is to clarify that a person who provides information regarding value-added tax related to a transaction that was part of a value-added tax fraud scheme is considered to have provided incorrect information if the person knew or should have known about this.

The proposed legislative changes are scheduled to come into effect on April 1, 2026.
