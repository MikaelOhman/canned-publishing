In plain language

The government has proposed changes to the laws governing how individuals are convicted and sentenced for crimes. The goal is to ensure that punishments better reflect the severity of the offenses, making them more just for both victims and society.

A key component of the proposal is the introduction of a system of conditional imprisonment. This involves modifying how courts choose between different types of penalties. The proposal also aims to make it easier to impose harsher sentences on individuals who commit multiple crimes by aggregating all offenses when determining prison time.

The proposal also includes the removal of certain regulations that were previously used to reduce sentences. The government also intends to improve rules related to, among other things, contact bans, care for substance abusers, and how individuals are monitored whether they are incarcerated or serving a sentence in the community. All changes will come into effect when the government determines.

Full text

In the proposal, the government presents several suggestions aimed at creating a more just penal system where punishments better reflect the severity of the crime, the imposed sentences appear more reasonable and fair, the perspective of victims is given greater weight, and societal safety is improved. The government proposes, among other things, the following:

  • A system of conditional imprisonment will be introduced. The presumption against imprisonment will be removed, and the penalties of suspended sentence and supervised release will be phased out.
  • Sentencing should be carried out in a more nuanced manner.
  • In cases of multiple offenses, subsequent crimes should have a greater impact on sentencing. Sentencing should be based on the total severity of the crimes for which the defendant is convicted.
  • Certain mitigating factors, known as "equity considerations," will be removed.

The proposed legislative changes are to come into effect on the date determined by the government.
