Per Daniel Amadeus Atterbom, born January 19, 1790, in the parsonage of Pålsbo in the parish of Åsbo, Östergötland, and died July 21, 1855, in Stockholm, was a Swedish author and critic. Atterbom was a professor at Uppsala University, a member of the Swedish Academy from 1839, and wrote the romantic work "Lycksalighetens ö" (The Isle of Bliss). He became a professor of theoretical philosophy at Uppsala University in 1828 and of aesthetics and modern literature in 1835. He was elected a member of the Swedish Academy in 1839. He was the grandfather of Ebba Atterbom and taught the future King Oscar I during the years 1819–1821. The Atterbom family originates from Lars Hemmingsson, a farmer in Ledberg, Östergötland, who was born during the second half of the 16th century. The surname was adopted by his great-grandson, the parson Jonas Hemmingi Atterbom, after the estate of Attorp in the parish of Rappestad, where he was born in 1663. Atterbom's literary works are characterized by their musicality and emotional depth. They often feature freely adapted fairy tales. Critics argue that Atterbom never managed to distance himself from his own self-centeredness, as his personal conflicts permeate his writing. He is best known for the poetic epic "Lycksalighetens ö" and "Fågel blå" (The Blue Bird), which he began working on in 1811. Both were referred to by him as fairy plays. "Lycksalighetens ö" was published in two parts in 1824 and 1827. "Fågel blå" was initially published starting in 1814 as a fragment in the "Poetisk kalender." Atterbom wrote "Svenska siare och skalder 1-6," Sweden's first true history of Swedish literature, from 1841 to 1855, and he received recognition for this work within Swedish aesthetics. Previously, he had published "Studier till philosophins historia och system" (Studies in the History and System of Philosophy) in 1835. Despite his great success, Atterbom was also sharply criticized by many of his contemporaries and sometimes openly mocked. His conservative Christian beliefs and lyrical romanticism stood in stark contrast to the emerging liberalism and its realism.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood === Atterbom was the son of the pastor Gabriel Atterbom and his deeply religious wife, Hedvig Kristina Kernell, and his childhood proved crucial to his future poetry and philosophical outlook. He was precocious and began writing at a young age. At the age of eight, he wrote a novel titled "Alphonsus and Marina, a story created by me." Introverted and physically frail, he became an avid reader. By the age of ten, he had independently learned German from Pufendorf's universal history and Hübner's geography, and had read Mörck's "Adalrik and Göthilda," Wallenberg's "Susanna," Dalin's poems, Snorre Sturlasson's sagas, the Völuspá, Cornelius Nepos, and more. In 1799, he was sent to school in Linköping, where he lived with his maternal uncle, Per Kernell (father of Per Ulrik Kernell), who was a member of the consistory. He was one of Carl Gustaf af Leopold's closest friends, and in Leopold's well-stocked library, he diligently expanded his knowledge. He became acquainted with the works of Leopolds, Kellgren, Lidner, Bellman, and Franzén, and was deeply impressed by them. However, he even more admired two German authors: Christoph Martin Wieland and, above all, Gottfried August Bürger.

=== A Romantic in Uppsala ===

In 1805, Atterbom became a student at Uppsala University, joining the Östgöta nation, and in the autumn of the same year, he joined the society "Vitterhetens vänner," which soon dissolved. His father's death in 1807 led to a depression that would recur and plague Atterbom throughout his life. During this crisis, he came into contact with the aesthetic and philosophical ideas of German early Romanticism, including works by authors such as Novalis and Tieck. Together with like-minded friends, he founded the society "Musis amici" in 1807, which changed its name to "Auroraförbundet" the following year. From 1810, the society published the journal *Phosphoros*, where intense propaganda was conducted. Atterbom's main contribution to this was as a lyric poet, but he also wrote polemical writings. The journal ceased publication in 1813.

The "Auroraförbundet" soon took on the task of reforming the Swedish cultural elite, and the most important dates in Atterbom's life during the second decade of the 19th century mark significant events in Swedish literary history. Through their study of modern German literature, the members of the "Auroraförbundet" had developed entirely different concepts of the nature of poetry than those on which their contemporary Swedish lyric poets based their work. These Swedish poets were at the beginning of the 19th century in a period of epigonal imitation and decline, aptly described as the "iron age" of Swedish literature. Most of the poems written were moralizing verses and reflections, lacking sincerity and strength of feeling, and characterized by monotonous meter, with the alexandrine verse being particularly common. Through his dreamy and contemplative nature, his childhood impressions of the idyllic beauty of Åsbodal, and the Christian piety prevalent in his family home, Atterbom embraced the new directions that had emerged in German literature, replacing the French classical school.

These new directions were based on imagination rather than reason. The goal was to express feelings and moods in poetry through suggestive means, such as color imagery, visual expressions, and acoustic effects.
