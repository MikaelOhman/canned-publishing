Carl Jonas Love Almqvist (also known as Carl Jonas Lovis Almquist; on his gravestone, Carl Jonas Ludvig Almquist, and he called himself Carl Westermann for a period; born November 28, 1793, in Stockholm; died September 26, 1866, in Bremen) was a Swedish author, teacher, priest, romantic economic critic, and composer. Almqvist wrote in various genres, including prose, drama, poetry, as well as journalism and textbooks. A significant portion of his literary works were collected within the framing narrative "The Book of the Briar Rose."

Almqvist grew up on the estate of Antuna, near Lake Edssjön in present-day Upplands Väsby, a few miles north of Stockholm. His childhood home played an important role in his writings, with various locations described with strong feelings of nostalgia. In 1808, he was admitted as a student at Uppsala University. In 1823, he abandoned all his commitments and traveled to live an idealized rural life in Värmland, inspired by Jean-Jacques Rousseau. In 1824, he married Anna Maria Andersdotter Lundström. In 1829, he became the headmaster of Nya Elementar. In 1839, he joined Aftonbladet and subsequently supported himself as a journalist and author, which proved difficult despite his prolific output. Financial problems led him to borrow money from lenders. In 1851, he fled the troubles in Sweden and went to the United States. He was accused of various crimes, including counterfeiting and attempted arsenic poisoning, but could not be prosecuted in Sweden because he was in the USA. He remarried without having divorced in Sweden, making him guilty of bigamy.

In 1865, he left the United States and traveled to Germany. He died in Bremen the following year, never returning to his homeland.

== Biography ==

Carl Jonas Love Almqvist was the son of Carl Gustaf Almquist (1768–1846), a military commissioner, and Birgitta Lovisa Gjörwell (1768–1806), daughter of the journalist C.C. Gjörwell. The parents married in 1791. The mother passed away in 1806 after a long illness, leaving behind three minor children.

Almqvist's maternal grandfather was an enthusiastic supporter of diverse academic interests, which Almqvist displayed early on and which Gjörwell mentions with great pleasure in his letters. Almqvist's reading and writing skills developed significantly during his childhood. He often spent his free time at the estate of Antuna in the parish of Eds, Uppland, where his father lived as a farmer.

The family home and its surroundings became the focal point of his world; he frequently used them as settings for his stories, where parishes, farms, streams, and other places are constantly mentioned with a strong sense of belonging and affectionate descriptions.

Almqvist was unusually well-read and educated for his age, and in 1808 he enrolled as a student at Roslags nation at Uppsala University. From 1814 to 1820, he worked for Johan Hisinger as a tutor for his son, Michaël, who later became a cavalry officer in the Swedish Royal Guard. The family spent the winters in Stockholm but summers on their estate, Fagervik, near Ekenäs in Finland. At the same time, Almqvist pursued his own studies and was awarded a doctorate in philosophy in 1815, along with Atterbom, Palmblad, and K. E. Fahlcrantz.

=== Trip to Värmland === Shortly after his graduation, Almqvist became an extra permanent clerk in the ecclesiastical department and, in 1817, a copyist and, in 1822, a regular clerk in that office, where Stagnelius also worked. However, he resigned from his clerical position in 1823 and moved to Värmland at the beginning of the following year, to live an idealized rural life. Having been influenced early on by Rousseau, his mother's favorite reading material, Almqvist had long dreamed of a lifestyle that harmoniously combined a life of piety and contemplation with physical labor in nature.

In 1816, he was elected to the Manhemsförbundet, a religious patriotic society, and became its chairman. He developed an organizational plan, which was published in 1820. In this plan, he expressed ideas that he now, together with two other members of the Manhemsförbundet, Johan August Hazelius and Jonas Wærn, were trying to put into practice.

From the estate of Skillingsfors, located in the parish of Köla, which belonged to Wærn, he purchased a farmstead called Gravsund for 1,000 Riksdaler, and had a simple farmhouse moved there. In the church of Skillingmark in Värmland, he married Anna Maria Andersdotter Lundström (1799–1868) on March 4, 1824. She had been raised at Antuna, where she worked as a nanny for Love's half-siblings. "She was then fourteen years old," Almqvist wrote in a letter to a friend. "Our love blossomed and grew – unnoticed, unknown, like a simple rose in a dark grove." He had been her boyfriend since 1812, although an official engagement did not take place until 1823. The couple had a son, Carl Ludvig Wilhelm (1825–1886), and a daughter, Maria Lovisa (1829–1893). "I abandoned the entire conventional life," Almqvist wrote, "not only in terms of literature, but also in lifestyle, and settled in a rural area, not as a lord, but as a very real farmer, performing the work with my own hands." He called himself "the educated man Love Carlsson."
