Full text

Abrahamus Andreæ Angermannus, also known as Master Abraham or Abraham Angermannus or Abraham Andreæ Angermannus, was born around 1540 in Sidensjö and died in December 1607 at Gripsholm Castle. He was a Swedish archbishop from 1593 to 1599.

== Biography == After studying at the Gävle preparatory school and later at Rostock University, Angermannus became a schoolmaster and preacher in Stockholm, where he sharply criticized Johan III and those sympathetic to Catholicism, including sending bitter verses to the Jesuit Laurentius Nicolai Norvegus, known as "Klosterlasse." This conflict led to his transfer to Öregrund in 1576, where he served as a preacher under the special supervision of the Archbishop. The following year, he was transferred again to Saltvik on Åland, where he was appointed pastor. However, the situation eventually became untenable, and after a period of imprisonment at Turku Castle, he was forced into exile in Germany, where he lived for 13 years with his entire household, generously supported by King Charles IX (who was then Duke of Södermanland). This controversy gave Angermannus considerable support in Sweden, as many considered him to be a man of integrity who dared to stand up for what he believed in.

When Johan III died in 1592, Angermannus was elected Archbishop the following year and ordained as bishop in 1594 by Petrus Benedicti. The election of Angermannus as Archbishop was reluctantly approved by King Sigismund, but it was the Bishop of Strängnäs who performed the coronation. It was Angermannus who personally insisted on the requirement that Sigismund accept the decisions of the Uppsala Assembly and that Sweden be freed from foreign influence. As part of this effort, he undertook a visitation tour of Sweden in 1596-1597, with the approval of Duke Charles, where he used harsh methods to try to eliminate anything that did not conform to Lutheran dogma in the various parishes. This included everything from old Catholic and pagan traditions to witchcraft and extramarital sexual activity; however, unlike the major witch trials of the 17th century, Angermannus's investigations apparently did not result in any death sentences (see Gunnil from Visingsö). The records from the investigations in the Diocese of Linköping (which then included Östergötland, Öland, and Småland, excluding Värend, Finnveden, and Njudung) are preserved in a manuscript published in 1901 by Otto Holmström under the title "Archbishop Abraham's Investigations." Despite this religious conflict, Angermannus stood with the clergy on Sigismund's side when Charles IX declared war (see the war against Sigismund). This was partly because Charles IX had prevented him from visiting the country's churches to ensure that a purely evangelical line was maintained throughout the land, but more importantly, it was due to a principled loyalty to the king (opposition to rebellion). This loyalty to Sigismund resulted in Angermannus being imprisoned at Gripsholm Castle after a trial at the Diet of Jönköping in 1599, where he was succeeded as Archbishop by his opponent, Nicolaus Olai Bothniensis. He died there after eight years of imprisonment in 1607. Angermannus was married to Magdalena, daughter of Archbishop Laurentius Petri Nericius.
