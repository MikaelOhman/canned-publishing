Hans Karl August Simon von Euler-Chelpin, born February 15, 1873, in Augsburg, Germany, and died November 6, 1964, in Stockholm, was a German-Swedish chemist and Nobel laureate in chemistry in 1929. He often referred to himself as Hans Euler. In Sweden, he worked at the University of Stockholm, starting in 1906 as a professor. During World War I, he served as a German officer, first in the artillery and later in the air force.

== Biography ==

=== Scientific Career === Von Euler-Chelpin began his studies in Munich in 1890, initially studying art for two years. An interest in the properties of colors led him to switch to the natural sciences. He then studied chemistry at Humboldt University of Berlin, where he earned a doctorate in philosophy in 1895. Subsequently, he dedicated himself to physical chemistry, first at the University of Göttingen under Walther Nernst, and after 1897 at the University of Stockholm under Svante Arrhenius. During his travels abroad, he worked in physical chemistry with van 't Hoff in Berlin, in organic chemistry with Arthur Hantzsch at the University of Würzburg, and with Friedrich Karl Johannes Thiele at the University of Strasbourg, as well as in physiological chemistry at the Pasteur Institute in Paris. In 1899, Euler-Chelpin was appointed as a docent (associate professor) in physical chemistry, in 1904 as a laboratory director in chemistry, and in 1906 as a professor of general and organic chemistry, all at the University of Stockholm. In 1907, he founded the Chemical Society in Stockholm. Von Euler-Chelpin was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1914 and became a member of the Royal Institute of Technology in 1930. In 1929, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry jointly with Arthur Harden, for "studies on the fermentation of sugars and fermenting enzymes."

=== Research Activities === Initially, Euler-Chelpin worked in various fields of chemistry and was a prolific researcher and author of numerous scientific papers across different areas. His early work focused on physical chemistry, including his studies on the theory of catalysis, solubility phenomena, and ammonia-containing metal compounds; organic chemistry, such as his investigations of diazo compounds; and biochemistry, including his research on metabolism, carbon assimilation, sugar formation in plants, and the action of enzymes.

During several investigations in the early 20th century, he collaborated with his then-wife, Astrid (Cleve) von Euler-Chelpin, daughter of the renowned chemist Per Teodor Cleve. He built a significant research environment at Stockholm University and later concentrated his efforts on biochemistry. He dedicated much attention to alcoholic fermentation and the factors influencing it. He provided a comprehensive overview of this in "Neuere Forschungen über alkoholische Gärung" (in Fortschritte der naturwissenschaftlichen Forschung, volume 10, 1913) and in "Chemie der Hefe und der alkoholischen Gärung" (together with P. Lindner, Leipzig, 1916). Together with his students, he investigated the effects of light, temperature, acidity, oxidizing agents, toxins, and nutrients on enzymes, including a series of publications under the collective title "Über die chemische Zusammensetzung und Bildung der Enzyme" (numbers 1-15, 1910-17), in 4 publications (jointly with Professor O. Svanberg) titled "Über giftwirkungen bei Enzymreaktionen" (in Fermentforschung 1920), and in "Darstellung hochaktiver Saccharasepräparate" (together with Svanberg, 1919). His research showed that a coenzyme, NAD, is necessary for the action of enzymes during glycolysis. It was his research on fermentation and enzymes that earned him the Nobel Prize. von Euler-Chelpin also devoted considerable effort to researching vitamins, whose chemical properties remained enigmatic well into the 1920s. von Euler-Chelpin published most of his research results in "Arkiv för kemi," "Zeitschrift fur physiologische Chemie," and "Biochemische Zeitschrift." He also wrote several monographs, including "Växtkemi" (I, 1907; II, 1908), "Untersuchungsmetoden biochemisch wichtiger Lichtwirkungen" (in Handbuch der biochemischen Arbeitsmethoden, Berlin, 1913), "Allgemeine Chemie der Enzyme" (Wiesbaden, 1910; English translation by Pope, New York, 1912), and "Chemie der Enzyme" (I, 1920; II, 1922, Munich and Wiesbaden).

=== Officer During World War I === During World War I, from 1914 to 1918, Hans von Euler-Chelpin served as a German officer. From 1914 to May 1915, he was an officer in the Bavarian 1st Field Artillery Regiment, serving at Somme, and after a brief training period as an aerial observer, at Cambrai (1915). He participated as an observer in the battles of the 6th Combat Squadron at Verdun (summer 1916) and in the operations of the 4th Army in Flanders (summer 1917). From December 1916 to February 1917, he carried out a mission in Turkey. During the winter of 1917–1918, he was the commander of an aviation unit at Douai, and in the summer of 1918, he commanded a bombing squadron in Lorraine.

=== Pro-German === Von Euler-Chelpin, who was born in Germany, was a member of the Swedish-German Society. He was also a member of the Rik (National) organization, which was founded in 1937.
