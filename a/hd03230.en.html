Compensation for restrictions on use due to nature conservation regulations.

Source Riksdagen Read the original at Riksdagen →2026-04-08
TL;DRThe government proposes new regulations to provide landowners with compensation if the protection of animals and plants makes it difficult to use their land. The purpose is to clarify what rights individuals have when the state imposes restrictions on nature.
In plain language

The government has proposed an amendment to the Environmental Code, the law that governs how we manage our natural resources. The proposal concerns granting compensation to individuals who own land or forests if state regulations for protecting animals and plants make it difficult to use the land as they previously did.

Currently, there are regulations for compensation when the state, for example, creates nature reserves or water protection areas. The new proposal would also allow compensation if the state denies permission for certain activities on the land due to species protection regulations. Species protection regulations are the rules designed to ensure that protected animals and plants are not harmed.

The proposal also includes a provision that the state can reclaim the money if the restriction on the landowner is lifted or if the landowner does not comply with the established rules. This provision already exists for other types of protected areas.

The goal of the amendment is to make it easier for the state and landowners to reach agreements without resorting to legal proceedings. If the parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on a date determined by the government.

Full text

The proposal includes suggestions aimed at ensuring that property owners receive adequate compensation when regulations to protect animals and plants result in restrictions on ongoing land use. According to the proposal, the Environmental Code's rules regarding compensation for interventions by the government, which apply, for example, to restrictions on property rights due to certain area protection decisions, should also apply to restrictions on property rights that are a consequence of decisions to completely or partially deny exemptions from the species protection ordinance's protected species regulations.

The proposal means that the state should have the possibility to reclaim the compensation in situations where the restriction resulting from the species protection no longer exists, or if measures are taken that are inconsistent with the decision that provides the basis for the compensation. The same applies to compensation already paid for area protection decisions.

By clarifying the right to compensation in law, the conditions are created for the state to be able to reach voluntary agreements with property owners who claim compensation due to the species protection regulations.

The proposed amendments to the law should enter into force on the date determined by the government.
