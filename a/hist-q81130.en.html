Anders Celsius, born on November 27, 1701, in Uppsala, and died there on April 25, 1744, was a Swedish scientist and astronomer who served as a professor of astronomy at Uppsala University. He is best known today for the Celsius scale, a hundred-degree thermometer scale. A unit of temperature is named after him and is denoted by a capital C: °C.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Career ===

The Celsius family originates from the vicarage of Högen ("Höjen") in Ovanåker, Hälsingland. Anders Celsius was the son of astronomer Nils Celsius (1658–1724) and Gunilla Spole (1672–1756), daughter of professor of astronomy Anders Spole. Even Celsius' grandfather, Magnus Celsius, was a prominent researcher in astronomy and mathematics, and his uncle, Olof Celsius the Elder, was a distinguished philologist.

Anders Celsius was a student of Eric Burman, who had himself been a student and successor to Anders Celsius' father as professor of "higher mathematics" (astronomy). When Burman died, Celsius was unanimously elected in 1730 to succeed him as professor. Two years later, he embarked on a journey outside of Sweden that lasted five years. The main purpose was to thoroughly study more prominent observatories in Europe. In 1730, he published "Nova Methodus distantiam solis a terra determinandi" (A new method for determining the distance between the sun and the earth).

Abroad, he first gained attention with his publication in Nuremberg (1733) entitled "Observationes de lumine boreali ab A. MDCCXVI ad A. MDCCXXXII partim a se, partim ab aliis, in Suecia habitas" (Observations of the aurora borealis in Sweden from 1716 to 1732, partly by the author himself, and partly by others). In Rome, where the Pope granted him access to his large gallery on Monte Cavallo, he conducted optical experiments and discovered, among other things, that the moonlight during a crescent moon is 8 times weaker than the full moon's light, that the sun has a brightness 320,000 times greater than the moon's, and that at its highest point in the sky, the sun shines with a light 130 times stronger than at the horizon.

Celsius supported the establishment of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1739, although he was not one of the original six founders, and he suggested the name "Academy of Sciences" instead of the initial proposals of "Economic Science Society" or "Science Guild." Celsius and Samuel Klingenstierna became the first members elected to the academy. In 1730, he was elected a member of the Royal Scientific Society in Uppsala.

=== The Paris Project === In Paris, Celsius participated in an intense scientific debate regarding the shape of the Earth. Some believed, like Newton, that the Earth was flattened at the poles, while others, following Cassini's ideas, envisioned it as a lemon or an egg. Astronomical measurements of one degree along a meridian arc within France, conducted by Picard in 1670, were not accurate enough to resolve the dispute. To solve the problem, two sets of measurements were needed: one closer to the equator and another closer to the pole.

The French Academy of Sciences decided to fund such measurements. An expedition led by Bouguer was sent to Peru (present-day Ecuador) in 1735 and lasted for seven years. At Celsius's suggestion (inspired by an idea from Polhem), a second expedition, led by Maupertuis, was sent to Tornedalen in 1736–37. This expedition included Celsius, Anders Hellant, and Jonas Meldercreutz.

The observations were accompanied by arduous journeys. There were no roads, and it involved not only living in the wilderness for months but also transporting delicate and expensive instruments through forests and swamps to remote mountain peaks. A detachment from the nearest Finnish regiment was dispatched to assist. The soldiers cleared trees on eight mountaintops, and conical markers made of barked tree trunks were erected. The results from both expeditions showed that a degree of latitude is longer in Lapland than in Peru, which confirmed Newton's predictions.

During his stay in Torneå, Celsius established a meteorological station there, whose records for the years 1737–49 are still preserved.

=== Uppsala Observatory ===

Upon his return to Uppsala, Celsius advocated for the establishment of an observatory there. Funding was approved, and the Uppsala Astronomical Observatory, the first in Sweden, was completed in 1741. Celsius then enthusiastically continued his observations, through which he shed new light on the movement of planets, comets, stellar aberration, the refraction of light in the atmosphere, and more. Celsius also contributed to overcoming Sweden's hesitation in finally adopting the Gregorian calendar.

Celsius believed that the prevailing view that weather was dependent on the positions of the planets was unreasonable. Through easily accessible writings, he attempted to eradicate the superstitions, particularly those related to celestial bodies and their movements, that were deeply ingrained among the common people. In popular publications, he made it easy for people to learn about what scientists had discovered.
