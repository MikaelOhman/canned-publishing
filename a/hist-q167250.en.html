Full text

Albrecht of Mecklenburg (German: Albrecht III. von Mecklenburg), born around 1338 in Mecklenburg, died on April 1, 1412, in Doberan, Mecklenburg. He was King of Sweden from 1364 to 1389 and Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin from 1379 to 1412. He was the son of Duke Albrecht II and the Swedish princess Eufemia Eriksdotter, and his reign marked the introduction of the House of Niklot (also known as the Mecklenburg dynasty) into the line of Swedish rulers.

Albrecht reigned as King of Sweden from 1363/1364 to 1389, when he was deposed (although he formally abdicated in 1405). He also served as Duke of Mecklenburg, first as co-regent from 1379 after his father's death, and then as sole regent from 1384 following the death of his brother Henrik. It wasn't until 1395, when Albrecht was released from Queen Margaret's captivity, that he could personally return to Mecklenburg and resume his rule there. As ruler of Mecklenburg, his correct titles were Prince Albrecht III of Mecklenburg and Duke Albrecht II of Mecklenburg and Schwerin.

== Becoming King of Sweden ==

Albrecht was the second son of Duke Albrecht the Great of Mecklenburg and the Swedish King Magnus Eriksson's sister, Eufemia. Albrecht was next in line to inherit the Swedish crown if King Magnus and his son, Håkan Magnusson, were to die. Furthermore, according to some sources, the Dukes of Mecklenburg may have descended from the Sverker dynasty through Kristina of Mecklenburg, who would then have been the daughter of Sverker the Younger.

The background to the uprising against Magnus Eriksson is a complex story. Briefly, Magnus's son, Håkan Magnusson, King of Norway, imprisoned his father because Magnus refused to accept the peace treaty at Greifswald in 1361, arguing that the Swedish envoys had overstepped their authority. In February 1362, Håkan was formally elected King of Sweden at Mora Sten, but shortly thereafter, father and son reconciled and co-ruled Sweden, with Håkan receiving his share of Sweden (the area of Skara diocese). During this time, a brief war was fought against Denmark and Valdemar Atterdag, but peace negotiations began in the autumn of 1362. During these negotiations, Håkan chose to marry Valdemar's daughter, Margaret. One reason for this may have been to regain Skåne. However, Swedish nobles acting on Håkan's behalf had already arranged an engagement with Elisabeth of Holstein, which was now broken. Protests erupted among the nobles, and the situation escalated into a more or less open rebellion. Even Saint Bridget expressed her displeasure with Magnus. As a result, several leading nobles were exiled by King Magnus in 1363.

The exiled nobles, led by Chancellor Bo Jonsson (Grip), went to northern Germany, where they turned to Duke Albrecht the Great of Mecklenburg and offered the Swedish crown to his son, Albrecht the Younger. Albrecht the Elder eventually agreed to allow a council aristocracy to participate in governing if they supported his takeover in Sweden. The Germans landed in Sweden with 1,600 men, including knights, soldiers, and mercenaries. They marched north without encountering significant resistance, and the German-friendly cities of Stockholm and Kalmar surrendered with little bloodshed. In November 1363, Albrecht the Younger was acclaimed in Stockholm. Magnus was deposed and fled to Norway, and on February 18, 1364, Albrecht was formally elected King of Sweden at Mora Stenar. The nobles who had been exiled by Magnus were rewarded and given various high positions by Albrecht.

Albrecht of Mecklenburg was the first king to use three crowns as the national emblem. He felt that he could not use the previous national symbol, the Folkung lion, but also not his family crest, a crowned black bull's head, because this was used by his father, who was the ruling Duke of Mecklenburg. The issue must have been crucial: the king's coat of arms would not only be a unifying symbol of the realm and an official mark of possession; its appearance on seals also had a decisive legal significance, since the legal validity of an issued document depended on its sealing. The seal itself could not convey what colors Albrecht chose for his new coat of arms. Only in a rhyming chronicle, which Ernst von Kirschberg began in 1378, does it appear that the crowns were gold, set in a blue field with red emeralds (i.e., the Mecklenburg colors: red, blue, and yellow). These colors are still used today. In current legislation on the national emblem, it is called the "small national emblem."

In 1365, Magnus returned to Sweden with his son, Håkan Magnusson, along with a Norwegian army and a force of Swedes loyal to Magnus. Near Enköping, at Gata forest, Magnus and Håkan were defeated by the Germans, and Magnus was captured by Albrecht.

King Albrecht was supported not only by his father but also by several North German princes and the Hanseatic cities. On King Håkan's side, Danish King Valdemar Atterdag joined the cause. The Mecklenburgers also faced a difficult enemy in the Swedish peasantry, who disliked the German influence. Parts of the peasantry rose up and supported Håkan, and the situation became critical for Albrecht when Håkan laid siege to Norrmalm near Stockholm in 1371.
