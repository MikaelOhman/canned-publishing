Katarina Gustavsdotter (Stenbock), born July 22, 1535, at Torpa manor near Borås in the present-day municipality of Tranemo, and died December 13, 1621, at Strömsholm, was Queen of Sweden from 1552 to 1560, as the wife of Gustav Vasa in his third marriage.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and before the marriage ===

Katarina was the daughter of Gustav Olofsson (Stenbock), a member of the royal council, and Brita Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud), and the niece of Gustav Vasa's second queen, Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). She was also the cousin of the king's eight children with Margareta. Her parents, like the other members of Margareta's family, were known as "royal relatives" and held a prominent position at the Swedish court during Margareta's reign. The king often attended their family celebrations, such as weddings, just as they attended his. Katarina's parents were considered close friends of Gustav Vasa: her father rose to prominence in the royal council due to his loyalty to the king, and the monarch was said to have "always had great trust" in her mother: "He always had great trust in her [Margareta]'s sister, Birgitta."

Little is known about Katarina's life before her marriage. The typical upbringing for a Swedish noblewoman during this time involved learning to read and write by studying moral religious texts. This education prepared young women to become wives capable of managing a large household and acting as their husbands' representatives in their absence, which also meant they learned arithmetic, estate management, and economics. It is possible that she spent some time at court as a lady-in-waiting, although there is no record of this, or that she at least met the king during family celebrations. Katarina was approximately 155 centimeters tall, had light blonde hair, and blue eyes, and was described as beautiful. According to tradition, Katarina was unofficially engaged to Gustav Johansson (Tre Rosor) before her marriage, an engagement that was broken off for the sake of the king. After her marriage, she was once said to have been overheard by the king talking in her sleep: "I love King Gustaf very much; but I will never forget Rosen."

When Queen Margareta died in August 1551, the king first asked Kristina Gyllenstierna to take over responsibility for the royal children. However, when she was no longer able to do so due to old age, Katarina's mother, "Lady Birgitte," was asked, along with her aunt Märta and Märta's husband, to take over the responsibility at Christmas 1551. It was considered a given that the king would remarry: it was expected of noble widows with minor children, and the king himself stated that he needed a queen for his court and a mother for his children. In March 1552, Katarina's mother, her aunt Märta, Märta's husband, and Per Brahe were summoned to a family council at the king's residence in Västerås, and it is likely that this was when the king proposed marriage between himself and Katarina. The negotiations are believed to have been conducted with her mother. The Stenbock family welcomed the proposal, which would ensure that the influence the family had gained through Margareta's marriage would be secured through Katarina.

The clergy, represented primarily by the Archbishop and the Bishops of Strängnäs and Västerås, opposed the marriage because it would not be in accordance with the laws of the Bible according to Leviticus 18, due to the close relationship between Margareta and Katarina. Although it was not explicitly stated that marrying a deceased wife's niece was forbidden, marriages with a husband's widow were prohibited, which was taken as a reference to the prohibition against marrying relatives of a deceased spouse. However, Gustav Vasa asserted his will, arguing that the Old Testament only applied to Jews. He appointed an investigation led by Georg Norman, who pointed out that the Bible allowed marriage to a deceased wife's sister, therefore a deceased wife's niece should be permitted. The king called for an official meeting with the bishops in Linköping in July, where they submitted formal protests against the marriage. The king then persuaded the members of the royal council to support the marriage and confirm the king's right to marry regardless of the church's opinion, which the bishops then promised to respect.

At this time, the marriage with Katarina was justified by the fact that the king needed to remarry to have a queen and a mother for his children. Because international politics made it difficult, time-consuming, and expensive to begin lengthy negotiations for a marriage with a foreign princess, it was instead considered best to choose a bride from the Swedish nobility. According to the official justification, it was not about personal preference that Katarina was chosen. At this time, there were also few candidates of the right age in the Swedish nobility, and Katarina's family and parents were highly regarded by the king. In the spring of 1552, the king visited the estate of Torpa to propose marriage to Katarina in person. According to tradition, she allegedly tried to hide behind a bush in the garden.
