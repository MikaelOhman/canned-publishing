Karin Maria Boye, formerly Björk, born October 26, 1900, at Vasaplatsen 11 in Gothenburg, and died on the night of April 24, 1941, in Alingsås (registered as living in the parish of Oscars, Stockholm), was a Swedish author, poet, and translator. She was best known for her poetry, but she also wrote several novels, as well as short stories and articles. Of her prose works, the dystopian science fiction novel *Kallocain* has received the most attention.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Education ===

Her father, civil engineer Fritz Boye (1857–1927), and her mother, Signe (1875–1976), née Liljestrand, both came from wealthy families; Fritz's father was the merchant Eduard Boye. Karin Boye felt that she was treated "exceptionally well." In 1907, she began attending Mathilda Halls school in Gothenburg. She achieved high grades in school. Her siblings were Sven (1903–1974) and Ulf (1904–1999). When her father changed jobs to the Swedish Insurance Inspectorate in Stockholm, the family moved there in 1909. In 1915, the family settled in Huddinge, at the location where Sjödalsgymnasiet high school is now located. There, in a wooded area with birch trees, tall pines, and oaks, a tall residential building had been built, painted Falun red with white trim. The family named their new home Björkebo (Birch Home). She wrote much of her youthful poetry, short stories, and plays there, as well as drawing and painting watercolors. Less known about Karin Boye is that she created many personal watercolor paintings featuring mythical figures. These later occupied an entire room at Prince Eugen's Waldemarsudde museum.

=== University Studies === Karin Boye graduated from Åhlinska school in Stockholm in 1920, and obtained her teaching certificate from Södra seminariet in Stockholm in 1921. She then moved to Uppsala to study Greek, Scandinavian languages, and literary history. In 1918, at a Christian summer camp in Fogelstad, she met Anita Nathorst, whom she later reconnected with in Uppsala. Nathorst, who was seven years older, had studied theology and humanities at Uppsala University. During her final year at university, Boye joined the Clarté organization. She obtained a philosophical degree from Stockholm University College in 1928; however, severe overexertion and stress had forced her to leave Uppsala, although the specific reasons are unclear. In addition, she served as chair of the Women's Student Association and wrote for the student newspaper Ergo.

=== Early Career ===

In 1922, she debuted with the collection of poems "Moln" (Clouds), which conveyed a young person's reflections on God, the imperfections of life, and her own future. However, it also showcased her unique characteristics and recurring themes. Formally, one notices the flowing rhymes and the tendency towards alliteration (which would be further emphasized when she read the poems of the Edda in Icelandic at Uppsala University). But above all, the distinctive rhythm is striking, with stressed syllables following each other. In the subsequent collections "Gömda land" (Hidden Land) in 1924 and "Härdarna" (The Hearthstones) in 1927, she calls for courage, struggle, and sacrifice, drawing on Christian themes. In 1925, she delivered her famous speech "Tal till mannen" (A Speech to Man) at the student union's spring celebration. Her first novel, "Astarte," won a prize in a Nordic literary competition in 1931.

In 1927, Karin Boye became a member of the editorial board of the socialist journal "Clarté." She also helped found the journal "Spektrum" and was part of its editorial team from 1931 to 1932, along with Josef Riwkin, Erik Mesterton, and Gunnar Ekelöf. In "Spektrum," Boye published her essay "Språket bortom logiken" (The Language Beyond Logic), in which she argued for a new poetic language based on psychoanalytic principles. She also contributed some of the capital for this initiative (her father had died in 1927, and the inherited money from him made her financially independent for a few years).

In 1931, she was elected to the "Samfundet De Nio" society.

=== Coming to Terms with Herself === During a stay in Berlin from 1932 to 1933, she took the step of living more openly with her homosexuality than before. Her marriage to Leif Björk dissolved during that year in Berlin, and when she returned to Sweden, her friends perceived her as having changed. She was more elegant, less interested in the actively Marxist side of Clarté, and perhaps more vulnerable than previously. After a while, she invited a young German-Jewish woman, Margot Hanel, whom she had met and "seduced" (her own words) in Berlin. The two women lived together from 1934 until Boye's death – at a time when homosexuality was still illegal in Sweden. Boye's deep love for Anita Nathorst was never reciprocated.

Boye likely became aware of her lesbian or bisexual side sometime around the publication of her debut book. This attraction was something she struggled to accept, because it seemed to require her to live as a man; moreover, it was difficult to talk openly about it. Margit Abenius, who knew Boye from her student years and significantly influenced perceptions of her, traces this problem back to her teenage poems. Even in the poems and legends she wrote during her adolescence, Boye often identified with male heroes, and their acts of sacrifice could often be seen as erotic.
