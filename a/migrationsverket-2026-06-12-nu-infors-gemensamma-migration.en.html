June 12, 2026

On June 12, 2026, the EU's migration and asylum pact will come into force across the entire European Union. Together with 26 other countries, Sweden will have a common framework for managing and receiving individuals seeking international protection (asylum). The EU's migration and asylum pact represents significant changes in how migration is managed to and from the EU.

"The goal of the EU's migration and asylum pact is to create clearer and more uniform rules within the EU and to improve control at the EU's external borders. At the same time, the right to asylum must be protected," says Maria Mindhammar, Director General of the Swedish Migration Agency.

The background is the large number of refugees received in recent decades, particularly during 2015, which led EU countries to believe that something needed to be done to establish a common approach and crisis preparedness in the area of migration. Following extensive cooperation, member states agreed in 2024 on a new framework that will be implemented on June 12, 2026, and which reforms the asylum and immigration systems of member states.

The migration and asylum pact involves changes in a number of areas with the aim of improving efficiency and shortening the time it takes for individuals to receive decisions within the reception system. It also includes faster border checks for individuals who, for example, are not considered eligible for international protection.

There will be stricter controls at the external borders of member states for migrants who lack the necessary entry permits. This control is called "screening" and involves checking identity, health, vulnerability, and security.

In Sweden, this screening process is carried out in collaboration with the Police Authority and regional healthcare providers at the Swedish Migration Agency's reception and return centers in Malmö, Mölndal, Boden, and Märsta. The Police Authority is primarily responsible for the screening, while the regional healthcare providers are responsible for the health checks.

The migration and asylum agreement also introduces a so-called solidarity mechanism, which means that EU countries can jointly plan and provide support to member states that receive a large number of people seeking international protection.

Over the past two years, the Swedish Migration Agency has worked intensively to prepare for the new regulations. In this preparatory work, the agency has collaborated within the EU and with various national stakeholders and authorities, including the Police Authority, the Judicial Administration, the Prison and Probation Service, and the municipalities and regions of Sweden.

"The Swedish Migration Agency will continue to develop its operations in collaboration with other actors, so that we can have an efficient and legally sound process that protects the rights of migrants," says Veronika Lindstrand Kant, Director responsible for the project at the Swedish Migration Agency.
