In plain language

Gideon Sundbäck was born in Sweden in 1880 and later moved to the United States. There, he worked as an inventor and industrialist. He became famous for improving the zipper so that it functioned well and achieved great success.

Although the idea of a slide fastener existed previously, Sundbäck created the modern zipper through his patents in 1913 and 1917. He also invented a machine that could automatically manufacture these fasteners.

Before Sundbäck's involvement, zippers were difficult to produce well and were sewn by hand onto fabric strips. Thanks to Sundbäck's machines, mass production became possible, which made the product a huge international success.

Full text

Otto Fredrik Gideon Sundbäck, born April 24, 1880, at Sonarps farm in the parish of Ödestugu, and died on June 21, 1954, in Meadville, Pennsylvania, was a Swedish-American inventor and industrialist.

== Biography == Sundbäck was the son of Jonas Otto Magnusson Sundbäck (born 1846) and Kristina Karolina Klasdotter (born 1852), and he had five siblings. Sundbäck developed and improved the zipper. His first patent, for the "Hookless #1" or "Plako" zipper, was approved on April 29, 1913. However, it was his second patent, "Hookless #2," approved on March 20, 1917, that became the modern zipper we know today. He also invented the machine that automatically manufactured zippers, which contributed to its worldwide success. Previously, they were sewn by hand onto fabric strips. The idea of a zipper was not new; Elias Howe received a patent for an "umbrella fastener" in 1851. Whitcomb Judson was the first to manufacture zippers on a larger scale, but none worked well until Sundbäck was hired by his company in 1906.
