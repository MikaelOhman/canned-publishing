Johan Gustafsson Banér, also known as Jan Banér, was born on June 23, 1596, at Djursholm, and died on May 10, 1641, in Halberstadt. He was a Swedish military commander; he held the rank of colonel in 1620, became a member of the Council of State and a general in 1630, and Field Marshal in 1634. He belonged to the noble family Banér.

== Biography ==

Johan Banér was born at Djursholm Castle on June 23, 1596. His parents were Gustav Axelsson Banér and Kristina Svantesdotter Sture; he was their twelfth child. At the age of four, he lost his father, who, like his uncle Sten Axelsson Banér, was executed in the Linköping Bloodbath in 1600.

Banér is considered one of Sweden's greatest military commanders of all time. As a 19-year-old, he participated in the war against Russia. Due to his skills as an organizer and troop leader, he quickly rose through the ranks.

In the Battle of Breitenfeld in 1631, one of the Swedish army's greatest victories, he commanded the right wing.

After Breitenfeld, Banér was sent north with a force to secure supply lines to the Swedish-controlled ports on the Baltic Sea. In August 1632, he rejoined Gustav Adolf's army. During a reconnaissance mission, Banér was wounded in the arm by a musket ball.

In 1634, Banér was appointed Field Marshal to replace Field Marshal Gustaf Horn, who had been captured during the Battle of Nördlingen. His task was to rebuild and train a new army, as the troops were nearly disbanded, which he reportedly accomplished successfully, quickly restoring order and readiness.

In 1636, the Swedish army, under his leadership, won a major victory over the combined forces of the Holy Roman Empire and Saxony in the Battle of Wittstock. The following year, in 1637, he besieged Leipzig, but after being forced to retreat by Austria under Gallas, he successfully led the army's withdrawal to northern Germany. It was at this time that the Imperial generals reportedly commented that they now had "Banér in the bag," a remark he later responded to with the words: "They forgot to tie it." Banér capped his successful military career with victories in the Battles of Chemnitz and Melnitz in 1639. He also led a devastating campaign into Bohemia, during which his army largely destroyed the region.

Banér was known for his large appetite for food, women, and strong drinks. He was considered to be quick-tempered, but due to his good leadership qualities, he was appreciated by his subordinates. His enemies saw him as a rough and cunning individual, the epitome of a commander where initiative and courage compensated for a lack of tactical skill. His temperament and lack of diplomatic skills often led to conflicts with allied commanders.

There are different opinions about the cause of his death. Banér was ill for several weeks before his death and suffered an "attack" before dying of cirrhosis of the liver, according to author Peter Englund. Author Gunnar Wetterberg suggests that he may have also had malaria. Banér died at 4:15 AM on May 10, 1641, and is buried in Riddarholmskyrkan (Riddarholm Church) in Stockholm, in the Banér family vault.

Marriages:

  1. Married in 1623 to Catharina Elisabet von Pfuel.
  2. Married on July 25, 1636, to Countess Elisabeth Juliana of Erpach.
  3. Married on September 16, 1640, to Margravine Johanna Margareta of Baden-Hochberg. His last wedding was planned to take place near Banér's army at Arolsen Castle, but as it was still within range of the enemy, he brought a guard of 3,000 cavalry and 1,000 musketeers, who provided protection during the ceremony.
