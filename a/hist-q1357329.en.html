Full text

Per Brahe the Elder, born in May 1520 at Lindholms Manor, and died on September 1, 1590, in Stora Sundby, Södermanland, was a knight, Swedish Lord High Chancellor, Count of Visingsborg, and Lord High Constable. He was the son of Joakim Brahe, a member of the Swedish Council of State, and Margareta Eriksdotter (Vasa), who was the sister of Gustav Vasa.

== Biography ==

Per Brahe was kidnapped along with his mother and two older sisters in Danish captivity following the Stockholm Bloodbath in 1520. He accompanied her when she fled from Viborg Castle to Reval (Tallinn) and Germany in the summer of 1534, together with her second husband (married in 1525), Count Johan of Hoya, after he had fallen out of favor with the king. He inherited Rydboholm Castle and Lindholmen estate from his mother when she died in 1536, and was summoned to Stockholm by Gustav Vasa in 1538, while his half-brothers Jost and Johan remained in Germany. As early as 1539, he was appointed chamberlain, and in 1540, he became one of the governors of Tre Kronor Castle in Stockholm. In 1540, he was granted Gudhem Monastery by Gustav Vasa, and later in the latter half of the 16th century, he was also granted Norra Ljusterö.

Brahe distinguished himself during the Dacke Rebellion, where his role was to recruit and manage contacts with the hired mercenaries who were supposed to suppress the uprising. He became a member of the Council of the Realm in 1544 and was mentioned around 1546 as a knight. He was highly regarded by Gustav Vasa and received several important assignments. During the reign of Erik XIV, he worked to limit the power of Dukes Johan and Karl. He contributed to the adoption of the Articles of Arboga in 1561. The same year, he was ennobled as Count by Erik XIV during his coronation, and in 1562, he received almost the entire island of Visingsö as a county.

During Erik XIV's reign, Brahe participated in foreign missions, such as to Scotland in 1562 and to Poland in 1564. He participated in the Nordic Seven Years' War, and commanded the sieges of Älvsborg and Bohus Fortress in 1565, but neither his status as Count nor his kinship with the king protected him from criticism from the king. After the Sture murders in 1567, the central government was mainly managed by Sten Eriksson Leijonhufvud and Per Brahe during Erik XIV's mental illness. During Daniel Rantzau's attack in 1567, he was appointed advisor to Field Marshal Hogenskild Bielke.

Per Brahe cautiously joined the rebellion of the Dukes only in 1568 when the prospects of success seemed secure. In 1569, his county was significantly expanded by Johan III (from 61 farms to just over 500), and he was appointed Lord High Constable during the Diet that same year. Like most nobles, he had a strained relationship with Duke Karl, partly due to the fact that the duke was involved in inheritance disputes with him. In religious matters, he seems to have been wavering for a time. The Jesuits had great hopes of converting him. He confessed to Catholic priests and received communion from them, but his will contains no Catholic tendencies. Despite being close to the royal family, he, like many other members of the Council of the Realm, lost Johan III's trust in 1589 and was stripped of his office as Lord High Constable. He died on September 1, 1590, at the main estate of his son-in-law, Stora Sundby, and is buried in Östra Ryd Church.

Per Brahe left behind material for a continuation of Peder Swart's chronicle, which was published by Otto Ahnfelt in 1896–1897. He also wrote "Oeconomia eller Huuszholdzbook, för ungt adelsfolck" (first printed in 1673, reprinted in 1920), and a religious work titled "Tröstebok Dom," which was first printed and published in 1673 by Brahe's grandson, Per Brahe the Younger.

== Family ==

Per Brahe married Beata Stenbock (1533–1583) on March 10, 1549, at Gripsholm Castle. Beata was the daughter of Gustaf Olofsson (Stenbock), a Field Marshal, and Brita Eriksdotter Leijonhufvud, who was the sister of Gustav Vasa's second wife, Margareta Leijonhufvud. Per and Beata had thirteen children:

  • Joakim (1550–1567)
  • Erik (1552–1614)
  • Margareta (1553–1553)
  • Cecilia (1554–1554)
  • Ebba (1555–1634)
  • Katarina (1556–1596)
  • Gustav (1558–1615)
  • Margareta (1559–1638)
  • Anna (1562–1565)
  • Magnus (1564–1633)
  • Johan (1566–1566)
  • Sigrid (1568–1608)
  • Abraham (1569–1630)
