Katarina Karlsdotter Vasa, born on November 10, 1584, at Nyköpingshus, and died on December 13, 1638, in Västerås, was a princess of Sweden. She was the daughter of the future King Charles IX and Maria of Palatinate, and she married her cousin, Count John Casimir of Palatinate-Zweibrücken, on June 11, 1615 (the cousins were related through Philip the Noble of Hesse, who was John Casimir's maternal grandfather and Katarina's paternal great-grandfather).

After Queen Christina abdicated, Katarina's son, Queen Christina's cousin, became King of Sweden under the name Charles X Gustav, thus becoming the first ruler from the Palatinate lineage. Katarina is not only a link between the Vasa dynasty and the subsequent Palatinate royal house, for which she is the matriarch through her son, Charles X Gustav, but also, through her daughter Christina Magdalena, a link between the Vasa dynasty and the two later Swedish royal houses, Holstein-Gottorp and Bernadotte.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Katarina was orphaned at the age of five in 1589, and her upbringing was overseen by her lady-in-waiting, Euphrosina Heldina von Dieffenau, known as "Miss Heldina," who was a widow of a German officer serving in the Swedish army. She was employed at the court in 1590 and remained there after her marriage, continuing to serve as Katarina's lady-in-waiting. Katarina maintained contact with Heldina until the latter's death in 1636. Following her father's remarriage to Kristina of Holstein-Gottorp in 1592, Kristina took over Katarina's upbringing. The relationship with her stepmother was described as good, but correspondence between Katarina and her stepfather revealed that it wasn't always without friction. Katarina's personality was described as a combination of her father's "strength and wisdom" and her mother's "gentle kindness." Her half-brother, who succeeded their father as King Gustav II Adolf in 1611, considered her intelligent and valued her advice.

Her father's marriage to her mother, Maria of Palatinate, had been a union with the anti-Habsburg faction in Germany, which in turn had connections with Henry IV of France and French Huguenot leaders. Between 1599 and 1600, there were plans for Katarina to marry a relative of Henry IV, the Huguenot leader Vicomte, Duke Henry II of Rohan. Despite her substantial dowry, marriage negotiations for Katarina were difficult due to Sweden's precarious political situation following her father's seizure of the throne. It was only after the Treaty of Knäred in 1613 that her international standing on the marriage market improved. With the support of her stepmother, Queen Kristina, and through mediation by Kristina's brother, Archbishop Johan Fredrik of Bremen, a marriage was arranged with her relative, the Reformed Johan Casimir of Palatinate. Johan Casimir was poor, but his political connections were considered valuable for Sweden. Axel Oxenstierna, however, opposed the marriage.

The wedding took place in Stockholm on June 11, 1615. After her wedding, Katarina remained in Sweden. Through her parents' wills and the law regarding Swedish princesses' dowries, she was one of Sweden's largest creditors at a time when the country was in a difficult economic situation, and she therefore remained in Sweden to protect her interests. It was only in January 1618 that the couple left Sweden for Germany. Upon arriving in Germany, the couple settled at Kleeburg Castle in northern Alsace, offered by Katarina's brother-in-law, Johan II. In 1619, her husband began construction of the Renaissance castle Katharinenburg near Kleeburg. Based on the couple's correspondence, it appears that they eventually developed a happy relationship. In 1620, the couple was forced to flee to Strasbourg due to the outbreak of the Thirty Years' War.

=== Upon her return to Sweden === In 1622, her half-brother, Gustav II Adolf, asked her to return to Sweden, which she did with her family. They arrived in June 1622. Because the king himself was still childless and his younger brother had died, there were concerns about the succession. Katarina's position was strengthened by the birth of her son shortly thereafter. The couple received Stegeborg Castle and its surrounding lands in Östergötland from the king. This grant was given to them as collateral for Katarina's unpaid dowry. In 1626, Katarina herself received Skena Manor as a personal possession. Her main interest seems to have been managing the family's estates. In her correspondence, she appears as an enterprising and efficient housekeeper, frugal, and financially savvy.

The couple reportedly enjoyed life at Stegeborg and maintained a court of sixty people, including ladies-in-waiting, gentlemen, and an official table served at eight tables according to rank. Johan Kasimir emphasized Katarina's royal status during her time in Sweden and was careful to maintain a princely standard and ceremonial protocol. Katarina had a very good relationship with her siblings, especially with Gustav II Adolf. The king sometimes sought her advice and, during his travels, asked her to comfort his wife, Maria Eleonora. In 1631, when Maria Eleonora was allowed to accompany Gustav II Adolf to Germany, Katarina, at his request, took responsibility for the heir apparent, Kristina, and retained custody until Maria Eleonora returned to Sweden after the king's death.
