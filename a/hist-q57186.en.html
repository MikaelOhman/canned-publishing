Samuel von Pufendorf, born January 8, 1632, in Dorfchemnitz, Saxony, and died October 26, 1694, in Berlin, was a German historian, Swedish baron, and political theorist, known for his theories on natural law and international law.

== Biography == Pufendorf was the son of Lutheran pastor Esaias Elias Pufendörfer and his wife, Margarete Hickmann. He had two brothers: Jeremias Pufendorf and Esaias von Pufendorf.

From 1645 to 1650, he attended the Fürstenschule Grimma secondary school. Subsequently, at his father's request, he began studying theology in Leipzig, but soon switched to law. In 1656, he enrolled at the University of Jena, where he was greatly influenced by Professor Erhard Weigel, who introduced him to the works of Hugo Grotius, Thomas Hobbes, and René Descartes. Two years later, he earned a master's degree.

Shortly thereafter, he obtained a position as a tutor in the household of Peter Julius Coyet, who served as minister to Charles X Gustav with duties in Copenhagen. Pufendorf's brother, Esaias, simultaneously became a Swedish diplomat. Due to political tensions between Sweden and Denmark, Pufendorf was imprisoned. During his imprisonment, he studied and wrote. After his release, he followed Coyet's sons to Leiden, where he published a work on universal law, "Elementorum iurisprudentiae universalis libri duo," which earned him a professorship in Heidelberg. He was appointed to a professorship at Lund University upon its founding in 1666 and held this position until the university was closed during the Scanian War. In 1677, he became the Royal Historiographer in Stockholm, and in 1688, he was invited to the court in Berlin by Frederick William of Brandenburg (sovereign Duke of Prussia).

Pufendorf also wrote a highly critical "Introduction to Swedish History," in which he defended Sweden's conquest of Scania and parts of Norway, arguing that these provinces had originally been Swedish: "the old borders of Sweden restored." This interpretation of history was controversial and difficult to support with sources, and Pufendorf's already strained relationship with Danish scholars worsened.

He was ennobled in Sweden and elevated to the rank of Baron.

== Political Philosophy == Pufendorf's political philosophy is a form of natural law contract theory. He himself linked his thinking to other natural law philosophers such as Hugo Grotius, John Selden, Thomas Hobbes, and Richard Cumberland. Pufendorf believed that natural law aims to teach people how to become virtuous members of society, and therefore primarily concerns the relationship between individuals. Although Pufendorf saw God as the source of natural law, he distinguished between natural law and moral theology, meaning that societal norms did not necessarily need to be linked to biblical revelation.

Pufendorf's contract theory is most thoroughly discussed in *De jure naturae et gentium* (1672) and in a more concise form in *De officio hominis et civis juxta legem naturalem* (1673; hereinafter referred to as *De officio*). In these works, the influence of Grotius and Hobbes is evident. Like Grotius, Pufendorf emphasizes the human right to self-preservation and the need for society. Unlike Grotius, who was a rationalist, Pufendorf's natural law philosophy was voluntarist. Pufendorf's contract theory and his emphasis on absolute sovereignty were influenced by Thomas Hobbes' *Leviathan*, but he also differed in important ways, primarily regarding the view of human life in the state of nature.

Both Grotius and Hobbes wrote their major works during the Thirty Years' War, but while Grotius sought to limit the damage caused by war through law, Hobbes theorized about how conflicts could be limited through the creation of strong nation-states. Pufendorf's generation (he was born in the same year as John Locke and Baruch Spinoza) was the first whose political philosophy was based on the Westphalian system of nation-states that followed the Thirty Years' War.

Like many natural law philosophers, Pufendorf starts with a state of nature. Unlike Hobbes, who asserted that human life in the state of nature was "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish and short," Pufendorf paints a positive picture of a nature filled with endless and free resources (*De officio* I.12.2). The image of humanity is darker. Humans, Pufendorf points out, are extremely weak creatures not at all suited to living alone. A human raised without contact with other humans would live alone in the wilderness, subsisting on what nature provides; naked and without language, a human would be like any other animal (*De officio* I.2.1). Humans are therefore dependent on each other for survival.

However, humans are dangerous to other humans and are not naturally social. In this way, Pufendorf breaks with the Aristotelian, teleological view that humans are by nature political and social beings meant for community. What causes people to cooperate is their "sociality" (Latin: *socialitas*); a kind of moral principle that does not stem from human nature but from something else.
