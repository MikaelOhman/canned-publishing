Charles XIV John, born Jean Bernadotte on January 26, 1763, in Pau, Béarn, France, and died on March 8, 1844, at Stockholm Palace, was King of Sweden and Norway from 1818. In Norway, he became known after his death by the regnal name Charles III John. He married Désirée Clary on August 16, 1798, in Paris; she later became the Swedish-Norwegian queen under the name Desideria and was the sister-in-law of Napoleon's brother, Joseph Bonaparte. Upon his arrival in Sweden in 1810 as Prince of Ponte Corvo, his full name had evolved to Jean Baptiste Jules (which was translated into Swedish as Johan Baptist Julius, the form used in the Succession Order). He adopted the name Charles after his adoptive father, Charles XIII.

== Biography ==

=== Charles XIV John's Background ===

Charles XIV John was born on January 26, 1763, in Pau, the capital of the province of Béarn in southwestern France. He was the youngest of five siblings, two of whom died in childhood. His father and uncle were both lawyers, as was his older brother who was eight years his senior. Today, you can still visit his birthplace in the French city of Pau, which is now a museum with both a French and a Swedish flag hanging above the entrance. According to Charles XIV John's own accounts, he was born in the house "la maison Balagué" at number 24 rue Tran, whose main entrance faces the street that later became known as rue Bernadotte. The birth took place in a room on the balcony one floor up in the part of the house with windows facing rue Tran. Later in his life, as king, he tried to purchase the property to establish a military hospital, but was unsuccessful due to the owner's demands.

The birth was not expected for several more weeks, but the mother was startled by some carnival participants who burst into the room where she was working on needlepoint. "Madame Jeanne" became so frightened that she suffered a nervous breakdown and had to be taken to bed. After a restless night, her son Jean was born in the morning of January 26th. Because there were concerns that the premature baby would not survive, he was baptized the very next day at the church of Saint Martin. Originally named Jean Bernadotte, he was also called Jean Baptiste Bernadotte (as he was named after John the Baptist) to distinguish him from his older brother, Jean Évangéliste Bernadotte (1754–1813). His sister was named Marie Bernadotte Justis (1759–1795). His father was Henri Bernadotte (1711–1780), and his mother was Jeanne Saint-Jean, born de Saint-Jean (1728–1809). Jean Baptiste, whose education seems to have taken place at home, was initially expected to follow in his father's profession. However, he showed no interest in this, and after his father's death, he had to seriously consider his future. Driven by a strong interest in military life, on September 3, 1780, he enlisted in the 60th French line regiment, Royal-la-Marine, which was then stationed in Corsica.

The records noted the following about the new soldier: "height 5 feet, 3 inches and 5 lines, dark hair, dark eyebrows, brown eyes that slant slightly downwards, a long, pointed nose, a small mouth with thin lips, a short, rounded chin, a round and full face, a narrow forehead with two scars, one in the middle and one above the eyebrow, caused by a small stone." Recruit Bernadotte number 651 received a soldier's name, as was customary. He was called "Belle-Jambe," which translates to "Beautiful Leg." This nickname apparently alluded to his ability to charm the opposite sex.

=== Advancement within the army ===

Bernadotte spent three years on Corsica. After a visit to his home for a few months at the end of 1783 and the beginning of 1784, to improve his health, he followed his regiment to new garrison towns – including Grenoble, Vienne, Marseille, and Saint-Martin-de-Ré. During this time, he advanced from grenadier to corporal on June 13, 1785, to furier (sergeant) on June 21, 1786, to sergeant-major (field warrant officer) on May 11, 1788, and finally to adjutant on February 7, 1790. Perhaps his career advancement would have stopped there if the French Revolution had not broken down the barrier that was established in 1781 between non-commissioned officers and commissioned officers. During his time as a non-commissioned officer, Bernadotte demonstrated initiative on several occasions. For example, after arriving in Marseille on September 6, 1789, he managed to save his commander, Marquis d'Ambert, from a popular uprising in 1790. On May 1, 1792, he was appointed lieutenant in the 36th Regiment (formerly Anjou), stationed in Saint-Servant in Brittany. At another time, while the regiment was stationed in southern France, in the small town of Lambesc near Marseille, where it was based for a few weeks, Bernadotte used his eloquence to prevent the soldiers from mutinying against the officers. This incident occurred in an abandoned church where some companies were housed. Bernadotte climbed onto the pulpit and delivered a reprimand to the rebellious soldiers, who then abandoned their attempt at mutiny.

While stationed in Marseille, Bernadotte was provided with lodging papers and presented himself to the wealthy and highly respected merchant in Marseille.
