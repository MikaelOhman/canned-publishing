Charles XV, Carl Ludvig Eugen, born May 3, 1826, at Stockholm Palace in Stockholm, and died September 18, 1872, at the residence in Malmö, was King of Sweden and Norway from July 8, 1859, until his death in 1872. Before ascending to the throne, he was Duke of Skåne. In Norway, he was known by the regnal name Charles IV, but during his lifetime, he was also called Charles XV there. He was the son of King Oscar I and Queen Josephine. He married Louise of the Netherlands on June 19, 1850. He was popularly known as "Kron-Kalle."

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Education ===

Like his siblings, Karl was raised under the supervision of Christina Ulrika Taube. He received education from professors such as Christopher Jacob Boström and Fredrik Ferdinand Carlson, and he took a private university entrance exam in December 1843. In 1845, he enrolled at Uppsala University, and during the spring of 1844, he studied in Christiania (Oslo). Agrén at the Art Academy, the genre painter Lundqvist, was the prince's art teacher, and Captain Johan Christian Berger taught him watercolor painting. The Crown Prince demonstrated a talent for art and literature, with a particular interest in patriotic works such as Pehr Henrik Ling's "Asarne," an epic poem published in three parts.

On Midsummer Eve 1841, he received his first officer commission from his grandfather, King Karl XIV Johan. After his father ascended the throne in 1844, he was, according to a tradition dating back to the 18th century, appointed Chancellor of the universities of Lund and Uppsala in April of the same year, and in 1853, also Chancellor of the Royal Academy of Fine Arts. He began his studies at Uppsala University in the autumn of 1844. On February 11, 1846, he became the first honorary member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. Within the Swedish army, he held subordinate officer ranks in the Svea Life Guards, the Svea Artillery Regiment, and the Kronprins' Hussar Regiment, but eventually rose to Colonel in both units. On April 21, 1847, he was appointed Major General and commander of the Lifeguards Brigade. In 1849, he became Quartermaster General, and on April 19, 1853, he was promoted to Lieutenant General. Similar promotions were also bestowed upon him in the Norwegian army.

During a trip abroad in 1849, he visited the court in The Hague, and on June 19, 1850, he married Princess Louise of the Netherlands. The wedding was celebrated in Stockholm.

Karl was appointed Vice King of Norway on February 7, 1856. During his visits to Norway, he spent much time in nature, sketching scenes that he later transformed into paintings. He was interested in politics and wrote a prose story, "Fosterbröderne," which was published in 1848. This work, along with another book, "Heidi, Gylfes dotter" (1855), were only published for friends. In 1852, he unsuccessfully competed for the Swedish Academy's prize for poetry.

During the late 1850s, the good relations between the Scandinavian countries led to a close friendship between Crown Prince Karl and King Frederick VII of Denmark, who had known each other since 1846, after a visit to Copenhagen. This positively influenced Scandinavian politics. King Oscar later initiated a Scandinavian pact with the aim of strengthening cooperation between the countries and adopting a common stance on various issues.

Oscar I had long suffered from episodes of unconsciousness, and by the summer of 1857, it became clear that he had an incurable brain tumor. Because for half a year, there had been doubts about whether the Council of State, consisting of 10 Swedish and 10 Norwegian government ministers, could achieve its goals, the Riksdag (Swedish parliament) and Storting (Norwegian parliament), following a proposal from Oscar I in September 1857, decided that the Crown Prince should take over the governance of Sweden and Norway to prevent a year-long interim government. The proposal was considered questionable by Louis De Geer, the chairman of the constitutional committee, who believed it might possibly contradict the literal wording of the Instrument of Government. However, all estates, as well as the Norwegian Storting, approved the proposal. On July 8, 1859, King Oscar died, and Crown Prince Karl became King Karl XV.

=== Karl XV as King ===

Karl XV ruled Sweden and Norway from September 25, 1857, until his death on September 18, 1872. He was crowned in Sweden on May 3rd and in Norway on August 5th, 1860. Karl XV's accession to the throne was met with distrust by the liberal party, a sentiment as strong as the hopes that had once greeted his father at the beginning of his reign. Karl was known for seeking companionship among the conservative so-called "Junker" party, and liberals feared their worst anxieties were confirmed when he appointed his former governor, Count Henning Hamilton, who was considered a leading figure in this party, to the government. Henning Hamilton's tenure in the government was not long. The leadership within the council soon passed to the Minister of Justice, Louis De Geer, and Hamilton resigned from the ministry already in 1860. The driving forces within the government, alongside De Geer, were the Minister of Foreign Affairs, Ludvig Manderström, and the Minister of Finance, Johan August Gripenstedt. Primarily through these three ministers, the government adopted a liberal and reform-minded approach, which became even more pronounced when it was reinforced by Fredrik Ferdinand Carlson as the Minister of Ecclesiastical Affairs.
