Johan Gadolin, born on June 5, 1760, in Åbo, and died on August 15, 1852, at Sunila estate in the parish of Virmo, was a Swedish and later Finnish chemist, physicist, and professor at the Royal Academy of Turku from 1798 to 1822. Johan Gadolin was the son of Jakob Gadolin and the brother of Gustaf Gadolin.

He discovered the element yttrium in 1794, and the element gadolinium was named after him.

== Biography == Johan Gadolin was the son of Jakob Gadolin and Elisabeth Browallius, daughter of Johan Browallius and Elisabeth (Kippius) Ehrenholm. In 1775, he began studying chemistry, first in Åbo and then, under the guidance of Torbern Bergman, in Uppsala, where he received his master's degree in philosophy in 1782. He was a close friend of Johan Gottlieb Gahn and Carl Wilhelm Scheele.

He published his first independent works in 1784 with the dissertation "De theoria caloris corporum specifici," and an essay on the absolute heat of bodies. Thanks to these works, Gadolin was appointed as an adjunct professor in the philosophical faculty in Åbo in 1785, and shortly thereafter as a full professor. From 1786 to 1788, Gadolin traveled abroad. During this time, he conducted a series of experiments on the specific and latent heat of ice with Adair Crawford, which he expanded with many new experiments upon his return. These were published in 1792. The values he provided for the latent and specific heat of water and ice were the most accurate measurements of these constants to date and held great significance.

In the following years, Gadolin published numerous treatises. They demonstrated great originality and had a significant influence on contemporary research, particularly his work on the nature of combustion ("Einige Bemerkungen über die Natur des Phlogiston," in Crell's "Annalen," 1788), which was even adopted by Jacob Berzelius during the first decade of the 19th century.

His studies of a mineral from the Ytterby mine ("Undersökning av en svart tung stenart ifrån Ytterby stenbrott i Roslagen," Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences Transactions, 1794) are also important, as he discovered a new element, yttrium. In 1794, he published "Inledning till chemien" (Introduction to Chemistry).

Later, he presented a series of academic treatises (also in German translation in Crell's "Annalen" 1802-03) defending specific important points in Lavoisier's teachings, against which objections had been raised by two prominent German chemists, Crell and Leopold Gmelin.

Gadolin was also involved in activities outside of scientific research. He was keenly interested in contemporary patriotic endeavors; for example, the idea for the establishment of the Finnish Agricultural Society originated with him. In this society's publications, there is an essay he wrote that won a prize in 1802, which was a response to the society's question: "Which crafts and manufactured goods are most beneficial for our Finnish cities?" As early as 1789, Gadolin had been granted permission to serve as a professor of chemistry, and he took up this position in 1798. Four years later, he resigned from the professorship but continued to care for the university's mineral collection and preside over dissertations for a few more years.

Gadolin was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1790 and an honorary member of the Finnish Society of Sciences in 1843. He was also a member of numerous other academies, including those in St. Petersburg, Dublin, Göttingen, and Uppsala.
