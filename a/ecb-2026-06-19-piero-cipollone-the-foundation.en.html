Full text

Rome, June 19, 2026

It is a great honor to be here today at Sapienza University for this conference, which marks the 80th anniversary of the Italian Republic. [1]

Eighty years ago, in the aftermath of fascism and the devastating Second World War, Italy regained its independence, its people's government, and its democratic freedoms, thanks to the referendum of June 2, 1946, and the subsequent entry into force of the Italian Constitution on January 1, 1948. Our Constitution established the principle that "sovereignty resides in the people, who exercise it in the forms and within the limits established by the Constitution."

Today, we commemorate not only the end of the dictatorship, but also the birth of a constitutional democracy, where the sovereignty of the people is exercised with full respect for fundamental rights and freedoms. This principle laid the foundation for a democratic republic based on work, solidarity, and the protection of rights.

Today, we live in a completely different world, one where the protection of our national sovereignty is inevitably linked to defending the sovereignty of the European Union. However, rather than implying a relinquishment of sovereignty, European integration has become essential to fully exercise sovereignty in a globalized environment. Article 11 of the Constitution states that Italy must accept "such limitations of sovereignty as may be necessary to ensure peace and justice among the nations." Italy's participation in the European Union puts this principle into practice: as a member of the Union, Italy is better positioned to achieve the goals established in its Constitution. Sharing our sovereignty actually means protecting and strengthening it.

---

As a member of the ECB's Executive Board, I would like to focus on a particular aspect of sovereignty, namely monetary sovereignty.

Monetary sovereignty – the control over a central bank's currency regarding its issuance, value, and role in payments and financing – is one of the most important aspects of sovereignty in the modern sense. If we lose control over our money, we lose control over our economic future. One of the main goals of our common currency, the euro, is to protect and strengthen our monetary sovereignty.

Central banks are tasked with, on behalf of the state, issuing the sole currency that is legal tender. This central bank currency acts as a stabilizing factor for the financial system: it guarantees that all other forms of money can be exchanged at a fixed value, and it allows authorities to maintain control over price stability and financing conditions in the economy.

Our mission is to preserve the euro's central role over time, even in the face of ongoing technological and geopolitical changes.

---

Currently, our monetary sovereignty is strong thanks to the euro's stability, which has proven to be resilient time and again since its introduction, despite the many crises we have experienced. The euro is the second most important currency in the international monetary system. Based on a range of indicators, its share of global currency usage is close to 20%, which is higher than the euro area's share of global GDP. [2] This share has gradually and steadily increased over many years. In the current economic climate, there is an opportunity for the euro to take on a global stabilizing role. When trade and geopolitical tensions shook financial markets in recent months, the euro served as a safe haven.

The euro has therefore strengthened our monetary sovereignty both nationally and internationally. A very significant and growing proportion of people in the euro area seem to be fully aware of this, as 82% of them say that they trust our currency.

This is a matter of trust that requires us, now more than ever, to take responsibility for protecting the common currency and our monetary sovereignty in the face of new geopolitical and technological challenges. In a less stable world like today's, external dependencies for critical economic functions can quickly become serious vulnerabilities.

Therefore, we must adapt to this new reality. As a central bank, we must ensure that external dependencies within payments and finance do not undermine Europe's hard-earned monetary sovereignty. We must ensure that our currency, the euro, remains suitable for the digital age and maintains its role both within and outside of Europe.

Currently, we have a significant dependence on non-European solutions for digital payments to businesses and individuals. Given our mandate to ensure that payment systems function smoothly, we cannot accept a situation that risks their stability.

Payments with debit and credit cards are a clear example of this. International card systems account for two-thirds of all card transactions within the euro area. 13 out of the 21 countries in the euro area do not even have a domestic card system. Currently, we do not have a European solution that functions across the entire euro area for all digital payments.

In a fragmented world, we can no longer afford to be dependent on non-European solutions for a critical function such as daily payments.

If Europeans can no longer make payments, they lose control over their money. Even without reaching that point, the current levels of dependence can be used as a tool against Europe's interests.

Therefore, we must address our dependencies within payments to businesses and individuals and reverse this trend. Fortunately, we have the resources needed to do so.

The central bank's mission is to provide means of payment. This is what we do when we issue banknotes. For many years, cash has not only contributed to uniting Europe, but has also helped us maintain control over how we make payments. Within the Eurosystem, we still believe that cash is essential, and we are fully committed to ensuring that it continues to be widely available and accepted. In fact, we are currently preparing for the production and issuance of the third series of euro banknotes, which will feature a new design.

However, more and more Europeans are choosing digital payments, and physical cash no longer meets all of their needs.
