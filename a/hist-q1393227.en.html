Eva Ekeblad, born De la Gardie on July 10, 1724, in Stockholm, and died on May 15, 1786, in Lidköping, was a Swedish countess and scientist. In 1748, she became the first woman to be elected as a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. She is perhaps best known for being the first to describe how to produce spirits from potatoes. Therefore, she could be considered Sweden's first female chemist, although this honor is usually attributed to Anna Sundström or Louise Hammarström.

== Biography ==

=== Private Life ===

Eva De la Gardie was the daughter of Count Magnus Julius De la Gardie and Hedvig Catharina Lillie, and sister to Carl Julius De la Gardie. Through her younger brother, she was the sister-in-law of Catherine Charlotte De la Gardie and Hedvig Taube. She married Councilor Count Claes Claesson Ekeblad on New Year's Day 1741; he was 32 years old, while she was only 16.

Between 1742 and 1754, she had seven daughters and one son, including Claes Julius Ekeblad and Hedvig Catharina Piper.

Upon her marriage, she received the manors of Mariedal Castle and Lindholmen Castle as a dowry. Her husband also owned Stola Manor. Because her husband was often away, Eva Ekeblad effectively took responsibility for managing the estates. She represented him at estate meetings at Mariedal and Stola, presiding over gatherings that often included 200 people. She was described as respected and authoritative, but fair according to the standards of the time. She protected the commoners from abuses by officials in exchange for their obedience ("only if they fulfill their duties"), and approved the count's candidate in the election of priests, punishing those who violated this agreement. She also ensured that officials who mistreated the peasants were held accountable, summoning them to Stola where she would hold court and punish them for their "misdeeds." Contemporary accounts stated that "Stola was far more pleasant than any of the other estates due to its location, amenities, and particularly the order that prevailed there." She had a good working relationship with Håkan Bergdahl (1735–1789), the estate manager at Stola.

In Stockholm, the Ekeblad house on Arsenalsgatan became "a venue for cultural gatherings." Among the events held there was Johan Helmich Roman's Swedish Mass. Eva Ekeblad was known there as "one of the few society ladies whose reputation remained impeccable." This assessment was made by the wife of the Spanish minister in Stockholm, the Marquise de Puentefuertes. For a time, Eva Ekeblad had a friendship with Louisa Ulrika: when Ekeblad visited Stockholm in 1778, Louisa Ulrika called her, along with "Lady Fersen," as her only remaining friends in Sweden. However, Ekeblad stated that her mother no longer considered Louisa Ulrika a friend due to a conflict that arose between them during her previous visit.

After her husband's death in 1771, Eva Ekeblad received Mariedal Castle as a widow's pension for life and sold the Ekeblad house in Stockholm. However, she continued to live alternately at Mariedal and Stola, and continued to manage all the estates on behalf of her son, who was also absent from the estates due to his career at court. Only after her son's marriage in 1775 did she gradually begin to transfer the management of Stola to her daughter-in-law, Brita Horn. Three years later, Stola officially became Brita Horn's dowry.

Eva Ekeblad attended the birth of the heir apparent in 1778 as a member of the Council of State. There was talk of offering her a position at court, such as First Lady of the Court, replacing Ulrika Strömfelt, or as the heir apparent's lady-in-waiting instead of Carl Sparre's wife. She was highly celebrated during her visit to Stockholm and received lodging at Karlberg Palace. At the same time, Eva Ekeblad became ill, which kept her in Stockholm until 1780, and for the rest of her life she seemed to be sickly and sometimes disabled. Around this time, her relationship with her son Claes Julius and daughter-in-law deteriorated because they were unable to repay a loan she had given them; furthermore, her son wanted her to transfer Mariedal and Lindholmen to them in exchange for financial support and that she move to Stockholm, which she refused. She continued to live at Mariedal until her death, her health declining, and was cared for by doctors. However, she maintained a respected position and continued to participate in social life until her death. As late as September 1785, Brita Horn wrote that the Ekeblad house was full of guests: "There are so many young men here; I don't know how your mother would manage if she were not as old as she is."

=== Scientific Activities === Eva Ekeblad was elected as the first woman to the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences on December 3, 1748. Her contact with them began during one of their meetings on November 5, 1748. At that meeting, it was announced that "Countess Ekeblad had made several attempts to use potatoes to produce starch and powder." She had also sent samples and a description.
