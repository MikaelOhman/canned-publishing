Oscar I, born July 4, 1799, in Paris, and died July 8, 1859, at Stockholm Castle in Stockholm, was King of Sweden and Norway from 1844 to 1859. He married Josefina of Leuchtenberg (great-granddaughter of the French Empress Joséphine) in 1823. Oscar I's full name was Joseph François Oscar, which translates to Josef Frans Oscar in Swedish. The king himself and many modern writers have spelled the royal name "Oscar," while the Swedish Royal Family uses the spelling "Oskar."

== Childhood ==

Oscar was born on July 4, 1799, in Paris at 291 Rue Cisalpine (the current address is 32 Rue de Monceau). He was the son of Jean Baptiste Jules Bernadotte, then Minister of War in France and later King Charles XIV John of Sweden, and Bernhardine Eugénie Désirée Clary, who later became Queen Desideria. He was named Joseph after his godfather, Joseph Bonaparte, who was married to his mother's older sister, Julie, and later became King of Naples and Spain; he also received the name François Oscar. The latter name, of Gaelic origin, was chosen by Napoleon Bonaparte after a hero in Ossian's poems, and it was used within the family, primarily by his mother and aunt. He lived with his mother and aunt, partly in Paris, and partly at Joseph Bonaparte's Château de Mortefontaine or at Bernadotte's (source needed) estate, Grosbois, both located near Paris.

In 1807, he had his first tutor, Le Moine. When Oscar's father, who was then Prince of Pontecorvo, was proposed as a candidate for the Swedish throne in 1810, it was emphasized that he already had a son, which was considered an important reason for his election. A portrait of young Oscar, circulated during the electoral assembly in Örebro, helped to promote Bernadotte, who was elected heir to the Swedish throne on August 21, 1810. On September 26, the order of succession for his "legitimate male heirs" was established. In connection with this event, Oscar was appointed Duke of Södermanland. The new heir traveled to Sweden in October 1810, while Oscar and his mother moved from Paris to Stockholm in January 1811. However, in the summer of 1811, Oscar's mother, Desideria, returned to France, and she did not see him again for eleven years.

Oscar, who was accompanied to Sweden by Le Moine, immediately received a Swedish tutor and soon began serving as his father's interpreter. In 1812, he had a Swedish tutor, Baron G.L. Cederhielm, who had left government service after the coup-like constitutional change of 1789 under Gustav III. Charles XIV John set clear guidelines for the prince's upbringing, including: "Always tell him that a weak ruler is one of the greatest punishments that heaven can inflict on a people." In 1812, he was appointed Lieutenant Colonel in the three regiments: Svea Life Guards, Göta Life Guards, and the Mounted Guard. He later became Colonel in 1815 and Major General in 1817.

During the 1810s, Oscar also studied Norwegian, humanities, administration, constitutional law, science, and art, primarily under Nils Magnus af Tannström. Per Daniel Amadeus Atterbom taught him German, Jacob Berzelius taught him chemistry, and Johan Olof Wallin taught him "Lutheran religion." He was confirmed in 1815. A few years later, he had Johan Gabriel Richert as a tutor in legal subjects. Fredric Westin was the prince's painting teacher, and Adolf Fredrik Lindblad later became his music teacher.

Crown Prince Oscar composed a funeral march that was performed at the burial of Charles XIII, which marked his first publicly recognized achievement. News of this spread widely, and Ludwig van Beethoven wrote to his father, Charles XIV John, offering to help further develop the crown prince's talent. This event is linked to the fact that there was an old friendship between Beethoven and Charles XIV John, dating back to the period when Bernadotte served as French Minister in Vienna in 1798.

== The Regency Period ==

When Carl XIV John became King of the Union in 1818, Oscar became Crown Prince of Sweden and Norway. In 1819, he was appointed General in both the Swedish and Norwegian armies and commander of various military units: the Life Guards and Royal Guard, Commander-in-Chief in Scania, Inspector General of Artillery for Sweden and Norway, and Chief of a cavalry brigade. In 1818, he was also elected Chancellor of Uppsala University, where he spent one autumn semester. Among other things, the Crown Prince attended lectures by Erik Gustaf Geijer, Nils Fredrik Biberg, Jöns Svanberg, and Lars Georg Rabenius. As early as January 17, 1816, he had been elected "first member" (later termed honorary member) of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences.

In 1815, he experienced a minor illness. At this time, politicians began to show interest in the Crown Prince's marriage. After much preparation, the prince embarked on his courtship journey in 1822, during which he met his mother in Aachen. Following his engagement to Joséphine of Leuchtenberg, daughter of Prince Eugène and Duchess Augusta of Bavaria, which was formalized in Eichstätt, the journey continued to Italy. On the return trip, the prince managed to visit Verona during the ongoing Congress and was received by the assembled princes and statesmen.

Oscar and Joséphine's marriage took place on May 23, 1823.
