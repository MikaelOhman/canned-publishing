Full text

Viking voyages were characterized by a combination of raiding and trade. There was also emigration and colonization, with some Norse people settling in new areas, such as Dublin and York, but also in present-day Russia, Poland, and Ukraine. The Viking Age followed the Bronze Age, Iron Age, and Vendel period, during which extensive voyages also took place. The Vikings' trading networks were inherited by the Hanseatic League during the Middle Ages. Voyages primarily followed coastlines, as venturing out into open sea was too risky, especially with smaller boats. While larger ships and favorable weather made sailing on open water possible, the coastline offered safety and landmarks for navigation.

Starting from Birka in the Mälaren region, we can see that Birka initially had connections with Staraja Ladoga, Grobin, and Wolin to the east ("the eastern route"). To the west, trade occurred with Hedeby, Kaupang, and Dorestad. In a later phase, the network expanded to include Volga Bulgaria near the Ural Mountains, Kiev, and Constantinople. There was also exchange with the Abbasids in the Middle East. From a Norwegian-Danish perspective in the west, contacts were established with England, Ireland, Greenland, Spain, and even Canada.

== Background == Maritime activities, long-distance trade, and connections with distant lands have a long history in Scandinavia. Evidence of this can be seen in the numerous rock carvings and ship settings, which were created at least two thousand years before the Vikings. We also find clear similarities between the findings at Sutton Hoo in England and the graves in Vendel, Uppland. Brooches/clasps from Gotland have been found on the other side of the Baltic Sea, and they arrived there even before the Viking Age. Artifacts from the Roman Empire have been discovered in Uppåkra and Öland, suggesting that these Roman objects reached Scandinavia long before what we call the Viking Age. An early international network is also indicated by the so-called "gold figurines" found throughout southern Scandinavia. The Viking Age was therefore a continuation of a trading culture and a network that had gradually developed over a long period.

Shipbuilding advanced, boats became larger, and at some point, people in Northern Europe learned to sail. Slavery and serfdom were commonplace during this time, and certain essential raw materials required long journeys to obtain. Important trade goods included furs, salt, iron, copper, blacksmithing products, amber, slaves, honey, tar, silver, etc. Amber from the Baltic Sea has been found in Mediterranean sites dating back long before the beginning of the Common Era. The capture of slaves was a lucrative business, and due to the nature of this trade, slave traders had to be skilled in weaponry and often resorted to violence. The actual Viking raids, as we know them, were preceded by isolated instances of plundering. The people of Northern Europe became increasingly familiar with navigation and seamanship. In Sweden and Norway, their main routes were fjords, rivers, and lakes; in Denmark, they used straits. This allowed them to venture out on trading expeditions along the coasts and up rivers in neighboring countries. Disputes often arose between the local populations and the Norsemen, frequently leading to conflicts.

== To the West (vestrvegr) ==

It was primarily Danes and Norwegians who sailed west, although there were also some Swedes. At this time, people from Bohuslän and Scania were considered to be either Norwegian or Danish. The ships in the west were larger than those in the east. Among the largest ships we know of is Skuldelev 2, which has been reconstructed and is now known as the Havhingsten fra Glendalough. These longships could sail directly onto the beach, and with the help of the tides, they could travel very quickly. The bulkier and heavier cargo ships might need a pier or quay, which we often see in reconstructions of Viking-era settlements (e.g., Hedeby, Birka, and Truso). More examples of ships from the west can be found at the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo.

=== Warfare, Raiding, and Colonization === The first raids on England occurred near Dorchester sometime between 786 and 793. This was followed by the infamous attack on the Lindisfarne monastery in 793. Many consider this to be the beginning of the so-called "Viking Age." In 794, Vikings attacked St. Egferth's monastery at Wearmouth in Northumbria. The inhabitants resisted, and one of the chieftains was killed. A severe storm then sank several of their ships, and those who made it to shore were slaughtered. Until 835, there are no more records of Viking attacks on England's eastern coasts. However, during the period from 834 to 850, the coasts were sporadically raided by smaller groups of Viking ships. In 851, a setback occurred when a larger Viking army was defeated at Aclea in Wessex. In 865, a large fleet arrived in East Anglia, and this force ravaged the area for thirteen years, capturing York in 867. Northumbria was conquered. More boats and men from Scandinavia arrived in 871, and Mercia fell in 873. Wessex fell to the Vikings in 878. The origin of these Vikings is debated, and some have suggested that they came from Ireland, while others came from Western Francia. York, which the Norse called Jorvik, became an important Viking center.

How certain are we about this?

Raidings and trading expeditions are well documented through foreign chronicles (often written by affected monks, and therefore potentially biased), archaeology, and later sagas. The extent and brutality of these events can be either exaggerated or understated depending on the source.
