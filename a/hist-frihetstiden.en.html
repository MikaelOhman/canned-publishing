Full text

The Age of Liberty is a period in Swedish history that lasted from 1719 to 1772 and preceded the era of the House of Bernadotte. The Age of Liberty was followed by the Gustavian era. During this time, Sweden was largely governed by the Riksdag (parliament), especially after 1738 when the "Hat" party emerged, which soon led to their opponents organizing into the "Cap" party. In terms of foreign policy, Sweden was significantly weakened, and two unsuccessful wars – the Russo-Swedish War fought by the Hats and the Pomeranian War – demonstrated that Sweden was no longer the military superpower it had been in the previous century.

Significant progress was made in science and culture during this period, with Carl von Linné and Carl Michael Bellman being prominent examples. Major efforts were also undertaken in legislation, primarily through the Act of 1734 (which still forms the basis of Swedish and Finnish law) and the Freedom of the Press Act of 1766. In 1753, Sweden adopted the Gregorian calendar.

The name "Age of Liberty" was already used during that time and refers to the fact that absolute royal power was abolished by law in 1719, making the country free from absolute monarchy. This period ended with Gustav III's coup d'état, which resulted in more power being placed in the hands of the king and the privy council.

== History ==

=== The Monarchy Loses Power to the Parliament and Government ===

Much of the political life during the Age of Liberty must be understood against the backdrop of events during the Great Northern War. The heavy sacrifices demanded by Charles XII from the public in order to maintain Sweden's status as a great power had led to widespread discontent with absolute monarchy. Among the officials, who after the decline of the aristocracy became the most influential group in society, frustration was prevalent, especially regarding the arbitrary actions of the king and his first minister, Georg Heinrich von Görtz, without consulting the council. The losses, particularly those resulting from the Treaty of Nystad, were deeply felt: many hoped for revenge, believing that if an opportunity arose, Russia could be easily defeated, while simultaneously disrupting the state's economy. Throughout the Age of Liberty, the national budget was based on the 1696 budget and could only be balanced through temporary appropriations and foreign subsidies, which often tied the country to other nations' foreign policies.

When Ulrika Eleonora's disputed right to the throne gave Parliament free rein, the absolute monarchy was fundamentally dismantled. The influence of officials was greatly strengthened by the constitutional legislation of 1719 and 1720. From an aristocratic perspective, there was a desire in 1719 for a secure bureaucratic administrative organization modeled after the government form of 1634, under an independent governing council. However, since a large part of the nobility depended on income from the crown, either through military service or as officials, a system where most power was given to the high nobility became impossible.

The monistic view of the state that absolute monarchy represented still held sway, but now in favor of Parliament, which gained unrestricted legislative power. The kingdom was maintained, but the monarch had almost no real power – Kings Frederick I (1720-1751) and Adolf Frederick (1751-1771) had some influence within the council and over appointments of officials, but otherwise had little say. Lacking power, Queen Louisa Ulrika attempted a coup in 1756, which failed.

The actual governing body was now to be the Council of State, which could vote down the king. Initially, this theory roughly corresponded to practice, as Arvid Horn led Sweden for nearly two decades as Chancellor and sometimes also Lord Marshal. However, in 1738, the newly organized and parliamentarily dominant Hat Party found a way to dismiss unpopular council members. This form of parliamentarism was actually made impossible by the fact that officials could only be removed through legal proceedings, but a solution was found in "licentiation," a type of legal process where responsibility was demanded for what was formally considered negligence or misconduct. The charges could include misinterpreting the constitution or providing advice that did not correspond to "the indispensable welfare of the realm." The main document of this new system of government was called the Instrument of Government, and the organization of the government agencies was enshrined in law to protect against any interference.

Since the Council of State was also prohibited from sitting in Parliament, they became only the "representatives" of the estates – executors of their will, with only a limited ability for independent leadership. Thus, absolute monarchy and sovereignty were replaced by the rule of the Estates.

=== The Era of Arvid Horn ===

At the beginning of the Age of Liberty, there were no political parties as we know them today. Instead, Arvid Horn, in his roles as Chancellor and Marshal of the Realm, held significant power over Swedish politics. He led the council, and during his tenure, the council directed policy. He generally pursued a cautious foreign policy, avoiding involving Sweden in any wars.
