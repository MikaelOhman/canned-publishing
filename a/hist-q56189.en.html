Lise Meitner, originally Elise Meitner, was born on November 7, 1878, in Vienna, Austria, and died on October 27, 1968, in Cambridge. She was an Austrian-Swedish physicist specializing in radioactivity and nuclear physics. Lise Meitner entered the history of physics when, in December 1938, she proposed a revolutionary hypothesis based on experiments conducted by Otto Hahn: that nuclear fission occurs when uranium is exposed to neutron bombardment.

This hypothesis was confirmed in January 1939 through an experiment performed by her nephew-in-law, Otto Robert Frisch. This groundbreaking discovery was fundamental to the utilization of nuclear energy and initiated the process that, just over six years later, led to the development of the atomic bomb.

For 22 years, from 1938 to 1960, Meitner lived in Sweden. She did not share the Nobel Prize in Chemistry with Otto Hahn, who was awarded the prize in 1944 for the discovery of nuclear fission. Many researchers have called her exclusion from the award an "injustice," and Meitner has posthumously been awarded numerous honors, including the element meitnerium, which is named after her.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years === Meitner was born into a Jewish family in Austria-Hungary and was the third of eight children to Hedwig (Skovran) from the Kingdom of Hungary and Philipp Meitner from Austria. During her childhood, she had few friends and often felt lonely. In 1908, she converted to Protestantism and was baptized as a Lutheran. Despite some resistance from her family, she began studying physics at the University of Vienna under Ludwig Boltzmann.

=== Education === Lise Meitner attended elementary school for five years and then a secondary school for three years. Girls were not allowed to attend high school, which was the usual preparation for the maturity exam required to begin university studies. As a fourteen-year-old, Meitner's future options were limited because most women at that time did not have jobs outside the home. She chose to train as a French teacher, which was a common profession for women, although there is no indication that she had any particular interest in it. The reason for her choice is believed to be that she wanted a secure job to support her family after her father's death, something that would not have been easy as a female academic.

==== University of Vienna === After completing her teacher training in 1899, she decided to begin private high school studies in order to gain admission to the University of Vienna. Meitner studied intensively for two years and graduated in 1901. The same year, she began her studies at the University of Vienna and became one of the first female students to study physics there. At the university, she was inspired by the lectures of Professor Ludwig Boltzmann, which had a significant influence on her ambition to pursue her plans to become a physicist. She earned her doctorate in 1907, becoming the first woman at the university to do so.

==== University of Berlin ==== After defending her dissertation in Vienna in 1907, Meitner traveled to Berlin to attend lectures by Max Planck. According to the university's regulations, women were not allowed to attend lectures unless the lecturer was sympathetic to the idea and there were available seats. Planck therefore made an exception and allowed Meitner to study under him. This was despite his earlier statement, approximately 10 years prior, that female students with talent in physics are merely exceptions that prove the rule: that women are not suited for higher education. Planck's lectures were a completely different type of instruction than what she had been accustomed to during her time with Boltzmann, but she soon realized that the lectures provided her with more knowledge.

=== Collaboration with Otto Hahn === In September 1907, she noticed that the lectures in theoretical physics were not filling her time as much as she would have liked. Eager to gain more practical experience, she approached Heinrich Rubens, the university's professor of experimental physics, to see if she could obtain a laboratory space. To her dismay, the only available space was in his personal laboratory, which she found unthinkable given that she was very introverted and shy. Meitner felt it would be impossible to ask Rubens about things she didn't understand, which would be necessary to get started. When she first inquired with Rubens about a research position, he mentioned several younger colleagues who might be interested in collaborating with her. Rubens said that another young chemist, Otto Hahn, had expressed interest in working with her before she had a chance to decline Rubens' initial offer. A few minutes later, Hahn entered the room. From their very first encounter, Meitner felt confident that she would find the courage to ask Hahn about anything she didn't understand, as he seemed very approachable and informal. Meitner requested eight days to consider the offer, which was granted. During that time, Otto Hahn had to obtain permission from Professor Emil Fischer, head of the chemistry department.
