In plain language

Several government agencies, including the police and the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket), have collaborated to ensure that public funds are used correctly. By conducting unannounced visits to workplaces, they have been able to uncover instances of fraud where companies attempted to exploit the system.

In 2025, numerous violations were detected, leading to a total of 187 million Swedish kronor in payments being stopped. Of these amounts, the Public Employment Service (Arbetsförmedlingen) recovered over 11 million Swedish kronor from companies that had not complied with the regulations.

The agencies primarily uncovered so-called "sham employment" situations, where individuals are nominally employed but do not actually perform any work. They also found cases where employees were exploited by being denied the wages they are entitled to or forced to repay money.

The purpose of this collaboration is to ensure that taxpayer money does not end up in the hands of criminals. By verifying that employees are actually performing their jobs and receiving proper compensation, it protects both the workers and the funds intended to help people find employment.

Full text

The increased number of inspections and the identification of more crimes and violations are the results of last year's joint efforts by various authorities to combat labor market crime.

Often, officials from multiple agencies, including the police, participate in unannounced inspections aimed at detecting labor market crime.

In 2025, the number of joint authority inspections increased compared to the previous year, leading to the identification of more crimes and violations. This resulted in significant financial recoveries and payments that were stopped due to irregularities, totaling 187 million Swedish krona.

The Public Employment Service (Arbetsförmedlingen) decided on a recovery of 11,214,125 Swedish krona and denied payments of 4,129,681 Swedish krona in 2025 as part of the authorities' joint efforts to combat labor market crime.

"Every year, the Public Employment Service pays out several billion Swedish krona in various employment support programs to employers. The terms of employment must comply with applicable laws and regulations. We collaborate to detect and prevent situations where people are harmed and tax money is used for criminal activities. Taxpayer money should not go to entities that exploit welfare systems," said Stefan Wedin, acting unit manager at the Monitoring and Control Unit, Public Employment Service.

In the inspections conducted by the Public Employment Service, sham employment arrangements have been discovered. These refer to jobs that only exist on paper, with the aim of obtaining benefits from the agency without any actual work being performed. There are also cases where employers exploit employees by not paying them the wages they are entitled to. In other instances, the correct wage is paid out to create a false appearance in the authorities' systems, but the employer then forces the employee to repay part of the salary.

"Conducting unannounced workplace inspections is part of our mandate. We verify that individuals receiving support are actually working at the specified location and performing the tasks, according to the agreements made with the employer when decisions were made, for example, regarding subsidized employment programs," says Stefan Wedin.

The following agencies are involved:

  • The Swedish Public Employment Service (Arbetsförmedlingen)
  • The Swedish Work Environment Authority (Arbetsmiljöverket)
  • The Police Authority (Polismyndigheten)
  • The Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket)
  • The Swedish Social Insurance Agency (Försäkringskassan)
  • The Swedish Migration Agency (Migrationsverket)
  • The Public Prosecution Office (Åklagarmyndigheten)
  • The National Economic Crime Unit (Ekobrottsmyndigheten)
  • The Swedish Authority for Diversity and Equality (Jämställdhetsmyndigheten)
