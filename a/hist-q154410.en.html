Marie Under, born March 27, 1883, in Tallinn, Governorate of Estonia, Russian Empire, and died September 25, 1980, in Stockholm, was an Estonian poet who fled the Soviet occupation to Sweden in 1944. She published 13 collections of poetry and became one of the most influential and legendary Estonian poets of all time. She was nominated for the Nobel Prize in Literature several times but never received it. In Sweden, she lived largely forgotten by society in Mälarhöjden. She never returned to her homeland, and her poems were long forbidden in the Soviet Republic of Estonia. Under was married to Carl Hacker, a bookkeeper, from 1902 to 1924, and in her second marriage, from 1924 to 1977, to the Estonian poet Artur Adson.

== Background ==

=== Childhood === Under was born in 1883 in Reval – the former name of Tallinn in the Governorate of Estonia – as the daughter of teacher Friedrich "Priidu" Under (1843–1930) from Dagö and Leena, née Kerner (1854–1934). She learned to read at a young age, starting at four years old. She began reading both Estonian and German. Goethe and Schiller were among the first poets she was able to become acquainted with. She attended a German-language girls' school and also learned French and Russian there. After school, she found employment as a governess for the von Stackelberg family at the Kreuzhof estate – Risti Manor. Alongside her studies, she began writing poetry, but in German. Estonian was a language used at home, and in better schools, no Estonian was taught.

=== Youth and Early Poetry === From 1901 to 1902, she found work at a liberal Estonian newspaper, Teataja. There, she met several of the active and forward-thinking cultural figures of the time: Konstantin Päts, who later became a politician and President of Estonia; the writer Eduard Vilde; and Ants Laikmaa, one of the most Western-influenced artists in Estonia at that time. The young poet was eighteen years old when she first came into contact with a vibrant Estonian cultural life, and it was then that she began writing poetry in Estonian. However, the freedom that work provided to the young woman was short-lived; in 1902, she married Carl Hacker, an accountant. The couple moved to Kutjino, a suburb of Moscow. She quickly had two children. It was the Russian Revolution of 1905 that sparked thoughts of freedom in Estonia and also within her. Her poems were published anonymously in Estonian newspapers and in the first manifesto-like literary collection "Young-Estonia" (Noor-Eesti) in 1905. In Estonia, this collection of texts is considered a turning point and the beginning of the modern Estonian cultural era.

== The Princess of Siurus ==

In 1906, she returned to Tallinn and, on the advice of the artist Ants Laikmaa, began translating her German poems into Estonian. In 1913, she met the poet Artur Adson and decided to leave her husband. Her relationship with Adson inspired her, and he also gave her the space to work. Adson became a source of inspiration for her bold and unique erotic poetry. In 1917, the couple, along with some other then-unknown poets and writers, founded the "Siuru" society, influenced by symbolism. Siuru is a mythical bird in the national epic Kalevipoeg. The same year, they went on a tour of Estonia. At that time, she was still officially married to Hacker. It was during World War I and the Russian Revolution that Under published her first collection of poems, "Sonetid" (1917). This Estonian-language collection caused strong reactions and became very popular. She earned the nickname "Siuru's Princess," and writing became her full-time occupation.

In February 1918, Estonia declared its independence, but soon a new war began. Now, independent Estonia had to be defended against Bolshevik Russia and the Baltic German Landeswehr, and it was only in 1920 that peace finally came. Before then, Under had published three more collections of poetry. Artur Adson's loving care and a state scholarship gave her the freedom to dedicate herself to writing. In 1924, Under divorced Hacker and married Artur Adson. By this time, she had already published five collections of poems and become one of the most famous Estonian poets of all time. The couple lived a quiet life, and she preferred not to be in the public eye. They took several trips around Europe: from 1921 to 1923 to Germany, in 1926 to Paris, and later to Poland, Czechoslovakia, Austria, Hungary, Italy, and Holland. The couple found a home in a quiet residential area near Tallinn, Nõmme. She spent the summers by the coast, but never at large resorts. She became an honorary member of the international PEN club and received many literary awards, and her poems were translated into several other languages.

The idyllic life came to an abrupt end when Estonia was occupied by the Soviet Union, and repression became commonplace in Estonia. People disappeared without trial; in 1941, tens of thousands were arrested and sent to Siberia. Then came the occupation under Nazi Germany, with new executions and concentration camps.
