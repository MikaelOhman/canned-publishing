In plain language

The Swedish National Audit Office is an agency that monitors whether other government agencies are performing their duties correctly. They have now investigated how the Enforcement Agency (Kronofogden) operates in ensuring that people pay their debts, taxes, and fees. The findings indicate that the Enforcement Agency's work is fundamentally efficient, but that there are problems that need to be addressed.

The biggest problem is that processing times have increased. This means that it takes longer than before to have cases processed. While much is done quickly with the help of computers, delays occur when people have to perform tasks manually. It is particularly common for cases to remain pending for more than two months when the agency has to send documents personally to someone.

Another cause of the waiting time is that many applications are incomplete. More than half of all applications submitted through the Enforcement Agency's online service require changes or additional information. When information is missing, an employee at the agency has to review the case manually, which halts the process.

There are also many cases where there is disagreement, known as disputed claims. This means that those who are supposed to pay do not agree that the debt is valid. Because so many applications need to be corrected and many cases require manual handling, the workload on the agency is significant.

It is of great importance to society that those entitled to payment for goods or services can be certain that they will receive it. It is also crucial that everyone who is obligated to pay taxes and fees to the public sector actually does so. The Enforcement Agency has several responsibilities aimed at ensuring that unpaid debts are settled. The number of cases coming to the Enforcement Agency is high, while processing times have become longer. Against this background, the Swedish National Audit Office has reviewed the Enforcement Agency's operations for debt collection. The review covers the activities involved in establishing payment demands and enforcing those demands. The Swedish National Audit Office's overall conclusion is that the Enforcement Agency's debt collection activities are largely efficient. However, there are certain shortcomings in the processing that need to be addressed. While the tasks within the Enforcement Agency's processing are performed quickly, the processing times in many cases have been extended due to long waiting periods. This applies to both the process for payment orders and the process for enforcement. The processing of cases involving payment orders has a high degree of automation. Therefore, most cases can be processed quickly without waiting periods. Waiting periods occur when cases are left pending, awaiting manual processing. The longest waiting periods, often longer than two months, occur while waiting for physical notification. Almost half of all cases involving payment orders are subject to physical notification. Waiting periods also arise when applications need to be supplemented, as these are handled manually and cannot be processed by all caseworkers. Over half of all applications submitted through the Enforcement Agency's e-service require supplementation.
