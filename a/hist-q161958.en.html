In plain language

Eric of Pomerania was a king who ruled over three countries simultaneously: Sweden, Norway, and Denmark. This period is known as the Kalmar Union. He became king at a very young age and was entrusted with responsibility for a vast territory because he was the only male heir to the Norwegian kings.

During his reign, Eric aimed to create a strong and unified Nordic region. His motivation was to protect the countries from external threats, particularly from German states and trading organizations known as the Hanseatic League. To achieve this goal, he, for example, reacquired the island of Gotland and initiated wars to break the German trade monopoly in the Baltic Sea.

Eric also introduced new rules for governing the country. He appointed his own trusted officials, called bailiffs, to directly administer areas under his control. This allowed him to exercise more centralized authority, but it led to dissatisfaction among local nobles in Sweden and Norway, who felt they were no longer able to make their own decisions.

Eric of Pomerania (incorrectly referred to as Eric XIII), born in 1382 in Rügenwalde, now in Poland, and died on September 24, 1459, also in Rügenwalde (buried in St. Mary's Church in what is now Darłowo, Poland), was King of Sweden from 1396 to 1439 (with two interruptions), King of Norway from 1389 to 1442, and King of Denmark from 1396 to 1439. He was the son of Duke Vratislav VII of Pomerania and Maria of Mecklenburg, and a grandnephew of Queen Margaret, who adopted him after her son, Olav of Denmark and Norway, died in 1387.

With Eric, the House of Grips was introduced into Sweden as a one-time occurrence. In Pomerania, he was known as Eric I.

== Biography == Erik was born around 1382 as Bogislav into the Lechitic ruling family of Gryf (Grip). He was the son of Duke Vratislav VII of Pomerania and Maria of Mecklenburg. In 1388, only six years old, Erik was appointed co-regent and successor to his great-aunt Margaret in Norway, as he was the only living male descendant of the Norwegian royal house. Bogislav was crowned King of Norway at Easter in 1392. At the same time, Queen Margaret changed his Slavic name Bogislav to the more Scandinavian-sounding Erik. He was hailed as king in Denmark and Sweden in 1396, and at only 14 years old, he was crowned King of Denmark and Sweden in Kalmar on June 17, 1397. He was declared an adult at the age of 18 in 1400 in Helsingborg, and in 1401 he performed the traditional "Eriksgata" ceremony, but he did not fully rule independently until after Margaret's death in 1412. In 1406, he married Philippa of England (died 1430) in Lund; she was the daughter of King Henry IV of England. After his father's death in 1395, the government of Pomerania passed to his uncle, Bogislav VIII of Pomerania. After Bogislav VIII died in 1418, Erik became a guardian for his minor cousin, Bogislav IX of Pomerania, and traveled to Pomerania to take over his regency in Pomerania-Stolp. At the same time, he forged an alliance against the Teutonic Order with the Polish King Władisław II Jagiełło, and also promised Bogislav IX's hand in marriage to the Polish king's only daughter, Hedwig. Bogislav IX was designated as Erik's successor on the Nordic throne. Changes in Polish politics led to the annulment of the marriage agreement, and instead, Hedwig was betrothed to the 7-year-old son of Elector Frederick I of Brandenburg, also named Frederick. During King Erik's journey to Jerusalem, he visited Poland in 1424 and 1425 to try to have this marriage agreement annulled and to arrange for his cousin to marry Hedwig again. After returning to the Nordic countries in 1425, he was drawn into the war against Holstein, which prevented him from continuing his efforts to secure the marriage. Hedwig died unmarried in 1431.

== Erik's Reign ==

=== Purchase of Gotland, War Against the Hanseatic League and the Sound Dues === As a union king, Erik was driven to hinder and compete with the growing economic and military dominance of the Hanseatic League and the Teutonic Order in the Baltic Sea, which was also the intention behind the Kalmar Union. Erik was also influenced by the negative experiences his family in Pomerania, as well as the neighboring country of Poland, had experienced with German expansion. The ruling dynasty of Poland, the Piast, were related to Erik's Pomeranian lineage, the Gryf (Grip). Therefore, Erik's policy aimed to transform the Nordic countries into a strong unified entity that would strengthen the North against foreign threats, primarily from the German states. For example, in 1408, he bought Gotland back from the Teutonic Order, which had conquered the island from Denmark in 1398. The Teutonic Order, wanting to avoid a two-front war with the Kalmar Union before engaging in a war with Poland-Lithuania, quickly and at a low price, got rid of the island.

In 1410, Erik initiated a war against the Holsteiners who controlled Danish Southern Jutland, as well as against the Hanseatic cities that had established a devastating trade monopoly in the North. The war continued with interruptions until 1435. During this conflict, Erik sought an alliance between the Kalmar Union and the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth against the Teutonic Order. In 1413, Erik (as Danish regent) founded the city of Landzcrone at the fishing village of Søndre Sæby, which was quickly renamed Landskrone and later, after 1658, Landskrona, located on the Sound. In 1419, Erik entered into a defensive alliance with King Vladislav Jagiello of Poland-Lithuania against the Teutonic Order. He also established the Sound Dues in 1429 and, for this purpose, had the fortress of Kronborg built in Helsingør. Now, the wealthy Hanseatic League was required to pay customs duties to the Kalmar Union upon entering and exiting the Baltic Sea. Militarily, Erik's wars were also successful, and it appeared that the Hanseatic League would lose its Nordic trade privileges.

=== Centralized Governance with Foreign Officials; Engelbrekt's Rebellion === Erik introduced a modern system of centralized governance in his vast kingdom by appointing Danish and German officials to oversee the various provinces of Sweden and Norway, which were directly subordinate to him. However, Erik's rule led to conflicts within the Kalmar Union that hindered trade in the Baltic Sea. The Hanseatic League, which held a complete trading monopoly, imposed a blockade on the Kalmar Union. Furthermore, local nobles in Sweden and Norway were very dissatisfied with being overlooked by Erik's Danish and German officials.
