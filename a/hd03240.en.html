TL;DRThe government proposes new laws to modernize the electricity market in Sweden. The proposals aim to make it easier to share electricity between properties and change the rules regarding who is responsible for the safety of the electricity grid.
In plain language

The government has proposed new legislation to replace the old electricity law from 1997. The purpose is to align Swedish regulations with EU laws and to clarify which rules apply to companies that own the power lines.

A major change is that it will be easier to share electricity between different locations. With the new rules for energy sharing, a person who uses electricity in a building can be billed for electricity that has been produced in another building. This is called energy sharing and is part of a new EU directive.

The responsibility for ensuring the safe operation of the electricity system will also be changed. Previously, there was a special authority responsible for operational safety, but now it is proposed that the companies that operate the major power lines take over that responsibility. New roles will also be introduced to maintain balance in the electricity system.

Most of the new rules are planned to come into effect on January 1, 2027. Part of the proposal, which concerns companies that are obligated to supply electricity, will come into effect slightly later, namely on July 1, 2027.

Full text

The proposal includes drafts for a new electricity market law and a new law on electrical cables. These will replace the current Electricity Act (1997:857). The proposed electricity market law aims to regulate the functioning of the electricity market, while the law on electrical cables is intended to include provisions on network concessions and on electrical cables installed within the areas of transport routes.

In relation to the current regulations, the following changes are proposed:

  • New terminology is introduced to align Swedish law with EU law and to clarify that companies operating network activities have a system responsibility.
  • The tasks related to the operational reliability of the national electricity system, which have previously been carried out by a responsible authority, will instead be performed by the entity that owns the transmission system.
  • Two new roles, the balancing responsible party and the provider of balancing services, replace the previous role of the balancing responsible party.
  • The provisions on assigned electricity contracts are removed and replaced with a system of a delivery-obligated last-resort supplier.
  • New rules on energy sharing are introduced to implement the revised Electricity Directive. These rules make it possible for an electricity consumer to account for electricity produced at a property other than where it is consumed.

The laws are proposed to come into force on July 1, 2027, for delivery-obligated last-resort suppliers, and otherwise on January 1, 2027.
