Karl X Gustav, born on November 8, 1622, at Nyköpingshus, and died on February 13, 1660, in Gothenburg, was King of Sweden from 1654 until his death six years later. His brief reign as Swedish king was marked by war, and he spent most of his time in office leading the Swedish army in Poland, Germany, and Denmark. The army achieved significant successes, and the crossing of the Great Belt strait, which led to the Treaty of Roskilde, is considered one of Sweden's greatest military achievements.

He was the son of John Casimir, Count Palatine of Palatinate-Zweibrücken, and Princess Catherine of Sweden, daughter of Charles IX. He married Hedwig Eleonora of Holstein-Gottorp on October 24, 1654. Karl X Gustav reintroduced the House of Wittelsbach into Sweden, establishing its Palatine branch as the Swedish royal dynasty, and he was the first king of the Carolinian era, which is generally considered to encompass his own reign, that of his son Charles XI, and his grandson Charles XII. He was a cousin of Queen Christina.

== Early Life == Charles X Gustav was born on November 8, 1622, at Nyköping Castle as the son of Johann Casimir, Count Palatine of the Rhine, and Princess Catherine of Sweden. His parents had sought refuge in Sweden to escape the Thirty Years' War, and they never returned. Charles Gustav grew up in Sweden, and his upbringing was overseen by, among others, the Lord High Chancellor Axel Oxenstierna. As the son of a German father, he was raised with certain German traditions and learned German as his second native language. He also developed a strong personal interest in the Holy Roman Empire, largely due to the inheritance claims that existed within his family in that country. After the death of Gustav II Adolf, Charles Gustav's father and family were distrusted by Queen Christina's regency government, particularly by Axel Oxenstierna, which meant that the young Count Palatine's position was initially quite uncertain: he was both a Swedish heir apparent and the exiled son of a German count.

After his private studies, Charles Gustav spent a few months at Uppsala University and undertook an educational journey abroad from 1638 to the autumn of 1640. He spent most of these years in France, primarily at the court of Louis XIII in Paris.

== The Thirty Years' War ==

Upon his return from France, it became clear to Charles X Gustav that the conflicts between Axel Oxenstierna and the Palatinate family had intensified. Johan Casimir had always had friends in many circles, including within the council, and within these circles, an opposition had arisen against the aristocratic regency. Charles X Gustav was a potential heir to the throne after the young queen and wished to enter Swedish service, but the regency government refused. However, in the summer of 1642, he finally received permission to travel to the Swedish army in Germany, where he learned military tactics under the command of Lennart Torstensson. He participated in the Battle of Leipzig in 1642 and the Battle of Jankov in 1645, as well as in the campaign against Denmark from 1643 to 1644.

In 1645, Charles X Gustav returned to Sweden, where he sought to win the affection of his cousin, Queen Christina. For a couple of years, the matter remained undecided, and Charles X Gustav became a prominent figure in the political struggles. In the summer of 1647, Charles made one last attempt to win her hand. He told his friend Lorentz von der Linde, "I am the most miserable person on earth." Eventually, Christina rejected his proposal, but she offered him compensation: in January 1648, he was appointed Swedish Generalissimus in Germany. He did not have much time to accomplish anything there before peace was concluded in October 1648, but instead, he led the disbandment of the large conscripted armies and also the complex negotiations regarding the "execution" of the peace treaty, which continued until June 1650 in Nuremberg.

== Accession to the Throne ==

While Charles X Gustav was working to implement the peace treaty, Queen Christina had prepared another elevation for him. In 1649, despite the opposition of the nobility, she appointed her half-cousin, Charles X Gustav, as heir to the throne. This meant that when Christina abdicated on June 6, 1654, in Uppsala, Charles X Gustav was acclaimed and crowned king on the same day.

Before ascending the throne, Charles X Gustav had a son, Gustaf Carlson, with Märta Allertz, a daughter of a councilor from Stockholm. After his coronation on June 6, 1654, he quickly married Hedvig Eleonora of Holstein-Gottorp in October of the same year. This marriage held political significance, as the Duke of Holstein was a natural ally against Denmark. The couple had only one child, their son Karl, born in November 1655.

== The War in Prussia and Poland ==

For the newly crowned Charles X Gustav, significant and challenging tasks lay ahead. He needed to consolidate Sweden's newfound great power status both internally and externally. Internally, this involved integrating the conquered territories with the homeland, reorganizing administration, military affairs, and finances to meet the demands of these new acquisitions, while also resolving social conflicts. Externally, it required solidifying Sweden's position as a great power and creating policies that would deliberately promote its interests. It is unclear whether Charles X Gustav fully understood these tasks. What is certain is that his reign took a different course.
