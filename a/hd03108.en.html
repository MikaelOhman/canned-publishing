Reforming waste legislation to increase material recycling.

Source Riksdagen Read the original at Riksdagen →2026-02-03
TL;DRSweden aims to amend waste management laws to improve material recycling. The proposal would increase the responsibility of companies for their own waste and simplify the regulations for municipalities.
In plain language

The government wants to amend the Environmental Code, which is the law that determines how we should protect nature. The goal is to reduce the amount of waste and ensure that more materials can be reused. This is important for Sweden to achieve the environmental goals set within the EU.

A major change is that businesses selling goods in stores will be given responsibility for their own waste. This also applies to those who generate waste from things like cooking oil or paper. Previously, municipalities had a greater responsibility for this, but now producers themselves must ensure that it is handled correctly. Municipalities will have a secondary responsibility if those who generate the waste do not do their job.

It will also be easier for municipalities to plan their waste management work. They will no longer need to check that all rules are followed in the same way, and the rules for fees will be modernized. The government or another authority will instead determine the major national plans for how waste should be handled throughout the country.

The proposal also includes new rules for how soil and stone from construction projects, so-called excavation materials, are handled. The government also wants the state and municipalities to be exempt from certain financial requirements when disposing of waste in landfills, which are places where waste is buried. If the proposal is approved, the new rules will come into effect on July 1, 2026.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes to the Environmental Code, resulting in a reformed and modernized waste legislation. The purpose is to prevent the generation of waste and increase material recycling, thereby contributing to Sweden's ability to achieve the goals in this area that have been decided within the EU. The proposals also aim to increase resource efficiency and create long-term stability and better planning conditions for companies, municipalities, and government agencies. They also promote investments in innovative technical solutions.

New liability provisions are proposed, clarifying what responsibility for waste entails and when that responsibility ends. Producers of waste in the retail sector will have their own responsibility for waste classified as municipal waste, instead of the municipality. Producers of waste such as used cooking oil, used office paper, and recycled paper will also have their own responsibility. The municipality is proposed to have a secondary responsibility for the waste. Furthermore, municipalities will no longer be responsible for supervising compliance with their own obligations in the waste sector. The government or the authority that the government designates will be able to decide on the national waste plan and the waste prevention program. Municipal waste plans and waste charges will be simplified and modernized. It is also proposed that the municipality should provide a separate financial accounting for the waste sector.

A more appropriate and sustainable management of excavated materials is also proposed, which will contribute to a more circular economy.

Furthermore, it is proposed that the state, municipalities, regions, and municipal associations should be exempt from the requirement to provide financial security when depositing waste and in mining waste operations.

The amendments to the law are proposed to come into force on July 1, 2026.
