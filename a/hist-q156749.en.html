Nils Adolf Erik Nordenskiöld, born on November 18, 1832, in Helsinki, Finland, and died on August 12, 1901, at Dalbyö in the parish of Västerljung, Södermanland, was a Swedish-Finnish baron, geologist, mineralogist, polar explorer, and discoverer. He was a member of the Swedish Academy and also served as a member of the Swedish Parliament.

Nordenskiöld is best known for his explorations of the Arctic Ocean, through several expeditions to places including Svalbard, Novaya Zemlya, and Greenland. The Vega Expedition, conducted between 1878 and 1879, achieved the first successful navigation of the Northeast Passage.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years and Education === Nordenskiöld was the son of Nils Gustaf Nordenskiöld and Margaretha Sophia Haartman, and the grandson of Lars Gabriel von Haartman. He married Baroness Anna Maria Mannerheim in 1863, making him a maternal uncle to Carl Gustaf Emil Mannerheim. He is buried in the Västerljung churchyard. He was also an uncle to Erik Nordenskiöld.

After graduating from high school in 1849, Nordenskiöld studied at the University of Helsinki, where he earned his doctorate in philosophy in the spring of 1857. His career in Finland came to a sudden end when Nordenskiöld delivered a speech that angered the Russian authorities. Nordenskiöld was both liberal and a Finnish nationalist. He hoped that Finland could become independent from Russia, which was not popular with the Russian leadership. Even before this, he had been given a disciplinary punishment by Friedrich Wilhelm Rembert von Berg for, along with friends, expressing dissatisfaction that Sweden had not attempted to liberate Finland while Russia was preoccupied with the Crimean War. Following this new speech, he was sentenced to either make a public apology or be exiled. He chose the latter and moved to Stockholm in 1858.

After moving to Sweden, Nordenskiöld became a professor at the mineralogical department of the National Museum of Natural History in 1858. Attempts were made in 1867 and 1874 to persuade Nordenskiöld to return to Finland as a professor, but he declined. From 1860 to 1878, he was also a teacher of geology and mineralogy at the Technological Institute.

=== Svalbard and Greenland === Nordenskiöld led five research expeditions to Svalbard between 1858 and 1873, and one expedition to West Greenland in 1870. His first two expeditions were led by Otto Torell, but he subsequently led them independently. Among his early works are "Geografisk och geognostisk beskrifning öfver ö. delarna af Spetsbergen och Hinlopen strait" (1863), "Utkast till Spetsbergens geologi" (1866), and several works co-authored with Nils Dunér.

When Nordenskiöld planned his third independent Svalbard expedition, the Swedish Spitsbergen Expedition of 1868, he found that no government funding was available. He therefore turned to Albert Ehrensvärd, then Governor of Gothenburg, who managed to interest Oscar Dickson in the project. This initiated a generous and unusual patronage relationship between Dickson and Nordenskiöld, which financed many of Nordenskiöld's subsequent expeditions.

During Nordenskiöld's Greenland expedition in 1870, primarily intended to study the inland ice, fifteen blocks of iron containing nickel were discovered at Ovifak on Disko Island. The largest block, weighing approximately 22 tons, is now located at the Swedish National Museum of Natural History. Two smaller blocks, weighing 6.5 and 4 tons respectively, were donated to the Universities of Copenhagen and Helsinki. A further small block is housed at the Gothenburg Natural History Museum. Initially, these blocks were believed to be meteorites. However, investigations revealed that the basalt on the island contained small amounts of metallic iron. Subsequent studies by Knud Steenstrup confirmed this, and after renewed metallographic examinations by Carl Benedicks in 1910, the origin of the blocks was confirmed to be from the unusual bedrock.

During the Spitsbergen expedition of 1872, for which Nordenskiöld detailed the scientific results in "Utkast till Isfjordens och Belsounds geologi" (1874–1875), he had hoped to reach the North Pole but was prevented by ice. Early on, Nordenskiöld had been interested in Willem Barentsz's theories that, due to the numerous rivers with relatively warm water flowing from Europe and Asia into the Arctic Ocean, there should be an ice-free channel along the coast. Nordenskiöld adopted this theory, adding that the Earth's rotation should create an eastward current that would strengthen this flow. During this expedition, he had Svenskhuset built, intended as a first step towards phosphate mining in Isfjorden.

=== Journeys Northeast === In 1875, a first expedition was undertaken to the Yenisei River and Novaya Zemlya. Using a small fishing vessel from Tromsø, they reached Novaya Zemlya and then crossed the Arctic Kara Sea to the mouth of the Yenisei River. The ship navigated around Novaya Zemlya but could not complete the journey and had to return home. He himself undertook a boat trip up the Yenisei River and discovered that Siberia is not entirely a desolate tundra region, which was the prevailing belief at the time, but rather contains areas of significant value.
