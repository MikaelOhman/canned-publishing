In plain language

The Nordic Bronze Age lasted approximately from 2000 BC to 500 BC. During this period, people began using bronze – a metal composed of copper and tin – to create tools and objects, replacing the previous reliance on stone or flint.

Researchers divide this era into six distinct periods based on the types of artifacts found in archaeological excavations. During the early part of the Bronze Age, many items originated from other countries, indicating trade with regions far away.

The Bronze Age ended as iron began to be used and the climate changed towards the end of the period. After that, it became much less common to find luxury items such as gold or large burial mounds, giving way to the Iron Age.

Full text

The Nordic Bronze Age culture was an archaeological culture that existed in southern Scandinavia and the northernmost part of Germany during the period 2000/1750–500 BC.

This culture emerged around 1750 BC, developing from the Battle Axe Culture (a variant of the Corded Ware culture) and the Bell Beaker culture, as well as through influences from Central Europe. This latter influence likely came from the Únětice culture in northwestern Germany, particularly regarding metallurgy. The Bronze Age in Scandinavia can be said to have begun shortly after 2000 BC with the initial introduction of bronze tools, followed by a more systematic use of bronze from 1750 BC onwards.

The Nordic Bronze Age is typically divided into six distinct periods based on stylistic features and combinations of artifacts. This division was established around 1900 by the Swedish archaeologist Oscar Montelius. His chronological divisions were based on artifact assemblages, primarily in Sweden, but he made comparisons with the entire European continent and the Mediterranean region. This time division has proven to be surprisingly accurate when compared to more recent 14C dating of artifacts. The first three periods (Period I-III) constitute the Early Bronze Age, while Periods IV-VI represent the Late Bronze Age.

Period 1 of the Bronze Age (1700–1500 BC) is relatively poor in bronze artifacts, and most types of objects from the Neolithic period continued to be used, as well as the practice of building simple stone cists. During Period I, and also later, imported items were found both from the west, such as Ireland and England, and from various regions to the south, including present-day Hungary and Romania. The Pile find is a known hoard dating back to this early period, containing objects from Western Europe and even Central Europe.

It was only during the transition to Period II around 1500 BC that the Bronze Age in Scandinavia experienced a major breakthrough, leading to a large number of new types of bronze objects replacing the previous flint tools. More bronze was deposited in graves as grave goods, and these were primarily items manufactured in Scandinavia.

In the 10th century BC, something drastic happened around the Mediterranean – the Late Bronze Age collapse. The Hittite culture declined, the Mycenaean culture suffered, and the New Kingdom of Egypt was severely threatened. The use of iron and partially disrupted trade connections would have consequences for the rest of Europe. In Central Europe, the Tumulus culture transitioned to the Urnfield culture, which may have been less aristocratic. Urn burials also arrived in Sweden. The introduction of cremation did not mean that burial mounds ceased to be built; instead, new large mounds such as Lusehøj, Hågahögen, and Lugnarohögen were constructed during the later Bronze Age, and the Nordic Bronze Age culture persisted.

After a new climate change around 850–760 BC, followed by another sudden climate change around 650 BC, the Bronze Age soon came to an end. In Scandinavia, iron gradually replaced bronze during the 5th century BC. During the following centuries, grave finds became fewer and sometimes completely absent. There are no signs of luxury items such as gold finds, and the construction of large mounds ceased. The culture was followed by the pre-Roman Iron Age (most of southern Scandinavia) and the Jastorf culture (northern Germany).
