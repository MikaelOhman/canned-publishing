In plain language

Erik Knutsson ruled as King of Sweden from the years 1208 to 1216. He was the son of King Knut Eriksson and married a princess from Denmark. During his reign, it is said that the harvests were good, which at the time was considered a sign that he was a just ruler.

Erik was crowned in 1210 by Bishop Valerius in Gamla Uppsala. It is uncertain whether the previous king had actually been crowned, making Erik the first king of whom we can be certain was crowned in this manner. He died from a severe fever in 1216 and is buried in the Varnhem monastery church.

There is not much written information about what he did during his time as king, but he was involved in several important battles. Shortly before his death, he also received an important letter from the Pope in Rome, which gave him support and the right to rule over Sweden.

Full text

Erik Knutsson, also known as Erik X (during the 13th century, he was called Erik II), born year unknown, died on April 10, 1216, from a sudden fever at Näs fortress on Visingsö. He was King of Sweden from 1208 to 1216. He is buried in Varnhem monastery church.

He is believed to be the first Swedish king to be crowned, by Archbishop Valerius in Old Uppsala in 1210. This depends on whether his predecessor, Sverker the Younger, was crowned in Linköping in 1200, which is uncertain.

== Biography == King Erik was the son of King Knut Eriksson of Sweden and his wife, whose name is unknown. In 1210, he married Rikissa of Denmark, daughter of Valdemar the Great of Denmark and Sofia of Minsk. In 1205, he escaped from the Battle of Älgarås, where his three brothers were killed. He then spent three years with relatives in Norway, returning in 1208 and defeating Sverker the Younger at the Battle of Lena. Erik was elected king that same year, but the coronation did not take place until November 1210, after the Battle of Gestilren, when Sverker the Younger's troops were defeated again, and Sverker the Younger himself was killed. King Erik's coronation is, in fact, the earliest known coronation in Sweden, and it was performed by Bishop Valerius, who had previously been a supporter of King Sverker the Younger. During the early part of his reign, King Erik had a jarl (earl) named Folke, who died at the Battle of Gestilren. In 1216, Pope Innocent III took Sweden under his protection and confirmed King Erik's right to rule not only over Sweden but also over the territories he could conquer from the pagans. Previously, Innocent III had been on the side of King Sverker the Younger. The letter was sent on April 4, 1216, and therefore did not reach King Erik before his death a week later (April 10).

Very little is known about the reign of King Eric. However, in the Westrogothic Law's list of kings, it states that he was a "good year-king," meaning that the harvests were good during his rule. The banner that King Eric carried in the Battle of Gestilren was kept by the lawman Eskil in Skara, who in 1219 presented it as an honorary gift to the visiting Icelandic legal scholar Snorre Sturluson. He was styled "Ericus D.G. Rex Sueorum" (Eric, by the Grace of God, King of Sweden) around 1210.

=== Family === Eric Knutsson married Rikissa of Denmark in 1210, a daughter of Valdemar the Great and Sofia of Minsk. Together they had the following children:

  • Sofia (died before April 24, 1241), married to Prince Henry Burwin III of Mecklenburg (died 1277/1278)
  • Marianne, Princess of Pomerania, also known as Mariana and Marina.
  • Ingeborg, married to Birger Jarl.
  • Eric, posthumously called Eric the Learned and Lame (born in 1216 after his father's death).
  • Katarina Eriksdotter. The Skänninge Annals state that the Östergötland lawman Lars Petersson and the "folkung" Filip Petersson were sons of King Eric Knutsson's daughter, Katarina Eriksdotter.

The Chronicle of Karlskrona states that Eric the Learned and Lame had a sister, Märta Bonde, which would imply that she too was a daughter of Eric Knutsson. This is considered by historians such as Dick Harrison to be political propaganda for Märta's relative, Karl Knutsson (Bonde), who would thereby have become related to the Erikian dynasty, and is therefore likely false according to Harrison.
