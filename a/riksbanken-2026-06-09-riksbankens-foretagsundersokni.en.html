The Riksbank's business survey: "A new 'black swan' event" that risks delaying the recovery.

TL;DRLarge Swedish companies believe that the economy is still weak, and that the war in the Middle East is creating uncertainty which could prolong the time it takes for the economy to improve.
In plain language

The Swedish National Bank, the authority responsible for Sweden's currency, has surveyed many large companies to assess their current situation. Most companies reported that the situation is uncertain due to the war in the Middle East. While they can currently absorb higher prices, such as those for oil, this uncertainty makes it difficult for the economy to grow rapidly.

Companies plan to raise their prices next year because their own costs have increased. The actual extent of these price increases will depend on future developments and whether people continue to want to buy goods and services.

Some industries are performing better than others. Retail stores and restaurants are noticing an increase in customer spending, although consumers remain cautious with their money. Other sectors, such as aviation and mining, are doing well, while the construction industry is struggling due to a decrease in new housing projects.

The National Bank conducts these surveys to gain a better understanding of how companies are performing. Starting in 2026, they will conduct these surveys more frequently so that they can monitor developments more closely.

Full text

Press Release

According to Swedish large companies, the economic situation remains weak. Before the war in the Middle East broke out, there was a sense of cautious improvement in the economy. The war does not seem to have significantly altered this picture. However, uncertainty has increased again, and companies believe this risks delaying the already protracted recovery.

Published 2026-06-09

"Even if there is peace today, there will be cost increases that need to be passed on."

The effects of the war in the Middle East are currently assessed as being less severe than the crises of recent years. The war and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz have so far gradually increased costs for companies, primarily through higher oil prices. However, the situation is still considered manageable. This is partly because companies have become more resilient to various types of disruptions after learning from the crises of recent years. But the longer the war lasts, the greater the expected impact will be on both companies and the economy as a whole. Even if the war is resolved in the near future, some costs are likely to remain elevated for a period.

Companies intend to raise their sales prices in the coming year. However, they emphasize that it is highly uncertain to what extent these higher costs will actually translate into price increases. For now, they are talking about smaller price increases, but this depends on how costs and demand develop.

The economic situation for the retail and tourism sectors continues to improve gradually. Households are slowly starting to spend more, but remain price-conscious. The grocery sector is seeing increased sales volumes following the temporary reduction in VAT on food, but other industries are also experiencing improved sales trends.

Among companies that sell to other businesses, opinions on the economic situation vary widely. Companies operating in or serving the data center, defense, aerospace, and mining sectors continue to experience strong demand. The wood products and pulp industries are still struggling, partly because new residential construction has not yet started. There is some positive news in the automotive industry, which is experiencing slightly better demand from Europe.

The Swedish National Bank regularly interviews Sweden's largest companies in the industrial, construction, retail, and parts of the service sectors, as well as employer organizations. Representatives from 41 companies, with a total of approximately 230,000 employees, were interviewed via visits, telephone calls, or online services. The interviews were primarily conducted during the period of May 7-19, 2026. The results of these interviews are presented in the Swedish National Bank's business survey. The quotes above come from these interviews.

Starting in 2026, the Swedish National Bank will increase the number of business surveys to four per year. The reports will be published quarterly: in March, June, September, and December.
