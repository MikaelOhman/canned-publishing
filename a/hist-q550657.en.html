Gustaf Carlsson Horn (Horn af Björneborg), born on October 22, 1592, at Örbyhus Castle in Uppland, during his father's imprisonment there, and died on May 10, 1657, while visiting Skara, was a Swedish field marshal, member of the council of state, and general governor. He was the son of Carl Henriksson Horn af Kanckas.

After studying abroad, he was appointed chamberlain to Gustav II Adolf in 1618, became a member of the council of state in 1625, and that same year received Marienborg Castle as a gift from the king. He became a field marshal in 1628, was elevated to the title of Count of Björneborgs kungsgård in 1651, along with its associated 357 Finnish farms. Later, he served as general governor of Livonia in 1652, as Lord Marshal in 1653, and that same year as vice-president of the War College. During the Thirty Years' War, he is considered to have been one of the most prominent military commanders in the Swedish army.

== Biography == Gustaf Horn studied at German universities and subsequently participated in the Russian War. He learned military tactics from Moritz of Orange in the Netherlands. In 1618, he became a chamberlain to Gustav II Adolf and negotiated marriage arrangements between Gustav II Adolf and Maria Eleonora of Brandenburg. He was appointed colonel of the Norrland regiment in 1621. As such, he participated in the siege of Riga, where he was seriously wounded. From 1625 to 1628, he defended Livonia against Poland alongside Johan Pontusson De la Gardie. Gustaf Horn became a member of the Swedish Council of State in 1625 and Field Marshal in 1628. Horn demonstrated great skill in battles such as Wallhof and Wenden in 1626. This led to King Gustav II Adolf appointing him his closest advisor in the Thirty Years' War in 1630.

Horn won the first major battle at Falkenberg and then commanded the Swedish forces under the king at the Battle of Breitenfeld in 1631. He was responsible for the left flank, where the Saxon army was positioned, while the right flank was led by Johan Banér. After the retreat of the Saxon army, he was in great danger, but he saved Sweden from defeat through skillful leadership by forming a new front and, with the help of the king, breaking up Tilly's formations. The subsequent Swedish victory was largely due to Gustaf Horn's efforts. Subsequently, Horn moved to Upper Franconia and, with 7,000 men, captured the Teutonic Order's residence city of Marienburg and the Bishopric of Bamberg. After this, Horn and Gustav II Adolf crossed the Danube and Lech into Bavaria, from where Horn was sent to the Rhineland, where he captured Koblenz and Trier. He also defeated the Imperial army at Wiesloch and Schwaben. In April 1633, Horn joined forces with Duke Bernhard of Weimar. However, this cooperation failed, and on the advice of, among others, his father-in-law Axel Oxenstierna, the two commanders were separated. Later that year, in August 1633, Gustaf Horn marched to Lake Constance.

After Wallenstein's death in 1634, Horn captured several places in Swabia but again clashed with Duke Bernhard of Weimar. The two were forced to cooperate to save Regensburg and Nördlingen, but on August 27, 1634, the Battle of Nördlingen was lost to the Spanish-Imperial army. This defeat was attributed to his being influenced by Duke Bernhard's eagerness. Gustaf Horn became a prisoner of war and spent many years in German prisons. His release was delayed because the Imperial forces did not want to risk him commanding a Swedish army again. He was finally released from captivity in March 1642 in exchange for three Imperial generals: Hans Christoph von Buchheim, Lorenz von Hofkirchen, and Johann von Werth. He returned to Sweden and married Sigrid Bielke in Stockholm on July 9, 1643. His family accompanied him on his many travels throughout the kingdom and on various military assignments. During the war against Denmark in 1644, Gustaf Horn attacked Scania and conquered the entire region except for Malmö and Kristianstad. In 1645, a siege of Malmö was initiated, but it was interrupted by the Treaty of Brömsebro. This military campaign became known as "Horn's War." In 1644, he became the district judge (lagman) of Söderfinne legal district. In 1652, Horn became Governor-General of Livonia and in 1653 he was appointed Field Marshal, General Commander-in-Chief, and Vice President of the War College. At the beginning of the Polish War in 1655, Charles X Gustav tasked Horn with leading the Swedish defense.

Horn had the Stora Hornsberg iron mill built on Kungsholmen according to designs by architect Jean de la Vallée; the building was demolished in the 1890s. On April 28, 1628, Gustaf Horn received the estates of Häringe and Hammersta with subordinate farms and properties in Södertörn as a fief from Gustav II Adolf. During the 1650s, while he was on campaign, he had Häringe Castle built. Through his wife's inheritance, he acquired the medieval Wik Castle in Uppland. He also acquired Boo Castle from Alexander Leslie.

Gustaf Horn died in Skara in 1657 and was buried in the Horn family vault in St. James' Church in Stockholm on August 11, 1660, when the church bells rang eight times. He was succeeded by Per Brahe as commander-in-chief. Gustaf Horn was one of Gustav II Adolf's best field commanders, alongside Johan Banér. Horn's specialty was defense. He was also a prominent official. He was feared by soldiers for his strict military discipline.
