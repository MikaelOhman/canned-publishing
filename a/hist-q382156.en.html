In plain language

Emund the Old was a king who ruled Sweden in the mid-10th century. He was called "the Old" because he was the oldest when he became king. There are many old texts about him, but they say different things. One text from Adam of Bremen describes that Emund was not a very devout Christian, while other sources tell about his son who died in a war.

A significant part of the story concerns an agreement with Denmark. According to an old Swedish law text, Emund is said to have determined the border between Sweden and Denmark using six large stones. This border was then used for several hundred years. Today, however, researchers are not entirely certain whether it was actually Emund who signed this agreement or if the text has been altered later.

Because the sources from this period are very old and often written by people with their own opinions, it is difficult to know exactly everything about him. Some historians believe that he may have been the father of another well-known person named Ingvar the Far-Traveled, who led a large Viking expedition eastward.

Full text

Emund the Old, or Emund the Wicked, was a Swedish king from approximately 1050, who died around 1060. He was the son of Olof Skötkonung and the noblewoman Edla, and the half-brother of his predecessor on the throne, Anund Jakob. Emund the Old became the last Swedish king of the dynasty of Erik Segersäll.

==Emund according to the sources== Emund is primarily known through accounts from Adam of Bremen, but also from Hervararsagan, Snorre Sturlasson's Heimskringla, and the royal list in Västgötalagen. A contemporary runestone, Gästriklands runinskrifter 11, mentions Emund. According to Adam of Bremen, Emund was the son of one of Olof's noblewomen, and although he acknowledges that Emund was baptized, Adam implies that Emund's Christianity was not entirely genuine. He claims that Emund allowed the Christian mission to be led by the self-proclaimed archbishop Osmundus. When the Catholic archdiocese of Hamburg attempted to replace him with their own bishop, Adalvard the Elder, Emund allegedly rejected him. According to Adam, Osmundus gave Emund and the people false teachings about Christianity. Adam's negative judgment likely stems from the fact that Emund actively supported the English missionary activities at the expense of the archdiocese of Hamburg-Bremen. In the same passage, it is also stated that the only one who supported Adalvard was Stenkil, but Adam is unclear whether this person was Emund's nephew (grandchild or possibly great-grandchild) or stepson. Later, he is simply referred to as a nephew. Some historians have interpreted this as meaning that Emund was married to Astrid Nialsdotter, who, according to Hervararsagan, was the mother of Stenkil in her second marriage. A scholion also states that Emund sent his son Anund on a military campaign to "the land of women," where he died after the defenders poisoned their drinking water. The royal list in Västgötalagen places a king named Emund "slemme" (meaning bad, ignorant, unreliable, or awful) after Anund Jakob, and states that he signed a treaty with the King of Denmark which defined the borders of the two countries:

Snorre Sturlasson has little to say about Emund, mentioning only that he was raised in the family of his mother's relatives in "Vindland," and that he may have been the king who helped Magnus the Good in 1035 when he attempted to reclaim his father's throne.

In Old Swedish, Emund is referred to as Æmunðær gamlæ or Æmunðær gammal, meaning "the old one." He received this epithet because he was elderly when he became king. According to a hypothesis by Mats G. Larsson, besides his son Anund, Emund may have also been the father of Ingvar the Far-Traveled, who led a large Viking expedition eastward and died in Särkland in 1041.

== The Boundary Treaty with Denmark == According to the royal list in the West Gothian Law, King Emund formalized the boundary between Sweden and Denmark. The Boundary Treaty established an agreement between Emund Slemme and the Danish king Sven Tveskägg. This was marked by six boundary stones from Halland to Brömsebro. With minor changes, this border remained in effect until the 17th century. Since Sven Tveskägg reigned from 985 to 1014, which is 35 years before Emund the Old, the information about the boundary treaty presents a chronological problem. This issue is usually resolved by assuming that it was not Sven Tveskägg but Sven Estridsson (king 1047–1074) who is meant. However, Lauritz Weibull suggested that this Emund Slemme may not be identical to Emund the Old, but rather to Emund Eriksson. In that case, the boundary demarcation would have taken place at the beginning of Sven Tveskägg's reign, as it is certain that Erik Segersäll was king in 992. Weibull later retracted his own theory, but other authors such as Åke Ohlmarks continued to support it. Peter Sawyer argued instead that the treaty was a Danish forgery from the 13th century. Modern research questions the authenticity of the boundary treaty.
