In plain language

The Swedish Civil Defence Agency has developed new regulations that will come into effect on July 15, 2026. These regulations aim to make it easier for both companies and investors to understand which activities are considered essential services. This means reducing the amount of unnecessary paperwork for companies that do not require review.

As a general rule, these regulations apply to companies with a turnover of at least 5 million Swedish krona or employing at least five people. For certain types of businesses, the requirements may be higher or lower depending on their importance to society. Small businesses in industries where security is not considered threatened will now be subject to review much less frequently.

The purpose of the entire law is to ensure that foreign powers do not gain control over activities that are important for Sweden's security. The Swedish Civil Defence Agency, formerly known as MSB, is responsible for reviewing these investments to protect Swedish infrastructure and security.

Full text

Anyone considering investing in a Swedish company that carries out essential services must be able to determine whether the business falls under the regulations governing the review of foreign direct investments. The regulations need to be clear enough for investors and their representatives to use.

The Act (2023:560) on the Review of Foreign Direct Investments aims to prevent undesirable control by foreign powers in Swedish companies engaged in activities deemed sensitive.

In the regulation MCFFS 2026:13, published on June 22, 2026, the Agency for Civil Defense clarifies which essential services are covered by the Act. The regulation comes into force on July 15, 2026, and replaces the previous MSBFS 2024:9.

"The changes to the regulations mean that some activities have been added while others no longer fall under them. However, we also wanted to make it clearer which activities are subject to the general conditions of the regulation," says Niklaz Kling, head of the unit for supply chain analysis at the Agency for Civil Defense. "We hope this will reduce administrative burdens for both companies and the relevant authorities."

Companies that typically fall under the regulations have a minimum turnover of 5 million Swedish krona or employ at least five full-time equivalents. However, higher or lower criteria for turnover and staffing levels apply to certain types of activities.

Furthermore, limitations have been introduced so that fewer newly established companies are covered in industries with many small businesses where the level of sensitivity is considered limited.

Some may have noticed that we published regulation MCFFS 2026:10 with the same name on June 15. Unfortunately, this regulation contained an incorrect date for coming into force. It has been withdrawn and is now being replaced by MCFFS 2026:13.

Act (2023:560) on the screening of foreign direct investments.

Regulation (EU) 2019/452 of the European Parliament and of the Council of March 19, 2019, establishing a framework for the screening of foreign direct investments in the Union (the EU Regulation), entered into force on April 11, 2019, and has been applicable since October 11, 2020. The Swedish Parliament adopted Act (2023:560) on the screening of foreign direct investments on September 21, 2023. On November 2, 2023, the government decided on Regulation (2023:624) on the screening of foreign direct investments. The Act and the regulation came into force on December 1, 2023.

According to section 5 of Regulation (2023:624) on the screening of foreign direct investments, the Swedish Civil Defence Agency is authorized to issue regulations specifying which critical infrastructure activities are covered by the law. On November 29, 2023, the Swedish National Board of Rescue Services (MSB) issued regulations (MSBFS 2023:4) specifying which critical infrastructure activities are covered by Act (2023:560) on the screening of foreign direct investments. These regulations came into force on December 1, 2023. The regulations have been revised once since then, and the currently valid regulations, MSBFS 2024:9, came into force on October 1, 2024.

As of January 1, 2026, the Swedish National Board of Rescue Services (MSB) will be renamed the Swedish Civil Defence Agency.
