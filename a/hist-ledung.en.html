"Ledung" was an old term used during the Middle Ages to assemble a naval force. The purpose was to allow the king to quickly deploy warriors to defend the country against other nations or pirates attacking the coastlines.

The system worked by dividing the country into different regions, such as harbors and ship districts. There are differing opinions among researchers regarding the age of the system and how it functioned in practice. Some believe it is very old, while others argue that the organized form of "ledung" developed later during the Middle Ages.

Full text

"Ledung" (Old Swedish: *leþunger*, Old Norwegian and Old Icelandic: *leiðangr*) was a medieval military term referring to naval expeditions, as well as legislation concerning conscription and "leding" taxes. The "leding" system was implemented in the three Nordic kingdoms. In Sweden, it is known through references in some of the regional laws and indirect allusions within the medieval tax system. The organization encompassed two main functions: a military force for warfare ("utrodd") and coastal defense ("wardhald").

The purpose of the "leding" was to provide the king with a popular army to use against other kingdoms, or against Vikings and pirates. The "leding" differed from peasant armies and levies called up from homes that could be used for the kingdom's defense, as well as from the king's personal retinue (hird). Each year, the king had the right to call out the "leding" in a specific order. A nobleman who was able to equip an entire "leding" ship was entitled to certain privileges according to some laws. Even within the "leding" system, one can see the beginnings of the formation of a nobility.

== Structure == The obligation to provide "leding" varied by region, sometimes based on districts ("härad" or "hundare"), and in other cases based on naval divisions ("skeppslag," which often later became judicial districts). These units were responsible for equipping a certain number of ships. Sometimes, the "skeppslag" was a sub-division of a "hundare." Subdivisions within harbors also existed.

== Regional Variations == The "ledung" system of naval service was applied in the areas along the eastern coast of the kingdom; from Ångermanland in the north down to the border with Blekinge in the south, as well as Öland, Gotland, and at least the southwestern part of Finland. Particularly within the old Svealand region, the "ledung" obligation extended far inland to Närke. However, in Västergötland and the interior of Småland, the "ledung" system was not implemented. In Bohuslän, the term used was "Skeppsreda." The legislation and timing of implementation varied between different regions. On Gotland, the "ledung" was introduced around the middle of the 12th century and revised in 1285. The laws of Hälsingeland exempted the people of Umeå and the land north of it from the "ledung" obligation, in exchange for a bow tax.

=== Laws of Uppland === Each "hundare" (a unit of area) in Uppland provided four ships, and each ship district likely contributed one ship or a smaller war vessel. The laws of Uppland, in its royal code, tenth section, state the following about the "ledung":

The Uppland districts of Attundaland, Tiundaland, and Fjädrundaland consisted of eight, ten, and four "hundare" respectively. From this recruitment area of 22 "hundare," approximately 2200 soldiers were gathered, divided among around 88 war vessels. In addition, there was the coastal region of Roden (which extended into Lake Mälaren). The name of the Finnish province of Satakunta is a direct Finnish translation of the word "hundare," and it is speculated that some form of "ledung" may have also existed in medieval Finland. The word "ledung" has been suggested as an etymology for the island of Lidingö, but the island's name likely has a different origin.

== Equipment and Organization == Each time the king called for a levy (ledung), a fully armed man from each harbor would assemble at the ship with a shield and sword, spear, iron helmet, mail or armor, and a bow with three dozen arrows. These weapons were known as "folkvapen" (people's weapons) and were always kept ready in case of threat of war, which was called the "landvärn" (national defense). A "hundare" would assemble with 100 men and four warships, also called "snäckor," which were used during the levy. Each ship therefore had twelve rowers on each side, a total of twenty-four, plus a helmsman who may have been the commander. There might also be a cook on board, responsible for food preparation and provisions (he "broke" the bread), and he likely came from the local village where the ships were normally sheltered. Norway had a similar system of organization, called "fylken." According to the laws of Södermanland, the levy fleet sailed at Pentecost. If someone failed to provide a ship and rowers, they would be fined, and this fine may have been the first form of taxation. Therefore, if a levy was not required, one could pay in cash: three marks for a harbor, and forty marks for a ship's complement.

== Research and Source Criticism == The primary source for information about the "leding" (a type of organized levy of ships) and its organization are the medieval regional laws. Older research often assumed that the legal texts described actual conditions as they existed at the time. In older research (for example, Hafström, see sources below), it was believed that the leding organization emerged early and had roots stretching back to prehistoric times. However, contemporary archaeologists and medieval historians argue that the leding organization developed later.

During the Viking Age, local chieftains likely formed temporary peasant militias. The organized royal leding is generally thought to have first appeared in Denmark in the 10th century. The term "hundare" (a unit of measurement) is mentioned on Runestone Upplands runinskrifter 212 from the late 10th century. However, when considering the folkland divisions as they existed in the 13th century, it's unlikely they are older than the early 12th century. Therefore, it must be assumed that the leding was initially organized differently.
