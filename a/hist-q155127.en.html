Axel Oxenstierna ([ˈǎksɛl ˈʊ̂ksɛnˌɧæːɳa]), born June 16, 1583, at Fånö estate in the parish of Löts, Uppland; died August 28, 1654, in Stockholm, was a Swedish count and statesman. He became a member of the Council of State in 1609 and was appointed Chancellor in 1612. In practice, he served as regent in 1632 when Queen Christina was underage. Oxenstierna is considered the founder of the Swedish state apparatus. He established government agencies and ensured their cooperation, and he divided the country into equally sized provinces with fixed borders that are largely still in effect today. As a reward for his contributions to the negotiations leading to the Treaty of Brömsebro in 1645, Axel Oxenstierna was granted the title of Count with Södermöre in Kalmar County as his domain.

== Background ==

=== Family History === Oxenstierna was the son of Baron and Council member Gustaf Gabrielsson Oxenstierna (1551–1597) and Barbro Axelsdotter Bielke (1556–1624), and the brother of Gabriel Gustafsson Oxenstierna (1587–1640). On May 5, 1608, he married Anna Åkesdotter Bååt of the Bååt family, who resided at Oldenburg, a late medieval castle near present-day Tidö Castle, southwest of Västerås in Västmanland. Anna Bååt was born in December 1579, died on June 23, 1649, and was buried in Jäders Church. She was the daughter of Åke Johansson Bååt and Christina Trolle.

=== Childhood and Early Years ===

Axel Oxenstierna belonged to a highly respected noble family that had always shown strong loyalty to the Vasa kings. His father, Gustaf, was entrusted by King John III in September 1584 with the demanding task of being governor of Reval Castle (Narva), one of Sweden's most important outposts against Russia. Axel, who was only a year old at the time, accompanied his father to Reval (Tallinn) and spent four years of his childhood in the city.

In 1588, Gustaf Oxenstierna returned to his estate, Fånö, near Lake Mälaren. Here, Axel grew up among a growing number of siblings. When he was eight years old, the family moved from Fånö to Fiholm, an estate in western Lake Mälaren, where his father died in 1597.

In 1599, Axel Oxenstierna, along with his brothers Krister and Gabriel, was sent by their guardian, Johan Oxenstierna, to the most prestigious universities of the time: Wittenberg University, Jena University, and Rostock University. He studied primarily theology and Latin. Upon returning in 1603, he was appointed as a chamberlain (a court official) to King Charles IX. The times had changed since he left Sweden. The major conflict between Duke Charles and the nobility had been resolved with Duke Charles' victory and the Stockholm Bloodbath. When Axel Oxenstierna swore allegiance to Charles IX, it was a different group of nobles who sat on the Council of State than those that Gustaf Oxenstierna had once belonged to.

In 1605, internal conflicts flared up again, and King Charles IX's suspicions focused particularly on Axel Oxenstierna's relative, Hogenskild Bielke. Charles seized Hogenskild's papers and obtained evidence of connections between him and Barbro Bielke, Axel Oxenstierna's mother. Searches were then conducted at Fiholm, and Charles threatened Barbro with banishment and the loss of all her property. However, the crisis passed without serious consequences.

In 1606, Axel Oxenstierna was entrusted with a mission to Mecklenburg, and during his absence, he was appointed to the Council of State. In 1608, through his marriage to Anna Åkesdotter Bååt, he also became the owner of Tidö Castle, located about a mile east of Fiholm. When he was not required to be elsewhere due to council meetings, diplomatic missions, or other duties for the kingdom, he alternated between living at Fiholm and Tidö.

In 1610, when relations between Charles IX and King Christian IV of Denmark became very strained, Axel was tasked with participating in a mission to the Danish king. Unlike Charles, he was eager to avoid war with Denmark at a time when Sweden was economically weak, and the country's king was elderly. However, in Denmark, he encountered a warlike atmosphere that drowned out all voices for peace. Back in Sweden, he tried, along with other councilors, unsuccessfully to curb the king's provocative behavior.

Towards the end of Charles IX's life, Axel Oxenstierna was somewhat sidelined from political deliberations. Instead, decisions were made by those who shared the king's thirst for battle, and Axel Oxenstierna was forced to witness with concern the outbreak of war with Denmark in the spring of 1611. When the king went into the field, Axel was one of the councilors entrusted with the demanding task of managing the government, along with Queen Kristina and Duke Johan. He became the driving force behind this effort.

Through Charles IX's will in 1611, Axel Oxenstierna became a member of Gustav II Adolf's regency council. Oxenstierna gradually took more and more leadership within the government. It soon became customary for people from various quarters to appeal to him when important decisions were needed, and he emerged as the most significant advisor to the royal family.
