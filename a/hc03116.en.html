In plain language

The government has proposed changes to several laws governing how government agencies and other organizations conduct procurement. These changes cover regulations related to everything from standard public purchases to transportation and contracts for water and energy. The proposal is based on new EU regulations known as the IPI Regulation.

A key element of the proposal is to clarify what rights companies have if a government agency makes a mistake. If an agency fails to comply with the regulations, companies selling goods or services should be able to exercise their rights to file complaints and seek redress. It will also be made clear that a specific supervisory authority is responsible for ensuring compliance with the new EU regulations.

If a government agency makes a mistake that harms a company, the agency will be required to pay compensation. This means that the company may be able to recover the costs they incurred in submitting bids and participating in the process. This applies if the agency's error made it more difficult for the company to win the contract.

If the Parliament approves the proposal, the new regulations will come into effect on July 1, 2025. The proposal affects several different laws that govern how public procurement, i.e., when the state purchases goods, should be conducted.

Full text

The government proposes certain amendments to the procurement legislation in light of the IPI Regulation. These proposals aim to clarify suppliers' rights to legal remedies in the procurement laws, specifically regarding the incorrect application of EU law provisions on public procurement by procuring authorities or entities. It also clarifies that the application of the IPI Regulation's provisions on procurement falls within the scope of the supervisory authority's oversight. The proposals involve amendments to the following laws:

  • Law (2016:1145) on Public Procurement
  • Law (2016:1146) on Procurement within the Utilities Sectors
  • Law (2016:1147) on Procurement of Concessions
  • Law (2010:1065) on Public Transport

The proposed amendments are scheduled to come into force on July 1, 2025.

Proposed Parliamentary Decision

The government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to Law (2016:1145) on Public Procurement.
  2. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to Law (2016:1146) on Procurement within the Utilities Sectors.
  3. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to Law (2016:1147) on Procurement of Concessions.
  4. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to Law (2010:1065) on Public Transport.

A procuring authority that has failed to comply with the provisions of this law or with EU law provisions on procurement shall compensate a supplier for any damages incurred as a result. The right to compensation includes reimbursement to a supplier who participated in a procurement process for the costs incurred in preparing a bid and otherwise participating in the procurement, if the violation of the provisions has diminished the supplier's chances of being awarded the contract.
