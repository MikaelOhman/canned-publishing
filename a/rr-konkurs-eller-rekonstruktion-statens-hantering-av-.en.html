Bankruptcy involves closing the business because debts cannot be paid, while restructuring is an attempt to save and rehabilitate the operations. Full text

The Swedish National Audit Office has reviewed the state's handling of insolvency proceedings for companies, including bankruptcies and corporate restructuring processes. A company is declared bankrupt when it cannot pay its debts, and this situation is not expected to improve in the future. If a company experiences financial difficulties but is fundamentally viable, a restructuring process can be initiated with the aim of saving the company. In Sweden, responsibility for managing insolvency proceedings is shared between the state and appointed administrators (bankruptcy trustees and restructuring officers). The audit reveals that the state's handling of insolvency proceedings for companies is generally efficient, although there are also shortcomings. The management and division of responsibilities will change in 2026, but these changes will not automatically resolve the existing deficiencies. For each bankruptcy and restructuring case, an administrator is appointed by the district courts. In Sweden, there is no formal licensing system for administrators, but the district courts' and the Swedish Enforcement Agency's (Kronofogden) management of the pool of available administrators constitutes a form of informal authorization, not regulated by law. To be eligible for appointments, an administrator must effectively be approved by the Swedish Enforcement Agency and listed on the district courts' lists of approved administrators. Because only a limited assessment of the suitability of potential administrators can be made at the beginning of a bankruptcy or restructuring case, this management of the pool of available administrators is crucial to the efficiency of insolvency proceedings. The audit shows that the lack of legal regulation leads to differences in how district courts handle these matters and some degree of duplication of effort, as both the district courts and the Swedish Enforcement Agency assess the suitability of potential administrators when they are being considered for approval. Furthermore, decisions by the Swedish Enforcement Agency to exclude administrators from the pool of available administrators cannot be appealed.
