Carl von Linné the Younger, born January 20, 1741, in Falun, and died November 1, 1783, in Uppsala, was a Swedish natural scientist. He was the son of Carl von Linné and his wife Sara Elisabeth Moraea, and he succeeded his father as professor of botany and medicine. He was appointed at the age of 22 through a system called "survivance," which was not uncommon at the time for sons of prominent fathers.

== Childhood == Carl von Linné the Younger was already earmarked by his father from an early age to succeed him in his professorship at Uppsala University, despite the fact that he did not show any particular aptitude for natural sciences or other studies, and he himself does not seem to have aspired to follow in his father's footsteps. His father ensured that, even as a child, at the age of 9, he was enrolled at Smålands nation, in the university's registry and in the medical faculty. As tutors in his home education, his father assigned some of his most promising students: Pehr Löfling, Daniel Rolander, and Johan Peter Falck. However, Carl von Linné the Younger never took any academic exams, attended any oral examinations, or defended any academic dissertations. Despite this, at just 18 years old (1759), he was appointed "demonstrator at the botanical garden" by his father's recommendation. As a reason for his proposal, Linné stated that his son had been familiar with the garden from childhood and would inherit the extensive collections, and that the father now wanted to dedicate himself to "nurturing him into becoming a botanist."

== Academic Career == In the following years, the younger Linné described a number of rarer plants from the university garden in "Decas I et II plantarum rariorum Horti Upsaliensis" (1762–1763) and "Plantarum rariorum Horti Upsaliensis fasciculus primus" (1767). After the Riksdag proposed in 1762 that Carl Linnaeus the elder should have the privilege of choosing his successor, and he nominated his son as suitable for the position, Carl Linné the younger was appointed professor by His Majesty the King in 1763, but initially without salary, as long as the elder Linnaeus continued to perform the duties. Before the recommendation, Linné had inquired with his former student Daniel Solander, who was employed at the British Museum in London. However, after a lengthy delay, Solander declined the opportunity to become Linnaeus' successor in Uppsala. In 1765, Carl Linné the younger was promoted to honorary doctor of medicine, as Crown Prince Gustaf, acting as university chancellor, had expressed this wish to the medical faculty. This undeserved promotion caused widespread resentment towards him, and the unchosen role as Linnaeus' successor and scientific heir led to depression and a lack of self-confidence in the son. In 1777, he was formally installed in his professorship. In his later years, Carl Linnaeus the elder had stipulated that his extensive herbarium should not go to his son, who had not shown sufficient interest in science, but should be sold for the benefit of his daughters. This was contrary to what had previously been promised. However, since the younger Linné needed the herbarium to fulfill his duties, and he had a very strained relationship with his mother, he had to provide promissory notes for substantial amounts and forgo certain other benefits in order to obtain it. Thus, it is Carl Linné the younger's merit that the invaluable Linnaean collections remained intact; he conscientiously cared for them and expanded them, especially during his two-year journey to England, France, the Netherlands, and Denmark from 1781 to 1783. In London, he developed the beginnings of a serious illness (jaundice), and shortly after returning home, he suffered from intermittent fever and a stroke, which led to his death. He died unmarried, and with him, the noble von Linné family line ended in the male branch. He is buried alongside his parents in the family vault in Uppsala Cathedral.

== Publications == In addition to the publications he produced during his time as a demonstrator, he published his father's unfinished additions to "Systema Naturae" (the botanical section), along with his own contributions to the work, under the title "Supplementum Plantarum" (1781). The author abbreviation "L.f." can be used for Carl von Linné the Younger when referring to a scientific name in botany; see Wikipedia articles that link to this author abbreviation.
