Olaf Skötkonung (Old Norse: Óláfr skautkonungr), born around 980 (or possibly in the 960s), and likely died during the winter of 1021–1022, was King of Sweden from approximately 995 to 1022. He was the son of Erik Segersäll.

Olaf is the first king for whom it is definitively known that he ruled over both Svealand and Götaland. He is also the first Swedish king who was baptized, remained Christian, and contributed to the Christianization of Sweden. Furthermore, Olaf Skötkonung was the first to mint coins in Sweden. Beyond these facts, not much else is known with certainty.

== War with Denmark and Norway == At the beginning of his reign, Olof Skötkonung was at war with the Danish king Sven Tveskägg, whose kingdom his father, Erik Segersäll, had ruled over for a short time, and which Olof claimed as his own. However, the war ended with Olof recognizing Sven Tveskägg as King of Denmark, and the latter's marriage to Olof's mother (this detail is mentioned in sources that identify her as Swiatoslawa of Poland, as well as those that claim she was named Sigrid Storråda) led to a rapprochement.

There has been speculation about whether Olof Skötkonung is the same person as the king "Anlaf" who, according to the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, together with King "Swein" (Sven Tveskägg) and a Viking fleet of 94 ships, made an unsuccessful attempt to conquer London and subsequently ravaged southeastern England until the English king Æthelred the Unready offered them 16,000 pounds in Danegeld.

According to Icelandic sources, Olof, again allied with Sven Tveskägg, along with Norwegian rebels, participated in the Battle of Svolder in the year 1000 against the Norwegian king Olav Tryggvason. As a result of this victory, Norway was divided between the two kings, and Olof Skötkonung received Bohuslän and southern Tröndelag, territories that he later lost in further conflicts with the Norwegians. From 1015 onwards, Olof Haraldsson successfully claimed the Norwegian crown and managed to kill Olof Skötkonung's jarls (earls) in Bohuslän. According to Icelandic sagas, Olof later had to make peace with his Norwegian namesake (see Torgny lagman) and marry his daughter to him.

== Conversion to Christianity ==

Olaf Skötkonung was the first Swedish king to be baptized and remained Christian until his death. His father, Erik Segersäll, had also been baptized but later abandoned Christianity. According to traditional historical interpretation, Olaf Skötkonung was baptized in the year 1008 at Husaby spring (now called St. Sigfrid's Spring) in Västergötland by the English missionary Sigfrid, who supposedly became the second bishop of the Diocese of Skara and later the first bishop of the Diocese of Växjö.

However, the available sources are problematic. The year 1008 comes from another source that is difficult to interpret and may not even be related to this baptism. Olaf's coinage suggests that he was baptized earlier. The coins, minted between 995 and 1020, feature both crosses and the text "in nomine Domini" ("in the name of the Lord"), which does not appear in the original models for the coins. It is possible that even though he early embraced Christianity, his baptism may have been delayed. According to Adam of Bremen, Thurgot founded the Diocese of Skara in 1014 and was also the one who baptized Olaf Skötkonung's queen and children. A missionary named Sigfrid did indeed come to Sweden, but not until 1016. However, Adam of Bremen does not mention who baptized Olaf Skötkonung himself, leading to the assumption that it must have happened earlier and was performed by an English missionary, not a representative of the Archdiocese of Hamburg. Saxo Grammaticus identifies an English missionary named Bernhard who was active in Scania. However, this man arrived in Denmark with King Canute the Great only in 1019, which is considered too late for him to have been involved.

Snorre Sturlasson wrote in Heimskringla that the one who baptized Olaf Skötkonung was the English missionary Sigurd, who served in the retinue of Olaf Tryggvason before entering Swedish service after the Battle of Svolder. Neither Saxo nor Snorre mention any details about how the baptism took place, and therefore it has been tempting to rely on the more detailed Sigfrid legend from around 1200.

According to the Sigfrid legend, Sigfrid baptized a king named Olaf at Kinnekulle (where there are two springs near Husaby). The Sigfrid legend was likely used as a source by the royal genealogy in the Laws of Västergötland, which briefly describes how the baptism took place. However, it states that it occurred at Brigida Spring, located west of the church and just below the bishop's castle, not at St. Sigfrid's Spring. Practical considerations suggest that it was unlikely to have taken place at St. Sigfrid's Spring because baptism involved complete immersion, which was or is possible at Brigida Spring and on the south side of the road. The problem with the Sigfrid legend is that it is a biased source intended to portray Sweden's then-youngest diocese (Växjö) as the oldest.
