REGULATION (EU) 2026/1455 OF THE EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT AND OF THE COUNCIL

concerning the adjustment of customs duties on certain goods originating in the United States of America, and concerning the opening of tariff quotas for imports of certain goods originating in the United States of America.

THE EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT AND THE COUNCIL OF THE EUROPEAN UNION HAVE ADOPTED THIS REGULATION:

Having regard to the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, in particular Article 207(2), Having regard to the proposal from the European Commission, After transmission of the draft legislative act to the national Parliaments, In accordance with the ordinary legislative procedure (1), Whereas the European Union and the United States of America have the most extensive and profound bilateral trade and investment relationships in the world, and their economies are highly integrated. The total trade in both directions amounted to more than EUR 1.6 trillion in 2024. This deep and comprehensive partnership is based on significant mutual investments in each other's markets, with a value of approximately EUR 5.3 trillion. Ensuring the continued integration of these economies, which forms the basis for the broader partnership between the Union and the United States, is a strategic necessity, especially at a time when Russia's war of aggression against Ukraine threatens the essential security interests of the Union.

The Union reaffirms its unwavering commitment to a transparent, fair, and rules-based multilateral trading system based on the principles of the World Trade Organization (WTO). In accordance with agreements, the Union continues to uphold its values and interests on the global stage, particularly by promoting open and fair trade and strengthening international law. The WTO remains the cornerstone of the global trading order and the primary forum for developing, implementing, and enforcing international trade rules. Close cooperation with like-minded partners is crucial for maintaining and strengthening this system, protecting a predictable and rules-based global trading environment, driving necessary WTO reforms, and establishing a well-functioning dispute resolution mechanism.

The Union remains committed to ensuring that trade and investment relations between the Union and the United States develop in accordance with the principles of free and fair trade between the parties, and in line with the WTO's rules-based trading system, without undermining the Union's other trade policy measures, including those related to trade defense.

In 2025, the United States implemented a series of tariff measures that impacted the Union.

  • Effective March 12, 2025, the United States imposed additional tariffs of 25% on imports of steel and aluminum, as well as products derived from these materials.
  • Effective April 3, 2025, the United States implemented an additional tariff of 25% on imports of automobiles.
  • Effective April 5, 2025, the United States imposed an additional tariff on all imports from all trading partners, with the possibility of exemptions. This additional tariff included a base tariff rate of 10% on all imports. Depending on bilateral trade balances, this base tariff rate could be replaced with country-specific tariffs. For the Union, the announced country-specific tariff rate was 20%.
  • On April 9, 2025, the United States announced that the implementation of the country-specific tariffs would be postponed by 90 days, and that the base tariff rate of 10% would remain in place for all partners.
  • Effective May 3, 2025, the United States imposed an additional tariff of 25% on imports of automotive parts.
  • Effective June 4, 2025, the United States increased its tariff rate on imports of steel and aluminum, as well as products derived from these materials, to 50%.
  • On July 12, 2025, the President of the United States announced that the base tariff rate of 10% on goods from the Union would be replaced with a country-specific tariff rate of 30%, effective August 1, 2025.
  • Effective August 1, 2025, the United States imposed additional tariffs of 50% on imports of copper and products derived from copper.

In this context, and with the aim of establishing a stable framework for trade between the Union and the United States, the President of the Commission and the President of the United States reached a political agreement on July 27, 2025 (the Political Agreement of July 27, 2025), which was subsequently reflected in the Joint Statement of August 21, 2025, regarding a framework between the European Union and the United States for an agreement on mutual, fair, and balanced trade (the Joint Statement).

In the Joint Statement, the United States committed to, consistent with the Political Agreement of July 27, 2025, modify certain tariffs applicable to imports from the Union into the United States, and reduce the applicable tariff rate to an overall tariff cap of 15%. The United States also committed to apply the most-favored-nation (MFN) tariff rate only to certain products originating in the Union, such as unavailable natural resources, including cork, all aircraft and aircraft parts, generic pharmaceuticals and their ingredients, and chemical precursors. The Union and the United States agreed to consider which other sectors and products are important for their economies and value chains and should be included in the list of products for which only MFN tariff rates will apply.

It is the intention of the Union and the United States that the Joint Statement shall constitute a first step in a process that can, over time, be expanded to include additional areas and continue to improve market access and increase their trade and investment relationships. The Union remains committed to continuing negotiations with the United States with the aim of reaching a mutually beneficial agreement for other important sectors of its economy, such as the agricultural food sector and the industrial goods sector.

In the joint statement, the union committed to eliminating tariffs on all industrial goods from the United States and to granting preferential market access for a wide range of fish, seafood, and agricultural products from the United States, including tree nuts, dairy products, fresh and processed fruits, fresh and processed berries and vegetables, processed foods, planting seeds, soybean oil, and pork and bison meat. The union and the United States agreed to negotiate the rules of origin that will apply to these trade benefits.

Therefore, the union should adjust tariffs on imports of certain goods and open tariff quotas for imports of certain goods originating in the United States by adopting provisions for preferential tariffs according to Regulation (EU) No 952/2013 of the European Parliament and of the Council (2).

This regulation does not affect the union's ability, in accordance with EU law, and particularly Regulation (EU) 2023/2675 of the European Parliament and of the Council (3), also known as the anti-coercion instrument, and Regulation (EU) No 654/2014 of the European Parliament and of the Council (4), also known as the enforcement regulation, as well as in accordance with international law.
