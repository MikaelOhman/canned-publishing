In plain language

The government has proposed a new law that will come into effect on August 1, 2025. This law outlines how the state can provide financial support to companies that want to build new nuclear power plants in Sweden. This is important to make it safer and easier to carry out major investments in electricity production.

The support can be provided in two ways. First, the state can provide loans to the companies to cover the costs of construction and preparation. Second, a mechanism called "two-way contract for difference" is proposed. This is an agreement between the state and the company that acts as an insurance policy. If the electricity price is too low, the company receives compensation, but if the electricity price is very high, the company must repay a portion of the profits to the state.

There are certain rules regarding when support can be granted. The new nuclear power plants must be located in the same area and, together, have a capacity of at least 300 MW, which is a measure of how much electricity they can produce. However, if there are special reasons, the government may decide to support even smaller projects. All agreements for financial support will have clear conditions that the state and the company agree upon.

Full text

In the proposed legislation, the government suggests a new law regarding state support for investments in new nuclear power. This law outlines the fundamental conditions and forms of state support provided to companies investing in new nuclear reactors in Sweden. Support can be provided in the form of government loans and two-way difference contracts, which are agreements between an operator of a power generation facility and the state, providing both a minimum compensation guarantee and a limit on overcompensation. Government loans can be granted for the construction and testing of new nuclear reactors, as well as for design and other preparatory activities leading up to construction. Two-way difference contracts can be entered into for the routine operation of new nuclear reactors. The support must be accompanied by conditions regulated in agreements between the state and the company receiving the support. Support can only be provided if the new nuclear reactors are located at the same site and have a combined installed electrical capacity of at least 300 MW. In exceptional circumstances, the government may decide to provide support even if the reactors have a combined installed electrical capacity that is less than 300 MW.

The new law is proposed to come into effect on August 1, 2025.
