After his trip to Asia, he continued to work with science and religion in Sweden. Later in his life, he obtained a doctorate in theology, which is the highest academic degree in religious studies. He wrote several books about plants and nature for the rest of his life.

Full text

Pehr Osbeck, born May 9, 1723, at the small farm of Oset under the estate of Livered in the parish of Hålanda in Västergötland, and died December 23, 1805, was a Swedish priest, botanist, and one of Carl Linnaeus's students.

== Travels == Osbeck began his studies at Uppsala University in 1745, where he became a student of Carl von Linné (Linnaeus). At the urging of Linnaeus, Osbeck took a position as ship's priest on the Swedish East India Company's ship "Prins Carl" in 1750. The voyage included an extended stay in Guangzhou, southern China, and the island of Java. Upon his return three years later, he donated his 900 newly discovered plant species from the region to Linnaeus. These collections are still preserved in Sweden and England. On the outbound journey from China, a highly sought-after plant by Linnaeus was also brought along: a tea bush. Unfortunately, it fell overboard during the ship's farewell salute with cannons and therefore never reached Sweden. His travel notes were published in 1757 under the title "Dagbok öfwer en ostindisk Resa åren 1750, 1751, 1752" (Diary of an East India Voyage, years 1750, 1751, 1752). Osbeck is the most well-known of Linnaeus's China travelers, and Linnaeus named the plant genus *Osbeckia* after him.

== Later Career == After his return, Osbeck was appointed as a household priest for Carl Gustaf Tessin in 1755, who also made him an intendant (curator) of the natural history cabinet at Läckö. Osbeck was appointed parish pastor in survivance (temporary position) in Hasslöf and Våxtorp by the Diocese of Gothenburg. He took up the duties of parish pastor in 1760 and became a dean in 1773, and a Doctor of Theology in 1778. Osbeck was a member of the Royal Society of Sciences and Literature in Gothenburg and of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (in 1758). Osbeck published several writings, including "Utkast till Flora hallandica" (1788), and left behind handwritten manuscripts such as "Prodromus insectorum Hallandiæ" (1773) and "Utkast til beskrifning öfver Laholms prosteri" (1796), of which the topographical section was published by B. Möller in "Svenska bygder" (Swedish Landscapes) in 1922.

== Family == Osbeck married Susanna Dahlberg on August 29, 1758, in Hasslöv. Susanna was the daughter of clergyman Eberhard Dahlberg and Anna Christina Lunnaea. Together they had one son, Carl-Gustaf Osbeck, and three daughters who lived to adulthood: Rachel Osbeck, Ebba Greta Osbeck, and Magna Brita Osbeck. All three daughters married clergymen. Rachel married Tobias Banck, Ebba Greta married Anders Banck, and Magna Brita married Nils Österberg. Their son, Carl-Gustaf, married Christina Aulin from Ystad. They later moved to Stockholm, where Carl-Gustaf became a prominent physician (surgeon).

== Other Information == On the slope leading down from the small farmstead towards Vanderydsvattnet, the Hålanda local history association erected a memorial stone on June 2, 1973, to commemorate the 250th anniversary of Pehr Osbeck's birth. The Osbeck Gymnasium in Laholm is also named after him. The author's name "Osbeck" can be used for Pehr Osbeck when referring to a scientific name within botany. Osbeck has been described as exceptionally diligent and an astute observer of nature. As evidence for this description, he himself noted in the manuscript of his first completed parish description:

"While others have been drinking, I have been reading. While others have been sleeping, I have been writing. While others have been gambling, I have been examining herbs. While others have died, I have been thinking about death. Now he does not amaze me."
