New nuclear power in Sweden – more potential locations along the coast.

TL;DRThe government wants to amend the law to make it possible to build new nuclear power plants in more locations along Sweden's coast. The proposal involves removing certain previous prohibitions on nuclear power plants in coastal areas.
In plain language

The government has proposed an amendment to the Environmental Code, the law that protects our natural environment. The purpose is to make it easier to expand nuclear power in Sweden by removing prohibitions in several locations along the coast and in archipelagos.

The change applies to areas in Bohuslän, Småland, Östergötland, Ångermanland, and Öland. Certain parts of Gotland, such as Östergarn, Storsudret, and Fårö, are also included in the proposal. This means that it will be possible to plan for nuclear power plants in areas where it was previously not permitted.

Despite the removal of the prohibitions, it does not mean that anyone can build anywhere. All new facilities must still undergo a thorough review and assessment by the authorities. It is also important to continue to consider protecting nature and important cultural heritage.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on July 15, 2026.

Full text

The proposal includes suggestions aimed at enabling the expansion of nuclear power in more coastal locations, while maintaining the protection of natural and cultural values.

The proposed changes would remove the ban on nuclear facilities in coastal areas and archipelagos in Bohuslän, from the border with Norway to Brofjorden; in Småland and Östergötland, from Simpevarp to Arkösund; and in Ångermanland, from Storfjärden at the mouth of the Ångermanälven river to Skagsudde; and on Öland.

The changes also include the removal of the ban on nuclear facilities in coastal areas and archipelagos from Brofjorden to Simpevarp, and from Arkösund to Forsmark, along the coast of Gotland, on Östergarn and Storsudret on Gotland, and on Fårö, except in areas where there are already certain industrial and similar facilities.

The changes are proposed to apply to all nuclear facilities covered by the government's permitting process according to Section 17, paragraph 1, subsection 1 of the Environmental Code. The changes do not affect the assessment of nuclear facilities in other respects. Therefore, a case-by-case assessment will still be required before a nuclear facility can be established.

The changes are proposed to take effect on July 15, 2026.
