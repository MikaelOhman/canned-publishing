Do not confuse with the politician and marshal Jacob De la Gardie (1768-1842).

Jakob Pontusson De la Gardie, born June 20, 1583, in Reval, died August 12, 1652, in Stockholm. He was a Swedish field marshal, held the rank of field marshal, served on the Swedish Council of State, and from May 10, 1615, was Count of Läckö. Jakob De la Gardie played an important role in the De la Gardie campaign and in the Ingrian War.

== Biography ==

Jakob De la Gardie was born in Reval (now Tallinn) in Estonia, the son of Pontus De la Gardie and his wife Sofia Johansdotter (Gyllenhielm), an illegitimate daughter of Johan III. His mother died in childbirth when Jakob was born, making him the third child in the family. His father drowned in 1585 near Narva. After their parents' deaths, Jakob had a sister, Brita, and a brother, Johan.

De la Gardie entered military service at a young age and was appointed colonel of a regiment in northern Sweden when he was only 18 years old. He participated in the defense of Wolmar against the Poles but was captured. He was released from captivity in 1606 and then traveled to the Netherlands, where he served as a colonel under Moritz of Orange and learned about Dutch military tactics.

Upon his return to Sweden in 1609, he became a field marshal and received command of the troops that King Charles IX sent to support Tsar Vasily IV of Russia, known as De la Gardie's campaign. The mission was difficult, but with the help of Tsar's relative Mikhail Skopin-Shuisky, he managed to reach Moscow and capture the city in March 1610. It is likely that during this time, he became the first and only Swede to visit the grave of Gustav Eriksson Vasa, Erik XIV's son and former Crown Prince of Sweden, who was buried in the town of Kashin near Moscow.

When Sigismund also began to actively intervene in Russian affairs, De la Gardie, along with reinforcements from Sweden and a Russian force, attacked the Poles. Disputes over pay caused discontent among the mercenary troops, and when fighting broke out between the Swedish-Russian army and the Polish forces under Stanisław Żółkiewski on June 24, 1610, near Klushino, De la Gardie's foreign infantry refused to participate. The Russians fled, and De la Gardie was forced to make an agreement with Żółkiewski not to provide further support to Tsar Vasily, an agreement whose validity he later denied.

With 400 Swedes and Finns, the remnants of his army, he made his way back to the Swedish border under difficult conditions. He received reinforcements but also entered into negotiations with anti-Polish elements in Russia. In addition to Kexholm Fortress, which had been promised to the Swedes as a reward for their support since 1609 but had not been handed over, De la Gardie now demanded the fortresses of Nöteborg and Staraja Ladoga. The Russians refused this request but instead elected Gustav Adolf as Tsar of Russia in June 1611. De la Gardie referred this matter to the King, but to secure a guarantee for Swedish claims, he captured the important city of Novgorod on July 16, 1611. The city then recognized Charles IX as its protector and chose either Gustav Adolf or Karl Filip as Tsar, hoping for support from the rest of Russia.

After Gustav Adolf became King of Sweden, Karl Filip was considered the only candidate who could be expected to receive Russian support. However, his candidacy was opposed by Dowager Queen Kristina of Holstein-Gottorp, and his journey to Russia was delayed. Meanwhile, in 1613, Mikhail Romanov was elected Tsar. De la Gardie, who was quite dissatisfied with the Swedish government's stance, tried to secure as much as possible under Swedish control, but more and more cities began to revolt in support of Romanov. However, they managed to prevent the city of Novgorod from falling by using troops stationed there.

In the autumn of 1614, De la Gardie returned to Sweden with Gustav II Adolf, who had participated in the campaign for a year to learn about warfare under De la Gardie. He returned with the King in 1615 and led the siege of Pskov, which ultimately failed. The successes in the Treaty of Stolbovo, where De la Gardie was one of the representatives, can largely be attributed to De la Gardie's conquests. In 1613, he was appointed a member of the Council of Sweden, and in 1615, he was elevated to the nobility with Läckö as his estate. This was the only count title that Gustav II awarded during his twenty-one years on the throne. On June 24, 1618, De la Gardie married Ebba Brahe. The couple settled at Svanå Manor in Harakers parish in Västmanland. In 1619, he became governor of Estonia, participated in the capture of Riga in 1621, and then became Governor-General of Livonia. While the King was away, he commanded the troops in the Baltic provinces and often led negotiations with the Poles. In 1625, he captured Dorpat but had few other military successes. Instead, his actions were considered to be a major reason why the victory at Wallhof in 1626 could not be better exploited. In 1628, Gustav II Adolf characterized him as someone "in his counsel somewhat slow and hesitant, and also very prone to error."
