Georg Stiernhielm, originally Göran Olofsson, was born on August 7, 1598, in the parish of Vika, Dalarna, and died on April 22, 1672, in Stockholm. He was a Swedish civil servant, linguist, and poet. He is often referred to as "the father of Swedish poetry."

Stiernhielm's most famous poetic work is the first poem in *Musæ Suethizantes*, titled *Hercules*, which is written in hexameter verse and borrows names and stories from ancient Greece.

In 1631, Stiernhielm was ennobled. During his career as a civil servant, he held various positions, including that of national antiquarian, and from 1667, he served as the governor of the newly established National Antiquities Board.

== Biography ==

Stiernhielm was born in 1598 at Gammelgården in Kniva, Vika parish, Dalarna. His father, Olof Markvardsson of the Stierna family, held a position related to mining. He received his initial education at Västerås school, and in 1619 he enrolled at Uppsala University under the name Lillia (previously he had used the names Göran Olofsson and Georgius Olavi).

After completing his studies in Germany, including at Greifswald University, he returned to Uppsala in 1624. The following year, he traveled back to Germany as a tutor for a young Gyllenhielm, and also visited Italy, France, the Netherlands, and England. In 1626, he was appointed lecturer at the grammar school in Västerås by Bishop Johannes Rudbeckius, and shortly thereafter, King Gustav II Adolf named him "reading master" for the House of Nobility (Riddarhuset) within the Collegium illustre.

In 1630, he was appointed assessor to the Dorpat provincial court. He was ennobled in 1631 with the name Stiernhielm and granted the estates of Stjernlund and Vasula in Livland. From 1639, he became a governor in Livland.

In 1641, he was appointed acting governor in Dorpat, replacing the ailing Fabian Wrangell. On July 13, 1641, at a christening, Stiernhielm encountered two of Wrangell's sons with whom he had previously had some disagreements. A quarrel broke out, and the heavily intoxicated Stiernhielm decided to seek revenge with a sword, resulting in a serious wound to his right arm during the ensuing fight. He was bedridden for a long time and suffered permanent damage from the injury, forcing him to write with his left hand afterward.

In 1642, he was summoned to Stockholm to participate in a legal commission. He spent several years in the capital, gaining attention for his talent and knowledge, and also became highly regarded by Queen Christina due to his poetry.

In 1648, he was appointed vice president of the Dorpat provincial court, but he had barely taken up this position when he was recalled to Stockholm to become the national antiquarian. During the journey, the ship he was traveling on sank; he survived, but arrived in the capital as a destitute man. He was received with great kindness by the Queen. He took up the position of national archivist in 1649 and was highly appreciated at court for his poems, ballets, and "antics."

However, it wasn't long before he fell out of favor after making negative remarks about one of the Queen's favorites. He therefore returned to Livland, but the Russian war of 1656 forced him to leave there as well. He returned to Stockholm with his wife and children and struggled financially until 1658, when he was appointed by Charles X Gustav as a district judge in the Trondheims region, which had been ceded to Sweden. However, when this region was lost again in 1660, Stiernhielm was once again without employment, until he was called to the war council in 1661 and the following year to the Reduction Collegium, a position he declined.

After the establishment of the Antiquities Collegium, he was appointed its first director in 1667, securing his old age against the financial worries that had plagued him throughout his life. He died in Stockholm on April 22, 1672. After his death, disputes arose between the heirs. The funeral took place in June 1674. His remains were kept in Klara Church in Stockholm for 16 years and then moved to Sånga Church. In 1799, the coffin was unearthed and placed in a storage shed. It was stolen in 1835. In 1791, the Swedish Academy created a medal in his honor. His gravestone reads "Vixit, dum vixit, laetus," which translates to "He lived happily, as long as he lived."

He was married to Cecilia Burea, Johannes Bureus's niece.

== Scientific and Literary Achievements == Stiernhielm's literary career was as diverse as it was extensive. He wrote fifty to sixty distinct works encompassing poetry, mathematics, natural science, law, heraldry, statistics, and numerous contributions in philology. Approximately one-fifth of these have been published over a long period, while roughly the same number are now lost. As Erik Gustaf Geijer put it, "he was a great beginner in everything." His collected works were published in the 1970s by the Swedish Academy of Literature.

As a mathematician, he was among the first Swedes to gain recognition in the field. The mathematical sciences, which had been a major focus during his studies at foreign universities, he brought with him to Sweden, where they were previously little known. The government utilized his expertise to standardize the kingdom's measurements and weights.
