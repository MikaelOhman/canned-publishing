In plain language

The Ledbergssten is a large granite stone with texts and images on several sides. The stone features carvings of people, animals, and a ship. The inscription tells us that the individuals Bisse and Gunna erected the stone to commemorate Torgöt, who was Bisse's father.

It is difficult to know exactly who Gunna was, but she may have been Bisse's wife or Torgöt's daughter. In the past, the stone was embedded in a church wall, but it was moved to the cemetery during the 19th century so that it could be seen more clearly.

At the end of the text, there are words that were previously thought to be a story about a war, but researchers have later determined that they are actually what is known as a magical incantation. The words in the incantation are "Tistel," "mistel," and "kistel."

Full text

Ledbergsstenen, with the identification number Ög 181, is a runestone that now stands on the north side of Ledbergs Church in the parish of Ledberg and the municipality of Linköping, in the province of Östergötland. Its original location is unknown. The motifs share similarities with the Tullstorpstenen (Tullstorp Runestone), both featuring a similar angular boat and a wolf.

== The Stone == The stone is made of granite and has a height of 287 cm, a width of 55–95 cm, and a thickness of 40 cm. There are inscriptions on three of the stone's sides. The eastern narrow side features an ornamented cross, while the western side has no inscription. The runic text is located on both broad sides of the stone, and the surface is also covered with depictions of human figures, four-legged animals, and a ship. The text, which is damaged in places, begins on the southern side (side A), facing Ledbergs Church, and continues on the northern side (side B).

== The Inscription == Transliteration of the runic inscription:

§A (b)isi · sati : st[(n)] : þisi : iftiʀ : þurkut : u----þi : faþur §B : sin : uk : þu : kuna : baþi : þmk:iii:sss:ttt:iii:l[(l)]l : Normalization to Old Swedish:

§A Bisi satti stæin þennsi æftiʀ Þorgaut ..., faður §B sinn ok þau Gunna baði. Þistill/mistill/kistill. Translation to modern Swedish:

§A Bisse set this stone in memory of Torgöt..., father §B his, and they both (Bisse and) Gunna (set the stone). Thistle, mistletoe, chest.

== History and Interpretation ==

The runestone was erected, as the inscription indicates, by Bisse and Gunna in memory of Torgöt. Bisse was Torgöt's son, but it is unclear whether Gunna was Torgöt's wife or daughter, or perhaps Bisse's wife. It is unknown where the stone originally stood, but in the 17th century, it was built into the wall of the sacristy in Ledberg's old Romanesque church from the 12th century, with side B facing outwards. When this church was demolished in 1847, the stone was moved to the cemetery wall and, in 1852, placed in its current location within the cemetery. The National Antiquities Inspector, Bror Emil Hildebrand, suggested this placement, while the priest and diocesan librarian, Levin Christian Wiede, wanted to place it on top of the nearby burial mound, Ledbergs Kulle.

Regarding the interpretation, the first part of the inscription is relatively straightforward, although the meaning of the text immediately following Torgöt's name has not been deciphered. The cryptic writing at the end of the runic text presented a greater challenge:

þmk : iii : sss : ttt : iii : l[(l)]l : According to Erik Brate, this section should be read as follows:

þmi : iii : sss : ts : iii : fli :. He interpreted this in turn as a comment on where Torgöt died. According to him, "iii : sss" would refer to the third rune (þ) of the third family, while 't' would remain unchanged, and "s : iii" would refer to the third rune (m) of the first family.

By interpreting "þmi" as a metathesis of "miþ" (meþ, "mixed") and "fli" as a metathesis of "fil" (fell, "fell"), and also assuming that "þtm" was an abbreviation for "þrantum," i.e., the people known as the Trønders in Norway, he concluded that it all stood for "meþ þrantum fell," meaning "he fell among the Trønders." He considered this to make it likely that Torgöt had been killed during the Battle of Stiklastad in 1030, since this also corresponded with the stone's approximate age. The male figures on the stone were interpreted as depicting Torgöt on the battlefield. However, this entire interpretation proved to be incorrect when a runestone DR 239 was found in 1921 at Gørlev Church in Denmark, which had exactly the same inscription but was 100 to 200 years older. The so-called "secret script" turned out to be a magical formula obtained by selecting the first, second, and third letters of each group in turn from the original text: þmk:iii:sss:ttt:iii:lll:.

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary native sources from the Viking Age – carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they state what was inscribed, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some readings are uncertain – such readings are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
