Enhanced oversight of the dental care sector.

TL;DRThe government wants to introduce licensing requirements for private dentists in order to increase patient safety. If these regulations are not followed, healthcare providers may lose their right to practice.
In plain language

The government has proposed a plan that would require private dental clinics to obtain permits to operate. These permits are applied for from the Swedish Inspectorate for Healthcare and Welfare, often referred to as IVO. To be granted a permit, healthcare providers must demonstrate that they operate safely, prioritize patient health, and comply with all applicable laws.

If a healthcare provider fails to meet these standards, IVO can demand that they correct the deficiencies. In the most severe cases, IVO can revoke the permit, forcing the clinic to close. It will also become illegal and punishable to operate a dental practice without a valid permit.

The proposal also stipulates that regional authorities, which are responsible for healthcare in each part of the country, must report to IVO if they suspect that someone is operating without a permit or is otherwise unsuitable. Furthermore, state dental care subsidies, which help individuals pay for dental treatment, will not be provided to dentists who lack the proper credentials.

If the Swedish Parliament approves the proposal, the new regulations will come into effect on January 1, 2026. The proposal involves changes to several laws that govern dental care and patient safety in Sweden.

Full text

The proposal includes, among other things, the introduction of a licensing requirement for private healthcare providers in the dental sector. Licenses must be applied for from the Swedish Inspectorate for Healthcare and Welfare (IVO). To obtain and maintain a license, the healthcare provider must, among other things, conduct systematic patient safety work, have knowledge of the regulations applicable to the business, and be deemed suitable. An ownership and management assessment must be carried out if the healthcare provider is a legal entity.

If the requirements for a license are no longer met, IVO must be able to order the healthcare provider to rectify the deficiencies. The proposal also suggests that IVO may, in whole or in part, revoke a license, both when a healthcare provider fails to comply with an order and without a prior order. IVO must be able to prohibit activities conducted without a license. It should also be punishable to conduct activities requiring a license without having one.

Furthermore, the proposal suggests that the region must report to IVO if there is reason to believe that a private healthcare provider is operating a dental practice without the necessary license, or if the suitability of a licensed provider is questionable.

The proposal also includes, among other things, a suggestion that state dental care subsidies should generally not be paid out if the practitioner lacks the necessary qualifications or special authorization to practice the profession.

The proposed amendments to the law are intended to come into force on January 1, 2026.
