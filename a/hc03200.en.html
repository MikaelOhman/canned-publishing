Supplementary provisions regarding more flexible rules for payouts from pension insurance.

TL;DRThe government is proposing new regulations regarding pension payouts. The aim is to make it easier for pension providers to pay out smaller amounts during the first five years, particularly if this is due to fees or changes in calculations.
In plain language

The government has presented a proposal to the Parliament regarding payouts from pension insurance plans. The aim is to make the rules more flexible and to address problems that have arisen when the new rules have been applied in practice.

Currently, there is a rule known as the "five-year rule." This rule states that payouts from a pension insurance plan should not decrease during the first five years. The new proposal would allow for exceptions to this rule. This means that the payout may be reduced if the decrease is due to fees, changes in calculations, or if the frequency of payments is changed.

The proposal applies to both regular old-age pensions and survivor pensions, which are payments made to families when someone dies. The government also wants the rules to be the same for different types of insurance plans, such as both investment funds and managed accounts, when it comes to changes in the value of the funds chosen.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on January 1, 2026. The proposal involves a change to the Income Tax Act, which is the law that governs how taxes on wages and pensions are calculated.

Full text

The government, following an agreement within the Pensions Group, has submitted a proposal to the Parliament regarding more flexible rules for payouts from pension insurance, etc. (see prop. 2023/24:159, bet. 2024/25:SkU4, rskr. 2024/25:16, SFS 2024:974). The amendments to the law came into effect on January 1, 2025.

This proposal includes supplementary provisions to address certain outstanding issues that have been identified in the practical application of the new, more flexible payout rules. The proposals introduce a number of exceptions to the condition that payouts from a pension insurance must be made with the same or increasing amounts during the first five years (the five-year rule). According to the proposals, a pension can be paid out at a lower amount than the amount paid out in the immediately preceding payout if the reduction is due to changes in actuarial assumptions applied in calculating the payout amount, the deduction of fees stipulated in the insurance agreement, or changes in the number of payouts per year. The proposals apply to the payout of old-age pensions and survivor pensions from a pension insurance.

The proposal also includes a provision aimed at equalizing the conditions for fund-based and deposit-based insurance. The exception to the five-year rule that currently applies to fund-based insurance, which concerns adjustments to payouts due to changes in the value of fund units, is proposed to be extended to also apply to deposit-based insurance.

The new provisions are proposed to come into effect on January 1, 2026.
