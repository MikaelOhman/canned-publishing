In plain language

During the summer months, the number of people who require sick leave due to physical injuries increases. These injuries often result from activities such as hiking in the mountains, installing a trampoline, or building a deck. The Swedish Social Insurance Agency (Försäkringskassan), which is the government agency that provides financial support to individuals unable to work due to illness, observes this pattern in its statistics.

At the same time, the number of sick leaves for other reasons decreases during the summer, such as back problems or mental health issues. Analysts believe this is because people are more active and moving around more during their vacation time. The problem arises when individuals attempt something challenging or new and overestimate their own abilities.

If you are employed and become ill, your employer initially pays your salary for two weeks. After that, you may be eligible to receive payments from the Swedish Social Insurance Agency (Försäkringskassan), which is called sick pay. The statistics clearly show that injuries resulting from high levels of activity increase when the weather is pleasant.

Trampolines, hiking, and deck building: during the summer, the number of physical injuries leading to sick leave increases.

"Of course, it's good to be active outdoors, but we see in the statistics that many seem to overestimate their abilities and get injured," says Ulrik Lidwall, an analyst at the Swedish Social Insurance Agency.

Employees who become ill can apply for sickness benefits from the Swedish Social Insurance Agency after 2 weeks – before that, they receive sick pay from their employer. The number of new cases of illness decreases during the summer, but one type of sick leave increases: sick leave due to injury.

"Sick leave due to mental health issues and musculoskeletal diseases, such as back pain, decreases during the summer, while sick leave due to injuries increases. This is likely related to people being more active and spending more time outdoors, doing things they may not be used to, and therefore actually getting injured," says Ulrik Lidwall.
