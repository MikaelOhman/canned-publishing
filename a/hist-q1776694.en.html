Full text

Didrik Slagheck, also known as Didrik Slaghök or Didrik Slaghöök (Danish: Didrik Slaghøk), died on January 24, 1522. He was a Danish priest, bishop of Skara from 1520 to 1521, and Archbishop of Lund from 1521 until his death. He was identified as having actively participated in the Stockholm Bloodbath.

== Biography == Slagheck was born as an illegitimate son of a Westphalian priest and seems to have been employed early on in the papal chancellery. He came to Norway in 1510 and quickly became highly regarded by both Christian II and his advisor, Sigbrit. After being in a country outside Scandinavia, he returned in 1517 as an assistant to the Pope's envoy, the indulgence seller Arcimboldus. This man soon abused the king's trust and instead allowed himself to be bribed by Sten Sture the Younger. Didrik Slagheck informed King Christian about this, and was subsequently sent to Rome to report on Arcimboldus' betrayal. In 1520, he followed the union king to Stockholm and is said to have been an inspiration for the harsh sentences at the Stockholm Bloodbath. Shortly after the event, Slagheck was appointed bishop of the Diocese of Skara, succeeding the executed Bishop Vincent. He then became one of the three governors that Christian left in Sweden. Didrik Slagheck was unpopular in Sweden and also made enemies with the other governors. In the autumn of 1521, he returned to Denmark to be appointed Archbishop of Lund by Christian II. However, this position was short-lived, as Pope Leo X was angered by the execution of two bishops in Stockholm. The king blamed Didrik Slagheck entirely and ensured that he was sentenced to death. When he stood with a rope around his neck, his sentence was changed to being burned alive, which also occurred on January 24, 1522, at Gammeltorv in Copenhagen.
