Full text

Sofia Eriksdotter of Denmark, likely born no later than 1240 in Denmark, and died in 1286 in Denmark, was the Queen of Sweden from 1260 to 1275 through her marriage to King Valdemar Birgersson. She was the daughter of King Erik Plogpenning of Denmark, making her a Danish princess, and also, through her husband, the daughter-in-law of Birger Jarl.

== Biography == Sofia's father, Erik Plogpenning, was murdered in 1250. It is said that when she was informed that she would be marrying the Swedish King Valdemar Birgersson, she went into her chamber and prayed to Mary: "Grant me happiness with him, and him with me." They married in 1260 in Jönköping (the location may also have been Öninge in Ödeshög; the Erik Chronicle mentions Ymninge). Queen Sofia was described as a quick-witted, temperamental, and ambitious beauty with a sharp tongue, and there are many quotes about her biting remarks. She referred to her husband's brothers as "Magnus Kittlarböter," possibly "kittelflickare," which was considered an insult, and "Erik allsintet." She was known for her interest in chess. In 1269, she visited her father's grave and her sisters Agnes and Jutta in a monastery in Denmark. In 1272, Jutta came to Sweden and had a child with Valdemar in 1273, which led the king to have to perform penance through a pilgrimage to Rome in 1274. "Woe to me. I will never overcome that sorrow. Woe to me that my sister ever saw the kingdom of Sweden," she is said to have said. After her husband was deposed following the Battle of Hova in 1275, they were at Ramundeboda monastery in Närke; he was asleep, while she reportedly received the news while playing chess. In 1277, she returned to Denmark; her husband had by then begun openly living with his mistresses and was rebuked by the bishops for having cast her aside. In 1283, the former queen donated her salmon fishing rights in Norrköping to St. Martin's Monastery in Skänninge, and it is in her donation document that the place name Norrköping appears in writing for the first time, as "Norkøponge."
