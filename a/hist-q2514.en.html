Willy Brandt, born Herbert Ernst Karl Frahm on December 18, 1913, in Lübeck, German Empire, and died on October 8, 1992, in Unkel (near Bonn), Landkreis Neuwied, Rhineland-Palatinate, was a German Social Democratic politician. He served as the Foreign Minister of West Germany from 1966 to 1969 and as the Chancellor of West Germany from 1969 to 1974.

Brandt grew up in the workers' movement in Lübeck and lived in exile from the Nazi regime before returning to Germany and beginning a political career with the SPD (Social Democratic Party). He became known as the Mayor of West Berlin during a period of heightened tensions during the Cold War. In 1969, he was elected Chancellor and initiated the "New Ostpolitik" (Eastern Policy), for which he received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1971. Brandt was Germany's first Social Democratic Chancellor. He resigned in 1974 due to the Guillaume affair but remained involved in politics.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood === Brandt grew up with his mother, Martha Frahm (1894–1969), who worked as a salesperson at Konsum-föreningen, and her stepfather, Ludwig Frahm (1875–1935). His father's name was John Möller (1887–1958), but his mother did not include the name on his birth certificate. The father was from Hamburg and worked as a teacher until 1933, but after distributing leaflets for the Social Democrats, he was forced to leave his job and later worked as an accountant. Brandt never met his biological father, and it wasn't until 1947 that his mother told him who his father was. When he needed to provide information about his father's name in order to regain his German citizenship, he wrote a letter to his mother asking her for the information. As a child, Brandt never asked about his father because the subject was taboo; many other working-class children in Lübeck were in similar situations. Brandt described his childhood as a "family chaos."

Brandt was baptized at St. Lorenz Church in Lübeck, because children born out of wedlock were not allowed to be baptized in the parish church. In 1927, his mother remarried Emil Kuhlmann, and Brandt's half-brother, Günther, was born in 1928. Brandt called his mother's new husband, who came from Mecklenburg, "Uncle." Brandt then lived with his maternal grandfather, who worked as a truck driver, and his new wife, Dora, whom Brandt called "Aunt Dora." Brandt's maternal grandfather, who was the stepfather of Brandt's mother, had started as an agricultural worker in Mecklenburg and later moved to the city to work at Drägerwerke. The maternal grandfather played a central role as a father figure; Brandt called him "Papa," and the grandfather was involved in ensuring that Brandt went to grammar school. At the time of Brandt's birth, the family was part of the workers' movement in Lübeck, and Brandt became a member of a children's group within the movement at an early age, where he learned to play the mandolin, which was later used in a political campaign in the 1970s when Brandt posed with a mandolin. Brandt was also involved in the youth group Rote Falken. His maternal grandfather was active in the SPD as a local representative in the Holstentor-Süd district and was even nominated as an SPD candidate for the Lübeck city council elections. Both his mother and stepfather considered themselves to be integral parts of the workers' movement and the SPD.

In 1932, Brandt graduated from Johanneum in Lübeck, a progressive grammar school. His favorite subjects were German and history; his final paper in history was about August Bebel. Brandt, who at that time called himself Karl Frahm instead of Herbert, as his mother did, was able to attend the grammar school because he performed exceptionally well in elementary school. In 1930, the young socialist Frahm appeared in court in Lübeck after a fight with young Hitler supporters, but he was acquitted. After graduating, he worked as a shipping clerk for the company F.H. Bertling. The job at the shipping agency gave Brandt the opportunity to practice the Scandinavian languages because the company had many Nordic customers who needed help with customs formalities. He dreamed of becoming a journalist and wrote political articles for the SPD newspaper Volksbote in Lübeck, whose editor and later resistance fighter Julius Leber had a significant influence on Brandt. Brandt published articles about a visit to Norway where he visited distant relatives. From 1930 to 1931, Brandt was a member of the SPD. In 1931, Brandt broke with Leber and the Social Democrats and joined the Sozialistische Arbeiterpartei (SAP), a left-wing socialist group that opposed the policies of the SPD.

=== The Years in Exile ===

==== Norway ==== Brandt's political involvement led him to go into exile in Norway during the mid-1930s when the Nazis came to power. When the Nazis seized power, the SAP (Social Democratic Workers' Party) decided to become an underground organization at a meeting in Dresden. For the first time, Herbert Frahm used the pseudonym Willy Brandt. In March 1933, Willy Brandt was tasked with arranging Paul Frölich's trip to Oslo, but Frölich was arrested, and Brandt then received the assignment to travel to Oslo and establish a cell there. Brandt was considered to be in danger of being arrested, partly because he had spoken at a large gathering in Holstenhalle in Lübeck in February 1933 about the importance of a united struggle against fascism. In 1928, Brandt had visited Scandinavia for the first time.
