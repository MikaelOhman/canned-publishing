Arne Wilhelm Kaurin Tiselius, born August 10, 1902, in Stockholm, and died October 29, 1971, in Uppsala, was a Swedish chemist and professor of biochemistry at Uppsala University from 1938 to 1968. He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1948 for his research on electrophoresis and adsorption analysis, particularly for discoveries related to serum proteins – he had demonstrated that blood serum consists of four different proteins.

== Childhood and Youth == Tiselius was the son of mathematician Hans Tiselius and homemaker Rosa, née Kaurin, of Norwegian descent. He grew up in Stockholm with his two siblings, one of whom, his brother Nils, died from appendicitis. After his father's death, the family moved to his grandfather's hometown of Gothenburg, where his maternal grandfather, Nils Abraham Tiselius, was the rector. The family had limited financial resources, so his mother gave piano lessons and sewed Hardanger embroidery, and Tiselius tutored younger students to contribute to the household income. Tiselius' interest in chemistry was encouraged by his teacher, Master Johansson, who allowed him to borrow keys to the school so he could visit the chemistry lab as often as he wanted.

== Career == After graduating from high school, Tiselius moved to Uppsala, where he got a job at the laboratory of the renowned chemist Theodor Svedberg after completing his studies. Svedberg was interested in substances from the animal kingdom and wanted to separate the individual components of blood in order to study them.

They initially worked with ultracentrifugation, but needed a better method for separating individual molecules. Svedberg had used electricity to separate salts during his studies in the United States, and upon returning to Uppsala, he entrusted this project to Tiselius. His idea was to exploit differences in electrical charge to separate the proteins in blood. This method, called electrophoresis, had been tried by other researchers before without success, but Tiselius was knowledgeable in chemistry, physics, and medicine, and after persistent effort, he finally managed to make the method work without damaging the molecules. Tiselius demonstrated that blood contains a variety of different proteins, but despite the revolutionary nature of his findings, he was denied publication in a biochemical journal because it was considered physics rather than biochemistry.

Tiselius's apparatus was soon copied all over the world. Today, it is used in many different areas, such as food control (to determine which animal the meat comes from), legal cases (DNA identification), and kinship studies. Tiselius and his biochemical institute in Uppsala had a crucial influence on the development of the Swedish pharmaceutical and biotechnology industries. Many of the products that made Pharmacia a successful company originated in the institute's research. [Source needed]

Tiselius was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1948 for his "electrophoretic and adsorption analytical work, especially his discoveries concerning the complex nature of serum proteins." He had shown that blood serum consisted of four different proteins: albumin, as well as alpha-, beta-, and gamma-globulin.

Tiselius was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1939 and became a member of the Royal Institute of Technology in 1944. [Source needed] He became a member of the Nobel Committee for Chemistry in 1946, and from 1960 to 1964 he was chairman of the Nobel Foundation – having been vice-chairman since 1947. From 1946, he was a member of the Swedish Natural Science Research Council, and from 1951 to 1955 he was chairman of both the Cancer Fund's research council and the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry.

A research vessel has been named after Tiselius: the R/V Arne Tiselius, which was previously based at the Kristineberg marine research station.

== Family == Tiselius was married to Ingrid Margareta Dalén and had two children: a daughter, Eva (born in 1932), and a son, Per (a doctor, born in 1934). Arne Tiselius is buried in the old cemetery of Uppsala.
