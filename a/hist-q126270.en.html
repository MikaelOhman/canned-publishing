Sophia Elisabet Brenner, born Weber on April 29, 1659, in Stockholm, and died on September 14, 1730, was a Swedish author.

Sophia Elisabet Brenner opposed the prevailing views of women in 18th-century society and was Sweden's first female writer of fiction, as well as a pioneer for women's intellectual liberation.

== Biography == Sophia Elisabet was born as the daughter of Niklas Weber, a stonemason and guild master from Germany, and his wife, Kristina Spoor. She received a good education at the German School in Stockholm, where she attended classes with boys, and learned six languages fluently enough to write poetry in all of them during her youth (Swedish, German, French, Italian, Dutch, and Latin; as well as some English). She was one of Sweden's first female students, following in the footsteps of Ursula Agricola and Maria Jonaea Palmgren. In 1680, she married Elias Brenner from the Brenner family, who was known as a prominent miniature painter and the father of Swedish numismatics, in his second marriage. Her husband and their common circle of friends, including Aurora Königsmarck, Anna Maria Ehrenstrahl, Johan Runius, Urban Hiärne, and archdeacon Petrus Tillaeus, were impressed by her poetry. This led to her publishing her first collection of poems, "Poetiske dikter," in 1713, making her the first woman to write and publish poetry in Swedish. Due to the success she achieved with these poems, she was called "the second Sappho" and "the tenth muse" by her contemporaries. Sophia Elisabet Brenner was also arguably the country's first active feminist. In 1693, she published "Det Qwinliga Könetz rätmätige Förswar," which seems to have been inspired by her friendship with Aurora Königsmarck. She believed that men and women were essentially equal except for their physical bodies, a sentiment she expressed in a celebratory poem dedicated to Queen Ulrika Eleonora at her coronation:

The feminist tradition she initiated was continued by Hedvig Charlotta Nordenflycht. In 1710, she published "Wårs Herres och Frälsares Jesu Christi alldraheligaste pijnos historia," which is widely considered her most important work. Several other poems were later published posthumously. Sophia Elisabet Brenner had many descendants in the families Stenhammar, Höijer, and Fahlcrantz; Benjamin Höijer was her great-grandson, Sophia Brenner's only child. She is also an indirect main character in Carina Burman's novel "Den tionde sånggudinnan" (1996), which tells a fictional story about a research project from the early 20th century focusing on Brenner's personal letters.

Sophia Elisabet Brenner is buried in Riddarholmskyrkan, Stockholm.
