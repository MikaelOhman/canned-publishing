Joseph Martin Kraus, born June 20, 1756, in Miltenberg, Germany, and died December 15, 1792, in Stockholm, was a German-Swedish composer. Kraus is buried within the Royal National City Park.

==Life and Works== Joseph Martin Kraus's father was an official, and it was originally planned that he would follow in his father's footsteps. Joseph Martin Kraus studied in Mannheim from 1768 to 1771. He studied law and philosophy at the Universities of Mainz, Erfurt, and Göttingen between 1773 and 1778, but simultaneously studied music and composed, which was his primary interest. Kraus came to Sweden in 1778 with Carl Stridsberg, a Swede whom he had met in Göttingen. On November 29, 1780, he was elected as member number 76 of the Royal Swedish Academy of Music. After the opera *Proserpina*, with a libretto by Kellgren based on an outline by Gustav III, was performed at the Ulriksdal Palace Theatre and approved by the court, Kraus received funding from the king to travel back into Europe for five years in order to further develop his musical skills. He met, among others, Haydn, Gluck, and Salieri. It is possible that he also met Mozart, as they were both Freemasons. Kraus was a member of the Masonic lodge *Zur gekrönten Hoffnung* in Vienna. Upon returning to Stockholm, Kraus was appointed court conductor at the Royal Opera, and he composed, among other works commissioned by Gustav III, a march for the opening of Parliament in 1789, known as the "Riksdagsmusiken." He became good friends with Carl Michael Bellman, and together they wrote, among other pieces, the cantata *Fiskarstugan* (1792) and the memorial song *Mozarts död* (1792).

The opera *Dido and Aeneas*, based on Kellgren's *Aeneas in Carthage*, which was written for the opening of the new opera house, premiered seven years after his death. It is considered his greatest work, lasting approximately 5 hours in its complete form, and is a monumental piece featuring arias, duets, ensembles, marches, choirs, and ballets, making it akin to a Wagnerian opera. It has been performed at the Stuttgart State Theatre in 2006, among other venues.

Kraus, sometimes called "the Swedish Mozart," passed away in 1792 at the age of 36, suffering from illness and poverty. He is buried on the Tivoli peninsula in Bergshamra. His friends carried his coffin across the ice of Brunnsviken. The current monument, inscribed with: "Here lies the earthly remains of Kraus; the heavenly lives on in his music," was erected in 1846. It replaced the original monument from the 18th century, which was pyramid-shaped. He had expressed a desire to be buried there, as he often walked and enjoyed the area and its views. He was a close friend of the Barck family, who owned the Bergshamra peninsula, and frequently stayed in their home where he also composed music. A study examining Kraus's choice of burial site has been published.

In 1992, the Swedish Kraus Society was founded. In 2006, a square in Bergshamra, located in the municipality of Solna, was named Kraus Square, and a relief depicting Kraus by Björn S. Jonsson was placed there. Kraus's works are preserved at Uppsala University Library. Several of Kraus's stage works have been performed at Drottningholm Palace Theatre over the years.
