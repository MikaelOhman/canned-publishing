Johan August Arfwedson (also spelled Arfvedson), born January 4, 1792, at Skagerholms ironworks in the parish of Finnerödja, Skaraborg County, and died on October 28, 1841, at Hedensö, was a Swedish chemist best known for his discovery of the element lithium. He is the great-great-grandfather of the chemist Tomas Lindahl.

== Biography == Johan August Arfwedson belonged to a prominent bourgeois family from Stockholm. However, his branch of the family was involved in the iron and steel industry, as was his father, Jacob Arfwedsson. His mother, Anna Elisabeth Holterman, was the daughter of Martin Holterman and related to Niclas Sahlgren.

After studying at Uppsala University, Johan August Arfwedson obtained a degree in mining in 1812 and was employed as a clerk in the Mining Board. There, under the guidance of Jöns Jacob Berzelius, and in his private laboratory, he dedicated himself with great enthusiasm to chemical research. In 1817, while analyzing samples of the mineral petalite from the Utö iron mines, he discovered the oxide of a previously unknown element, lithium. Lithium proved to be an alkali metal and, at Berzelius' suggestion, was given the name "lithion," meaning "from stone." Arfwedson became famous during his lifetime for this discovery.

Following a trip to England, France, and Germany between 1818 and 1819, partly in the company of Berzelius, he was elected as a corresponding member of the French Academy of Sciences.

Arfwedson wrote several valuable treatises, both on chemistry and mineral analysis, which are included in the publications of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences and in the works published by Hisinger, Berzelius, and others on physics, chemistry, and mineralogy. After Arfwedson inherited a significant fortune, including the Skagersholm and Brunnsberg ironworks, he spent the last 20 years of his life on his purchased estate, Hedensö in Södermanland, along with the associated Näshulta ironworks, where he established a chemical laboratory. Due to extensive financial affairs, he soon had to abandon his research. However, he followed scientific progress with interest and maintained lively correspondence with Berzelius. After Arfwedson, the Greenlandic mineral "arfvedsonite" was named in his honor.

He became a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1821. In 1827, he was awarded the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences' gold medal for his discovery of lithium.
