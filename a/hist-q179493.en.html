Bertil Gotthard Ohlin [ˈbæʈil uˈliːn], born April 23, 1899, in Klippan, Skåne, and died August 3, 1979, in Vålådalen, Jämtland County (registered in the parish of Kungsholm, Stockholm), was a Swedish economist, professor, and politician (member of the Liberal Party). In 1977, he received the Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel (shared with James Meade). His most important contribution to economics is the so-called Heckscher-Ohlin theorem.

Ohlin served as the leader of the Liberal Party from 1944 to 1967. For much of this time, he was considered the opposition leader. He led the Liberal Party to significant successes in public opinion during the 1940s and 1950s. Under Ohlin's leadership, support for the Liberal Party peaked in 1952 at 24.4 percent of the vote, before leveling off at a level just below 20 percent until the 1970s.

== Early Life == Bertil Ohlin was the son and third child of eight to Elis Ohlin (1867-1946), who served as a district governor and later as a public prosecutor, and Ingeborg Sandberg (1872-1955). His mother was a prominent figure in his childhood and she was strongly politically engaged, with left-liberal views, admiring Karl Staaff, and was a Nordicist. Ingeborg Sandberg was the cousin of the grandmother of the Norwegian Prime Minister Gro Harlem Brundtland. Another brother was Holger Ohlin.

== Education == The young Ohlin demonstrated a great aptitude for mathematics in school and attended Nicolaiskolan (formerly the boys' grammar school) in Helsingborg, where he met Ruben Rausing, beginning a lifelong friendship. During his high school years, his political awareness began to awaken, and Karl Staaff and Arvid Lindman were among the politicians whose rallies he attended.

After graduating from high school, Ohlin enrolled at Lund University, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1917. Among his professors there were Knut Wicksell and Emil Sommarin. He joined the Lund Students' Temperance Association and was a member of D.Y.G. He then went to the Stockholm School of Economics, where Eli Heckscher ensured that he joined the prestigious Economic Club. Gustaf Cassel, professor of economics and finance at Stockholm University from 1904 to 1933, also became an important figure in his intellectual development. In 1919, he earned a degree in economics from the School of Economics and received the title of Diploma from the Stockholm School of Economics (DHS). He then joined the board of the Liberal Youth Association. Curious about Fabianism and the ideas of the liberal economist John Maynard Keynes, Ohlin traveled to Cambridge and Harvard for study trips. There, he earned a Master's degree at Harvard in 1923. Upon his return, he defended his dissertation at Stockholm University in 1924 under the supervision of Professor Gustaf Cassel, on the topic of "The Theory of Trade" (the Stockholm School of Economics did not begin awarding doctoral degrees until 1946). After defending his dissertation, he became a docent.

== Career ==

Between 1924 and 1929, Ohlin was a professor of political science at the University of Copenhagen, and he became a Danish citizen in 1925. During his time in Copenhagen, he solidified his position in the ongoing debates through numerous articles on the future of liberalism and problems with socialism. When Keynes published "The End of Laissez-faire" in 1926, it marked a turning point for Ohlin, who was ideologically positioned to the left among liberals and could sympathize with social democracy. In his farewell speech in Copenhagen, he surprised everyone by highlighting the poverty among groups that were typically not included in political discourse, namely small farmers, small business owners, widows, pensioners – "the forgotten Sweden."

He then returned to Stockholm, where he was a professor of economics at the Stockholm School of Economics from 1929 to 1965, and regained his Swedish citizenship in 1931. He married Evy Kruse (1908–1990), who had a degree in philosophy, in 1931. She was the daughter of physician Charles Kruse and Elsa Sandberg; they were related through their mothers. Together, they had three children, including their daughter, Anne Wibble, who served as Sweden's Minister of Finance from 1991 to 1994.

When Ohlin returned to Stockholm, his contemporary, the social democrat Gunnar Myrdal, had become prominent in the economics circles at the Stockholm School of Economics. Gunnar Myrdal was a professor of economics at Stockholm University from 1933 to 1947. Despite belonging to different political parties, they would later be regarded as a scientific partnership and formed the core of what became known as the "Stockholm School." From this time on, he also began to have personal contact with Keynes, whom he admired but sometimes felt competitive towards. His support for Keynes' theories on stabilization policies led to a rift with Heckscher in 1935.

During the 1930s and beyond, Ohlin served as an expert member of several commissions for the Swedish government and the League of Nations, where he was the leader of its commission regarding the global economic depression following the Wall Street crash of 1929.
