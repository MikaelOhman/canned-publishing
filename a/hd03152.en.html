In plain language

The Swedish National Audit Office is an agency that ensures the state is fulfilling its responsibilities. They have investigated the Swedish National Property Board (Statens fastighetsverk), which is responsible for many government buildings. The report shows that the agency performs well with certain types of buildings, but there are problems with how they manage buildings that receive funding from the state. There is a lack of a long-term plan for how these buildings should be maintained in the future.

There are also problems with how the government oversees the agency. The government has not controlled the regulations that determine how much profit the Swedish National Property Board should generate. The National Audit Office believes that these regulations need to be reviewed, and the government agrees that they need to examine how the profit requirements are calculated.

To address the problems with building maintenance, the government has already allocated additional funds. Between 2024 and 2026, the agency has received an extra 530 million Swedish krona. This money is intended to be used to repair and maintain the buildings in a better way, so that emergency repairs can be avoided.

The government has now instructed the agency to report on what they are doing to improve their buildings. This announcement concludes the work of responding to the National Audit Office's criticism.

In this document, the government addresses the observations and recommendations made by the Swedish National Audit Office (Riksrevisionen) to the government in the report "Management of Properties – The Swedish National Property Agency and Government Oversight" (RiR 2025:30). The Swedish National Audit Office's overall conclusion is that the Swedish National Property Agency's management of market-rate and subsidized properties is largely efficient, but that there are certain shortcomings in the agency's management and government oversight. The government has tasked the Swedish National Property Agency with adapting the property portfolio, but the Swedish National Audit Office believes that a more long-term solution for the subsidized properties is lacking. The Swedish National Audit Office also notes that the government has not reviewed the calculation model for the Swedish National Property Agency's return on investment requirements, despite the agency's results exceeding the requirement.

The government welcomes the review and agrees with the Swedish National Audit Office's assessment that there is a need to review the calculation model for the return on investment requirements.

The government further states that several measures have already been taken to ensure a long-term solution for the financing of the subsidized properties. During the period 2024–2026, the allocation for these properties has been increased by a total of 530 million Swedish krona to enable more planned and preventative maintenance. The government has also tasked the Swedish National Property Agency with reporting on measures taken to adapt the property portfolio.

With this document, the government considers the Swedish National Audit Office's report to be finalized.
