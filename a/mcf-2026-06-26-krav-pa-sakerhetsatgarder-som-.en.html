In plain language

Starting October 1, 2026, new regulations will come into effect that clarify how organizations should protect themselves against cyberattacks, which are digital attacks from sources such as hackers. These rules apply to businesses that are critical to society, such as electricity supply or healthcare.

Instead of simply following a fixed list of rules, each organization must independently assess its own risks and choose the appropriate security measures based on its size and operations. This means that leaders in these companies and government agencies need to take an active responsibility to ensure that their digital systems are secure.

The Swedish Civil Defence Agency has developed these regulations, but as of July 1st, the responsibility for cybersecurity will be transferred to a new organization called the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC). This center is part of the Swedish Armed Forces Radioelectronic Warfare Centre (FRA) and will provide support and guidance to companies affected by the new regulations.

Full text

The Cybersecurity Act, which came into force in January 2026, sets requirements for companies and government agencies within critical infrastructure to maintain a high level of cybersecurity.

On October 1st, the act's overall requirements will be supplemented with regulations that clarify what organizations need to do to comply with the legislation.

"Cybersecurity is a central part of our ability to withstand disruptions. Strengthening cybersecurity contributes to increasing the resilience of society as a whole," said Mikael Frisell, Director General at the Swedish Civil Defence Agency.

"Proactive leadership is needed to build robust cyber defenses in an increasingly serious security environment," emphasized Mikael Frisell, addressing decision-makers within the affected organizations.

Since the EU's NIS2 directive was adopted in 2022, it has been known that more actors would be subject to stricter cybersecurity requirements. The new regulations on security measures and management training now clarify which requirements apply to most organizations covered by the Cybersecurity Act in Sweden.

The regulations describe the areas that organizations need to work on and the security measures that should be considered based on the organization's specific circumstances.

Cybersecurity is not about simply checking off a list of actions. The regulations are based on a risk-based approach, where each organization needs to assess its risks, value its information assets, and implement appropriate security measures.

Organizations are expected to work systematically and continuously on these issues to prevent and reduce the consequences of cyber-related disruptions.

The regulations were developed through an extensive consultation process involving many stakeholders. The result is a regulation that combines clear requirements with the ability to adapt the work to the organization's own risks, size, and circumstances.

This means that different organizations may need to implement various measures to achieve an adequate level of protection.

The regulations will be published in June 2026 and will come into effect on October 1, 2026. Initially, general information and a webinar introducing the regulations will be available. Starting in August, additional guidance and more detailed support will be released.

The regulations have been developed by the Swedish Civil Defence Agency. On July 1st, the agency's cybersecurity operations, including responsibilities and expertise related to the Cybersecurity Act, will be transferred to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) at the Swedish Armed Forces Radio Agency (FRA).

"Sweden needs a collective improvement in cybersecurity capabilities. The ambition with the new national cyber security centre is to consolidate efforts and create a clearer division of responsibilities. The Cybersecurity Act is also a very important part of strengthening society's overall capability," said Björn Lyrvall, Director General of FRA.

Once the transition is complete, the work on providing information, support, and guidance to organizations covered by the new regulations will continue.

"We are taking over and continuing the work of supporting those affected by the Cybersecurity Act," said John Billow, Head of NCSC.
