In plain language

Iceland and the European Central Bank have agreed to connect the country to a system called TIPS. This system allows for direct payments between banks, businesses, and individuals. Starting in 2028, it will be possible to send Icelandic krona instantly.

This means that the Icelandic krona will be the fifth currency that can be used in this fast system. Previously, it has been possible to use the euro, Swedish krona, and Danish krona, and soon Norwegian krona will also be available. The goal is to make payments in Europe more secure and efficient.

The governor of the Central Bank of Iceland, Seðlabanki Íslands, is Ásgeir Jónsson. He says that this is an important step in modernizing Iceland's payment system. By using the European system TARGET, payments become more resilient and follow the same rules as the rest of Europe.

The TARGET system is operated by the Eurosystem, which is a collaboration between the European central banks. They use funds from the central banks to help banks send money to each other. With TIPS, banks can offer their customers the ability to send money in real-time, meaning instantly, regardless of the time of day.

Full text

June 24, 2026

The European Central Bank (ECB) and the Central Bank of Iceland today signed an agreement for Iceland to join the Eurosystem's system for instant payments, TARGET Instant Payment Settlement (TIPS). This will enable instant payments in Icelandic krona to be settled using central bank funds starting in 2028.

The Icelandic krona will be the fifth currency that can be used to instantly settle retail payments through the Eurosystem's TARGET services. The euro, Swedish krona, and Danish krona are already available for settling payments via TIPS, while the Norwegian krona is planned to join in 2028.

"We are pleased to see more and more European countries, including those outside the European Union, joining the Eurosystem's TARGET services," said ECB President Christine Lagarde after the agreement was signed. "This strengthens the integration of Europe's financial infrastructure, supports its strategic independence, creates economies of scale, and strengthens economic relations."

"This is an important step in modernizing Iceland's payment system and ensuring that it is fit for the future," said Governor Ásgeir Jónsson of the Central Bank of Iceland. "Joining TIPS will strengthen both the security and efficiency of instant payments in Iceland. It will increase the resilience of our payment infrastructure and align our payment processes with SEPA standards, while also allowing Iceland to benefit from the economies of scale and expertise of the Eurosystem's TARGET services."

TARGET services are developed and operated by the Eurosystem and use central bank funds to facilitate transfers between banks, businesses, and individuals. All TARGET services are designed to handle transactions in currencies other than the euro.

TIPS is the system for processing small payments, enabling banks and other payment service providers to offer their customers real-time transfers 24/7, 365 days a year.
