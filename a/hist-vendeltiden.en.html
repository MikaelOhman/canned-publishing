Full text

The Vendel Period is the middle phase of the Late Iron Age in Sweden's prehistory, named after the rich archaeological finds from the boat grave field in Vendel, near Vendel Church north of Uppsala. The corresponding period in Norwegian history is called the Merovingian Period, and in Denmark it is known as the Younger Germanic Iron Age.

==History== This period spans from the Migration Period to the Viking Age, approximately from the year 550 to 800. From this era, we have burial grounds and boat graves from Vendel and Valsgärde, located north of Gamla Uppsala, and a burial ground at Ultuna, just beside the Fyrisån river, about half a mile south of Uppsala.

In 1881, the boat graves in Vendel were discovered during an expansion of the cemetery, and Hjalmar Stolpe subsequently excavated eleven boat graves, some of which had been plundered. Three more graves were excavated in 1893. At that time, these graves contained the richest artifacts ever found in Sweden. In Boat Grave XII, the deceased was buried with two double-edged swords, one single-edged sword, two shields, a helmet, a spear, and arrows for a bow. Additionally, there were glass goblets, game pieces, horse tack, tools, and outside the boat, horses and dogs were sacrificed.

During the Vendel period, considerable wealth accumulated around Gamla Uppsala, likely from mining and trade. Ships became increasingly important, and the horses from Vendel-era Sweden were renowned for their quality. The 16-character runic alphabet, used on most runestones, replaced the 24-character alphabet throughout Scandinavia, suggesting strong kings with close relationships with each other.

According to sagas, the last kings of the old Yngling dynasty ruled during the early Vendel period. A concentration of power can be inferred from the large royal mounds and the later numerous boat graves in Svealand. Regarding the presumed burial mounds of the two last kings of the Yngling dynasty, Bröt-Anund and Ingjald Illråde, Anundshögen (Västmanland), which is the largest royal mound in Sweden, and Uppsa kull (Södermanland), the largest royal mound in Södermanland, no detailed archaeological investigations or dating have been conducted.

Over time, the mounds became significantly smaller, and during the Viking Age, they were only a few meters in diameter, although often located near the old mounds in Gamla Uppsala. Another burial custom that emerged during the Vendel period was boat graves. These are found in several places in Svealand, but the most famous ones are those in Valsgärde, about a kilometer north of Gamla Uppsala, and those in Vendel, which gave the era its name. The boat graves, like the ship settings from the Bronze Age, demonstrate that waterways were important. The boat graves from the Vendel period are rich in grave goods, and the importance of contacts with foreign countries is evidenced by the fact that finds similar to those found in Valsgärde have been discovered in the Sutton Hoo burial site in England.

The sagas, which here are more like poetry, also tell of how Ivar Vidfamne, somewhat later, as the first king after the Yngling dynasty, created a kingdom or union consisting of parts of England, Scandinavia, and provinces on the other side of the Baltic Sea. This was supposedly dissolved by the battle of Bråvalla, mentioned in several sources, between Harald Hildetand and Sigurd Ring.

During the 7th century, particularly after Birka was established as a replacement for Helgö, trade gradually intensified, mainly with Eastern Europe. Therefore, it is not possible to specify an exact year when Sweden entered the Viking Age, unlike the Western Viking expeditions, where the traditional starting point is the Vikings' appearance at Lindisfarne in 793.

== International Connections == During the Vendel period, several trading posts were established, primarily in the Baltic region, such as Seeburg, which is now Grobin in Latvia. These colonies foreshadowed the Viking Age trade expeditions eastward and laid the groundwork for them.

An example of international connections to the west is the magnificent boat grave at Sutton Hoo on England's east coast. It shows great similarities to the boat graves in the Mälardalen region, and the deceased warrior's weapons were made in the Kingdom of Sweden. During the Vendel period, Germanic animal ornamentation developed in Scandinavia, which may be the most exquisite of our prehistoric art forms. Goldsmithing techniques also reached their peak, including magnificent cloisonné work. The Gotlandic picture stone art also flourished during the late Vendel period.
