June 25, 2026

There is now a ceasefire in the Middle East, and oil prices have fallen. What does this mean for the economy in Europe, and particularly in Germany?

This is good news for Europe and the global economy. The war has slowed growth, and its end could now provide renewed momentum. Within the ECB, our primary concern is naturally inflation. In recent months, this increase has largely been driven by high energy prices, which have fallen sharply following the preliminary peace agreement.

Are you saying that prices at gas stations have decreased? Does this mean that the worst of the inflation is now over?

I wouldn't say that. Energy prices have been reduced, but they are still noticeably higher than before the war. We are particularly looking at energy prices for deliveries in the coming years, and these remain high. Even though the situation looks better in the short term than we had anticipated, the ceasefire is not a reason for policymakers to ease up.

Why aren't medium-term energy prices falling more significantly?

There are many reasons for this. It is unclear how sustainable the peace agreement truly is. It may also take months before transportation through the Strait of Hormuz and global supply chains return to normal. Transportation could permanently remain more expensive, for example, due to higher insurance premiums for the Gulf region. Therefore, we will probably not be able to fully return to the situation before the war. The energy infrastructure has also been damaged. In addition, the strategic oil reserves that were depleted during the war need to be replenished. In Europe, gas storage levels are relatively low and need to be filled before winter. All of this affects energy prices, and uncertainty remains high.

Is future inflation solely dependent on energy prices?

Absolutely not. Inflation data for May already shows that the reduction in energy prices has had broader effects. Inflation on goods and services not directly related to energy has already increased, and this is likely at least partially a consequence of the war. Prices on goods directly affected by the blockade and energy prices were the first to react – such as diesel, aviation fuel, and fertilizers. Surveys show that producers of other goods and services are also planning to pass on some of their significantly increased production costs to consumer prices. This increases the likelihood of chain reactions, particularly stronger wage increases as a result of rising inflation. So far, however, we have not seen any evidence that wage growth is accelerating or that people expect higher long-term inflation. But the memory of the period with high inflation is still fresh, so expectations may now react more strongly to the rising inflation.

Is it realistic to believe that trade unions will be able to push through unreasonable wage demands in Germany and other European countries, given the current difficult economic situation?

The economy is relatively resilient despite the significant increase in energy prices. According to our forecasts, growth has not slowed down as much as one might have expected. This is also due to the AI boom, which overshadows the Iran conflict and stimulates the global economy. At the same time, governments in many countries are spending money to protect their populations from the rise in energy prices. In Germany, a significant amount of investment is being made in infrastructure and defense. Furthermore, the recent decline in oil prices could make it easier for companies to pass on previous cost increases to consumers, as demand is likely to increase again. All of this can contribute to inflation in the medium term.

What about wages?

The effects on wages will only become visible much later, but in any case, we must not allow the situation to escalate to the point where prices and wages enter a self-reinforcing spiral.

You raised interest rates just over a week ago. Then came the energy crisis. In hindsight, was that interest rate increase premature? It does affect the economy.

No, this decision regarding the interest rate was appropriate in all the scenarios we considered, including a milder scenario where oil prices normalize quickly. It was necessary to prevent high energy prices from causing chain reactions and even higher inflation over the medium term. Economic growth is also part of our considerations. Without the interest rate increase, inflation would remain above our target of 2% for an extended period. This would not be consistent with our mandate to ensure price stability. Many people have experienced in recent years how painful it can be when inflation remains above the target for a long time.

Are you planning any further interest rate increases?

Based on the current situation, we will need to raise interest rates further to bring inflation back to our target of two percent over the medium term. However, the extent and timing of any further measures depend on how the conflict, the economy, and inflation evolve.

Many people are probably not fully clear on how an interest rate increase actually leads to lower inflation. Can you explain it in a simple way?

Essentially, it works like this: the rise in energy prices has significantly increased costs for businesses. This is amplified by the fact that it is a global event that spreads throughout the world via supply chains. Companies must now decide whether to absorb these costs, which reduces their profits, or pass them on to their customers. That decision depends on how strong demand is. This is where monetary policy comes into play: by raising interest rates, we dampen demand. This gives companies less room to raise prices.

To be more specific, if you raise interest rates to the point where some people can no longer afford to build a house, is that the intended outcome? Would this then lead to reduced demand for building materials, tradespeople, and architects?
