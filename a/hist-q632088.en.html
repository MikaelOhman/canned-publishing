Olof Peter Swartz, born on September 22, 1760, in Norrköping, and died on September 18, 1818, in Stockholm, was a Swedish naturalist, primarily a botanist and illustrator. In botany, "Sw." is often used as an abbreviation for his author's name.

== Biography ==

Olof Swartz began his studies at Uppsala University in 1778, focusing primarily on botany and undertaking extensive botanical expeditions throughout Sweden from 1779 to 1782. He became a medical candidate in 1783 and, the same year, funded a research trip to America. There, he initially stayed in Boston and Philadelphia, followed by Jamaica. In 1785, Swartz visited northeastern South America, as well as Cuba, Puerto Rico, and Haiti. From 1786 to 1787, he resided in England for comparative studies at the British Museum. In 1785, he was awarded a medical doctorate *in absentia* from Uppsala, and in 1791, he was appointed as the first director of the Bergian Garden, which had been donated to the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences by Peter Jonas Bergius. He also served as an intendant at the academy's museum (from 1807) and as a professor of natural history at the Karolinska Institute (from 1813). He was the permanent secretary of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences from 1811 to 1818, and in 1810, he was appointed to assist Collegium Medicum in developing a new pharmacopoeia (published in 1817).

Through his scientific works, Swartz was one of the leading experts on cryptogams of his time, and also one of the most prominent natural scientists of the generation following Carl von Linné. He was highly versatile and published numerous writings on flowering plants, ferns, mosses, fungi, and lichens, covering both the flora of Sweden and that of America. His most significant works are those from his time in the Caribbean, including *Nova genera et species* (1788), *Observationes botanicce* (1791), *Icones plantarum* (1794), *Flora Indice occidentalis* (3 volumes, 1797–1806), and *Lichenes americani* (1811). He published numerous articles on newly discovered Swedish plants and was the first to apply Johann Hedwig's moss classification and Christiaan Hendrik Persoon's fungal classification to Sweden's flora. Through careful detailed analysis, he advanced the systematics of orchids and ferns (*Genera et species Orchidearum* 1805, *Synopsis Filicum* 1806). A genus of trees in Central and South American rainforests, *Swartzia*, is named after Olof Swartz.

His extensive herbarium was purchased by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (later transferred to the National Museum of Natural History), and his collection from the West Indies was particularly valued as a research resource. He also wrote practical works on plant diseases, cultivation, and other topics. For Johan Wilhelm Palmstruch's illustrated work *Svensk botanik* 1802, he created many of the templates for the colored engravings that served as illustrations for the text, and he wrote the text for volumes 5–7 and half of volume 8 (1806–1817), as well as the text for issues 2–11 of *Svensk zoologie* (1806–1818).

Through his scientific expertise and ability to build relationships, Swartz established extensive connections and exerted a significant influence on the development of science in Sweden. He was a member of approximately twenty scientific societies, including the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (1789), the Royal Agricultural Academy (1812), and the Institut de France. In 1805, he was elected as an honorary foreign member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences. In 1789, he became a member of Leopoldina, in 1808, a foreign member of the Bayerische Akademie der Wissenschaften, and in 1812, a corresponding member of the Preußische Akademie der Wissenschaften.

=== Family === Swartz was the son of tobacco manufacturer Olof Swartz and Maria Elisabeth von Brobergen. From 1794, he was married to Sara Elisabeth Bergh, and he was the nephew of Petter Swartz.
