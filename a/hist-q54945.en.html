Hannes Olof Gösta Alfvén, born on May 30, 1908, in the parish of S:t Olai in Norrköping, and died on April 2, 1995, in Djursholm, was a Swedish physicist who received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1970.

== Background == Alfvén was the son of Johannes Alfvén and Anna-Clara Romanus, both medical doctors. In 1935, he married Kerstin Erikson (1910–1992). The couple had five children: Cecilia, Inger, Gösta, Reidun, and Berenike. He was the nephew of Hugo Alfvén and a cousin of Sven Romanus and Margita Alfvén.

== Academic Career == Alfvén defended his doctoral dissertation at Uppsala University in 1932 and became a docent there that same year. Subsequently, he served as a professor at the Royal Institute of Technology (Kungliga Tekniska högskolan) in theoretical electrical engineering with instrumentation from 1940 to 1945, in electronics from 1946 to 1963, and in plasma physics from 1963 to 1973. In 1970, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for his work in magnetohydrodynamics. Alfvén was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1947. He was also a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Engineering Sciences from 1947, but resigned in 1980 as part of his opposition to nuclear power.

=== Important Discoveries === Throughout his career, Alfvén made several fundamental theoretical discoveries. He is best known for founding and developing the field of magnetohydrodynamics. Some of the phenomena he identified are now associated with his name:

The Alfvén wave The Alfvén velocity The Alfvén radius

He developed several other fundamental concepts, not all of which are directly named after him, including simplifications in the way ionized gas, or plasma, behaves. He realized that the standard method for calculating particle trajectories using the Størmer theory was impractical, especially within the energy range relevant to auroras. This led him to develop the guiding center approximation, a tool for describing the motion of charged particles in electromagnetic fields.

He also discovered the first invariant related to the motion of charged particles. He coined the term "frozen magnetic field lines," based on conclusions about the role of the solar wind as magnetized plasma in the formation of auroras. Overall, these methods established magnetohydrodynamics as a new resource and distinct area of research.

He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for these "fundamental contributions and discoveries in magnetohydrodynamics with fruitful applications in various fields of plasma physics."

== Other Activities ==

Alfvén was also involved in other related issues, such as energy supply, cosmology, and the associated public debate. He had his own ideas about cosmology that he developed together with Oskar Klein. In the United States, where he later conducted his research, Klein-Alfvén's cosmology became largely associated with Alfvén's neologism "ambiplasma." Their joint ideas were alternative models to both the Big Bang and the steady-state theory, involving antimatter versus matter in a "metagalaxy." Alfvén, whose own research area was relevant to the development of future fusion energy, took a stand against the use of existing nuclear technology in the form of fission energy. His opposition to the Swedish approach and its subsequent expansion led to him being labeled as persona non grata by financiers, which prompted him to relocate his later research to Berkeley, California, for several years. After the war, Alfvén worked with Torkel Wallmark on developing domestically produced Swedish vacuum tubes. On March 23, 1948, Alfvén, along with Hilding Faxén and Torbern Laurent, supported a proposal by Conny Palm to build an "electromechanical calculating machine" (i.e., a computer) that was very similar to the Harvard Mark III. This proposal was never implemented, but it encouraged Palm, who, with the support of the Mathematics Machine Committee, built Sweden's first computer, BARK, between 1948 and 1950. He was politically active, initially in the Center Party, where he may have influenced Thorbjörn Fälldin to adopt a similar critical stance on nuclear power issues in Sweden during the 1970s. Later, he became involved with the Green Party, where he remained politically active until his death. In addition to his scientific work and other involvements, Alfvén also wrote a work of fiction: "The Tale of the Great Calculating Machine."

== Honors and Recognition == The Alfvén Laboratory at the Royal Institute of Technology (KTH), founded in 1990, as well as asteroid 1778 Alfvén, Hannes Alfvén's Street in Albano, Stockholm, and Hannes Alfvén's Square in Norrköping, are all named after Hannes Alfvén. He was awarded an honorary doctorate in technology by KTH in 1985. The Alfvén couple are buried at the Djursholm cemetery.
