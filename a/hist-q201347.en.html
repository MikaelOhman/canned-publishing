Torbern Olof Bergman, born on March 9, 1735, in the parish of Låstad, Västergötland, and died on July 9, 1784, in Medevi, was a Swedish natural scientist and chemist.

== Biography ==

Torbern Olof Bergman was the son of Barthold Bergman, a royal bailiff, and Sara Hägg. His interest in botany was sparked by his teacher, Sven Hof, at Skara gymnasium. He studied botany, mathematics, and entomology at Uppsala University. Through his entomological collections and observations, he became acquainted with Carl von Linné, who recognized him for a dissertation on leeches. He received awards from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences for this work, as well as for discovering a method to capture female frost moths, which lack wings and climb into the crowns of fruit trees in autumn to lay eggs. The method involved attaching strips of birch bark around the tree trunks and coating them with tar to trap the insects.

He also defended his doctorate in astronomy and founded the Cosmographical Society in Uppsala. Through this society, he published a physical description of the world, "Physisk beskrifning öfver jordklotet," which was translated into several languages. This work was one of the first modern geography books and influenced Abraham Gottlob Werner, who is sometimes called the "father of geology." Bergman later became a docent in physics while researching the electrical properties of tourmaline, as well as meteorological phenomena such as auroras, thunderstorms, rainbows, and ball lightning. He was elected to the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1764.

In 1767, he became a professor of chemistry and pharmacology. In this role, he renovated and modernized the chemistry department, using his own funds. Among other things, he designed a laboratory that was built. Bergman founded qualitative and quantitative chemical analysis and developed methods for separating substances using wet techniques (through solutions) and improved dry techniques (using heat). As a chemist, he gained international recognition and was offered a position with Frederick the Great, but Gustav III intervened personally.

Among his other contributions to chemistry, he developed the theory of affinity by creating tables and conducted research on carbonic acid. In 1775, he discovered how to create mineral water artificially. With his "Handbook in Mineralogy," he became a precursor to René Just Haüy, and he was the first to distinguish between organic and inorganic substances.

In 1772, he was one of the first to be appointed a Knight of the Order of Vasa. He received awards from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences twice. However, Bergman lived in an era whose ideas were often later dismissed. He believed in the phlogiston theory, which is why some of his research is now considered outdated.

He died prematurely due to a stroke and was buried at Västra Ny Church. An oil portrait by Lorens Pasch d.y. is preserved at Uppsala University, and a plaster medallion by Johan Tobias Sergel is kept at Gripsholm Castle.

In 1771, he married Margareta Catharina Trast. The given name Torbern is another form of the name Torbjörn, and since 1901, Torbern Bergman's birthday, March 9th, has been named Torbjörn in his honor.
