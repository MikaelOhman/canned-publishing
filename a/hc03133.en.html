In plain language

The government has proposed changes to how the so-called buffer funds are managed. Buffer funds are a part of the Swedish pension system used to create stability in the economy. The proposal involves merging the Sixth AP Fund with the Second AP Fund. Additionally, the First AP Fund will be dissolved, and its assets and operations will be transferred to the Third and Fourth AP Funds.

By reducing the number of funds from four to three, the government hopes to make the management of the funds more efficient and cost-effective. This involves reducing administrative overhead, meaning the extra work and costs that arise when many different organizations perform similar tasks. The government also wants to ensure that the members of the boards of directors have the necessary expertise to manage the funds effectively.

The proposal also includes regulations on how the work within the funds will be carried out in practice. It clarifies that confidentiality applies, meaning that certain information cannot be shared with just anyone, particularly when hiring a senior executive, also known as a CEO. These changes also apply to the Seventh AP Fund.

If the Parliament approves the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on January 1, 2026. The proposal also includes numerous changes to various laws that govern how pensions and fees operate in Sweden.

Full text

The government proposes measures to modernize and streamline the overall administration of the assets managed by the First, Second, Third, Fourth, and Sixth National Pension Funds (the buffer funds). Several proposals are put forward to improve the conditions for efficient and cost-effective management:

  • The Sixth National Pension Fund will be integrated into the buffer fund system by transferring the fund's operations and assets to the Second National Pension Fund.
  • The three buffer funds based in Stockholm will be reduced to two funds by transferring the operations and assets of the First National Pension Fund to the Third and Fourth National Pension Funds.
  • The board of directors of any National Pension Fund must collectively possess sufficient expertise in certain areas that are particularly important for the fund's management.

The government also proposes changes related to the administration of the National Pension Funds. It clarifies that confidentiality applies when hiring a CEO for a National Pension Fund. The proposals concerning the boards of directors and administration of the National Pension Funds also apply to the Seventh National Pension Fund.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on January 1, 2026.
