Carl Gustaf Verner von Heidenstam, born July 6, 1859, in Olshammar, Hammars parish in Närke province, and died May 20, 1940, at Övralid in Östergötland province, was a Swedish author and poet. He was a member of the Swedish Academy from 1912 to 1940 and was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1916.

He debuted as an author in 1888 with a collection of poems that broke with the then-prevailing Swedish literary traditions of realism and naturalism, instead focusing on romanticism, individualism, sensualism, and the idealization of Sweden's period of great power, with particular attention to the fates of the soldiers of King Charles XII. The vibrancy and affirmation of a cheerful sensuality made a strong impression and became stylistically influential for many other Swedish authors during the 1890s and subsequent years. Von Heidenstam therefore became the foremost representative of these "writers of the nineties," and his books consistently contain numerous national and historical themes.

== Youth == Verner von Heidenstam belonged to the noble family von Heidenstam, number 2025. He was the only child of the mining engineer Nils Gustaf von Heidenstam (1822–1887) and Magdalena Charlotta Rütterskiöld (1837–1917). He was born at the industrial estate of Olshammar, now called Olshammarsgården, located in the parish of Hammars by Lake Vättern, which belonged to his mother's family. He spent his summers there during his childhood. The family spent the winters in Stockholm, but he considered Olshammar his childhood home and often returned to it in his writings.

In his autobiographical work "Hans Alienus," he described his childhood as lonely. The surname "Alienus" comes from the Latin word for "foreign." At Olshammarsgården, there was a large library containing classical literature by authors such as Xenophon, Tacitus, and Plutarch, among others. He also read Swedish authors like Tegnér, Runeberg, and Wallin. In Stockholm, the family first lived on Johannis Östra Kyrkogata and, from 1876, at Karlavägen 7, opposite Humlegården. Heidenstam attended Beskowska school. Initially, he received good grades, but due to poor health, his academic performance deteriorated. He had dyslexia and struggled with spelling throughout his life.

At the age of sixteen, Verner von Heidenstam was forced to interrupt his schooling in Stockholm for health reasons, and as he himself wrote, "he was fortunate to experience the cosmopolitan atmosphere of the southern countries while still possessing the full receptiveness of youth." In September 1876, Heidenstam traveled abroad with his cousin Ernst. The trip went to Alexandria and Cairo, where they stayed until spring 1877 before returning home via Greece and Italy. In the fall, he returned to Beirut, Jerusalem, and Cairo. In a letter to his mother, he stated that he wanted to become a painter, a career choice she strongly discouraged. He diligently sketched and painted in his sketchbook but also began writing poetry.

After his marriage to Emilia Uggla in 1880, Heidenstam settled in Rome with the intention of becoming an artist. He and his wife were well-regarded within the Scandinavian artistic community there. In July 1881, they moved to Paris, where Heidenstam intended to study painting at the École des Beaux-Arts under Jean-Léon Gérôme. However, Heidenstam abandoned his plans to become a painter, and in 1882, the couple moved to San Remo, where he focused on writing poetry. After a brief secret visit to Sweden and his childhood home during the summer of 1883, Heidenstam settled in the canton of Appenzell in Switzerland. With the village of Bühler as their base, they made frequent trips to France and Italy, about which Heidenstam later wrote travelogues in "From Col di Tenda to Blocksberg."

In the early 1880s, Heidenstam sent a few collections of poems to Zacharias Topelius for evaluation. They were mainly influenced by the style of late Romanticism and also by Johan Ludvig Runeberg.

It is likely that the publication of August Strindberg's "Det nya riket" in October 1882 had a significant impact on Heidenstam and influenced him towards more radical views. The book was an attack on the so-called "Oscarian" society. In December 1884, shortly after the trial against Strindberg for his play "Giftas" concluded, Heidenstam wrote to Strindberg, who was living in Switzerland, expressing his admiration and asking Strindberg to critique his poems. In February 1885, Strindberg visited the Heidenstam couple in Bühler, and all three of them traveled to Rome. This marked the beginning of a friendship between Heidenstam and Strindberg that lasted for several years. Heidenstam lacked support for his literary aspirations from his family, and Strindberg felt like an exile in Switzerland. When Strindberg was feeling depressed, he appointed Heidenstam as his successor. Together, they traveled to Paris and the Swedish artists' colony in Grez-sur-Loing. Strindberg read excerpts from his manuscripts to Heidenstam. It wasn't until February 1886 that Strindberg had the opportunity to read something written by Heidenstam. Strindberg's letter is not preserved, but it apparently contained devastating criticism. In his reply, Heidenstam attempted to soften the blow, but the tone in their subsequent correspondence became less familiar. When Heidenstam moved back to Sweden in the spring of 1887, contact ceased.
