In his book, Adam describes how the different peoples lived. For example, he wrote about the customs of Danish Vikings, the hospitality of the Swedes, and how Norwegians lived simply with their animals. Full text

Adam of Bremen (German: Adam von Bremen, Latin: Adamus Bremensis), died before 1095, was an author and historian who lived in Bremen during the 11th century. Adam likely came from southern Germany, possibly Bamberg. He arrived in Bremen around 1066 or 1067, possibly to work as a teacher (referred to as "magister scolarum" in 1069) at the cathedral school, serving Archbishop Adalbert.

Adam wrote a book about the history of the Archdiocese of Hamburg, titled "Gesta Hammaburgensis ecclesiae pontificum" ("The Great Deeds of the Archbishops of Hamburg"). Based on oral accounts and old manuscripts, he presented the history of the Archdiocese of Hamburg from 788 until the death of Archbishop Adalbert in 1072. The book also contains a great deal of information about life in Scandinavia. King Sven Estridsson of Denmark, a learned man for his time, made valuable contributions to this work regarding conditions in Denmark, Sweden, and Norway. When the work was published in print in 1595, it was titled "De situ Daniae et reliquarum quæ trans Daniam sunt regionum natura."

== Adam of Bremen on the Norse Peoples ==

According to Adam, the Danes had amassed a great deal of gold through piracy. Danish Vikings paid tribute to the Danish king for the right to plunder from the people around the Norwegian Sea. However, they often abused this right and attacked their own countrymen. "And as soon as anyone captures his neighbor, he sells him mercilessly as a slave to either a friend or a stranger." Adam further recounts: "Women are immediately sold if they have committed adultery, but if men are accused of treason or caught in the act of some other illegal activity, they would rather be beheaded than whipped."

"They know no other punishments than the axe and slavery, and even when a man is sentenced, it is considered an honor to be happy, because tears, lamentations, and other signs of remorse, which we consider right, are so abhorrent to the Danes that it is not even permitted for anyone to weep over their sins or over their deceased loved ones."

"The Swedes lack nothing except for the pride that we love, or rather worship. Everything that is merely related to empty vanity, such as gold, silver, magnificent horses, beaver and marten skins, things that we admire to madness, they consider worthless: It is only in the matter of women that they know no bounds; each has, if possible, two or three wives at the same time, but those who are rich or chieftains have countless."

"The sons they have are considered legitimate. However, anyone who has intercourse with his neighbor's wife or rapes a virgin, or plunders his neighbor's property, or does him wrong is punished with death. Although the Norse are known for their hospitality, the Swedes surpass them. They consider it the greatest shame to deny traveling guests hospitality, and there is an eager competition to see who is worthy of receiving the guest. All sorts of kindnesses are shown, and as long as he wishes to stay, he is taken into the homes of the host's friends one after another. Such beautiful customs exist among their traditions."

"The Swedes consist of many tribes that excel in both strength and weaponry, besides being equally outstanding warriors on land and at sea. Therefore, they have the power to maintain order over the other Nordic peoples. They have kings of ancient lineage, but their power is limited by the will of the people. What all have jointly decided, the king must confirm, unless his opinion seems better; then, sometimes, although reluctantly, they follow it. In peacetime, this equality suits them well, but in war, they blindly obey the king or whoever he appoints as the most capable."

"When they are in need during battle, they call upon one of their many gods, and after victory, they are devoted to that god and prefer him."

Regarding the Norwegians, Adam states among other things: "They manage with what they obtain from their livestock, using its milk for food and wool for clothing. Therefore, the country raises many brave warriors who attack more often than they themselves are attacked, because they have not been spoiled by abundant harvests. They live on good terms with the Swedes, but sometimes they are attacked, although not unpunished, by the Danes, who are as poor as they are. They are also very content and value simplicity and moderation in both food and customs."

"In many places in Norway and Sweden, shepherds are considered very noble people who live in a patriarchal way and earn their living through their own work."

Finland is referred to in his writings as "Terra feminarum," Latin for "land of women," likely a misinterpretation of Kvänland. This has led to the interpretation of "land of the Amazons," but it is actually a mistranslation of the land of the Finns (Kvens).

How certain can we be about this?

Adam of Bremen is a primary source for Nordic history of the 10th century, but he himself never visited Sweden and wrote based on secondhand information with political motivations. His famous description of the Blot temple in Uppsala may be partly fantasy or exaggeration – it is often used but must be read critically.
