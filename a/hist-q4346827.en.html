Carin Sophie Adlersparre, née Leijonhufvud, was born on July 6, 1823, at Helgerum in Västrum, Kalmar County, and died on June 27, 1895, at Ström near Södertälje. She was a Swedish baroness and a pioneer in the Swedish women's movement, also known by the pseudonym "Esselde." In 1859, she co-founded *Tidskrift för hemmet*, the first women's magazine in Scandinavia, and in 1884, she founded the Fredrika Bremer Association.

== Biography and Activities == Sophie Adlersparre was the daughter of Lieutenant Colonel, Baron Erik Gabriel Knutsson Leijonhufvud, and Sofie Emerentia Hoppenstedt. Her family was one of the most prominent in the country, and even as a child, Sophie Adlersparre came into contact with contemporary social movements and the women's rights issues being promoted in Sweden and abroad. Fredrika Bremer became an early role model for Sophie Adlersparre, and she organized the women's movement according to Bremer's guidelines. She was accompanied by her childhood friend Rosalie Olivecrona (née Roos) and her friend Fredrika Limnell, who financially supported many of Sophie's projects.

Few women in Sweden have had such a significant impact through their social and feminist work as Sophie Adlersparre. Together with Rosalie Olivecrona, she founded the magazine "Tidskrift för hemmet" (later "Hertha") in 1859, which became extremely popular. On her own, she ran schools for working-class girls, established reading rooms for women, and founded the organization "Handarbetets vänner" (Friends of Handicrafts). At this time, there was a great deal of interest in handicrafts, and numerous societies and organizations dedicated to handicrafts were formed throughout the country, including sewing circles. In 1884, the Fredrika-Bremer Association was founded with a program outlined by Sophie Adlersparre. She served on the government's "Flickskolekommittén" (Committee for Girls' Schools) in 1885, becoming one of the first women to serve on a state investigation, along with Hilda Caselli. Shortly thereafter, the Fredrika-Bremer Scholarship Foundation was established in 1887, and she spearheaded Sweden's membership in the Red Cross. Adlersparre was the editor of the magazine "Dagny." She also participated in the founding of the "Föreningen för sinnesslöa barns vård" (Association for the Care of Mentally Retarded Children).

Sophie Adlersparre married Axel Adlersparre (1812–1879) in 1869. He was a member of the second chamber of parliament and a commodore captain. They are buried at Galärvarvskyrkogården cemetery in Stockholm. His sister, Sofia Adlersparre (1808–1862), was a painter who converted to Catholicism. The same name was also shared by Sophie's stepdaughter, Sofie Heliodora Ottiliana Adlersparre, born in 1850 in Karlskrona and died in 1924 in Eksjö.
