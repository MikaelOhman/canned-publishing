Birger Magnusson, born in 1280 and died on May 31, 1321, was the King of Sweden from 1290 to 1318. He was the son of Magnus Ladulås, who was King of Sweden from 1275 to 1290, and Helvig of Holstein. Birger was a great-grandson of Birger Jarl and belonged to the Bjälbo clan, a noble family that for four generations had – and fought each other over – power in Sweden.

== Biography == As early as 1284, the 4-year-old Birger was designated as his father's heir. When his father died in 1290, a regency council led by Marshal Torgils Knutsson became the guardians of the sons Birger, Erik, and Valdemar. In 1302, Birger was crowned in Söderköping after having married Princess Märta, daughter of the Danish King Eric Klipping, in 1298. In the 16th century, the land steward in Småland, Olof Nilsson, told Rasmus Ludvigsson how Birger had appointed a Johan Ulfsson and his brothers Brand and Karl to fetch Märta from Denmark. On the way to Sweden, however, Johan is said to have "besieged" her, and his brothers are said to have assaulted her maids. The brothers were then forced to leave the country and reportedly lost their estates. A fourth brother, Ulf, who was innocent and considered Olof Nilsson's ancestor, allegedly tried to redeem some of the properties but did not complete the transaction before both he and Birger died, and the farms passed out of the family. According to the Eric Chronicle, Märta actually stayed in Sweden between the agreement of the marriage in 1282 and the wedding. Ulf's sons are also completely unknown in contemporary documents. This episode has been interpreted by Hans Gillingstam and Adolf Schück as a folk tale, while Bjarne Beckman believed that it could have some basis in reality, but that the one who overstepped the bounds in Birger's eyes was the Norwegian nobleman Ivar Jonsson, who is given a good reputation in the Eric Chronicle, and that the event was used in propaganda during the brothers' feud. The couple had sons Magnus and Erik, and daughters Agnes and Katarina. The son Magnus was executed in 1320.

=== The Second Brothers' Feud and the Håtuna Games ===

Birger's younger brothers were Erik, who was Duke of Södermanland, and Valdemar, who was Duke of Finland. After Birger's coronation, conflicts arose between the brothers. In 1305, the dukes yielded to the royal authority ("the Treaty of Kolsäter"), but the brothers subsequently strengthened their position and gained support in Norway. Duke Erik received estates in Bohuslän and northern Halland, and also regained most of his former possessions in Sweden.

The support Birger had from Marshal Torgils Knutsson disappeared when the latter was executed in 1306. The same year, Birger was captured by his brothers at Håtuna royal estate in Uppland. He was then taken as a prisoner to Nyköpingshus castle in Nyköping. This event became known as the "Håtuna Affair." In 1308, he was released from captivity after being forced to cede most of the kingdom.

In 1309, Sweden was attacked by both Denmark and Norway. The following year, a peace treaty was signed in Helsingborg. Birger was recognized as king but retained only certain parts of northern and eastern Sweden.

=== Birger Magnusson's Deposition and Death ===

Birger's joy was short-lived. Supporters of the Dukes Eric and Valdemar gathered from across the country against Birger. After prolonged battles during the uprising of 1318 against King Birger Magnusson, Birger was forced to leave the country along with his wife. The couple fled to Märta's homeland, Denmark, where Birger died on May 31, 1321, shortly after receiving news that his son, Magnus, who had been captured shortly after the Nyköping banquet, had been executed the previous year.
