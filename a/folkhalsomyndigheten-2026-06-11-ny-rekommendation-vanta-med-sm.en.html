New recommendation: Wait until a child is 13 years old before giving them a smartphone.

TL;DRThe Swedish Public Health Agency recommends that children should not have their own smartphones until they are 13 years old. This is to protect them from inappropriate content online, sleep problems, and addiction.
In plain language

The Swedish Public Health Agency has issued a new recommendation regarding how children should use technology. They believe that the risks associated with smartphones are too great for children under the age of 13. The potential negative impacts on a child's health and well-being outweigh the benefits, such as staying in touch with friends or entertainment.

The reason for this recommendation is that smartphones can lead to poorer sleep quality and the development of addiction in children. Additionally, there are risks associated with online interactions and social pressure. For children under 13, the agency suggests a simple mobile phone without internet access as an alternative.

The agency believes that children aged 13 have typically developed enough maturity to handle a smartphone responsibly. By that age, they often have learned more about technology and its potential risks in school, which helps them better control their impulses. They are then better equipped to manage the dangers present online.

The Swedish Public Health Agency recommends waiting until a child is 13 years old before giving them a smartphone. The primary purpose of this recommendation is to reduce the risk of children being exposed to harmful content, experiencing sleep problems, or developing addictive usage patterns.

Discussions with children and young people reveal that the ability to stay in contact with family and friends and access entertainment are the main benefits of owning a smartphone. However, smartphone use is also associated with distractions, social pressure, exposure to harmful content and contacts. Research also shows that smartphone use can lead to poorer sleep and that children may develop addictive usage patterns. Overall, the Swedish Public Health Agency believes that the risks of owning a smartphone outweigh the benefits for children under 13 years old.

"Our hope is that this recommendation will be a support in everyday life," says Olivia Wigzell, Director General of the Swedish Public Health Agency. "It aligns with the recommendations from Denmark and Finland and complements our previous recommendations on safe and balanced screen time use."

Instead of a smartphone, children under 13 can use a simple mobile phone without internet access. This is one of several tips that the Swedish Public Health Agency provides to support parents and children. The agency will also continue to develop more detailed guidance and support materials in collaboration with other organizations.

"These are questions that can be difficult to navigate on your own, and many people seek knowledge and support. Our hope is that this recommendation will provide support and, over time, also help to change norms about when children receive their first smartphone," says Helena Frielingsdorf, a psychiatrist and investigator at the Swedish Public Health Agency.

The Swedish Public Health Agency has combined various methods and different data sources to gain a nuanced understanding of how children's and young people's health and well-being are affected by both the use of smartphones at different ages and by the age at which they received their first smartphone. Compared to younger children, 13-year-olds have had more time to mature and have developed better reasoning skills and impulse control. They have also gained more knowledge about digital technology and online risks through school and parents. As a result, they are better equipped to handle the risks associated with owning a smartphone. Furthermore, older children tend to benefit more from smartphones and have a greater need for independence in social interactions and other aspects of life.
