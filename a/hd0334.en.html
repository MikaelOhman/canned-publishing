Stricter rules for parole.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the rules regarding early release from prison. The goal is to increase safety in society and protect victims of crime by making sentences harsher.
In plain language

The government has proposed new laws that will change how prison sentences work. They want people serving prison time to serve a larger portion of their sentence before being eligible for early release, also known as parole. Instead of having served two-thirds of the sentence, they will now need to have served three-quarters.

It will also become easier to keep people in prison if they misbehave or if there is a high risk that they will commit new serious crimes upon release. For those who are released early, the level of supervision will be increased. The probation period, which is the time after release when individuals are under extra scrutiny, will be extended from one year to at least two years. Most people released will also have contact with monitoring from government authorities.

If a person commits new crimes during this monitoring period, the early release will be completely revoked. This means that the portion of the sentence that was served outside of prison will be forfeited. The government also wants to increase the minimum prison sentence to one month.

Most of these new rules are intended to come into effect on January 1, 2026. Some parts of the proposal may come into effect earlier if the government decides. The proposal is now being sent to the Parliament for them to vote yes or no on the new laws.

Full text

There is a need to reform criminal policy. The focus should shift from the perpetrator to the victim and the protection of society. To achieve this, key parts of the criminal code are being reviewed with the aim of, among other things, strengthening the content of existing penalties, particularly imprisonment. In the proposal, the government suggests, among other things:

  • Increasing the proportion of the prison sentence that must be served before parole can be granted, from two-thirds to three-quarters, and that this increase should be implemented gradually, starting with the longest prison sentences.
  • Lowering the threshold for postponing parole if the convicted person misbehaves during their imprisonment.
  • Making it possible to postpone parole if there is a risk that the convicted person will re-offend with serious crimes.
  • Increasing the minimum prison sentence to one month, and that all prison sentences should be subject to the system of parole.
  • Increasing the probationary period after parole to at least two years, instead of the current one year, and that the person granted parole should, as a general rule, be placed under supervision.
  • That the entire period of parole should be revoked upon re-offending during the probationary period, and that no new parole should be granted for the revoked portion.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on January 1, 2026, except for certain amendments regarding the increase in the proportion of the prison sentence that must be served before parole can be granted. These amendments are proposed to come into effect on a date determined by the government.
