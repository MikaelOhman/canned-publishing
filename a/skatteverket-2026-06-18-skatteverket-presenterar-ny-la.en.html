In plain language

The Swedish Tax Agency has calculated the number of people registered as residents in Sweden at the turn of the year. There were over 10 million people, which is more than the previous year. The report also reveals some errors within the system, such as individuals residing in the country who have not been assigned a personal identification number, or individuals who have moved from Sweden but remain registered in the records.

When information about who lives in the country is inaccurate, it can create problems for society. This can lead to money being paid out to the wrong people, or criminals using someone else's identity to commit crimes. The Tax Agency therefore wants better control and improved cooperation with other authorities to reduce these errors.

The Tax Agency proposes, among other things, that more individuals who do not reside permanently in Sweden should be issued a so-called coordination number. They also want to use more secure technology, such as facial images and fingerprints, to verify identities more effectively. The goal is to make it more difficult for criminal groups to misuse personal data.

Listen Tax Agency press release 2026-06-18 The Tax Agency presents a new overview of the population. As of the beginning of the year, 10,605,529 people were registered in Sweden. This is approximately 18,000 more than the previous year. The number of people registered after moving to Sweden decreased by 24 percent, while the number of people registered at birth was at the same level as last year. It is estimated that two to three percent are incorrectly registered.

This is the third consecutive year that the Tax Agency, on behalf of the government, has presented an overview of the population.

"The vast majority are correctly registered in the population registry. However, existing errors can cause significant harm, such as incorrect payments from social welfare systems, wrongful taxation, and identity theft. We need to continue improving our work," says Michael Högback, head of the Population Registry Department at the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket).

At least 65,000 people were registered in the population registry when they should have been deregistered as having moved abroad, while at least 23,500 people were not registered despite meeting the requirements. An estimated 96,000 to 154,000 people are registered at incorrect addresses.

This overview also includes individuals with personal identification numbers (known as "samordningsnummer"). These numbers are assigned to individuals who do not permanently reside in Sweden. As of the beginning of the year, there were 477,113 active personal identification numbers, categorized into three levels of identity verification: verified, probable, or uncertain. More and more people are verifying their identities. In 2025, 45% had verified their identity, compared to 12% in 2023.

"Unfortunately, not all government agencies can determine from their systems whether a number is verified or not. It would be desirable if this information were also used," says Anders Klaar, the Swedish Tax Agency's project manager for this overview. "The Swedish Tax Agency also proposes providing clear information about the right to reside and work in Sweden, in order to reduce misunderstandings and abuse."

Approximately 110,000 to 185,000 people live in Sweden without a personal or personal identification number. These individuals have still left some trace in government registers. This group includes 86,400 people whose whereabouts are known and who have the right to be here. Approximately 22,400 people have a known residence but do not have the right to be in the country. For approximately 30,000 people, their whereabouts are unknown. They may or may not have the right to reside in the country. "One way to make parts of this group more visible is by assigning them personal identification numbers more frequently," says Anders Klaar.

The Swedish Tax Agency has increased its population registry checks during 2025 and has also developed IT systems for control and services that can minimize errors.

"The errors that have the greatest consequences for society are inaccurate identities, which can be exploited in criminal schemes by organized crime. Biometrics, such as facial images and fingerprints, stored for as long as the identity is valid and potentially shared with law enforcement agencies, would be of great benefit. Another area that risks significant costs to society is when people have moved to other countries but remain registered in Sweden. This is an area we prioritize highly in our controls," says Michael Högback.

According to the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket), the number of registration errors has not decreased sufficiently. More control, technical support, and better information sharing and collaboration between authorities are needed.
