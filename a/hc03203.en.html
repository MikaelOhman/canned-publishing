The ban on uranium mining has been lifted.

TL;DRThe government wants to lift the ban on uranium mining in Sweden. This would allow for the exploration and extraction of uranium, ensuring a supply of important metals within Sweden and the EU.
In plain language

The government has proposed a change that would make it legal to mine uranium in Swedish mines. Currently, a ban in the Environmental Code prevents this, but by amending the law, companies could be granted permission to both explore for and extract uranium from the bedrock.

The proposal also includes changes to the regulations for smaller operations. If companies handle small quantities of uranium, the government will no longer be required to conduct a mandatory assessment of the operation. This also means that municipalities will no longer have the right to reject these smaller projects, a practice known as a municipal veto.

The purpose of these changes is to ensure that Sweden and the EU have better access to important minerals needed in the future. If the proposal is approved, the new regulations will come into effect on January 1, 2026.

The proposal involves changes to two important laws: the Environmental Code and the Mining Act. The Swedish Parliament must now decide whether to approve the government's proposal.

Full text

The proposal suggests removing the ban in the Environmental Code that prohibits granting permits for mining or facilities involving uranium-containing materials, and that uranium should be classified as a concession mineral according to the Mining Act. These changes would make it possible to extract uranium in Sweden and allow for a comprehensive geological survey during exploration. The changes would also make it possible to apply for and, under certain conditions, obtain exploration permits and processing concessions for uranium. These proposals are therefore significant for Sweden's and the rest of the EU's access to important metals and minerals.

The proposal also includes a change in the Environmental Code that limits the government's mandatory suitability assessment, as required by the code, to nuclear facilities that are assessed by the government according to the Nuclear Activities Act. This means that activities involving small amounts of uranium, which are assessed under nuclear legislation by the Swedish Radiation Safety Authority or are completely exempt from licensing requirements, will no longer be subject to the government's mandatory suitability assessment. The assessment of such limited activities will also no longer be subject to the so-called municipal veto.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on January 1, 2026.
