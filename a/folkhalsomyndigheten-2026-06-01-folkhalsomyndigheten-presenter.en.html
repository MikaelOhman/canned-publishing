The aim is to improve communication between adults and children, and to help children develop good habits in their daily lives.
In plain language

The Swedish Public Health Agency has conducted a new study on how parents' use of mobile phones affects children's health. Research shows that children can become upset when a parent suddenly looks at their phone instead of playing or talking with them. Furthermore, children whose parents spend a lot of time using screens often tend to use mobile phones more themselves.

The new guidelines include practical tips for everyday life. For example, adults can decide not to use their phones at the dining table or in bedrooms, just like the children. It is also helpful to explain to children why you are using your phone in different situations so that they understand what is happening.

Many parents already feel that they use their mobile phones too much. The agency wants to help these parents gain better control over their habits. Through small changes, adults can create a more secure everyday life and improve their connection with their children.

Mobile phones are part of everyday life for parents and other adults who interact with children. However, how adults use their screens when they are with their children can affect both interaction and children's own habits. This is the starting point as Folkhalsomyndigheten updates its recommendations regarding screen usage by parents.

The background is a government assignment where Folkhalsomyndigheten has compiled research on how caregivers' use of digital media affects children's health and well-being. This new compilation of knowledge reinforces previous research and provides a more complete picture of how children and young people are affected by their parents' screen usage.

Since 2024, Folkhalsomyndigheten has recommended that parents reflect on their own screen habits together with their children. Based on this new compilation of knowledge, the agency has now developed new recommendations, which are also supplemented with practical tips for everyday life.

"Mobile phones and other screens are part of daily life, even for parents and other adults in the household," says Helena Frielingsdorf, psychiatrist and investigator at Folkhalsomyndigheten. "The recommendations are intended to provide support and guidance for more conscious screen habits that can strengthen interaction with children and contribute to good habits within the family."

Research shows that parents' screen usage can negatively affect their interactions with children. In experimental studies, researchers have observed that young children often react with crying and irritation, and less frequently with smiles and laughter, when a parent interrupts an interaction to use their mobile phone.

Researchers have also observed a correlation between parents' and children's screen usage habits, where children of parents who use screens extensively tend to use screens more themselves.

The new guidelines also include practical tips for everyday life. For example, adults can establish the same "screen-free zones" as their children, such as at the dining table or in bedrooms. Another tip is to explain to the child what the mobile phone is used for in different situations.

"Children are not only influenced by what adults say, but also by what adults do. Therefore, small changes in daily routines can make a difference both in immediate interactions and in shaping children's habits over time," says Helena Frielingsdorf.

Statistics from the Swedish Media Agency show that many parents themselves experience mobile phone usage as a problem. More than half of parents with children between 0 and 12 years old report spending too much time on their phones.

In focus groups conducted in previous projects, children have also expressed frustration about their parents' mobile phone use.

It is also common for parents to share photos and videos of their children on social media, sometimes without the child's consent, which can lead to conflicts between children and parents regarding what is published online.
