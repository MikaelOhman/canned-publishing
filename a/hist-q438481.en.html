Full text

Kristina Nilsdotter (Gyllenstierna), born around 1494 and died on January 5, 1559, at Hörningsholms Castle, and buried in Trosa lands church, is one of the most famous women in Swedish history. She was married to Sten Sture the Younger, the regent of Sweden, and after his death, she led the Swedish struggle for independence from Denmark, organized the country's defense, and led the defense of Stockholm against Kristian II, who had been designated as heir by the Council of State.

== Biography ==

Kristina was the daughter of Sigrid Eskilsdotter (Banér) and Nils Eriksson (Gyllenstierna), and half-sister to Cecilia Månsdotter (Eka), the mother of Gustav Vasa. Therefore, Kristina was the aunt of Gustav Vasa. At the age of 17, she married Sten Sture the Younger (of the Natt och Dag family) on November 16, 1511, in Stockholm. He was 18 years old at the time. Sten Sture was the son of Svante Nilsson Natt och Dag and Iliana Gädda. The following year, he became the regent of Sweden, making Kristina the Regent's wife. He assumed the position of Regent on July 23, 1512, and died on February 3, 1520, on the ice of Lake Mälaren.

=== The Stockholm Bloodbath === At the beginning of 1520, Sweden was invaded by Denmark under Christian II. After the death of her 27-year-old husband in a sled on the ice of Lake Mälaren in February, she led the resistance against Christian II. Despite her deep grief and surrounded by her four young children, she took on the responsibility of continuing her late husband's work. She took command of Stockholm Castle and encouraged the city's citizens to bravely defend the city. She also sent letters to the countryside appealing for people to drive out the invaders. "Dear friends," read a letter to the commoners of Södermanland and Rekarne, "be well-disposed and comforted, and stand firm with your fatherland, as your fathers and ancestors have done before you, and as you always have done!"

Kristina tried in vain to convince Sweden's leading men to take charge of the common people. She sent several messengers to the Councilman Ture Jönsson (Tre Rosor), the district judge of Västergötland, and offered Stockholm Castle and all her property if he would accept the position of governor. However, Kristina failed to secure support from the majority of the nobility: "Some said they wanted to fight for Sten Sture's children, and some refused outright to support Lady Christina, Sten Sture's widow, along with Stockholm city and those she had with her, writing often that they should unite and resist the enemy, but such letters were disregarded." The majority of the nobility and council members proclaimed Christian II as King of Sweden on March 6 at the Uppsala assembly.

However, Kristina had the support of the majority of the common people and controlled Sweden's main strongholds: Stockholm, Västerås, Kalmar, and Nyköping, as well as Finland. Before Stockholm was besieged by Danish troops, she managed to send Sten Sture's chancellor, Peder Jakobsson (Sunnanväder), and her eldest son, Nils Stensson Sture, to Danzig to seek help. At this time, the citizens of Stockholm called her "our beloved princess," and in her embassy to Danzig, she declared herself as the regent of Sweden until her sons came of age.

When Christian's forces began an intense bombardment of Stockholm from all sides, the city's well-armed defenses responded powerfully and persistently. Kristina Gyllenstierna stood on the ramparts of the castle, inspiring the city's defenders.

Because of this, Christian was unable to conquer Sweden's capital by force this time. Instead, he tried to lure the city's defenders with promises: he offered to forgive all resistance and respect the kingdom's laws and rights. Hemming Gadh, who had switched sides and now represented Christian, played a crucial role in this. He was very familiar with the conditions in the city and personally acquainted with both Kristina and the city's leading men. He knew how to approach them individually and understood their weaknesses. Therefore, he succeeded in his mission.

In an essay from the fundraising campaign for the Kristina Gyllenstierna statue, erected in 1912 on the outer courtyard of Stockholm Castle, the committee that collected money for the monument wrote:

In the autumn of 1520, Kristina was persuaded to surrender on the promise of amnesty. In the amnesty letter issued in September 1520 by Christian, amnesty was promised to Kristina, her deceased husband, and all their helpers for actions taken against King Christian, his father King Hans (the union king), and his grandfather King Christian I, despite their involvement in the deposition of Bishop Trolle. This would avert the threat of a papal excommunication. Kristina was also given financial guarantees. She was to retain Tavastehus castle and its surrounding lands, Kuhmo estate in Finland, Hörningsholm castle in Södermanland, and Eksjö in Småland. Subsequently, Kristina and the commanders at Tre Kronor castle in Stockholm issued a letter in which they pledged to surrender the castle on September 7th to Christian.

However, King Christian broke his promise to Kristina shortly after his coronation in November 1520. This breach of promise was manifested in the Stockholm Bloodbath. After the coronation, a large ball and festivities were held for three days.
