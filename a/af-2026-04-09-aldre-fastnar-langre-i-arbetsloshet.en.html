Full text

Older individuals who become unemployed often find it more difficult to secure new jobs and are more likely to experience long-term unemployment. At the same time, Sweden has an aging population that needs to work for a longer period. An active labor market policy and measures against age discrimination can make a difference, according to a new report from the Swedish Public Employment Service (Arbetsförmedlingen).

Individuals over the age of 55 are generally well-established in the labor market and rarely become unemployed. However, when they do lose their jobs, it is common for them to experience prolonged periods of unemployment. The Swedish Public Employment Service therefore assesses that this group has a weaker competitive advantage in the labor market.

Research also shows that middle-aged and older job seekers are often overlooked during recruitment processes. The chance of being contacted by an employer decreases starting in one's 40s, and then further diminishes with increasing age.

"Age discrimination, which occurs regardless of the job seeker's qualifications, negatively impacts the demand for older workers," says Sandra Offesson, labor market analyst at the Swedish Public Employment Service. "If older individuals expect to be discriminated against due to their age, it may deter them from seeking jobs. This creates a risk that they will leave the labor market prematurely, which negatively affects the supply of labor."

To prevent unemployment among older workers, continuous skills development throughout working life is crucial. It is also important to raise the issue of discrimination and ageism with employers in order to influence attitudes. An active labor market policy can also make a difference.

"Given that Sweden has an aging population, it is particularly important for future workforce planning to utilize all those who want and are able to work, especially older individuals," says Sandra Offesson.
