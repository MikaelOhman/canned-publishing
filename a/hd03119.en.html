In plain language

The government has proposed new laws that are scheduled to come into effect on April 1, 2026. The most significant change is the removal of the additional requirement to pay down loans (amortization). This means that you will no longer be required to pay at least one percent extra each year if your loans are large in relation to your income.

There will also be new rules regarding how much you can borrow from a bank. When purchasing a new home, you can borrow up to 90 percent of the property's value. However, if you want to borrow more money to expand or renovate your current home, you can only borrow up to 80 percent of the value.

The proposal also involves giving the Riksbank, Sweden's central bank, more power. The Riksbank will have more responsibility for ensuring that banks have sufficient capital to withstand economic crises. This is intended to create a more stable economy throughout the country.

The proposal is now awaiting a decision in the Parliament. If the Parliament approves it, several laws will be amended simultaneously to create the new regulations regarding banks and mortgage loans.

Full text

The proposal suggests that a new law limiting mortgage loans should replace the Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority's regulations and guidelines regarding amortization requirements and loan-to-value limits. The proposal includes the following changes compared to the current regulations:

  • The stricter amortization requirement is removed, meaning the requirement for additional amortization of at least 1 percent is no longer in place if the borrower's total mortgage loans exceed 4.5 times their gross annual income.
  • The loan-to-value limit should be up to 90 percent of the property's market value when purchasing a new property, and 80 percent of the property's market value for loans taken to expand an existing mortgage.

The purpose is to contribute to a healthy culture of repayment, reduce barriers in the housing market, and improve opportunities for different groups to own their homes without compromising financial stability.

The proposal also suggests strengthening the role of the Swedish National Bank (Riksbank) in the area of macroprudential supervision. As part of this, the Riksbank will be responsible for setting the countercyclical capital buffer rate when applying the EU's capital adequacy regulations for banks.

The legislative changes are proposed to come into effect on April 1, 2026.

Proposed Parliamentary Decisions

The government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for a law limiting mortgage loans.
  2. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to the law (2004:297) on banking and financial services.
  3. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to the law (2014:966) on capital buffers.
  4. The Parliament adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to the law (2016:1024) on mortgage lending activities.
