Fredrika Dorotea Vilhelmina of Baden, in German: Friederike Dorothea Wilhelmine von Baden, born on March 12, 1781, in Karlsruhe, and died on September 25, 1826, in Lausanne, was Queen of Sweden from 1797 to 1809. She was the daughter of Karl Ludvig of Baden and Amalia of Hesse-Darmstadt, and she was married to Gustav IV Adolf of Sweden from 1797 to 1812.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years ===

The two were married in 1797 when Fredrika was 16 years old, making her the Queen of Sweden. She has been described by those around her as both beautiful and childish. In contrast to his father, Gustav III, Gustav IV Adolf showed a noticeable interest in eroticism within his marriage, so much so that the Lord Marshal, Axel von Fersen, gently urged the king to be careful and, as he put it, "protect the Queen's health." The couple lived an intimate family life, and Fredrika had the ability to calm Gustav Adolf's often volatile temper. Hedvig Elisabet Charlotta of Holstein-Gottorp described how Fredrika would play the piano for him for one or two hours when he was in a bad mood, and that he would then feel better – she apparently possessed musical talent.

Fredrika never became truly popular with the public, and she was often perceived as uninterested in official events and celebrations held in her honor. On one occasion, Fredrika attempted to intervene in politics. During the war with France, the king left Pomerania at one point (in 1807) and went to Ramlösa; he then met Fredrika in Varberg. She gave him a letter from her mother, in which her sister, the Russian Empress, urged Fredrika to use her influence to avoid breaking with France, as this would mean disaster. Gustav Adolf was very upset because he perceived that her relatives were acting as agents for Napoleon, and this caused a rift between the couple that was not repaired until the outbreak of the Finnish War.

=== The Finnish War === In 1809, Sweden lost Finland after the Finnish War. King Gustav IV Adolf wanted to reclaim Finland, but Swedish officers refused, and Gustav Adolf was arrested on March 13, 1809. He was deposed as king on May 10, 1809. Initially, Fredrika and her children were held under house arrest at Haga Palace. During her captivity, she behaved with dignity and humility, which earned her more sympathy from the public than ever before. She was visited by the new queen, Hedvig Elisabet Charlotta, who wanted to make Fredrika's son the heir to the throne. Fredrika declared that she was willing to allow her son to become the crown prince. From the beginning, she had been kept separate from her husband on the advice of Gustaf Lagerbjelke, who suspected her of trying to arrange a counter-coup. She requested to be reunited with her husband, and eventually, this was allowed through the efforts of Hedvig Elisabet Charlotta.

A "military faction" led by General Vegesack allegedly had plans to "abduct" Fredrika and her children from Gripsholm Castle and proclaim her regent for her underage son, but she refused: "The queen displayed a nobility of feeling that makes her worthy of an eternal crown of honor and elevates her above the sad earthly royalty. She did not listen to the secret proposals made to her by the faction that sought to preserve the succession for the heir prince and wanted her to remain in Sweden to become a guardian during his minority... she declared firmly that her duty as a wife and mother obligated her to share the king's and children's exile... It was up to the king to decide her fate, but her place was determined, and she would never take a throne where intrigues, conspiracies, and especially political divisions awaited her. She foresaw that Sweden would never find peace until the current dynasty was removed, and therefore, she would do everything in the world to persuade the king not to sacrifice his son to an unhappy future."

At the end of the year, the family was exiled and placed in a carriage addressed for departure from the country by boat from Karlskrona. After stopping in Copenhagen and then Frankfurt, they were welcomed by Fredrika's mother in Bruchsal, and subsequently, the family settled at the castle in Baden where Fredrika had grown up. They said goodbye to their Swedish entourage, General Skjöldebrand and Baron von Otter, and retained only a doctor, a stablemaster, and her son's tutor as Swedes in their household. They were offered by Fredrika's grandfather the opportunity to settle at Mersburg Castle on Lake Constance, but Gustav Adolf suddenly left, without warning and without informing Fredrika, alone to Switzerland.

== Family == Gustaf Adolf and Fredrika separated in 1810. Fredrika moved with the children to another castle near Karlsruhe, while Gustav Adolf settled in Basel. In 1811, divorce proceedings began. It was Gustav Adolf who filed for divorce, while Fredrika opposed it. Gustav Adolf stated that his reasons were that he wanted to have many children, but not royal ones, and that he wanted to live a simple religious life with a devout wife, preferably among the Herrnhutters, and that he preferred to marry someone of lower status in order to achieve this.
