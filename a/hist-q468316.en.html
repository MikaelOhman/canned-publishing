Carl Gustaf Mosander, born September 10, 1797, in Kalmar, and died October 15, 1858, at Drottningholm in the parish of Lovö, was a Swedish chemist and teacher.

== Biography == Mosander began his career as a pharmacist. In 1820, he was accepted as a student at the Karolinska Institute, where he quickly became one of Jöns Jacob Berzelius's best students. He earned his degree in surgery in 1825. In 1829, he became an adjunct professor, and in 1832, he served as acting professor before being appointed full professor of chemistry and pharmacy at the Karolinska Institute in 1836. From 1845, he also served as curator at the mineralogical department of the National Museum, and from 1845 to 1857, he was a teacher and, from 1846 until his death, an inspector at the Pharmaceutical Institute. Together with Anders Adolf Retzius and Carl Wilhelm Henrik Ronander, he founded the journal *Tidskrift för läkare och pharmaceuter* (Journal for Physicians and Pharmacists) in 1828, which ran until 1839. He was a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (in 1833), the Agricultural Academy, the Uppsala Scientific Society, and other learned societies.

Mosander was a sharp and skilled chemist. His most important work involved the difficult investigation of the rare earth elements found in cerite and gadolinite. Through his research, he demonstrated that what had previously been considered a homogeneous substance, cerium oxide, was actually a mixture of three new metal oxides: true cerium oxide, lanthanum oxide, and didymium oxide. He also showed that ytterbia (yttrium) was a mixture of true ytterbia, terbium, and erbia. As a result of this work, he was initially credited with the discovery of six new elements. However, later research revealed that both didymium oxide, as well as terbium and erbia, could be further separated into additional oxides.

=== Family === Carl Gustaf Mosander married Hulda Philippina Forsström (1813–1888) in 1832. She was born on Saint-Barthélemy. They had a daughter named Huldine Beamish. All three are buried in the Lovö churchyard.
