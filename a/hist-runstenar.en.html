In plain language

A runestone is a stone on which runes have been carved; runes are an ancient form of writing. Most of these stones were erected in Sweden, Norway, and Denmark between the years 300 and 1130. For a long time, they were primarily used to honor deceased individuals, while older stones often featured longer texts with more unusual words and images.

With the arrival of Christianity, the practice of creating runestones increased in Sweden, particularly in the regions of Uppland and Södermanland. These later stones often followed a specific pattern, describing who erected the stone in memory of a relative and identifying the person who carved the inscription. The appearance of the images on the stone, such as whether they depict snakes or dragons, can help to determine approximately when the stone was created.

Runestones are extremely important because they were written by the people who lived at that time. They tell us about common names and how people embarked on adventures. Although the texts are often short, they provide valuable information about kinship and history that would otherwise have been lost.

For place names, see Runsten (various meanings).

A runestone is a carefully selected stone or monolith inscribed with runes by a runemaster. Most were erected in the Mälardalen region during the 10th century. The exact meaning of the term "runestone" has varied over time; for example, the earliest volumes of the Swedish Runestones inscriptions used it to refer to rune-inscribed gravestones as well. Nowadays, the term generally refers to a runic stone intended to be erected upright. Fixed blocks of stone with runic inscriptions are called "runblocks," and inscriptions on rock faces are called "run slabs."

== Time and Context == Runestones were primarily created between the years 300 and 1130. They are found throughout the Nordic cultural region – Sweden, Norway, and Denmark, as well as in certain areas with Norse influence. The oldest runestones have been discovered in Norway, such as the Tunestenen, Hogganvikstenen, and Einangstenen, dating back to the 4th century (350s). In 2023, an even older Norwegian runestone was found, dated between 1 and 250 AD.

The younger runestones are mainly located in Denmark and Sweden. Runes were also used much later in both Sweden and Norway, but the practice of erecting runestones ceased before the year 1100. The vast majority of runestones are found in the regions surrounding Lake Mälaren in Sweden.

Older runestones (before the year 800) are generally inscribed with the runes from the older futhark, which consisted of 24 characters. They often lack ornamentation. Compared to Viking Age runestones, which were almost always memorial stones for deceased relatives, it is not always clear what the purpose of the older runestones was. During this period, the number of stones erected in Denmark, Norway, and Götaland was similar to, or perhaps even greater than, those erected in Svealand and further north. Furthermore, these are often more literary (written in verse, with poetic imagery) than the later ones, and several of the most famous examples date from this period, such as the Eggjastenen, Björketorpsstenen, and Järsbergsstenen.

Around the year 960, there was a significant surge in runestone erection, starting in Denmark. Harald Bluetooth had recently been baptized and erected the Jellingestenen.

This custom then spread northwards, with runestones being erected for a generation in each area until the practice died out, with one exception: Uppland and Södermanland. In these regions, the trend continued well into the 12th century, making them particularly rich in runestones. These stones largely followed a specific pattern: "X raised the stone in memory of his relative Y, who did such things and died so." "May God help his or her soul." "Z carved it."

== Styles and Dating == Main article: Runestone styles

According to the method developed by archaeologist Anne-Sofie Gräslund, the younger runestones can be divided into seven style groups based on the development, similarities, and differences in their ornamentation. These style groups were used to date the stones. The stones dating from around the year 1000 have relatively simple ornamentation, while those from around the year 1100 feature more complex and elegant patterns. The serpentine or dragon motifs found on the stones are an example of the animal ornamentation that dominated Scandinavian craftsmanship throughout the Iron Age. This type of decoration was used on everything from silver brooches to wood carvings on Viking ships, as well as on runestones. The animal style may have been a way to mark Scandinavian identity. The older stones tend to have more detailed inscriptions, while the later ones have shorter and more standardized text.

Style chronology and groups for stones in Sweden:

  • Rak = approximately 980 to approximately 1015
  • Fp = approximately 1015 to approximately 1050
  • Pr1 = approximately 1010 to approximately 1050
  • Pr2 = approximately 1020 to approximately 1050
  • Pr3 = approximately 1050 to approximately 1080
  • Pr4 = approximately 1060 to approximately 1100
  • Pr5 = approximately 1080 to approximately 1130 AD

=== Ornamentation ===

Run inscriptions are generally arranged within supporting lines, initially often just straight lines. Runestones read from right to left are common. Later, the stones were increasingly decorated with ornamental lines, first in the form of serpent figures, and later in the form of dragon motifs. The runes were carved into these patterns. The type of ornamentation helps runologists date the inscription and identify the carver. The heads of the animal figures that adorned the stones are either depicted as a triangle with two eyes (approximately 1010-1050) or from the side. The latter form changes from a blunt, abruptly cut nose to increasingly elongated heads.

On many stones, Christian symbols such as crosses appear. Very few have directly pagan symbols; only two out of two thousand runestones in central Sweden feature such symbols: both with Thor's hammer, and one also with what is likely the face of a god. More elaborate ornamentation also exists, such as in the Sigurd inscription, which depicts scenes from the saga of Sigurd Fafnesbane, but this is uncommon. The runestone carver Fot seems to have been among those most inclined to richly adorn the stones with images.

The "Rak" style includes stones with text arranged in a spiral, with straight endings on the bands of inscription and without serpent heads or tails. How certain are we about this?

Runestones are unusually strong sources: they are contemporary primary sources, carved by the people themselves. They reliably tell us which names, family relationships, and journeys were considered worthy of remembrance. The limitation is that they are short and formulaic – they state what was commemorated, not the entire context.
