In plain language

A new report from the Swedish Public Health Agency sheds light on sexual health in Sweden. Over half of all men and women are satisfied with their sex lives, and most feel they have control over their own bodies. However, there are significant problems that need to be addressed, particularly concerning violence against women.

The survey reveals that many women experience sexual violence or harassment online. To improve the situation, the agency wants society to talk more about consent, which means freely and voluntarily agreeing to something. This should happen in schools and healthcare settings so that everyone learns about their rights from a young age.

There are also differences between men and women regarding autonomy. For example, older men reported having more difficulty saying no to sex than women do. The agency believes that we need better support, more knowledge, and greater effort to improve equality for everyone.

Most people feel they can control their own sexuality, and approximately half of all women and men in Sweden are satisfied with their sex lives. At the same time, several challenges remain, according to a new report on sexual and reproductive health. For example, women are highly vulnerable to sexual violence, and older men feel less free to say no to sex.

The new population survey SRHR 2024, based on questionnaire responses from nearly 16,000 people, shows that the general sexual and reproductive health of the population is good. Approximately half are satisfied with their sex lives, and most feel they can say no to sex and choose their partner without pressure from family.

At the same time, there are clear differences between genders and age groups. Nearly one in six women have experienced sexual intercourse or other sexual acts while in a vulnerable or helpless situation. In the 16-29 age group, almost half of the women have experienced online sexual harassment.

"The survey shows that women are highly exposed to sexual violence, and there has been no decrease. This is a serious situation that needs to change. We must increasingly talk about and raise awareness of norms, consent, and bodily integrity from an early age, and at all levels of society. This includes preschools, schools, and healthcare. Preventive measures also need to be further developed and strengthened," says Director General Olivia Wigzell.

Regarding the freedom and ability to make independent decisions about when, how, and with whom to engage in sexual activity, the results reveal differences. Men feel less free than women to initiate sex and suggest activities during intercourse. Furthermore, men, particularly older ones, reported less frequently that they felt able to say no to sex or unwanted actions during sexual encounters.

Overall, the report highlights the need to strengthen conditions for good and equitable sexual and reproductive health within the population.

"We see a need for initiatives from various stakeholders to improve knowledge about sexual and reproductive health, and also to increase gender equality between women and men. We also need measures to address the shortcomings we observe in access to support and care," says Elin Jacobsson, head of unit.

SRHR 2024 is a national survey on health, relationships, and sex. It was sent to a random sample of 60,000 people aged 16-84 during the autumn of 2024. A total of approximately 16,000 people responded, resulting in a response rate of 26 percent.
