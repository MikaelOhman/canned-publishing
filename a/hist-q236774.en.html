Katarina of Saxe-Lauenburg, born September 24, 1513, in Ratzeburg, and died September 23, 1535, in Stockholm, was the Queen of Sweden from 1531 to 1535 during her marriage to Gustav Vasa.

== Biography ==

Katarina was the daughter of Duke Magnus I of Saxe-Lauenburg (died 1543) and Katarina of Braunschweig-Wolfenbüttel. She received a Protestant upbringing. The marriage was arranged for political reasons. After unsuccessfully exploring options in Poland, Denmark, and two German principalities, Gustav was advised to begin negotiations with Saxe-Lauenburg. While the duchy was poor, its dynasty was related to several of Europe's most powerful families, it served as a center for mercenaries that the king needed, and it was Protestant, while Katarina's mother was well-regarded by the emperor and could provide imperial connections. Through this marriage, Gustav could establish contacts with German princes, but also with Denmark, as Katarina's sister, Dorothea of Saxe-Lauenburg, was married to the Danish Crown Prince Kristian, later King Kristian III. The marriage also linked Gustav to the Bjälboätten (Bjälbo clan), from which Katarina descended in two ways: Her father, Duke Magnus I, was a descendant of both the Bjälboätten and the ancient Erik dynasty, through Ingeborg Birgersdotter (Bjälboätten) (1253–1302) (daughter of Birger Jarl and Princess Ingeborg Eriksdotter of Sweden), who was married to Duke Johan I of Saxe-Lauenburg (1249–1286). She also descended from the Bjälboätten through her maternal grandmother, Katarina of Pomerania-Wolgast.

Negotiations began in 1528 but dragged on for several years. She received the provinces of Korsholm and Kalmar, and the island of Öland as a dowry. In late summer 1531, Katarina was escorted to Sweden by a fleet led by her future sister-in-law and brother-in-law, Margareta Vasa and Johan of Hoya, and she met her future husband when the ship anchored in Strömmen in September. The wedding took place in Stockholm on the same day that Katarina turned 18, and was followed by festivities that were made as grand as possible for the Swedish court at that time. Shortly thereafter, Gustav's cousin Ebba Eriksdotter (Vasa)'s daughter Brita was betrothed to Gustav's favorite, Gustav Olsson (Stenbock), and Gustav's niece, Brita Joakimsdotter (Brahe), was betrothed to the Lord High Constable Birger Nilsson (Grip). These events were hosted by the royal couple and were the first official festivities that Katarina attended as queen after her wedding.

Very little is known about Katarina's personality or her time as queen. No lists of her staff have been preserved, although it is believed that she brought some ladies-in-waiting from Germany, and that some daughters of members of the Swedish Council were appointed as her maids of honor, including Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). In older Swedish literature, Katarina has been described as capricious, aloof, and constantly complaining about everything Swedish. The marriage has also been described, without confirmation, as unhappy. Her tomb inscription describes her as having a "fine" head and being 160 centimeters tall. Katarina gave birth to a male heir, the son Erik, in December 1533.

During a ball held in Stockholm in September 1535 in honor of her brother-in-law, King Kristian III of Denmark, Queen Katarina reportedly fell while dancing with Kristian and was confined to bed. Complications arose, and Katarina died the day before she turned 22, along with her unborn child. Katarina was buried in Stockholm Cathedral on October 1, 1535, but was not interred until December 21, 1560, in Uppsala Cathedral next to Gustav and his other wife, Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). A memorial plaque is located in the Vasa Chapel behind the altar.

According to rumors spread by Lübeck and Rostock during the Count's Feud, Gustav Vasa supposedly murdered Katarina by hitting her on the head with a "silver hatchet," a silver walking stick, after learning from a spy that she had criticized him to the Danish king during the dance. Examinations of Katarina's skull and bones in the 1940s did not reveal any injuries that could be linked to such an event. Kristian III of Denmark himself stated in a letter how Katarina fell while dancing during his visit to Sweden.

In traditional historical writing, Katarina has often been portrayed negatively and used as a contrasting example to Gustav Vasa's other wife, Margareta Leijonhufvud, who has been equally stereotypically presented as an ideal queen.
