Full text

The Swedish National Audit Agency has reviewed the system of state subsidies for Swedish schools abroad and concludes that the system is outdated and inefficiently designed. The link between the subsidy and a clear Swedish societal interest is weaker today than when the system was introduced. The regulations allow for a large degree of interpretation, which makes it difficult to ensure a purposeful and legally sound process. At the same time, the supervisory, evaluation, and quality assurance functions of the school authorities are not functioning effectively in practice. Overall, the National Audit Agency assesses that the state's responsibility is unclear and unevenly distributed among different groups of students residing abroad. When the current system was established, the intention was to facilitate recruitment for employers who need to station personnel abroad, such as embassies, aid organizations, and multinational corporations. It was considered to be in the interest of Swedish society that these employers could send personnel to operations abroad. In the early 1990s, there were more than 30 Swedish schools abroad in Europe, Africa, Asia, and South America. Today, there are 14 schools, including 2 in Africa, 7 in Spain, and the rest in European capitals. The National Audit Agency's review shows that the majority of the parents of students who are eligible for state subsidies today are residing abroad on their own initiative, while only a smaller proportion belong to the groups that the system was originally intended for. Nearly half of these students have parents who are either employed by the school or are self-employed. Against this background, the link between the subsidy and a clear Swedish societal interest is weaker today than at the time the system was created.
