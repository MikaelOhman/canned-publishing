Erik Axel Karlfeldt, originally Eriksson, was born on July 20, 1864, in Karlbo, Folkärna parish, Kopparberg County, and died on April 8, 1931, in Engelbrekt parish, Stockholm. He was a Swedish poet and author. He was a member of the Swedish Academy from 1904 to 1931 and posthumously received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1931.

In 1913, Karlfeldt became the permanent secretary of the Swedish Academy, succeeding Carl David af Wirsén, who had died the year before. He remained in this position until his death in 1931. He passed away on April 8, 1931, after a short illness.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood ===

Karlfeldt was born at Tolvmansgården farm in Karlbo village, Folkärna parish, in southeastern Dalarna province. His father was the farmer Erik Eriksson from Jularbo, and his mother was Anna Jansdotter from Hyttbäcken, north of Karlbo; the surname Karlfeldt was adopted later. The parish priest, Åkerblom, believed that the academically gifted Erik Axel should continue his education, and in 1878 he began attending the Västerås Higher General School. At school, he was one of the best among the fifteen boys in his class, and he was a member of the school's literary society. He lived as a boarder in what was then considered the outskirts of the city, Kyrkbacken. His vacations were spent at home in Karlbo. In the summer of 1882, he traveled on foot through Dalarna province. In the fall of 1884, he met a young woman named Anna Bolling, daughter of a gymnastics instructor, and fell in love with her. They did not see each other again after his graduation, but she seems to have served as an inspiration for many of his romantic poems. Erik Eriksson's father had been forced to take on debt to keep the farm running. He began forging the signatures of relatives on promissory notes and bills of exchange. Eventually, this was discovered. In May 1885, he was arrested, and in June, he was sentenced by the district court in Sala to two years of hard labor in the Västerås county prison for forgery. At the same time, Erik Axel graduated with good grades. The shame associated with his father's crime and the fact that his childhood home was sold at auction must have deeply affected the young Erik Axel.

=== Djursholm ===

In the autumn semester of 1885, Karlfeldt began studying at Uppsala University, majoring in aesthetics. Due to a lack of funds, he could only afford the simplest room on Götgatan 7. During the summer of 1886, he worked as a tutor in Bräcke, Jämtland, and remained there until the spring of 1887, when he continued tutoring two miles away at the home of a stationmaster in Gällö. In the autumn of 1887, he returned to Uppsala. At the beginning of 1888, his studies ended because he had completely run out of money and was so desperate that he even wrote to his uncle, one of whose signatures his father had forged. His plea for money went unanswered, and he spent the entire spring of 1888 unemployed at his parents' home in Krylbo. During the summer, he worked as a tutor for the provincial physician in Krylbo.

Karlfeldt's difficult situation improved when, in the autumn of 1888, he wrote a letter to Ernst Beckman, the editor-in-chief of Aftonbladet, requesting employment as a journalist. Beckman, who had written poetry and published a collection of poems in his youth, decided to give the young student a trial run at the newspaper after reading some of his poems. As part of his employment, the young student changed his last name to Karlfeldt. Beckman decided to help the young Karlfeldt financially so that he could complete his studies. He collected a few hundred krona from his acquaintances, and in February, Karlfeldt received the money to return to his studies at Uppsala University. Karlfeldt became a regular guest at Beckman's home. Beckman's wife, Louise Woods, was American, and they also lived with Louise's mother, Sarah Woods Baker. Karlfeldt had conversations with them in English. Although Woods Baker was born in New England, she published several books in Swedish in Sweden, and it is reasonable to assume that Karlfeldt proofread her manuscripts. In Beckman's circle of acquaintances were Carl Snoilsky and Viktor Rydberg.

Some of Karlfeldt's poems had previously been published under a pseudonym, but at the beginning of 1890, he wrote to Frans von Scheele, the literary critic and editor of Svensk tidskrift. Scheele gave a positive review of the poems that Karlfeldt had sent, and after Karlfeldt contacted him again in 1891, four of his poems were published in the magazine for the first time under Karlfeldt's own name.

In May 1892, Karlfeldt earned his bachelor's degree, which included Latin, Germanic languages, Scandinavian languages, mineralogy and geology, theoretical philosophy, as well as aesthetics with literary and art history. Beckman had been forced to leave Aftonbladet but became the CEO of Djursholmsbolaget instead. The company was founded by Henrik Palme with the aim of building a garden city near Djursholms Castle outside Stockholm, and Beckman was the first person to build a house in Djursholm. In a few years at the beginning of the 1890s, a small town, Djursholms villastad (Djursholm residential area), grew up around Djursholms Castle, with streets named after popular Old Norse themes.
