Full text

Brussels, June 23, 2026

It is a pleasure to be here for this important exchange of views on the economic and financial implications for Europe of the situation in the Middle East.

Today, I will focus on the implications of the situation for economic activity and inflation in the Eurozone, as well as for our response in the form of monetary policy.

The peace agreement in the Middle East is welcome, but the situation remains fragile, with a risk of setbacks or a renewed escalation. The full consequences of the conflict for medium-term inflation and growth will depend on the intensity and duration of the energy price shock, as well as the extent of its indirect and secondary effects.

Let me begin by outlining the consequences of the energy crisis for economic activity and future prospects.

The Eurozone economy unexpectedly contracted by 0.2 percent in the first quarter of this year, primarily due to a decline in national activity in Ireland. Excluding Ireland, the Eurozone economy grew by 0.3 percent, supported by domestic demand and exports. Consumption, both in the public and private sectors, contributed positively, while investment and inventories decreased.

However, the war in the Middle East is negatively impacting activity. So far, we have seen a more pronounced decline in the service sector than in the manufacturing industry; however, the support from cautious inventory accumulation appears to be waning, as new orders stagnated in May.

The labor market remains strong. Unemployment remains close to historically low levels, and stood at 6.3 percent in April. Recent surveys suggest that some companies are retaining their employees despite deteriorating business conditions. At the same time, demand for labor has decreased further, and companies and households expect the labor market to weaken.

We anticipate that domestic demand will be weaker than previously expected in March. The war is negatively impacting confidence, and higher energy costs are reducing real incomes. Despite this, households' financial positions are generally stable, and consumption should continue to be the main driver of growth.

Higher energy costs and lower confidence will dampen private investment in the short term, although these should be supported by corporate investments in new digital technologies. In addition, increased public investment in defense and infrastructure is expected to continue to support public investment. These factors are expected to provide some buffer against the negative effects of the war.

The Eurosystem's June forecasts predict a real GDP growth of 0.8 percent in 2026, 1.2 percent in 2027, and 1.5 percent in 2028.

Now, let's move on to what this means for inflation.

Overall inflation increased to 3.2 percent in May, compared to 3.0 percent in April. Although energy prices fell compared to the previous month, base year effects meant that the annual energy inflation remained unchanged at 10.8 percent. Inflation excluding energy increased by 0.2 percentage points, to 2.4 percent. Within this category, food inflation decreased to 1.9 percent from 2.4 percent, while underlying inflation increased to 2.6 percent from 2.2 percent. Goods inflation rose by 0.1 percentage points, to 0.9 percent, and service inflation increased by 0.5 percentage points, to 3.5 percent.

Domestic cost pressures decreased during the first quarter, due to slower growth in wages and profits. Profit margins continued to shrink, indicating that profits continued to absorb some of the effects of higher wage costs. The energy crisis has not yet led to increased wages. The annual growth rate of agreed-upon wages decreased to 2.5 percent during the first quarter of 2026, compared to 2.9 percent during the fourth quarter of 2025. Total compensation per employee is expected to grow steadily at 3.2 percent during 2026, 2027, and 2028, which means an increase in real wages each year.

A number of indicators point to inflation risks in the coming months, including the purchasing managers' index for input prices, import prices, food price developments, expectations for sales prices, and some disruptions in supply chains. In addition, the energy crisis has already driven up some indicators of underlying inflation.

The expected increase in energy prices is expected to keep inflation well above the target during the first half of 2027. On average, overall inflation is expected to be 3.0 percent in 2026, 2.3 percent in 2027, and 2.0 percent in 2028. The development of overall inflation is mainly driven by the expected development of energy prices.

Inflation expectations in the short term are still significantly above the levels observed before the outbreak of the war in the Middle East. At the same time, most measures of long-term inflation expectations are around 2 percent, which supports a stabilization of inflation around the target in the medium term.

The risks to economic growth are tilted downwards, while the risks to inflation are tilted upwards. Under the unfavorable and severe scenarios, inflation would remain above the target throughout the forecast period, while growth would be lower than the baseline scenario in the short term. In the milder scenario, inflation would reach a level similar to the baseline scenario, but would fall below the target in the medium term, while growth would be slightly higher than the baseline scenario in the years 2027 and 2028.

We now move on to our response in monetary policy. The information received regarding the intensity and duration of the energy crisis, as well as the likely persistent impact on inflation, indicated that a 25 basis point interest rate increase in June was appropriate.

This interest rate increase was a well-considered decision. Firstly, as I mentioned earlier, we are already seeing the first signs that the increase in energy prices is spreading to a broader inflation. Secondly, even in the milder scenario that the Eurosystem staff analyzed before the decision in June, inflation is expected to remain above the target for a sufficiently long period to justify a moderate action. Thirdly, even though the recent progress towards a resolution of the conflict in the Middle East is welcome, uncertainty remains, and the risk persists.
