Nicodemus Tessin the Elder, born December 7, 1615, in Stralsund, now Germany, and died May 24, 1681, in Stockholm, was a Swedish architect. He was the son of Nicodemus Tessin and Maria zum Felde. He was married to Maria Persdotter Svahn and father of Nicodemus Tessin the Younger.

In 1661, Nicodemus Tessin the Elder was appointed as the first city architect (later castle architect) of Sweden and Stockholm, a position he held until his death.

== Biography == Tessin came to Sweden at a young age. According to his own account (in a document from the early 1670s), he was accepted into Swedish service in 1636 as a fortification engineer under Olof Örnehufvud (although 1639 is often cited as the year of his employment). In 1641, as a fortification engineer, he was tasked with creating maps of several cities in northern Sweden and surveying rivers and streams, working together with secretary Daniel Behm. In 1646, he became a royal "architect and builder," responsible for preparing drawings for the crown's buildings and facilities. From 1651 to 1653, he made a trip to Italy via Germany, returning through France and the Netherlands. In 1660, he was appointed to the Building Council in Stockholm as one of two "architects" (Jean de la Vallée being the other). In 1667, he became a city councilor (apparently having left his position as city architect), and in 1674 he was ennobled. In 1676, he became a royal court architect, but resigned from all his positions in 1680.

Tessin, one of the most prominent architects of his time in Sweden, had a very extensive career. Initially, he adhered to the prevailing Germanic Renaissance style, characterized by brick buildings with sandstone ornamentation, richly decorated gables, high roofs. Later, he was heavily influenced by French, Dutch, and Italian architectural styles. For Axel Oxenstierna, he oversaw the completion of Tidö Castle and Fiholms Castle around 1640 (only the two wings were completed at Fiholms), both based on designs by Jean De la Vallée.

Following his travels in southern Europe, he received numerous significant commissions. In 1654, he began renovating Borgholm Castle in a French classical style – drawings in Erik Dahlbergh's "Suecia antiqua et hodierna" depict its intended appearance; construction ceased upon the death of Charles X Gustav (1660). For Carl Gustaf Wrangel, he participated in the construction of Skokloster Castle starting in 1653 and, around the same time, in the construction of Wrangel's palace on Riddarholmen. Jean de la Vallée was also involved in these two projects; it is likely that Tessin revised his designs. Among his other palaces in Stockholm are those for Seved Bååth and Carl Sparre (demolished in 1913), both with a French character both externally and in their layout. In the countryside, he designed Ekolsund, Mälsåker, likely Stavsund, Sjö Castle, and oversaw the renovation of Jakobsdals Castle by Hedvig Eleonora, as well as the construction of Strömsholm and, most notably, Drottningholm Palace (begun in 1662, interior work began in 1665). He died during the construction of this palace; his son, Nicodemus Tessin the Younger, completed it.

Tessin created a type of castle that remained dominant during the Carolinian era: a main building with a strongly emphasized central section flanked by corner pavilions or towers, which sometimes extended into prominent wings. Even in these castles, he drew inspiration from French models. The floor plans and facade treatments reflect richness and grandeur. He frequently used risalites with continuous pilasters on high bases, delicate console cornices and roof friezes, and richly profiled roofs. Above all, he was a master of designing grand staircases and entrance halls.

For himself, Tessin built a house at Skeppsbron 20 in Stockholm (demolished in 1899; the new building by Isak Gustaf Clason incorporated both the style and details – including interior elements – from the Tessin building). In a couple of other houses on Skeppsbron (36, and likely also 40), he followed Dutch models. Later in life, he provided designs for the Southern City Hall and the National Bank at Järntorget. The former, begun around 1664, is a fairly large complex encompassing two courtyards, with a distinct French character, although it incorporates Italian elements in the loggias of the inner courtyard. The building burned down in 1680, after which Tessin the Younger developed a reconstruction proposal in a purely Italian style. The National Bank began construction in 1676 in a Roman palace style. He was certainly influenced by his son here – his influence can be traced in many aspects of the elder's later work. He proposed three different plans for redesigning Stortorget (the main square) in Stockholm, but none of the proposals were implemented.

Tessin also created significant works in the field of religious art. Kalmar Cathedral (design 1653, foundation laid 1660, completed 1699) became a magnificent Baroque building, externally resembling a Roman structure, with a plan most reminiscent of the church in Sorbonne in Paris: a central church with two of the opposing transepts slightly extended, ending in semi-circular apses and with square, small domed towers at the corners. The church has groin vaults and is quite low on the outside. In Stockholm, he designed the transept and tower of Maria Magdalena Church (1675–1676).
