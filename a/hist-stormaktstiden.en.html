The era of Swedish Great Power status is a period in Sweden's history. The name comes from the fact that during this period, Sweden acquired and maintained a position as a major power in Europe.

The era of Swedish Great Power status, whose duration is defined differently, was preceded by the earlier Vasa era (1521–1611) and followed by the Age of Liberty (1719–1772). The era of Swedish Great Power status is divided into the later Vasa period (1611–1654) and the Carolinian era (1654–1718). This epoch is said to have ended before the peace treaty with Tsarist Russia in 1721. Sometimes, following the model of the long 19th century, this period is referred to as Sweden's "long 17th century."

The period is often considered to have begun in 1611 when Gustav II Adolf ascended the Swedish throne, although many believe its beginning was much earlier, as early as 1561, when Erik XIV initiated Sweden's expansion in the Baltic Sea by sending an army to Estonia at the request of the city of Reval. The era of Swedish Great Power status formally ended with the end of the Great Northern War and the Treaty of Nystad in 1721.

During the era of Swedish Great Power status, Scania, Halland, Blekinge, Bohuslän, Gotland, Härjedalen, and Jämtland became part of Sweden. Other territories that were conquered (including Kexholm County, Ingria, Estonia, Livonia, Bremen-Verden, and Pomerania) were lost in the following centuries. The era of Swedish Great Power status was not only a period of territorial expansion but also a long period of economic growth. From the year 1600 to the year 1700, the standard of living nearly doubled, measured by GDP per capita. This era of Swedish Great Power status was followed by a longer period of economic decline. It wasn't until the 1870s that Sweden achieved the same GDP per capita as in the year 1700.

Sweden's underlying resource base, in terms of population and industry, was limited. By conquering territories from several neighboring countries, Sweden made many enemies. The era of Swedish Great Power status ended with the Great Northern War and defeat at the hands of a modernized Russia, led by Peter the Great. After Charles XII was killed in 1718, the war chest was distributed, and the large army was dissolved. This was followed by a short but intense power struggle within Sweden between different factions, and shortly thereafter, peace treaties were quickly signed with various enemy nations.

== The Foundation of Sweden's Great Power Status ==

The foundation of Sweden's great power status was laid by Karl IX's son and successor, Gustav II Adolf (1611–1632). After achieving peace in Knäred with Denmark in 1613, despite facing significant difficulties and a substantial financial burden to secure the ransom for Älvsborg, he continued the war against Russia in Ingermanland. This resulted in Kexholm County and Ingermanland, previously belonging to Russia, becoming Swedish territories in 1617. His struggle to prevent Sigismund's claims to the Swedish crown led to Poland temporarily ceding Livland and several Prussian ports crucial for trade in the Baltic Sea in 1629. Through these acquisitions from Russia and Poland, a significant step was taken towards making the Baltic Sea an "inner sea" of Sweden – a concept that wasn't unreasonable at a time when oceans, due to the nature of communication, tended to connect rather than separate countries, and when the principle of nationality had not yet become a defining factor in state formation. The Baltic Sea was extremely important for Sweden. Many of the kingdom's most important cities (including Turku/Åbo, which was one of the largest cities at the end of the 17th century) were located on the Baltic Sea. Iron ore from Dalarna and agricultural surpluses from Svealand were shipped out via Stockholm. Control over the kingdom required control over the key trading centers. It was in this context that Helsinki was founded. The Baltic Sea, as a largely Swedish "inner sea," was an important advantage that contributed to a country with a relatively small population becoming a great power.

Despite Sweden's victory over Poland, Sigismund had not abandoned his claims to the Swedish crown. Furthermore, if the Catholic reaction prevailed in the major religious war that broke out in Germany in 1618, the Thirty Years' War, he could expect support for suppressing Sweden. This was the reason for Gustav II Adolf's involvement in the Thirty Years' War. The plans, largely shrouded in mystery to later generations, regarding Sweden's future position, which Gustav II Adolf had in mind after his initial victories, were left incomplete due to his untimely death. However, thanks to those who continued the work – Axel Oxenstierna and the leading military commanders from his "war school," such as Johan Banér and Lennart Torstenson, Johan Lilliehöök, among others – Sweden acquired territories in northern Germany after the war's end. During the Thirty Years' War, Sweden received significant subsidies from France, which was interested in weakening the dominant German princely family, the Habsburgs. Furthermore, land (a large part of Pomerania, Wismar, and Bremen-Verden) also came under Swedish control through the Peace of Westphalia, which ended the major war during the reign of his successor, Kristina (1632–1654), in 1648. Sweden emerged from the war as the leader of continental Protestantism.
