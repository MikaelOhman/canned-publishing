Full text

Saint Eric, also known as Eric Jedvardsson or Saint Eric (incorrectly sometimes referred to as Eric IX), was born around 1125 and died on May 18, 1160. He is considered the King of Sweden from approximately 1156 to 1160. Very few details about him are definitively confirmed. Saint Eric is the national saint of Sweden and the patron saint of Stockholm.

Eric was married to Kristina, likely the daughter of Björn Haraldsson Järnsida and a granddaughter of Inge the Elder. He was the father of Knut Eriksson, Margareta (who died in 1209 and married King Sverre Sigurdsson of Norway in 1185), Katarina (married to the Norwegian nobleman Nils Blaka), and possibly Filip Eriksson (likely an ancestor of Knut Långe). He also had a brother whose name began with "J," possibly Joar Jedvardsson. Eric had connections to, and himself owned estates in Västergötland, which he may have acquired through his marriage to Kristina. He held the Ängsö castle in Västmanland. It is possible that Eric was a jarl (earl) in Svealand before being recognized as king there. In Götaland, he was not accepted until 1157.

== Sources ==

While no contemporary sources about Erik have been found, the earliest written mention of him appears in a letter during the reign of his son, Knut Eriksson. In the letter, Erik is referred to as "king of the Swedes."

The most detailed source is the *Erikslegenden* (Legend of Saint Eric), which was likely written around 1270 with the aim of promoting his canonization. The oldest surviving manuscript containing the standard version of the legend, the *Registrum Upsalense*, dates back to 1344 and is kept in the National Archives. It includes what is believed to be the earliest mention of Erik's alleged crusade to Finland. Historians doubt that this occurred as a formal crusade due to the lack of evidence in contemporary sources. However, it is likely that military expeditions were conducted from Sweden during this period, which eventually led to the incorporation of Proper Finland into the Swedish kingdom.

The British Museum holds an older and much shorter version of the legend in a breviary written at the end of the 13th century for the Diocese of Linköping. The standard legend includes a biography (vita) and descriptions of 50 miracles. It was originally written by Prior Israel Erlandsson of the Dominican convent in Sigtuna, who later became Bishop of Västerås, meaning it must have been completed no later than 1311 when he died. Shortly thereafter, in 1314, a St. Eric's chapel was mentioned for the first time in Uppsala.

According to a note in the Danish *Vitskølkrönikan*, Erik was king of Västergötland in 1158. Erik is also mentioned in the royal list within the laws of Västergötland, which states that he died violently.

Because so few reliable facts are known about King Erik, some researchers have come to view him as part of a complex historical legend rather than a personal history. Lauritz Weibull took the most extreme position, arguing that Erik was a case where historiography must accept a "negative result," meaning that the source material is so fragmentary that it is impossible even in broad terms to reconstruct Erik's life and reign, and that any writing about Saint Eric is fundamentally impossible. Sture Bolin, Weibull's student, took a slightly milder position, and his results, published in 1951, are largely the basis for later encyclopedias. However, even Bolin's article focuses more on the cult of sainthood and the views of previous researchers than on King Erik himself. The *National Encyclopedia* has yet to summarize all securely documented facts about King Erik in a single sentence.

The main critical problem with a saint's legend is that its purpose is not to present factual information about the person but to legitimize their status as a saint. Therefore, one cannot rely on any individual piece of information in a saint's legend, and it is certain that any information unfavorable to the person has been omitted.

Erik's role as king is unclear. He may not have been formally recognized throughout the entire kingdom. The fact that he came to play a significant role as a posthumous symbol of the Erik dynasty and as a local saint reflects more the power of his successors (descendants) than his own.

== The Historical Figure ==

=== Family === The information about his patronymic name, Jedvardsson, originates from West Norse narrative sources, including Sverre Sigurdsson's saga, and is only indirectly and weakly supported in contemporary Swedish sources through the mention of Joar Jedvardsson within the circle surrounding his son, Knut. This Joar is believed to be identical to a brother of Erik, although we are only certain that his name began with the letter J. Genealogical records of the Swedish nobility state that his father was Jedward Thordsson, known as Bonde. It has also been suggested, though on flimsy grounds, that his father might have been English, but this assumption is based on an entirely unproven argument about the unusualness of the name in 12th-century Sweden. The information about his wife, Kristina, and her lineage from King Inge the Elder comes from two independent sources: the Vitskøl Chronicle and the Knytlingasaga, and can therefore be considered relatively reliable. However, a more precise determination of their relationship cannot be established.
