In plain language

The EU has decided to change the regulations for the control of medical devices and in vitro diagnostic tests (also known as "in vitro diagnostics"). Part of these changes involves the phased introduction of a new European database called Eudamed, which will help to keep track of all products.

Companies will also be required to report if they are unable to deliver certain products or if deliveries will be completely discontinued. This is important so that healthcare providers are aware of potential shortages of equipment. To ensure this works in Sweden, the Swedish law needs to be amended to align with the EU's new regulations.

The government also proposes regulations for how authorities should inspect the products. This also means that individuals or companies seeking exemptions from certain regulations may need to pay a fee. This applies to products that are not medical devices but are used in a similar way.

The new legislative changes are planned to come into effect on May 26, 2025. The proposal is now with the Swedish Parliament for a decision.

Full text

The European Parliament and the Council have decided to amend the transitional provisions for certain medical devices used for in vitro diagnostics. Furthermore, a phased implementation of the European database for medical devices (Eudamed) is being introduced, along with a requirement to provide information in the event of delivery interruptions or cessation of supply. These changes affect Regulation (EU) 2017/745 on medical devices and Regulation (EU) 2017/746 on medical devices for in vitro diagnostics.

The amended transitional provisions and the phased implementation of Eudamed necessitate corresponding amendments to the Act (2021:600) containing supplementary provisions to the EU regulations on medical devices.

The proposal includes legislative changes regarding the supervision of products covered by the EU regulations on medical devices, including a fee for those applying for exemptions from requirements applicable to products that are not medical devices but are closely related to them in terms of use.

The proposed legislative changes are scheduled to come into effect on May 26, 2025.
