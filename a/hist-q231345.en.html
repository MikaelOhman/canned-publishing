Jenny Lind, originally Johanna Maria Lind, later Jenny Lind-Goldschmidt, born October 6, 1820, in the parish of Klara in Stockholm, and died November 2, 1887, in Wynd's Point near Little Malvern in Worcestershire, England, was a Swedish opera singer (soprano) and court singer from 1840. She held the title of Kammersängerin at the Habsburg court in Vienna.

Lind received her musical education at Adolf Fredrik Lindblad's music school in Stockholm. She toured extensively, including several European countries. In what is now Germany, she met Felix Mendelssohn, who composed an oratorio for her voice. In England, Queen Victoria attended her concerts, and the story goes that the queen gave her a necklace with a nightingale adorned with rubies and emeralds. Lind was soon contacted by P.T. Barnum, who had traveled to England to try to engage her. Initially hesitant, she accepted when the guaranteed salary was $150,000 for 150 concerts, plus a bonus based on additional sales. She arrived in New York in September 1850. The reception was fantastic. Impresario P.T. Barnum had hired thousands of people, including New York firefighters, and promoted Lind as "The Swedish Nightingale." H.C. Andersen's "The Nightingale" is believed to have been written as a tribute to Lind – Andersen was in love with her and proposed several times, but she always declined.

The first concert in the United States was held at Castle Garden, which could accommodate approximately 6,000 people. The profit from the concert was significant; Lind's share, in addition to her salary, exceeded $10,000. She donated most of it to charity. After New York, she was scheduled to travel to Boston a few days later. The organizer, P.T. Barnum, auctioned off tickets for the following concert in Boston. The most expensive ticket for this concert was bid up to $625, equivalent to several years' salaries. P.T. Barnum then arranged 150 concerts, but Lind did not participate in all of them. A relatively unknown part of her career was that she traveled to Cuba. She first gave a concert there that was booed by the audience, but she performed another concert that was successful.

Lind married pianist Otto Goldschmidt in the United States in 1852. The couple later moved to England, and she died in Worcestershire, England, on November 2, 1887. Her memory is preserved through a plaque in Poets' Corner at Westminster Abbey.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years === Lind was born in the parish of Klara in Stockholm, as an illegitimate daughter to the bookkeeper Niclas Jonas Lind (1798–1858) and the teacher Anne-Marie Fellborg (1793–1856). During her early years, she lived with a clockmaker's family at Klockargården near Eds church in Uppsala County. Her father has been described as a "kind but irresponsible" man who was unable to support himself or others, so it was Jenny Lind's mother who held the family together. She earned a living by tutoring young girls, but the difficult circumstances had made her bitter and irritable. The mother was divorced but, for religious reasons, refrained from remarrying until her former husband died in 1834. Jenny Lind was then fourteen years old.

When Lind was nine years old, a maidservant working for Fredrika Lundberg, a prima ballerina at the Royal Opera, happened to hear her singing. Impressed by the girl's voice, the servant returned the next day with Lundberg. They arranged an audition and helped her gain a place at the Dramaten acting school, where she early on performed in several speaking roles. The theater's singing teacher, Carl Magnus Crælius, noticed her vocal talent. According to the terms of the contract, she was provided "with food, clothing, and lodging at the theater's expense, along with free instruction in singing, declamation, dance, and all the knowledge necessary for a well-educated woman and essential for an acting career."

Lind was barely more than ten years old when she gained attention in the press for a dancing role. The newspaper Dagligt Allehanda wrote: "The little Jenny Lind performs excellently – we might almost say too well. Such boldness and confidence in theatrical performance from a child, such complete freedom from embarrassment in a young girl performing in front of an audience of 1,200 people, is an exception to the natural order."

=== Breakthroughs === Lind received singing lessons from Isaak Berg. She also studied drama with Karolina Bock, and in addition to her singing, she gained recognition for her acting abilities. She possessed an unusually good ear. In 1837, she sang in Lindblad's "Frondörerna." On March 7, 1838, she made her debut in a leading role as Agathe in "The Merry Huntsman," and achieved great success. After this, she celebrated March 7th as her second birthday. She also had successful performances as Euryanthe, Pamina, Alice, and Lucia, and during summer concert tours, where she often stayed in Uppsala with the students and Erik Gustaf Geijer, she sang folk songs.
