Christopher Polhem (until his ennoblement in 1716, Polhammar), born on December 18, 1661, in the parish of Tingstäde on Gotland Island, and died on August 30, 1751, in Stockholm, was a Swedish inventor, industrialist, and physicist, often called "the father of Swedish mechanics." He worked in various technical fields and made significant contributions to the industrial development of Sweden during the 17th and 18th centuries.

Among Polhem's most famous inventions are the Polhem lock and the Polhem knot, as well as several improvements to mining techniques, water wheels (sometimes called Polhem wheels), other mining machinery, locks, and textile machines. He founded Laboratorium mechanicum, Sweden's first engineering school, which later evolved into the Royal Institute of Technology. He was ennobled in 1716 and elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences.

Polhem collaborated with several Swedish kings, including Karl XI and Karl XII, and was involved in projects ranging from mining and water management to military equipment and agricultural machinery. His life and work left a significant technological legacy that has continued to influence Swedish engineering long after his death.

== Biography ==

=== Background and Uppsala === Christopher Polhem was the son of Christina Eriksdotter Schening from Vadstena and Wulf Christopher Polhammar, a German merchant who had immigrated to Visby. He had at least two brothers, Johan and Jonas. According to family tradition, he descended from the Kingdom of Hungary, possibly originating from an Austrian noble family that early on intermarried with the Hungarian nobility. Countess Bora Szent-Györgyi was married to Farkas Polhem in the 14th century. Due to religious persecution by the Catholic Austrians, his father, who was a Hungarian Protestant, is said to have fled to Swedish Pomerania. During the journey, his father reportedly suffered a shipwreck off the coast of Gotland. After this, he settled in Visby and became "a rather prominent merchant with many ships at sea," as Christopher Polhem wrote in his autobiography. When Polhem was seven years old, his father died. His mother soon remarried to Jöran Silcker. Polhem attended school in Visby because the family could not afford private tutoring, and in 1671 he was forced to interrupt his studies. He then moved to his uncle Hans Adam Polhammar in Stockholm. When his uncle also passed away in 1673, he was taken in by Mattias Biörenklous widow, Margareta Wallenstedt, and worked as a clerk and bailiff on her estates, Vansta Manor in Ösmo and Kungshamns Farm. At the age of sixteen, Polhem established a workshop at Vansta, where he began to carry out various mechanical works. He received help with Latin studies from the local priests, in exchange for assistance with repairs and construction of clock mechanisms. In 1685, he left Margareta Wallenstedt's service and instead found employment at Fållnäs in the parish of Sorunda. The pastor of Sorunda, Erland Dryselius, helped him with his studies, and in 1687 he had the opportunity to go to Uppsala to help repair the medieval tower clock of the cathedral. By demonstrating his skills to Anders Spole, he was subsequently enrolled at the university. There, he studied physics, mathematics, and mechanics, and possibly also humanities. In practice, however, he spent most of 1691 repairing the tower clock and carrying out other practical mechanical tasks.

=== Inventions in the Bergslagen Region ===

Thanks to his work on the town clock, which he not only managed to repair but also improve, he was introduced at the Mining College in 1690. There, he designed a new mining machine that used water power to transport ore from the mine shafts and up to the surface. This invention was demonstrated to King Charles XI, who decided in 1691 to grant Polhem a lifetime pension of 500 silver coins. At the state's expense, he made a study trip to the mines in the Bergslagen region that same year and also designed a horse-powered pump machine for the Hällestads mine. However, this project ultimately failed. From 1692 to 1694, Polhem created a hoisting mechanism at Stora Stöten near the Falun copper mine, based on plans he had developed as early as 1690. A hoisting mechanism, or mining hoist, is a machine that transports mining materials from a lower level to a higher one. From 1694 to 1696, he traveled through England, Holland, France, and Germany to study technology – again funded by state grants. Upon his return, he founded the first school for engineers in Sweden, Laboratorium mechanicum in Stockholm, in 1697. For teaching purposes, he created a collection of models illustrating mechanical principles, known as Polhem's Mechanical Alphabet. In 1798, the school was transformed into an engineering school, called the Mekaniska skolan (Mechanical School). Following a proposal from the Riksdag (Swedish Parliament) in 1823 and a decision by the Royal Majesty in 1825, it was converted into the Teknologiska Institutet (Technological Institute), which is now known as the Kungl. Tekniska Högskolan (Royal Technical University). In 1699, he founded the Stjärnsund manufacturing plant in Dalarna, where they produced machines and pendulum clocks, among other things.

=== More Inventions === Polhem became famous for his "stånggångar" (pole roads). A "stånggång" – also called a "konstgång" – is a mechanical device for transmitting power.
