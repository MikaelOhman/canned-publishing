Gustaf V (full name Oscar Gustaf Adolf), born June 16, 1858, at Drottningholm Palace in the parish of Lovö, Stockholm County, and died October 29, 1950, at Drottningholm Palace, was King of Sweden from 1907, serving as the fifth monarch of the House of Bernadotte. He was the son of Oscar II and Queen Sofia, great-grandson of Charles XIV John, and great-great-grandfather to the current King of Sweden, Carl XVI Gustaf.

With a reign of nearly 43 years, Gustaf V is the third longest-reigning monarch in Swedish history, second only to Carl XVI Gustaf and Magnus Eriksson, and was also the country's oldest reigning monarch ever. Gustaf V served as Sweden's head of state during both World Wars, during which he made minor diplomatic efforts. He was also the first Swedish king to forgo a coronation ceremony, a tradition that had been observed in Sweden since 1210.

Among Gustaf V's personal interests were music, crafts, and sports. As a tennis player, he was known by the pseudonym "Mr. G."

== Early Life ==

Shortly after ten o'clock in the morning on June 16, 1858, the prince was born at Drottningholm Palace. Princess Sofia was assisted by royal physician Fritz Wästfelt. He was presented to his grandfather, Oscar I, who was now so ill that he was bedridden, on a silver tray. Two weeks later, Oscar decorated his grandson with all the orders, including the Order of St. Olav. Gustaf was baptized in the Chapel at Stockholm Palace on July 12th by Archbishop Henrik Reuterdahl and given the names Oscar Gustaf Adolf, receiving the title Duke of Värmland. The prince was carried to the baptism by his great-grandmother, Dowager Queen Desideria. As with all Swedish royal baptisms, the baptismal font by Jean-François Cousinet was used, along with the cradle that Frederick III of Holstein-Gottorp had donated for the christening of Charles XI.

The following year, his brother, Prince Oscar, was born in 1861, Prince Carl, and in 1865, Prince Eugen. The three oldest princes began at Beskowska School in October 1869. The family resided in the Crown Prince's Palace, and the mother played a prominent role in the prince's upbringing. Summers were spent at Sofiero Palace, which his father had acquired. During his early years, the prince was considered to have a weak constitution, so he underwent electrical treatment on May 10, 1871. As an adult, he became tall and slender.

== The Years as Crown Prince ==

On September 18, 1872, King Karl XV died without leaving any male heirs. His brother, Oscar Fredrik, then became king under the name Oscar II. As Oscar's eldest son, the fourteen-year-old Gustaf simultaneously became Crown Prince of both Sweden and Norway. The family moved into Stockholm Palace. In connection with this move, Gustaf's schooling at Beskovska ended, and the Crown Prince began his education as heir to the throne at the palace. In 1874, Crown Prince Gustaf, traveling incognito under the name Gustaf Fredriksson, made his first trip abroad with his tutor, Lundeberg, visiting Switzerland and Italy. Crown Prince Gustaf was confirmed on April 24th, and in August 1875, at the age of seventeen, he became a second lieutenant in Svea Life Guards, Livregementets Dragon Corps, Värmlands Regiment, the Norwegian Jaeger Corps, and the Cavalry Brigade. He was declared an adult at the age of eighteen on June 16, 1876, passed his final exams at the palace on December 16, 1876, and enrolled at Uppsala University in 1877 at Värmlands nation (student society). That same year, he served as regent for the first time during his father's trip to Germany. At the university, he studied Latin, economics, Nordic history, art history, civil procedure, and Swedish constitutional law. On January 10, 1877, he was elected as the first honorary member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. In addition to his university studies, he underwent military training with units in Sweden and Norway, as well as at the Military College. He became a lieutenant in May 1877, a major general in 1884, and a general in 1898.

On October 13, 1879, Crown Prince Gustaf returned from a year-long trip abroad, where he first visited the Danish royal family, then spent six weeks in France, followed by four months in Rome and Italy. The journey home passed through Constantinople; afterwards, Austria and England were visited. In the summer of 1879, the Crown Prince received an honorary doctorate in civil law from Oxford University on June 18th and subsequently stayed in England and Scotland for nearly four months. The trip continued to Belgium, Holland, and Germany. This journey was pivotal in his life because it was in Germany that he first met his future wife, Victoria of Baden, whom he proposed to on March 2, 1881. The engagement was announced on March 14th of the same year.

On September 20, 1881, he married Victoria in the palace church in Karlsruhe. They then traveled by the frigate Vanadis to Gothenburg and onward to Stockholm. The Crown Prince couple had three sons: Gustaf Adolf (1882–1973), Duke of Skåne, who later became King; Wilhelm (1884–1965), Duke of Södermanland; and Erik (1889–1918), Duke of Västmanland. As a family man, the Crown Prince was a "gentleman of the home," but later in life, as king, he also stripped three grandsons and one nephew of their princely titles due to their marriages. From 1890 for a year, the Crown Prince couple were abroad visiting Egypt. In 1898, Crown Princess Victoria fell ill.
