Expanded possibilities to impose a business ban due to crime

TL;DRThe regulations regarding business bans are being expanded to include individuals involved in organized or systematic criminal activity, even if they were not operating a business at the time the crime was committed. The duration of business bans is being extended from a maximum of 10 years to a maximum of 15 years, and the enforcement agency (Kronofogden) will be given better tools for oversight.
In plain language

A business ban prohibits a person from running a business. Currently, a connection to a business is usually required for the ban to apply.

The government proposes expanding the rules so that a business ban can be imposed on anyone who has committed organized or systematic crime, even if there is no connection to a business at the time of the crime – provided it is justified from a general perspective.

The maximum duration of a business ban is being extended from 10 to 15 years. The Swedish Enforcement Agency will also gain better opportunities to monitor compliance with imposed business bans.

Full text

The proposal suggests expanding the regulations regarding business bans due to criminal activity to include individuals who have committed organized or systematic crime, regardless of whether the person was engaged in or had a connection to a business at the time of the offense, if a business ban is deemed necessary from a general perspective. In cases of organized or systematic crime, a business ban should be considered necessary from a general perspective if the minimum prescribed penalty for the crime is imprisonment for six months, unless there are specific reasons against it. The proposal also suggests extending the duration for which a business ban can be imposed, from a maximum of ten years to a maximum of fifteen years.

To improve the efficiency of the Swedish Enforcement Agency's (Kronofogdemyndigheten) supervision of imposed business bans, the proposal suggests that the agency should be authorized to allow individuals who have been issued a business ban and are required to attend a personal meeting to do so via video conference.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on January 1, 2026.
