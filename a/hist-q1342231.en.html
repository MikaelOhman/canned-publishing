Full text

Ragnvald Knaphövde, also known as Ragvald, was a king of Sweden sometime between the mid-1120s and the late 1130s. His parentage is uncertain. According to one later source, he was the son of Olof Näskonung, but due to his name, he has also been suggested to be identical to Ragnvald Ingesson, the son of Inge the Elder.

Following the death of Inge the Younger in the early 1120s, a turbulent period began in Sweden. The available sources for this period are scarce. According to the Westrogothic Law's royal list, Ragnvald was elected king, but he was killed at "Karllsepitt" (likely Karleby near Falköping) after entering Västergötland without offering hostages.

Saxo Grammaticus does not mention Ragnvald, but reports that the Swedes, after the Goths had chosen the Danish prince Magnus Nilsson as king, elected their own king who was later killed by the Goths. However, Magnus does not appear in the Westrogothic Law's royal list, but Saxo's unnamed king has been identified with Ragnvald. Furthermore, Magnus' death in 1134, along with King Philip's death in 1118, have been used to date Ragnvald's reign.

The epithet "knaphövde" or "knapphövde," which is a later addition to the Westrogothic Law's royal list, may come from the fact that Ragnvald was said to have had a round head. The word appears elsewhere and refers to a drinking vessel of the size of a man's head.

The burial chamber over Ragnvald in Vreta Abbey Church, which dates back to the time of John III, was initially believed to be a result of confusion with a brother of Magnus Henriksson, who is known to have donated to the abbey. Later, other information about those buried in the church has been presented.

According to Laurentius Böker (died 1696), Ragnvald was buried in the stone sarcophagus known as King Ring's grave in Gothenburg, which has since been dated to 2400–1800 BC (see also King Ring).
