In plain language

The Swedish Tax Agency, along with several other government agencies, has produced a report on how the population is registered in Sweden. The report shows that most people have accurate information recorded, but approximately two to three percent of individuals are incorrectly registered. This means that the information about where people live and who they are is not always correct.

When someone is misregistered, it can lead to serious problems. This could involve people receiving incorrect payments or paying too much in taxes. Furthermore, there's a risk that fraudsters use false identities to commit crimes. It has become more difficult to know where people live because more people move frequently and travel between different countries.

The report also indicates that many people are missing a personal identification number or coordination number in the systems. When individuals are not included in the official records, it becomes difficult for government agencies to plan effectively and make informed decisions. To address this issue, government agencies need to collaborate more closely, implement better controls, and utilize more modern technical systems.

This indicates that the population registry maintains a consistently high quality, with most of the information being accurate. At the same time, it is estimated that two to three percent are incorrectly registered.

The report was prepared by the Swedish Tax Agency in collaboration with several other government agencies and provides a comprehensive overview of the population at the turn of the year 2025–2026.

Despite the high quality, approximately two to three percent of the information is estimated to be inaccurate. This poses risks both for individuals and for society, especially when this information is used in automated processes.

"Although most people have correct information registered, even a relatively small percentage of errors can have very significant consequences," says Anders Klaar, project manager for the work at the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket).

Errors in the population registry can lead to, for example, incorrect payments, wrongful taxation, and identity theft.

One important reason for these errors is that it has become more difficult to determine where people actually live.

More and more people are moving between different homes and countries without establishing a permanent residence.

At the same time, an estimated tens of thousands of people are registered in the population registry even though they have left the country, while others who live here lack registration.

The current situation also shows that the number of people with personal identification numbers (samordningsnummer) is high. At the same time, many people in Sweden completely lack any form of identification within the systems.

"When people are outside these systems, it affects the ability to control, plan, and make legally sound decisions. Society needs better knowledge about this group. One way to make parts of this group more visible is if they were assigned personal identification numbers more frequently," says Anders Klaar.

There is also a need for clearer information about which individuals have the right to reside and work in Sweden.

The report shows that errors are sometimes deliberately exploited for financial gain and in organized crime. Incorrect identities are highlighted as one of the biggest risks.

"The Swedish Tax Agency has increased its controls and is developing its technical support, but the assessment is that more needs to be done. To reduce these risks, more control, better information sharing, and improved systems are needed," concludes Anders Klaar.

The report was prepared by the Swedish Tax Agency in collaboration with the Social Insurance Agency, the Migration Agency, the Pensions Agency, the National Police Board, the Payments Agency, the Swedish National Board of Health and Welfare, and Statistics Sweden. Several municipalities and regions across the country also participated, sharing their knowledge about individuals residing in Sweden and the consequences that inaccuracies in population records have on their operations.
