Ernst Alfred Cassirer, born July 28, 1874, in Breslau, then Germany, and died April 13, 1945, in New York, was a German philosopher who also worked in Sweden. He operated within the Marburg Neo-Kantian tradition and developed a philosophy of culture based on a theory of symbols rooted in knowledge phenomenology and the history of science.

== Biography == Cassirer studied philosophy and literature at the University of Berlin, but as a Jew, he faced difficulties advancing his career. He earned his doctorate in 1899 under Hermann Cohen and Paul Natorp. After many years as a lecturer, he was appointed professor of philosophy at the newly founded University of Hamburg in 1919, where he worked until 1933, when the Nazis came to power. He was then forced to leave Germany. He first held a position at Oxford University and in 1935, he was appointed professor at the University of Gothenburg. In the same year, he was also awarded an honorary doctorate there. However, in 1941, he felt that Sweden had become too unsafe for him, and he moved to the United States, where he became a lecturer at Yale University, and from 1943 at Columbia University in New York, where he worked until his death. He died in 1945 on the university campus as a Swedish citizen. He was the grandfather of the Swedish linguist Peter Cassirer (1933-2020).

== Philosophy == Cassirer's magnum opus is "Philosophy of Symbolic Forms," volumes 1-4 (1923-1929), which is considered a foundational work in cultural philosophy. According to Cassirer, humans are "symbolic animals." While animals live according to their instincts, humans have created symbolic universes with structural meanings that constitute their understanding of reality. Therefore, Cassirer argues that philosophy is a philosophy of symbols, seeking to demonstrate the validity of these symbols in other fields of knowledge and, in doing so, to define culture. In this reasoning, Cassirer relies on Kant's transcendental idealism.

In his final major work, "The Myth of the State," published posthumously in 1946, he discusses the historical foundations of the totalitarian state, which he believes can be found in thinkers such as Plato, Dante, Machiavelli, and Hegel. He argues that the foundation of fascism is symbolized by the myth of the future and the promotion of irrationality.

In his early works, Cassirer focused on Swedish cultural philosophical questions, primarily concerning Axel Hägerström, Queen Christina, and Descartes.

Among Cassirer's most prominent followers are Hans Reichenbach and Leo Strauss.
