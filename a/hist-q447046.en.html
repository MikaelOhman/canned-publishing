Engelbrekt Engelbrektsson, likely born in the 1390s but of unknown origin, died on May 4, 1436, at what is now Engelbrektsholmen in the parish of Stora Mellösa, Närke. He was a Swedish miner and nobleman, and served as "rikshövitsman" (a high-ranking official) from 1435. He led the Engelbrekt Rebellion in 1434 against Eric of Pomerania.

== Biography == Engelbrekt Engelbrektsson belonged to a family originally of German origin, now known as the Engelbrektsätten (Engelbrekt lineage), who immigrated to Sweden no later than the 1360s. His father (Engelbrekt Englikoson) is mentioned as a member of the nobility in 1392 and was residing in Norberg in 1367. It is believed that his grandfather established what is now Ängelsberg, previously called Englikobenning. Engelbrekt's coat of arms featured a triangle formed by three half-lilies, while his father and brother Nils bore slightly different coats of arms with lilies. Engelbrekt was a knight. Vilhelm Moberg suggests that Dalarna and Västmanland have had their own "Engelbrekt rebellions" regarding his place of birth.

The administrative skills and military abilities that Engelbrekt demonstrated suggest that he gained experience as a professional soldier, likely during Erik of Pomerania's wars in Schleswig. In 1426, Engelbrekt appears in historical records, and less than a decade later, he became the leader of the uprising that would later be known as the Engelbrekt Rebellion. The rebellion took place against the backdrop of the Kalmar Union under the rule of Erik of Pomerania. In later historical accounts, Engelbrekt was pitted against the king's bailiff in Västerås, Jösse Eriksson, who was blamed for the suffering endured by the people of the Bergslagen region during his administration. Engelbrekt was tasked with presenting the grievances to the king in Copenhagen, but he eventually realized that a peaceful resolution was impossible without conflict. In 1433, the men from Dalarna appointed Engelbrekt as their leader, and later that same year, Engelbrekt led an army towards Västerås. The rebellion subsided after several council members appealed for peace, and the entire sequence of events repeated itself during the spring of the following year.

During the summer of 1434, Engelbrekt and those who supported him seemed to have lost hope in a peaceful path to improvement. By midsummer, a full-fledged rebellion began with the capture and burning of the Borganäs fortress. This event is often referred to as the "liberation war" in later historical writings, but today it is more commonly known as the Engelbrekt Rebellion. In just three months, Engelbrekt gained control of virtually all of Sweden. Members of the Swedish Council joined the rebellion, and King Erik was declared deposed.

=== The Assembly of Arboga === The Assembly of Arboga, also known as the Arboga Meeting, took place in January 1435 and was previously considered by nationalist historians to be the first national assembly held in Sweden. This claim was based on the incorrect assumption that commoners (farmers) were represented at the meeting. However, later research by Gottfrid Carlsson, Erik Lönnroth, and Herman Schück has clearly shown that this assumption was flawed and stemmed from a combination of wishful thinking and speculation about potential scribal errors in some versions of the Engelbrekt Chronicle.

The Assembly of Arboga was actually an initial gathering aimed at resolving the chaos caused by Engelbrekt's rebellion. A number of bishops, knights, and nobles, totaling 30-40 people, convened in Arboga to establish a new national council that could assume governing power. This meeting was part of a series of similar gatherings where Engelbrekt played a dominant role, starting with the one in Vadstena on August 16, 1434, where he more or less forced the participants to join the rebellion. The meeting in Arboga was followed by another in Uppsala in June 1435 and a second meeting in Arboga in January 1436.

Engelbrekt's political dominance at these meetings was first challenged during the election of the national marshal in Stockholm in 1436, when Marshal Karl Knutsson (Bonde) was almost unanimously chosen. However, Engelbrekt and Erik Puke used force to impose a shared marshalship. This arrangement proved short-lived, as Engelbrekt was soon murdered after leading a military campaign through southern Sweden and the then-Danish provinces of Blekinge, Skåne, and Halland.

=== The Murder of Engelbrekt === It is said that Engelbrekt was too weak to sit upright on a horse, but when he received an invitation to a council meeting, he immediately set off. The journey was supposed to be by sea from Örebro Castle, which Engelbrekt held as a fiefdom, to Stockholm. However, during a stop at an island in Lake Hjälmaren, now called Engelbrektsholmen, Engelbrekt was murdered on May 4th by Måns Bengtsson (Natt och Dag). The murderer's father, Bengt Stensson (Natt och Dag), allegedly burned a Lübeck ship despite a peace treaty being in place with the Hanseatic League. Engelbrekt had sentenced him for this, but just days before the murder, a reconciliation had been reached.

Regarding the exact date of Engelbrekt's murder, for a long time people relied on the information provided by the Engelbrekt Chronicle, which stated April 27th. However, other sources indicated a different date, May 4th. Prominent medieval scholars, such as Gottfrid Carlsson and Lars-Olof Larsson, have supported this later date, making it the generally accepted one today.

== Legacy == Engelbrekt was buried in Örebro Church and, during the following decades, became the subject of a widespread cult of veneration.
