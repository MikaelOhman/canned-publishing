Full text

The Middle Ages is a traditional term for a period in the history of Western civilization that lies between antiquity and the modern era. The Middle Ages are generally considered to be separated from antiquity primarily by the fall of the Roman Empire in the West in 476, the decisive victory of Christianity, and the major migrations of peoples. Later dates are sometimes used for the outer regions of Europe. The boundary between the Middle Ages and the modern era is also somewhat flexible. For Europe as a whole, 1500 is often considered to be the end of the Middle Ages. Events such as the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453, Christopher Columbus's first voyage to America in 1492, or the Protestant Reformation in 1517 are sometimes used as markers. While during the Renaissance and Enlightenment, and for a long time during the modern era, the Middle Ages were viewed as a dark and barbaric period, today's historians see this era as the foundational period for modern Europe.

The Middle Ages is divided into three main periods:

The Early Middle Ages or Dark Ages, a period of strong decentralization and slow growth, in Western Europe from approximately 450 to 1000. The High Middle Ages, characterized by strong economic growth and population increase, stabilization and centralization of power, lasted from around 1000 to 1350. The Late Middle Ages, a period when growth abruptly ended and a period of demographic stagnation and increased social unrest began, but also saw the consolidation of central powers that would continue into the early modern period, from approximately 1350 to 1500. In Nordic history, the Early Middle Ages are typically placed from around 1050 to 1200, the High Middle Ages from about 1200 to 1350, and the Late Middle Ages from approximately 1350 to 1520 (the Scandinavian Late Middle Ages). The latter two periods are sometimes combined into a single "Late Middle Ages," followed by the Vasa era in Swedish history. A defining characteristic of both the European High and Late Middle Ages is the social system later known as feudalism. This system involved personal bonds between vassals and lords, creating rights and obligations for both parties. A key difference that distinguishes the Middle Ages from antiquity is the technological development, particularly in agriculture, which occurred starting in the 8th century. Another defining feature is the spread of Christianity throughout Europe, which served as a unifying link between different regions. It was during the Late Middle Ages that Europe emerged as a relatively unified civilization, and the foundations were laid for many modern nation-states such as France, Spain, and the Nordic countries. The Middle Ages was also the period when strong central powers re-emerged in Europe. Many of today's major European cities grew during the Middle Ages, along with a powerful merchant class. Guilds and corporations were formed primarily during the Late Middle Ages and continued well into modern times. At the end of the Middle Ages, a significant European expansion and colonization of America, Africa, and Asia began. This was preceded by an earlier attempt at conquest within and outside Europe in the form of crusades against the Byzantine Empire, Muslim states on the Iberian Peninsula, Palestine, and pagans in Eastern Europe, the Baltic region, and Finland.

== Historiography == The concept of "the Middle Ages" originates from a division of history based on the idealization of antiquity. In the mid-14th century, Italian writers and scholars began to develop an increasing interest in the classical period. Francesco Petrarca (1304–74) was the first to formulate the idea that his own time represented a revival of forgotten ancient ideals, and he considered the period from the fall of the Western Roman Empire to the mid-14th century as a time of stagnation and intellectual darkness. Petrarca himself referred to this era as "tenebrae" (darkness), and in the early 15th century, the historian Flavius Blondus was the first to describe the period as an intermediary phase, a historical transition from the decline of the brilliant intellectual world of antiquity to its revival almost 1000 years later. The first historian to definitively establish the concept in early modern historical writing was the French historian Charles Du Cange, who published the two-volume work *Glossarium* in 1678, covering the classical and medieval history of Latin. Ten years later, *Historia Medii Aevi* ("History of the Middle Ages") was published by the German historian Kristoffer Cellarius. During the early modern period (approximately 1500–1800), the Middle Ages were viewed as "the Dark Ages" because it was believed that ancient culture completely disappeared during this time, only to be revived with the Renaissance's revival of ancient ideals. From the Enlightenment onwards, many thinkers criticized the absolute and intolerant authority of the Catholic Church during the Middle Ages. This view largely persisted until the early 19th century, when Gothic art, for example, became fashionable again. Many Romantic artists, however, had a positive image of at least the High and Late Middle Ages, partly through interpretations of epics and legends. Authors such as Walter Scott and Victor Hugo used medieval themes in their novels, and later, nationalist romantic artists like Richard Wagner drew inspiration from the early Middle Ages.

How certain are we about this?

With the Middle Ages came an increase in written sources (letters, laws, and church documents), which is well-documented. However, these sources are unevenly preserved, and they were most often written by the Church and those in power, which biases our understanding of what we know and what remains unknown.
