John (Johan) Ericsson, born July 31, 1803, in Långbanshyttan, Värmland, and died on March 8, 1889, in New York. He is buried in Filipstad. He was a Swedish inventor.

John Ericsson was the son of Olof Ericsson and Sophia Yngström, and the younger brother of the engineer Nils Ericson. As an innovator and engineer, John Ericsson achieved worldwide success. Some of his most famous inventions included the "sun engine," a steam engine powered by solar energy; the steam-powered fire pump, which was horse-drawn; and the hot air engine, a heat engine that used heated air as the heating medium.

He is best known as one of the inventors of the first functional propeller and as the designer of the ironclad USS Monitor, an American warship that helped the Union win the American Civil War in the 1860s. His most profitable invention was the hot air engine, also called the "fire engine" or "caloric engine." It was a machine that operated on the same principles as a steam engine, but used air instead of water vapor as the medium. He received a state funeral.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life ===

From a young age, he showed an aptitude for mechanical work: drawing and building small models of machines was a favorite pastime. When he was nine years old, he built a small sawmill using only a knife and a drill, powered by water. He had fitted a clock spring into a square wooden frame, transforming it into a saw blade with the help of a file borrowed from a blacksmith in the neighborhood. The axle was made from a broken tin spoon. Otherwise, the entire machine was made of wood. The following year, he completed a model of a water pump for mines. He used wind power as the driving force, similar to a windmill. Very early on, he also began creating maps and drawings of machines.

In 1810, Ericsson's father took up employment as the supervisor of blasting operations at the Göta Canal, with the main station located at Forsvik. There, his sons, Nils and John, caught the attention of Baltzar von Platen. The boys were appointed cadets in the Navy's mechanical corps and students in the canal construction project. At the age of fourteen, John Ericsson was already a surveyor working independently. In his free time, he made numerous drawings of the canal and the tools and equipment used in the construction work. Even at a young age, he demonstrated inventions that required a thorough understanding of several fundamental principles of mathematics and natural sciences.

In 1821, John Ericsson became a lieutenant in the Jämtland Field Ranger Corps in Frösön. Soon after, he was promoted to lieutenant. The young officer was assigned to assist with surveying and mapping northern Sweden. During this time, he also wrote a comprehensive book about canals, which was never published. He also designed a heat engine (steam machine) that directly used the combustion products from the firebox, which was fueled by wood. Ericsson requested a year of leave and moved to England in 1826 to better utilize his invention. However, the heat engine proved unusable with the coal that was the dominant fuel source in England. This caused him to exceed his leave period. Crown Prince Oscar intervened and arranged for Ericsson to be appointed captain in the Jämtland Field Ranger Corps in 1827.

Soon after, he became a partner in Braithwaite's machine shop and showcased other important inventions that were successful. During his stay in England, he obtained a patent for a steam boiler with tubes, in which the draft was achieved using a fan. He used this invention to design a steam carriage. This made it possible to increase speed far beyond what had previously been considered possible. In an October 1829 competition between various steam carriages organized by the Liverpool-Manchester Railway Company, Ericsson's steam carriage, "the Novelty," was the fastest for several days. However, on the last day of the competition, the steam boiler broke down. The prize went to the English engineer George Stephenson. Ericsson then used his new steam boiler for another invention: the steam fire extinguisher, which significantly improved firefighting methods. During a major fire in London, John Ericsson had the opportunity to demonstrate to onlookers how destruction could be stopped by harnessing the power of the very element that caused it. Furthermore, he invented the surface condenser, the deep-sea anchor, a connecting rod mechanism for reversing the slide in steam engines, the hot air engine, and the propeller in England.

=== The Propeller ===

Of these inventions, a significant improvement to the propeller was particularly important. Propellers had begun to be tested as early as the 1810s, but they hadn't gained widespread use. His experiments with two counter-rotating propellers inspired many other inventors. On July 13, 1836, Ericsson received a patent for his screw propeller in England (Patent A.D. 1836 No. 7149). Ericsson built a small steamship powered by his propeller. However, it did not generate interest in England. A propeller designed by Ericsson was used on the two-masted schooner Robert F. Stockton, which sailed in 1839.
