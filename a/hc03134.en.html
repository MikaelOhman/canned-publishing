Measures against abuse of the payment system

TL;DRThe government wants to change the laws to prevent criminals from using bank transfers to commit crimes. The new regulations will make it easier for banks and the police to detect and stop money laundering.
In plain language

The government has proposed measures to prevent criminal networks from exploiting payment systems. Currently, banks are required to monitor their customers and report any suspicious activity to the police. The proposal now extends this responsibility to clearing companies, which are the businesses that transfer money between banks, requiring them to also monitor payments and report suspected criminal activity to the police.

There are approximately 1,200 agents in Sweden who assist foreign companies with payments. Some of these agents are used to transfer money between countries, which can be exploited by criminals. To reduce this risk, the government wants the Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen), the agency responsible for ensuring that companies comply with regulations, to conduct more thorough checks on these agents. The Financial Supervisory Authority should also be able to investigate whether the individuals behind the agents have a criminal record.

The new regulations involve amending several laws that govern how money and payments are handled in Sweden. The purpose is to combat both money laundering and the financing of terrorism. If the proposal is approved and the Parliament votes in favor, the new laws will come into effect on July 1, 2025.

Full text

In the proposal, the government presents suggestions to counter the misuse of payment systems by criminal networks.

According to the anti-money laundering law, businesses, such as banks, are required to monitor their customers and report suspected money laundering to the police. To combat money laundering and the financing of terrorism, and consequently, criminal networks, it is important that banks and the police have access to information about suspicious payments. The proposal suggests that clearing companies, i.e., the companies that facilitate payments between banks, should be obligated to monitor transactions, provide information about suspicious transactions to banks, and report suspected money laundering or the financing of terrorism to the police.

In Sweden, there are approximately 1,200 representatives for foreign payment service providers. The activities of these representatives, such as money transfers within the framework of alternative payment systems (so-called "hawala" activities), can be exploited by criminal networks. Providing payment services in Sweden through representatives requires that the supervisory authority in the home country registers the representative. The proposed changes clarify that the Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen) should conduct a parallel assessment of the activities of these representatives in Sweden, including a suitability assessment. The Financial Supervisory Authority will then have access to information in the suspicion and criminal record registers.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2025.

Proposed resolutions for the Parliament

The government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposed amendment to the Act (2007:528) on the securities market.
  2. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposed amendment to the Act (2010:751) on payment services.
  3. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposed amendment to the Act (2011:755) on electronic money.
  4. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposed amendment to the Act (2017:630) on measures to counter money laundering and the financing of terrorism.
  5. The Parliament shall approve the government's proposed amendment to the Act (2025:000) amending the Act (2017:630) on measures to counter money laundering and the financing of terrorism.
