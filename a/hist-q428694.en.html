Full text

Boëthius de Dacia, who died around 1290, was a scholastic philosopher. His origins are disputed, and he is claimed to have been both Swedish and Danish. The words "de Dacia" after his name mean "from the ecclesiastical province of Dacia" (the Lund ecclesiastical province).

Boëthius de Dacia studied at the University of Paris, where he became a master and later worked as a teacher. He wrote numerous works in scholastic philosophy. The most famous of these was about Aristotle's *Topics*. Later, he became primarily known for advocating Averroism, an Aristotelian school of thought.

In connection with the Universalist Controversy, Boëthius de Dacia held nominalist views, along with Sigerius of Brabant. These views provoked responses from Raimundus Lullus and Petrus de Alvernia, after which the Archbishop of Paris condemned 204 statements from their works as heretical in 1277. Boëthius, like other Averroists, was forced to leave France, and according to some sources, he died in Italy; according to others, he died in Linköping, where he supposedly served as a canon.
