Full text

The Rök Stone, identified by the catalog number Ög 136, is one of Sweden's most famous runestones. Its inscription, containing approximately 760 characters, is considered to be the longest runic inscription in the world. The stone is located near Röks Church in the parish of Rök, within the municipality of Ödeshög and the district of Lysings in Östergötland. The Rök Stone is sometimes cited as the starting point for the history of Swedish literature, being considered the oldest surviving literary work.

The stone is a block of light gray, fine-grained granite, approximately 382 cm high (of which 125 cm are below ground), 138 cm wide, and between 19 and 43 cm thick. The stone block was likely quarried nearby.

== History ==

The Rök stone was likely erected during the first half of the 9th century. This is suggested by the archaic language it uses, which represents an early form of Old Norse, distinct from the language found on runestones from the 10th century. One passage has been interpreted as referring to Tjodrik (generally believed to be Theoderic the Great) having died nine "man's ages" ago, further placing the runestone in the early 9th century. The runemaster used a futhark consisting of only 16 characters, which likely made the text difficult to understand even for contemporary readers.

The original location of the stone is unknown, but it was probably situated near its current position. In the early 17th century, the national antiquarian Johannes Bureus discovered that the stone was embedded in a wall with its front facing outwards, within a storage building next to Röks church, which dates back to the 12th century. Bureus copied and published a drawing of the stone in his work "Monumenta Sveo-Gothica hactenus exsculpta," published in 1624. In the 1670s, the antiquarian and secretary of the Swedish National Heritage Board, Johan Hadorph, visited the site and reproduced the stone in a woodcut. This woodcut was published in 1750 in Johan Göransson's runological work "Bautil." In Carl Fredric Broocman's description of Östergötland from the 1760s, it states: "Among the old memorials, there is a runestone inserted in the church building wall, which is completely incomprehensible and unreadable."

When the old church and storage building were demolished in 1843, it was discovered that the stone had runes on all sides. The provost Hedmark copied the previously unknown sides and then had the stone embedded in the vestibule of the new church. At the request of the Royal Swedish Academy of Letters, History and Antiquities, the stone was removed from the wall again in 1862 and erected against the western cemetery wall. In 1933, it was moved to its current location outside the cemetery and provided with a protective roof. It is likely that the stone previously had a more upright position than it does now. If the vertical inscriptions are aligned vertically, the lower rows will end up horizontally.

On the right edge of the back side, there is damage that probably occurred when the stone was removed from the storage building in 1843. There is also damage on the left narrow side, which, judging by its appearance, occurred long before the church was built.

== The Stone's Name == The name "Rökstenen" (the Rök stone) is somewhat of a tautology. The stone is called that because it was found in the village of Rök, but it's possible that the village itself got its name from the stone. It's conceivable that the stone was erected in the 8th century at what was then the center of the region, and that the church was built on the same spot about 300 years later. The Old Norse word "raukr," which became "røk" in Old Swedish, means something like "pillar" or "pointed object," compare it to the Gutnish word "rauk" with the same origin. An alternative explanation is that the name Rök has a connection to water or mist.

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary domestic sources from the Viking Age – carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they state what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some readings are uncertain – such readings are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
