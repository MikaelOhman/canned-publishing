Carl Jacob Sundevall, born October 22, 1801, in the parish of Högestad, Skåne, and died February 2, 1875, in Stockholm, was a Swedish zoologist. He was the brother of the physician Fredrik Emil Sundevall and the naval officer Henrik Ludvig Sundevall.

Sundevall became a student at Lund University in 1817, earning his Master's degree in philosophy in 1823 and becoming a docent (lecturer) in economics in 1826. After a trip to the East Indies between 1827 and 1828, he obtained his medical license in 1829 and became a medical doctor the following year.

In 1833, he was appointed curator at the Swedish National Museum of Natural History in Stockholm, and in 1836, he was appointed associate professor of zoology and economics at Lund University. After returning from the French scientific expedition to places including Spitsbergen in 1838, he was appointed curator and professor at the museum in 1839. From 1841 to 1871, he worked in the department of vertebrates.

Sundevall was a member of the Royal Physiographic Society in Lund (1825), the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (1839), the Scientific and Literary Society in Gothenburg (1840), the Royal Agricultural Academy (1844), and the Royal Society of Sciences in Uppsala (1851). Most of Sundevall's works dealt with the systematics of vertebrates. In "Methodisk öfversigt af idislande djuren" (1844), he made a valuable contribution to the knowledge of the relationships between these animals. His textbook "Lärobok i zoologien" (1835), along with its accompanying atlas, was used for many years in secondary schools.

His most important works are in the field of ornithology, where he studied parts of bird anatomy. Among his most notable works are "Methodi naturalis avium disponendarum tentamen" (1872–1873), "Svenska foglarna" (1856–1871), and "Conspectus avium picinarum" (1866). Sundevall also proposed the use of phonetic letters.
