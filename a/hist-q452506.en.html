Sten Sture the Elder, often referred to as "Herr Sten," was born around 1440 and died on December 14, 1503, in Jönköping. He served as Regent of Sweden from 1470 (likely around midsummer) to 1497, and again from 1501 until his death. He was also a knight and commander of Stockholm. He was married to Ingeborg Åkesdotter (Tott), and he was the victor in the Battle of Brunkeberg.

== Biography ==

Sten Sture was the son of Birgitta Stensdotter, the half-sister of King Karl Knutsson, and Gustav Anundsson Sture, a member of the royal council and governor of Kalmar Castle. As early as 1444, when Sten was four years old, his father Gustav died. His mother remarried to the royal councilor Gustav Karlsson, and Sten Sture was likely raised in their home; partly at Kalmar Castle, where his stepfather served as governor for a time, and partly at the Ekholmen estate in Uppland.

His kinship with King Karl gave him an early role in domestic political conflicts, and during the king's seven-year exile, he visited him in Danzig. In 1464, he participated in Bishop Kettil Karlsson's uprising against King Christian I and together with Kettill, achieved victory at Haraker, where the assembled Dala army inflicted a decisive defeat on Christian. At the same time, he drew closer to the powerful family of the Axelsson sons, who had reason for hostility towards the Danish king. He first became engaged to Ivar Axelsson (Tott)'s daughter, Agneta, and after she died before the wedding, he married Åke Axelsson Tott's daughter, Ingeborg, in 1466. Sir Sten, who was already called a knight in 1462 and was later summoned to the royal council by 1466, lived at this time on his ancestral estate of Rävsnäs, where he was registered.

After successfully acting during the internal unrest of 1466–1467, together with Nils Sture and Erik Axelsson, he paved the way for King Karl's final ascension to the throne in that year. In 1469, he was forced to take up arms again due to an uprising by Erik Karlsson Vasa against Karl Knutsson, and in 1470, he won a significant victory over the rebel army at Uppbo ferry in Dalarna, with Nils Sture leading the Dala men. When King Christian subsequently invaded Västergötland, Sten quickly turned against him as well and defeated him at Öresten that same year.

On his deathbed, Karl Knutsson entrusted him with all the castles and cities under Karl's direct administration (including Stockholm), effectively designating him as the country's future ruler.

Upon Karl's death on May 15, 1470, Sten Sture immediately took the lead. The following midsummer, he was elected by the royal council as Regent, and the meeting in Arboga in May 1471, which also included representatives from the townsfolk and peasants, confirmed this status.

Sweden's independence and his own position were secured for twenty-five years through the victory at the Battle of Brunkeberg on October 10, 1471. After decades of almost constant feuds, Sweden experienced a period of relative peace, and the victory at Brunkeberg sparked a national revival led by the Regent. This was expressed, among other things, in the founding of Uppsala University, which opened in 1477 on the initiative of Archbishop Jakob Ulfsson, but with the interested involvement of Sten Sture. In the government, the Regent relied heavily on the lower social classes, who held him in high regard, and he probably saw himself as a successor to Engelbrekt. This fact alone caused a conflict between him and the aristocratic council, especially the bishops, and the Kalmar Union of 1483 can be seen as an early expression of this. Through the Kalmar Union, King Hans of Denmark was recognized as the Swedish king against Sten Sture's will.

However, Sten Sture was strong enough to prevent the implementation of the union and King Hans' takeover of power in Sweden for fourteen years.

In 1487, Ivar Axelsson's fiefdom was revoked, despite strong opposition from Sir Ivar, who was a significant nobleman. Through skillful diplomacy in Rome, primarily involving Hemming Gadh, it became possible for Sten Sture not only to counter Danish attempts at excommunication but also to obtain papal promises and concessions that jeopardized the independence of the Swedish hierarchy from the Regent. In foreign policy, he tried to continue Karl Knutsson's old idea that Sweden should take advantage of the Livonian Order's impending decline to gain influence there in one way or another. Unfortunate circumstances, which Sten Sture could not control, eventually led to a crisis in his position of power. In 1493, King Hans broke an alliance with the rising Muscovite Grand Prince aimed at Sweden, after which Russian troops invaded Finland in 1495.

During the subsequent campaign, Sten Sture disagreed with Svante Nilsson, who had shown skill in warfare. Svante Nilsson angrily returned to Sweden and, together with Archbishop Jakob Ulfsson, won the majority of the royal council for a policy aimed at removing Sten Sture and inviting King Hans.
