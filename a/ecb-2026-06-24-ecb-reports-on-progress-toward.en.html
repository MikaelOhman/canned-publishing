In plain language

The European Central Bank (ECB) has conducted a study to assess how close certain EU countries are to being able to adopt the euro as their currency. The results indicate that progress is limited. This is largely due to challenges in the global environment, such as the war in Ukraine and the conflict in the Middle East, which have increased energy costs and created uncertainty about the future.

When examining prices and inflation, i.e., how much more expensive things are becoming, countries like Romania, Hungary, and Poland have struggled to keep prices at acceptable levels. In Sweden and the Czech Republic, however, price increases were lower than the established thresholds. This means that prices in these three countries have risen too much compared to countries that already use the euro.

There are also issues related to the countries' economies and debt. In Hungary, Poland, and Romania, the government deficit, meaning they are spending more money than they are receiving, is too high. This has led the EU to initiate what is known as the Excessive Deficit Procedure, a type of warning that these countries must reduce their deficits to stabilize their economies.

Finally, the report shows that none of the five countries studied meet all the requirements for adopting the euro. This is not only about financial matters, but also about ensuring that the countries' laws are in compliance with EU regulations. The report also notes that countries like Hungary and Romania need to improve the functioning of their government agencies and laws to create a stable economy.

Full text

June 24, 2026

According to the report on economic convergence from the European Central Bank (ECB), published today, the EU member states that are not part of the Eurozone have made limited progress towards increased economic coordination with the Eurozone since 2024. A key factor hindering this economic coordination is the economic impact of external factors, including Russia's war against Ukraine, global trade tensions, and the war in the Middle East.

Despite these external challenges, economic activity in the countries reviewed has proven to be resilient, but the growth rate has varied between different countries. Looking ahead, the economic outlook is uncertain due to increased geopolitical tensions. The war in the Middle East has contributed to increased volatility in global energy markets and rising energy costs, which exacerbates the uncertainty surrounding the economic outlook. These developments have already had visible effects on inflation and economic optimism, and may affect future production growth. While the sensitivity and direct exposure to energy crises in these economies have decreased over time and are currently lower than in 2022, the long-term economic consequences will depend on the intensity and duration of the current energy crisis, as well as the extent of its indirect and secondary effects.

Regarding the criterion for price stability, the average inflation rate over the past 12 months exceeded the reference value of 2.7% in three out of the five countries examined. Inflation was significantly higher than the reference value in Romania, and slightly higher than the reference value in Hungary and Poland. In the Czech Republic and Sweden, inflation was below the reference value (Diagram 1). The reference value is based on the three member states that performed best over the past 12 months, namely Cyprus (0.9%), France (1.2%), and Denmark (1.6%), by taking the average of their inflation rates over the past 12 months and adding 1.5 percentage points. None of the member states were identified as outliers that should be excluded from the calculation of the reference value.

The fiscal deficits of most countries have worsened since the report on the convergence criteria for 2024, and the debt-to-GDP ratio has increased significantly in some cases. In 2025, Hungary, Poland, and Romania exceeded the reference value for a deficit of 3% of GDP (Diagram 2). The total gross debt of the government sector, as a percentage of GDP, was below or well below the reference value of 60% in all the countries examined, except for Hungary, in 2025. However, the European Commission's latest forecasts indicate that the debt-to-GDP ratio will exceed the reference value in both Poland and Romania in 2026.

The number of countries subject to an excessive deficit procedure has increased to three since the 2024 convergence report, with Hungary and Poland joining Romania. According to the European Commission's forecasts, none of these countries are expected to reduce their deficits below 3% of GDP before the end of 2027. Romania remains subject to an excessive deficit procedure, which began in 2020 and whose deadline for correcting the excessive deficit situation has been extended to 2030. Excessive deficit procedures were also initiated for Hungary and Poland in July 2024, based on deficits that exceeded 3% of GDP in 2023 – amounting to 4.7% and 7.3% respectively – with correction deadlines set for 2026 and 2028.

Regarding the exchange rate criterion, none of the currencies of the countries being assessed participate in the Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM II), and some currencies have experienced significant fluctuations against the euro in recent years (Diagram 3).

With regard to long-term interest rates, three of the five countries under review recorded an average long-term interest rate over 12 months that exceeded the reference value of 5.1% (Diagram 4). These were Poland (5.4%), Hungary (6.7%), and Romania (6.7%), and their long-term interest rates were also above the reference value in the 2024 convergence report. The reference value is based on the 12-month average of the long-term interest rates for the three member states with the lowest inflation rates, namely Denmark (2.6%), Cyprus (3.1%), and France (3.5%). The average interest rate is therefore 3.1%, and, with the addition of 2 percentage points, the reference value is 5.1%.

The quality of institutions is crucial for maintaining stable development. Stronger institutions and better governance would provide significant economic benefits, particularly in Hungary and Romania. With the exception of Sweden, indicators published by international organizations suggest that the countries under review still have the potential to improve the quality of their institutions and governance.

Regarding whether national legislation is compatible with the EU treaties and the statutes of the European Central Bank (ECB), none of the five countries reviewed met all the requirements for adopting the euro.
