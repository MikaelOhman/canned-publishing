Gebhard Leberecht von Blücher, Prince of Wahlstatt, was born on December 16, 1742, in Rostock, and died on September 12, 1819, in Krieblowitz, Silesia (now Poland). He was a Swedish-Prussian military figure who achieved the rank of Field Marshal in 1813.

Blücher, from an aristocratic family, entered the service of the Swedish Hussar Regiment after a basic education during the Pomeranian War in 1758. In 1760, he was captured by the Prussians and joined their army. He became a staff captain in 1771 but resigned in 1773 after being passed over for promotion. He then managed his estates in Grossraddow. In 1787, he re-entered Prussian military service as a major, becoming a colonel in 1790, a regimental commander in 1794, and a general major. Later, he became a General of Cavalry in 1809, Field Marshal in 1813, and finally Prince in 1814 before retiring in 1815.

Blücher was popular among his subordinates due to his charisma and straightforward manner. Despite his rapid career advancement, he played little political role before the Napoleonic Wars, largely due to his lack of education and a somewhat reckless lifestyle that included gambling and alcohol abuse. In 1805, Blücher advocated for war against Napoleon and belonged to the war party in 1806. At the Battle of Auerstedt, Blücher commanded the vanguard, but his attack was repelled by French defenses. He then retreated towards Lübeck but had to surrender with 6,000 men at Ratekau after the Battle of Lübeck in November 1806. However, he was exchanged for Claude-Victor Perrin in March 1807 and sent to operate alongside the Swedes in Pomerania, where he got along well with Gustav IV Adolf. After the Treaty of Tilsit in July 1807, Blücher became the military governor of Pomerania and was increasingly seen by the patriotic movement as its leader. In 1809, Blücher unsuccessfully advocated for an alliance with Austria. Following the alliance with France in 1812, Blücher was forced to resign due to his outspoken criticism of the French.

During the break with Napoleon, Blücher received a high command at the urging of Gerhard von Scharnhorst, but he could not prevent French victories at the battles of Lützen and Bautzen, both in May 1813. However, Blücher's successful raid at Haynau on May 26 boosted the morale of the army. After the armistice, Blücher was placed at the head of the Silesian Army with August von Gneisenau as Chief of Staff, and this marked the beginning of Blücher's most brilliant period. Blücher found his complement in the ingenious strategist Gneisenau, and he trusted him completely. At the Battle of Katzbach on August 26, Blücher decisively defeated Jacques MacDonald, then crossed the Elbe and joined forces with Charles XIV John. In mid-October, he participated in the Battle of Leipzig, where Napoleon himself was defeated. The energetic pursuit that Blücher advocated for did not happen, but on New Year's Day 1814, Blücher crossed the Rhine. Blücher's bold, somewhat isolated advance towards Paris led to a series of defeats for his scattered corps. However, when Napoleon turned his attention elsewhere, Blücher continued his advance, dragging the hesitant Karl zu Schwarzenberg along with him, and on March 31, 1814, the allied forces entered Paris. After Napoleon's return, Blücher, with Gneisenau by his side, commanded the Prussian army in the southern part of the United Netherlands. At the Battle of Ligny on June 16, 1815, Blücher, at Gneisenau's urging, bravely sacrificed his connections to assist the Duke of Wellington, leading to a decisive victory over Napoleon and Marshal Ney at the Battle of Waterloo two days later.

Blücher was rewarded for his victories with numerous donations. Two German warships, SMS Blücher and Blücher, were named after him.
