Expanded capabilities for intervention in security-sensitive operations.

TL;DRSweden aims to strengthen the protection of vital security interests by granting authorities greater power to prevent dangerous collaborations. The proposal would make it easier for authorities to intervene against agreements that pose a risk to Sweden's security.
In plain language

The government proposes new regulations in the security protection law to protect Sweden from risks. Due to the deteriorating geopolitical situation, the government wants to provide the authorities responsible for security with better tools to stop activities or collaborations that could be dangerous for the country.

An important part of the proposal is that companies and organizations involved in security-sensitive activities must report certain collaborations to the authorities. Failure to report such a collaboration may result in a penalty fee, known as a sanction fee. The authorities will also have greater authority to inspect the other parties involved in a collaboration.

The authorities will also be given the opportunity to make quick decisions to stop ongoing processes that are deemed detrimental to security. They can also require businesses to conduct more thorough investigations into how secure their collaborations actually are. This is called a security assessment and suitability assessment.

The new rules are part of a proposal to the Parliament, called proposition 2025/26:182. If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new laws are planned to come into effect on July 1, 2026.

Full text

Given the deteriorating security policy situation, it is crucial that the authorities responsible for overseeing security-sensitive activities have effective tools to intervene against practices that pose risks to Sweden's security. To provide supervisory authorities with expanded opportunities to intervene in contractual and collaborative relationships that expose security-sensitive activities to other actors, the following is proposed:

  • The supervisory authorities' ability to intervene in ongoing practices that are deemed inappropriate from a security perspective is expanded to also include practices that are not subject to a requirement for a security agreement.
  • To ensure that supervisory authorities are aware of inappropriate practices, a requirement is introduced for practices to be reported. Failure to report can result in a penalty.
  • Supervisory authorities are given the possibility to issue interim orders.
  • Supervisory authorities are given expanded opportunities to conduct oversight at other entities besides the operator of the activity.
  • Supervisory authorities are given expanded opportunities to require an operator to conduct a special security assessment and suitability assessment.

The proposed amendments to the law will enter into force on July 1, 2026.
