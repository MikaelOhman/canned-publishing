The government has proposed changes to the regulations governing how we use energy in our buildings. The purpose is to achieve new energy-saving goals and comply with new regulations from the EU. This means that it will become more important to assess the building's envelope, also known as the building's thermal envelope, when undertaking major renovations. All buildings owned or used by government agencies and municipalities must also have an energy performance certificate. Full text

The proposal suggests a new target for energy efficiency. Furthermore, it proposes amendments to the Energy Performance Certificates for Buildings Act (2006:985) and the Planning and Building Act (2010:900) to implement the revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive.

The Energy Performance Certificates for Buildings Act expands the requirements for when an energy performance certificate must be prepared, now including renovations that involve a significant portion of the building's thermal envelope (i.e., the elements that separate the indoor environment from the outdoor environment). Furthermore, all buildings owned or used by a public body must have an energy performance certificate. A simplified update process for energy performance certificates is introduced. Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems must be inspected at intervals of no more than three or five years, depending on the system's size. A new tool, energy renovation plans, is also introduced, which is optional for building owners to use. If used, the plan may sometimes replace the recommendations that would otherwise be included in an energy performance certificate. To facilitate oversight, a reporting obligation is introduced for building owners.

The technical requirement in the Planning and Building Act, which previously concerned the charging of electric vehicles, is amended to refer to sustainable mobility. This change makes it possible, in accordance with the revised directive, to require the provision of bicycle parking spaces.

The proposed amendments to the laws are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2026.
