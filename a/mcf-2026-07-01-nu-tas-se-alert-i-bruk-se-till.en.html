SE-Alert is now being implemented – make sure your phone is updated.

TL;DRStarting July 1st, a new system called SE-Alert will be used to send important warnings directly to mobile phones in the event of emergencies. Make sure your mobile phone is updated so that you can receive these messages if necessary.
In plain language

Starting July 1st, a new system called SE-Alert will be used to warn people when there is an immediate danger, such as in the event of large gas leaks or air raid alerts. This system complements existing warning methods, including outdoor sirens, information broadcast on radio and television, and text messages.

To ensure your mobile phone can receive these warnings, make sure it has the latest software installed. This software controls how your phone functions. In the event of a danger, your phone will sound an alarm for ten seconds and display a text message directly on the screen, allowing you to quickly access information.

The Swedish Civil Defence Agency is responsible for this system, along with other government agencies. During the autumn, they will conduct two tests of the system, one in September and one in December, to ensure that everything functions correctly before the system is fully implemented.

Full text

From July 1st, SE-Alert can be used to send Important Public Alerts (VMA). Initially, SE-Alert will be used for extreme events, such as the activation of air raid sirens or major gas leaks that immediately threaten the public.

Those who have not yet updated their mobile phones with the latest software should do so now. From July 1st, SE-Alert will be available as a complement to and in parallel with other VMA channels, such as outdoor warning systems (Hesa Fredrik), radio, television, SMS, and the internet. This means that authorized authorities and other authorized actors can choose to use SE-Alert to reach the public during serious events. Initially, until the system is fully developed later this fall, SE-Alert will be used for immediate threats and situations where the public is in danger, such as gas leaks that immediately threaten the public in a city, or when air raid sirens are activated, and this will happen in parallel with SMS messages. Otherwise, only SMS broadcasts will be used as before.

"With SE-Alert, important information will quickly reach people who are in an affected area," said Mikael Frisell, Director General of the Swedish Civil Defence Agency. "We are now ready to warn the population about immediate threats and situations where the public is in danger. We have strengthened society's overall ability to warn and inform during accidents, crises, and wars. The system is operational, and we can be confident that SE-Alert will be used when needed."

A school class receives a warning through an Important Public Alert sent via SE-Alert.

After being put into operation, SE-Alert will continue to be developed and tested. Two public tests for the general public will be conducted this fall – regionally in September and nationally in December. The Swedish Civil Defence Agency is in dialogue with the regional administrative boards for the area where the quarterly test will take place in September.

SE-Alert is an enhancement and complement to the existing information channels for sending Important Public Alerts (VMA), which currently include outdoor warning sirens ("Hesa Fredrik"), television, SMS messages, and the internet. In 2025, the Swedish Civil Defence Agency received a mandate from the government to implement SE-Alert. The work is led by the Swedish Civil Defence Agency, in close collaboration with the Swedish Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) and SOS Alarm.

SE-Alert reaches all compatible, updated mobile phones located within the geographical area where the public needs to be warned, regardless of the operator. A warning alarm sounds for 10 seconds, and the text message is displayed directly on the screen. The headline will indicate whether it is a VMA or an air raid alert. In the event of an air raid alert, the outdoor warning siren ("Hesa Fredrik") will also sound. Implementing the system now allows us to gather experience and data from its use, which is an important part of its continued development throughout the year.
