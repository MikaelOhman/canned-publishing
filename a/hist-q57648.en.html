Sophia Maria Victoria (Viktoria) of Baden, born on August 7, 1862, in Karlsruhe, and died on April 4, 1930, in Rome, was Queen of Sweden from 1907 until her death, through her marriage to King Gustaf V.

Victoria was born as a Princess of Baden and was the daughter of Grand Duke Frederick I. When she married the Crown Prince Gustaf of Sweden, she became the Swedish Crown Princess and eventually the Swedish Queen. In Norway, she was the Crown Princess until the dissolution of the union in 1905.

For much of her life, Victoria suffered from ill health and mostly resided abroad, in Germany and Italy. Her relationship with her husband deteriorated starting in the 1890s. Her relationship with the physician Axel Munthe was very close; there are differing opinions about the exact nature of their relationship. Villa Solliden on Öland is one of her accomplishments.

== Childhood == Victoria was the only daughter of Grand Duke Frederick I of Baden and his wife, Louise of Prussia, making her a granddaughter and cousin to the German Emperors Wilhelm I and Wilhelm II. She grew up in Karlsruhe Castle with her two brothers, Frederick and Ludwig. In the summers, the family moved to the summer palace in Mainau. They regularly visited the German Emperor's castle in Babelsberg, near Berlin. Occasionally, they traveled to St. Moritz in Switzerland, the French west coast, or a seaside resort in England. It has been said that Louise was very strict with her children and made them sleep in cold rooms on hard beds to toughen them up, which may have contributed to Victoria's poor health.

Formal schooling began in 1871, with Victoria attending classes alongside 15 other girls at the castle. In addition to her native language, she spoke French and English fluently. She learned to draw and paint, eventually becoming a skilled photographer. She was also a talented pianist, capable of playing works by Chopin, Beethoven, and her favorite composer, Wagner. Victoria memorized many ballads by Schiller and poems by Uhland.

At the age of sixteen, Victoria was confirmed on the Sunday before Advent in 1878 by the Archbishop of Baden, Prelate Doll. Afterwards, Grand Duchess Louise arranged courses at the castle in painting, music, languages, and history. The Austrian conductor Felix Mottl taught Victoria to appreciate Wagner, and when the composer Franz Liszt visited the castle, Victoria had the opportunity to turn the pages while he played Schubert.

Before she met the Swedish Crown Prince Gustaf (V), she was infatuated with Grand Duke Nicholas of Russia, a cousin of Tsar Nicholas. The Tsar refused to allow them to marry because they were cousins. Her mother wanted her to marry the German heir apparent Wilhelm, but this was rejected by the German Chancellor Bismarck, as it would also have been a marriage between cousins.

The question of Crown Prince Gustaf's marriage had occupied his parents since the late 1870s. King Oscar believed that closer ties with Germany were desirable, and a German princess would be suitable, such as Princess Charlotte of Prussia, daughter of the German Crown Prince. The Swedish press speculated about a possible marriage to Princess Victoria of Hesse-Darmstadt. Queen Sophia wanted them to also consider the British royal family, and Crown Prince Gustaf met Queen Victoria's youngest daughter, Beatrice of Britain, but this did not spark any interest.

In the autumn of 1879, Gustaf was sent as an observer to follow the German autumn maneuvers near Strasbourg. During a visit to London, Gustaf had become acquainted with Victoria's brother, Frederick, and he was able to meet Victoria and her parents in connection with the maneuvers. After the end of the maneuvers, the Crown Prince visited Baden-Baden as a guest of the Grand Ducal family, but there does not appear to have been any romantic feelings between the Crown Prince and Victoria at that time.

== Marriage ==

In February 1881, Crown Prince Gustaf was sent to attend the wedding of Crown Prince Wilhelm of Germany and Auguste Victoria of Schleswig-Holstein on February 27, 1881. While visiting Empress Augusta, the crown prince met Victoria. In Gustaf's letter to his mother, one can follow the development of their affection: "Everyone has noticed more or less that we like each other, but it doesn't bother me at all. I am completely certain in my choice and feel incredibly happy." Grand Duke Frederick asked Gustaf to stay a few extra days. Just a week after their first meeting, the crown prince proposed. On March 12th – which would later become her Swedish name day – the engagement was announced.

The crown prince then had to return to Stockholm because King Oscar had contracted pneumonia. Once the king recovered, Gustaf could return to Karlsruhe and celebrate Easter with his future in-laws. In May, King Oscar traveled to the German spa town of Bad Ems, where Victoria had the opportunity to meet him. Together with Queen Sofia, he traveled to Brühl Castle, between Cologne and Bonn, to meet Empress Augusta and finalize the practical arrangements for the wedding.
