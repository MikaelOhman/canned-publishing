Abraham Viktor Rydberg (known in parliament as Rydberg of Gothenburg), born December 18, 1828, in Jönköping, and died September 21, 1895, in Djursholm, was a Swedish author, poet, journalist, language reformer, religious philosopher, translator, cultural historian, and illustrator.

Rydberg was one of Sweden's most important authors during the late 19th century, bridging the gap between Carl Jonas Love Almqvist and August Strindberg. Among his works that remain popular in the 21st century are *Singoalla* and the poem "Tomten." Rydberg is also known for the poem "Betlehems stjärna," which was set to music by Alice Tegnér, and begins with the words "Gläns över sjö och strand." In his time, works such as *Vapensmeden* and "Den nya Grottesången" garnered significant attention. As a translator, he translated works including Johann Wolfgang von Goethe's *Faust*, Edgar Allan Poe's poem "The Raven," and Heinrich Heine's "Die Loreley." He was also a member of the Swedish Academy.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Family History === Rydberg was born in Jönköping castle in 1828 as the sixth child and third son of Johan Rydberg, the castle and prison warden, and Hedvig Kristina Duker, who had been trained as a midwife. His older brother, Carl August, also became a poet.

Viktor Rydberg's father belonged to a farming family that originally bore the soldier's name Frisk and had resided for several generations in Heljaryd Nederby in Rogberga parish, approximately 5 kilometers south of Huskvarna. The surname Rydberg was adopted by Viktor Rydberg's father after Heljaryd. Before that, his father's last name was Frisk. His mother's roots were in Poland, where the oldest known ancestor was Peder Jonson Duker (of Düker, Low German for diver), born around 1528, and the Swedish ancestral line originated in Småland at the end of the 16th century.

The father, as a non-commissioned officer, had participated in the war of 1808–1809 and fought in the battles of Sävar and Ratan, as well as in the war in Germany in 1813. The mother died of cholera on August 24, 1834, at the age of 39. The following day, Rydberg's youngest sister also passed away. The father was completely devastated by grief. Sometimes, overwhelmed with sorrow, he would take Viktor to the cemetery and throw himself over the gravestone. Father and son would spend entire nights there, crying and mourning. Due to his mental instability, the father could no longer perform his duties and resigned on September 23, 1834, after being granted a one-year leave of absence. He subsequently turned to alcohol and ended up living alone with a caregiver, and later in an institution for the disabled. The children were separated after their mother's death.

Rydberg's fondest memory from his childhood remained the memory of his mother, which stayed with him throughout his life. She instilled in him the belief that "one should not be afraid of anything other than becoming evil, that there is no greater danger." On several occasions, during important decisions, he felt as if he could hear his mother giving him advice.

Viktor Rydberg became a foster child through the welfare system, placed with Daniel Skog, a "horse doctor entrepreneur," and his wife in Jönköping. The Skog family soon moved to a farm on the western outskirts of town, which was destroyed in a major fire on August 21, 1835. Rydberg's new foster parents were the castle caretaker Blanks, arranged by Landshövding Bergenstråhle. Subsequently, he lived for several years with Sara Ottergren (who inspired Rydberg to write "Lille Viggs äventyr på julafton"), and from 1839 as a foster son with Kristina Pettersson, the widow of a bricklayer.

His guardian during those years, the shoemaker J. Ahlstedt, supported Rydberg in his early years through his involvement in the welfare board. In the summer of 1841, the support from the welfare system ceased, and Rydberg, at the age of thirteen, had to begin supporting himself through tutoring.

=== Education === Rydberg initially received his education at Gustav Adolfsstugan, where instruction followed the Lancaster method, which he disliked. From 1839, he attended the higher educational and apologetics school in Jönköping, but shortly thereafter, to his great sorrow, he had to leave and return home. For reasons unknown, his guardian, the shoemaker Ahlstedt, decided to place him with a widowed bricklayer, Kristina Pettersson, whose son Viktor was his classmate. From the end of 1839 until 1845, meaning from the age of twelve to seventeen, he was officially registered as living with her as a foster child. He did not enjoy this arrangement.

From 1845, he attended the gymnasium in Växjö. Rydberg's academic performance at the Växjö gymnasium was not as successful as it had been in Jönköping. This was largely due to his constant financial worries and his general dissatisfaction with the school and his classmates. Corporal punishment was common at the school. During his time in Växjö, Rydberg supported himself through private tutoring, and during the summer of 1846, he worked as a tutor at Bergs estate for a lieutenant colonel named Charpentier. However, financial difficulties forced Rydberg to leave the gymnasium in the spring of 1847. A note in a protocol indicates that Rydberg "... expressed his desire to withdraw from the intermediate level, but without completing the final examination." He writes about shoes.
