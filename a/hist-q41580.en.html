Axel Martin Fredrik Munthe, born October 31, 1857, in Oskarshamn, and died February 11, 1949, at Stockholm Castle, was a Swedish physician and author. He served as the personal physician to Princess Victoria, later Queen Victoria, from 1893 until her death in 1930. He is known for creating Villa San Michele on the island of Capri.

== Biography ==

=== Background and Family === Axel Munthe was the son of pharmacist Fredrik Munthe and Lovisa Ugarph. According to family tradition, he descended from a noble family originating from Flanders and later Germany, who first immigrated to Scania. Munthe married Ultima Hornberg (1861–1895) in 1880; they met in Paris. The marriage lasted until 1888.

In 1907, Munthe married Hilda Pennington Mellor (1882–1967), an English noblewoman who was 25 years younger than him. However, the marriage was not a happy one. The couple separated in 1919 but never divorced. They had two sons: the artist Viking (Peter) Munthe (1908–1976) and Malcolm Munthe (1910–1995), who became a British intelligence officer. After Hilda's death, the magnificent estate called Stengården, built in Leksand between 1910 and 1911 and designed by Torben Grut, was renamed Hildasholm.

=== Medical Studies and Paris === After studying at Uppsala University, where he earned degrees in medicine and philosophy, and at the University of Montpellier, Munthe obtained his doctorate in medicine at the University of Paris on August 2, 1880. From 1881, he worked as a gynecologist in Paris. It is likely that during this time, while working for the hypnotist and neurologist Jean Martin Charcot, he began to specialize in nervous system disorders.

During his time in Paris, he frequently socialized with the Scandinavian artistic community, particularly with Ernst Josephson, Hugo Birger, and Carl Snoilsky, as well as August Strindberg, whom he became a personal physician for. However, their relationship was anything but warm. In the 1880s, Munthe rented out his apartment in Paris to the Finnish artist couple Albert Edelfelt and Ellan de la Chapelle. His sister, Anna Munthe-Norstedt, was an artist who lived in Paris with her husband, and she likely facilitated his connections.

=== Naples, Rome, and Capri ===

In 1884, Munthe traveled to Naples, which was experiencing a cholera epidemic, where he made significant contributions that led to his fame. Following his divorce in 1888, he broke away from the Parisian social scene and moved to Capri. The following years were difficult: he suffered from depression, and his patients often lacked the means to pay for treatment. In 1890, he therefore relocated his practice to Rome, at Piazza di Spagna, in the building that now houses the John Keats museum. Munthe quickly became a popular doctor, especially among the English community in Rome. During the 1890s, Munthe became a wealthy man. The practice in Rome ceased operations in 1901.

Munthe then settled on Capri, where he built a villa, San Michele, during the 1890s, on the ruins of one of Emperor Tiberius' villas on the island. Munthe became a popular figure on the island, partly because he cared for the island's poor population without charge. The villa quickly gained attention due to its collection of antiques and soon became a destination for visits from Munthe's circle of royalty and various cultural figures.

Munthe strongly opposed Nazism, but in 1937, he negotiated the sale of his life's work, San Michele, to Hermann Göring. However, the villa was donated to the Swedish Institute in Rome in 1948, and today it serves as both a museum and a guest house for Swedish researchers and cultural workers.

=== Royal Physician ===

As early as 1892, Munthe was asked if he would become the personal physician of Crown Princess Victoria. She had long suffered from respiratory problems. He began his service around 1893. After a longer stay in London, he returned to Sweden around the turn of the century and became a close friend of the then Crown Prince, later King Gustaf V. In 1903, he was officially appointed royal physician, and in 1909, first royal physician. During his time in Sweden, he resided at Stockholm Palace and other palaces where the royal family stayed.

Caring for Queen Victoria increasingly occupied his time, and from 1926 until her death in 1930, he never left her side. The nature of their relationship was already a subject of gossip during that era. Many factors contributed to certain tensions, such as Munthe being progressive while she was conservative, but they seemed to have developed a friendship based on their shared interests. Even Munthe's medical practice in this case was not without scrutiny, and his treatment methods were sometimes heavily criticized.

Princess Maria, who occasionally stayed with the Queen and Munthe on Capri at King Gustaf V's request, believed that his influence was detrimental and that he possessed a hypnotic power. Years later, she claimed that he wanted her as a patient and made physical advances towards her, and that the fear she experienced because of the Swedish royal family's uncritical support for Munthe was the main reason she fled and divorced Prince Wilhelm.
