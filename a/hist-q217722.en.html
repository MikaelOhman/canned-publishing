Ragnar Arthur Granit, born on October 30, 1900, in Riihimäki, and died on March 12, 1991, in the Engelbrekt parish of Stockholm, was a Finnish-Swedish physiologist and neurophysiologist. Granit is one of the most renowned medical researchers in Finland's history, and he was a leading explorer of the nervous system and the sense of sight during the 20th century. He received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1967, jointly with Haldan Keffer Hartline and George Wald, for their discoveries concerning the physiological and chemical processes involved in vision. Granit worked at the University of Helsinki and later at the Karolinska Institute, where he was a professor of neurophysiology from 1946 to 1967.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Granit's parents were Arthur Granit, a forester, and Albertina Helena (Bertie) Malmberg. The family originally came from Korpo. Granit's childhood home was in Åggelby, Helsinki, and he attended the Swedish Normal School. He often spent his summers in Korpo with his cousins Lars-Ivar Ringbom, Anders Ringbom, and Nils-Eric Ringbom.

Granit had a strong interest in literature. He was the editor of "Studentbladet" from 1923 to 1926, a contributor to the first issues of the journal "Quosego," and the editor of the Finnish Red Cross magazine during the 1930s. He also published numerous literary articles and works. Granit was a member of Nylands nation, where he served as curator, and later became involved with the Finnish Red Cross and the Mannerheim League for Child Welfare.

=== Education and Early Research === Granit initially studied philosophy at Åbo Akademi, where he became interested in psychology. His interest in psychology, along with encouragement from relatives, led him to choose the medical faculty at the University of Helsinki. Even during his studies, he developed an interest in research. Initially, he focused on psychophysiological research, inspired by Eino Kaila. He earned his bachelor's degree in 1926 with the thesis "Farbentransformation und Farbenkontrast," and defended his doctoral dissertation later that same year, while still a medical student. In 1927, he obtained his licentiate degree and on the same day received his doctorate in medicine and surgery.

Granit early sought opportunities at foreign research institutes. A significant role model was Charles Scott Sherrington in Oxford, who was the leading researcher of the central nervous system of his time and who received the Nobel Prize in 1932. Granit began his research on the retina at the Johnson Foundation at the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia, which was a world leader in research on the physiology of vision in the 1930s. There, he worked alongside Haldan Keffer Hartline, one of the two researchers who later shared the Nobel Prize with Granit in 1967.

=== Professorship and Research Activities === In 1929, Granit became a docent (associate professor) in physiology at the University of Helsinki, serving as acting professor in 1935 and becoming a full professor in 1937. In Helsinki, he gathered a group of young researchers around him and continued to explore the function of the retina and the mechanisms of color vision. Together with Per-Olof Therman, he demonstrated that cells in the retina could react inhibitively to stimulation, consistent with what Sherrington had shown for other types of nerve cells. Together with Gunnar Svaetichin, he was able to show that the impulse sensitivity of the retina could be divided into three groups—for blue, green, and red—which was the first neurophysiological evidence for the Young-Helmholtz theory of color vision.

Granit received offers from various places around the world but declined them, including a professorship in Tartu in 1932 and an invitation to Harvard in 1940. However, when he was called to become the head of the neurophysiological institute at the Karolinska Institute in Solna, he decided to move. A significant reason for this decision was the considerably better research resources available there, but a contributing factor was the political climate of "purification" that had been prevalent at the University of Helsinki during the 1930s. He was appointed head of the newly established Nobel Institute and in 1946 received a personal professorship in neurophysiology, which he held until his retirement in 1967.

Granit conducted fundamental research on color vision, for which he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1967.

From the mid-1940s, Granit focused on the neurophysiology of human motor control. He investigated muscle function and the synapses that regulate muscle contraction, opening up a completely new area of research with his work. He presented his findings in numerous publications, including "Sensory mechanisms of the retina" (1947) and "Receptors and sensory perception" (1955).

Other works by Granit include "Ung mans väg till Minerva" (1941, revised edition 1958), "Finlandssvenskarna" (1944), and his memoir "Hur det kom sig" (1983).

=== Membership and Citizenship === Granit was elected a member of the Finnish Society of Sciences in 1937 and admitted as Swedish member number 912 of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1944. He served on the board of Samfundet Nordens Frihet from 1942 to 1945. He obtained Swedish citizenship in March 1941 and was a member of the Finnish parish in Stockholm.
