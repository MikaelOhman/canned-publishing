"Folkhemmet" is a metaphor for a society with a comprehensive social welfare system. It's a term that gained popularity after the Social Democrats began using it to describe the kind of society they aspired to create.

In Sweden, the concept of "folkhemmet" has become associated with the Swedish Social Democratic Labour Party and Per Albin Hansson, who introduced the term on January 18, 1928. He used it to describe a society that is a home for all its people, characterized by consensus and equality. Many welfare reforms between the 1930s and the 1950s were implemented through political consensus across party lines. While the term "folkhemmet" has been superseded by others, such as "the strong society," it has persisted with a nostalgic undertone, sometimes used poetically to refer to the Swedish welfare state.

== Background of the Concept ==

The term "folkhem" (literally "people's home") was used in Sweden from the late 19th century onwards, with various meanings, before it became a key concept within social democracy. In 1890, several Swedish newspapers reported on an initiative from Dresden, Germany, called "Volksheime," which was translated as "folkhem." These institutions were a combination of meeting places, libraries, and cafes, and can be seen as an early version of community centers, making the word in this sense related to concepts like public parks. In the following year, 1891, such a "folkhem" for workers opened in Sörnäs, Helsinki, initiated by Alli Trygg, and included a meeting hall, reading room, kindergarten, daycare center, and more. The idea gained traction in Sweden, including among the temperance movement. In 1895, city council member Josef Nyström proposed that Stockholm should also have a "folkhem" – sometimes referred to as a temperance restaurant – which was realized on October 1, 1898, when Adolf Fredriks Folkhem opened at Norrtullsgatan 7 in Stockholm. With this meaning, "folkhem" appeared in the 1908 edition of Nordisk familjebok, where it was defined as "a place where less affluent people can access books, newspapers, and writing materials for a small fee. Typically, coffee, tea, etc., are also served at the establishment."

In June 1909, the Swedish People's Association held a meeting in Högsby, where Minister Alfred Petersson gave a speech that began with: "The word 'home' is a word whose meaning none of us are unfamiliar with," and later continued, "Our society, what is it other than a human home, a large folkhem. Society is the form of human existence and human interaction. Our Swedish folkhem is a very old one." In a similar way, the term was later used by others, including Manfred Björkquist in a speech about defense issues at a meeting in Gothenburg on February 19, 1912, and later that same year by right-wing politician Rudolf Kjellén, who, in a debate article titled "Nationalism and Socialism" criticizing Hjalmar Branting, wrote: "One thing is certain: only on its own foundation can Sweden be developed into the happy folkhem it is meant to become. Only in nationalism is there salvation from the voices of this time that, in the name of misunderstood internationalism, want to lure broad segments of the population into disloyalty towards their divinely ordained living conditions..." Kjellén has been written about on several occasions as the one who coined the term.

== The Idea of the Swedish Welfare State ==

During a consultation debate in 1928, Per Albin Hansson incorporated the concept of "folkhemmet" (the people's home) into the Social Democratic rhetoric. Previously, he had also considered alternatives like "Medborgarhemmet" (the citizens' home), but ultimately emphasized the former term, which was more impactful (the speech was titled "The People's Home, the Citizens' Home"). In contrast to Rudolf Kjellén and other individuals in conservative or right-wing circles, he had already stressed in 1921 that the Social Democratic "home" would be established on a "firm foundation of democracy" and become "a good home for all Swedes."
