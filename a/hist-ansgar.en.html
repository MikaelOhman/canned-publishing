This article is about the apostle. For the name, see Ansgar (name).

Ansgar (also Latinized as Ansgarius), born September 8, 801, in Fouilloy near Amiens in present-day France, and died February 3, 865, in Bremen, was a Frankish Benedictine monk, missionary, and Archbishop of Hamburg-Bremen. He is best known for his early attempts to spread Christianity in Sweden and has been called the Apostle of the North after his death. Ansgar worked during the Carolingian era and had the support of Emperor Louis the Pious in his missionary journeys to Denmark and Birka in Sweden in the 820s and 850s. In Birka, he established a Christian community and preached the Gospel with some success. In 834, he was appointed Archbishop in the newly founded archbishopric of Hamburg, which later merged with Bremen into a joint archdiocese. Ansgar's life and work are described in the contemporary hagiography *Vita Anskarii*, written by his disciple and successor, Rimbert. Ansgar was canonized shortly after his death and is venerated within the Roman Catholic Church, with his feast day on February 3. Although his efforts did not have an immediate lasting impact on the establishment of Christianity in Sweden, he is considered one of the most important figures in the early Christian mission to Scandinavia.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Youth === Ansgar, who had Frankish parents, was born in the year 801 in Picardy, France. His mother died when he was only five years old, and shortly thereafter, his father left him, likely to the Benedictine monastery of Corbie on the Somme River, just south of Amiens. There, he received his education and became head of a school department. In 822, he was sent to the newly founded monastery of Corvey on the Weser River in North Rhine-Westphalia, where he served as a monk, leading the new monastic school and its instruction.

He often had strange dreams. One night, he dreamt that he was lying on his deathbed. He was then transported to a dark and terrifying place, where he suffered terribly and could not breathe. This was purgatory, which people of that time believed all humans must pass through in order to be cleansed of their sins. After torments that seemed to last for millennia, he was taken to heaven, to the dwelling place of the blessed. They sang heavenly hymns and turned towards the east, from where a light emanated. Ansgar understood that God resided in that light, and he heard God's voice: "Go forth, and return to me when you have won the crown of martyrdom!" From that time on, Ansgar had a burning desire: to suffer martyrdom for the teachings of Jesus, like other missionaries who were killed by pagan peoples.

=== The Mission Assignment === In 826, the Danish king Harald Klak was baptized. He requested missionaries, and Ansgar voluntarily traveled to Denmark from Cologne by boat across the Rhine, passing through Dorestad and Friesland. However, Harald Klak's position was not strong in Denmark, and Ansgar remained south of the Elbe River, where he established a school for Danish boys.

=== First Journey to Birka === Around the year 830, when Louis the Pious, a Carolingian emperor, was visited by two envoys from the Swedish king, who stated that there were many in their homeland who wished to convert to Christianity, he appointed Ansgar to spread the faith. Ansgar and his companion, the monk Witmar, traveled with a convoy of merchants, but halfway through, they were attacked by Vikings. The merchants defended themselves but were defeated. They lost their ships and everything on board. Only with great difficulty did they manage to reach land and escape. On this occasion, the missionaries also lost the royal gifts they were supposed to bring to Sweden, and everything they themselves possessed, except for a few small items they could carry away during their flight from the ship. Among the losses were nearly 40 books that they had collected for worship.

After this, some decided to return, but Ansgar refused to abandon his journey. He saw God's will in what had happened to him and decided not to return until he received a sign from God indicating whether he could be permitted to preach the Word in these lands.

The missionaries were now forced to travel to Birka on foot. The Swedish king mentioned for this period is Björn, as recorded in Ansgar's writings. In Birka, Ansgar and his companions were "received favorably by King Bjorn," according to Rimbert's account. "When the king learned why the foreigners had come, he consulted with his advisors. With their consent, he gave them permission to stay there and preach the Gospel of Christ. Anyone who wished could receive instruction from them."

A small congregation was formed, with Herigar, the chieftain of Birka, as a prominent member. In Birka, Ansgar proclaimed Christianity around 829-831. However, the success was modest, and the Swedish king's invitation should probably be considered a friendly gesture to the deeply Christian emperor. [citation needed]

=== The Bishopric === After Ansgar returned from Scandinavia, he was appointed bishop of the newly established archbishopric of Hamburg on May 15, 834.
