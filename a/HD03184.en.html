Private Copy Compensation

TL;DRThe government proposes new regulations for compensation when copying copyrighted material for private use. The goal is to ensure fairness for the creators of the works, while also preventing the cost from being too high for consumers who purchase electronic devices.
In plain language

When you copy music, movies, or images for private use, companies that sell electronics pay a small fee. This fee goes to the creators of the works, such as musicians, authors, and television companies. This is called a private copying compensation.

The government wants to change the rules to better suit modern technology. Currently, the rules only apply to devices that can record audio or video. The proposed changes will extend the rules to all types of electronics, meaning that other types of electronic devices may also be subject to the regulations.

To avoid making it too expensive for consumers, the government proposes lower fees for items such as hard drives, mobile phones, and computers. For printers and copiers, a fixed fee will be implemented instead. The government also wants to make it easier for companies and copyright holders to agree on what constitutes a fair payment.

If the parties cannot reach an agreement, there is a proposal for a mediator to help resolve the dispute. If the proposal is approved, the new laws will come into effect on September 1, 2026.

Full text

Authors and other rights holders have the right to reasonable compensation for private copying of their protected works and performances. In Sweden, and in most other EU countries, this compensation is paid by companies that manufacture or import electronic products that can be used for private copying. In the proposal, suggestions are made to ensure an equivalent right to reasonable compensation for rights holders, as well as simpler, more predictable, and competition-neutral rules for companies, and compensation levels that guarantee that the burden on consumers does not become too great. It is proposed that all authors whose exclusive rights have been infringed for private copying and whose works have been made available for commercial purposes shall have the right to compensation, unless they are only affected economically to a minor extent. The same right shall apply to performing artists, producers of audio or video recordings, and radio and television companies. Regarding electronic products that trigger the compensation obligation, it is proposed to remove the limitation to devices that can record audio or video. This means that the regulation will essentially be technology-neutral. A so-called voluntary secondary liability is proposed for the electronics industry, as well as a right to reimbursement of compensation in cases such as re-export. The compensation per storage space for storage media, such as hard drives, and multi-functional devices, such as mobile phones and computers, is reduced and combined with ceilings. For devices for reproduction, such as copiers and printers, a fixed compensation level is introduced. The parties involved may use the reduction rules to ensure that the compensation is reasonable. To facilitate agreements between the parties, a new possibility is proposed to have a mediator appointed by the Patent and Market Court. The amendments to the law are proposed to come into force on September 1, 2026.

Proposal for a Parliamentary Decision

The Government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament adopts the Government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (1960:729) on Copyright in Literary and Artistic Works.
  2. The Parliament adopts the Government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2016:977) on Collective Management of Copyright.
  3. The Parliament adopts the Government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2017:322) on Mediation in Certain Copyright Disputes.
