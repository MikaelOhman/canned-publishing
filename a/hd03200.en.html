New rules to promote central clearing of over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives in the EU.

TL;DRThe EU wants to change the rules governing the trading of financial agreements to make the economy more secure. The proposal includes, among other things, giving the Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen) greater powers to issue fines for violations.
In plain language

The government proposes new laws that comply with EU regulations for the trading of so-called derivatives. Derivatives are agreements between two parties that are traded outside of major stock exchanges. To reduce risks, the aim is to have more of these agreements go through a central counterparty, which is an intermediary that acts as a security in the transaction.

A key part of the proposal is that the Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen), the authority that oversees the financial market in Sweden, will be given more power. They will be able to levy special fees that function as fines if companies violate EU regulations. This will be possible in more situations than is currently allowed.

The proposal also addresses how information can be shared between authorities. The aim is to introduce rules that allow certain confidential information to be sent from other authorities to the government. This is necessary so that information can be shared quickly in the event of an economic crisis in the EU.

Finally, the rules for investment funds are being changed. These are funds that trade in securities. The aim is to limit the amount of risk that such a fund can take by investing too much money in the same counterparty in its agreements. If the new laws are passed, they are planned to come into effect on June 25, 2026.

Full text

The proposal outlines the legislative measures necessary due to changes in the EU regulation on over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives, central counterparties (CCPs), and transaction repositories, as well as the EU directive on undertakings for collective investment in transferable securities (UCITS). OTC derivatives are derivative contracts traded bilaterally between buyers and sellers outside of regulated markets. A central counterparty is an entity in the financial infrastructure that acts as an intermediary between parties in a financial transaction.

The changes primarily involve the following:

  • The Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen) will be authorized to impose a so-called "special fee" (a type of sanction fee) in more cases than currently possible, for violations of the EU regulation.
  • A provision regarding the transfer of confidential information from certain other authorities to the government is introduced, due to a new provision on information sharing in crisis situations in the EU regulation.
  • The restriction on the permissible exposure of a UCITS fund to a single counterparty in transactions involving derivative instruments will apply to all derivative instruments that have not been submitted for clearing with a CCP, and not only to OTC derivatives.

The proposed legislative changes are scheduled to come into effect on June 25, 2026.

Proposed Parliamentary Decision

The government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament approves the government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2013:287) on supplementary provisions to the EU regulation on OTC derivatives, central counterparties, and transaction repositories.
  2. The Parliament approves the government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2004:46) on undertakings for collective investment in transferable securities (UCITS).
  3. The Parliament approves the government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2009:400) on freedom of information and confidentiality.
