Olof Edvin Verner "Eyvind" Johnson, born July 29, 1900, in Björkelund, Överluleå parish, and died August 25, 1976, in Stockholm, was a Swedish author and translator.

Eyvind Johnson was a writer associated with the working class who was influenced by contemporary literary trends. He is considered one of the most important innovators of modern novel writing in Sweden. He debuted in 1924 and achieved his breakthrough in the 1930s with the autobiographical novel series "The Novel About Olof." His major works include the "Krilon Trilogy," "Strändernas svall," and "Hans nådes tid."

He was elected a member of the Swedish Academy (chair number 11) in 1957 and received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1974, jointly with Harry Martinson – the last occasion on which the Nobel Prize in Literature has been shared between two individuals.

== Biography ==

Eyvind Johnson (originally named Olof Edvin Verner Jonsson) was born on July 29, 1900, in the outskirts of Boden. His father, Olof Jonsson, came from Värmland, and his mother, Cevia Gustafsdotter, was from Fridlevstad in Blekinge. They moved to Northern Sweden because they followed railway workers as the railroad was being built. The father worked as a stonemason, building bridges and walls where the tracks would go, while the mother worked with a traveling bakery that supplied bread to the workers. Eyvind Johnson grew up in very modest circumstances. At the age of four, due to poverty, he was sent to live with a foster family, who were relatives, nearby. He attended elementary school in Svartbjörnsbyn until he was thirteen years old, which was his only formal education. In 1913, he moved with his foster parents to Hedberget/Näsberg, a few kilometers south of Murjek, where the foster father found work at the Näsberg quarry.

In the early summer of 1915, he left home and supported himself through various jobs, including working as a timber float operator, sawmill worker, and movie theater attendant. In the fall of the same year, his father died after a long illness. In 1917, he joined the Boden branch of the syndicalist labor movement and served as its secretary for a time. In 1919, he moved to Stockholm as a socialist agitator and became a member of the Young Socialists and LS of SAC (Swedish Association of Art Critics). There, he wrote articles for socialist newspapers such as *Brand*, *Syndikalisten*, and *Arbetaren*. He regularly contributed articles and revolutionary poems under the name Eyvind Ung to *Brand* until 1924. In Stockholm, he also became good friends with Rudolf Värnlund, and together with other young writers, they started the magazine *Vår nutid*. In 1921, he left Stockholm and traveled as a stowaway on a ship to Germany. He spent most of the next ten years on the continent, first in Berlin and then in Paris, where he lived a very poor life, supported by occasional jobs and meager payments from Sweden for newspaper articles, short stories, and novels.

In the fall of 1923, Eyvind Johnson temporarily returned to Sweden and became secretary of the executive committee of the Young Socialist Association, but left the association in 1924 after a split. The same year, he debuted as an author with the collection of short stories *De fyra främlingarna*. In 1925, he traveled to Berlin again and lived in Paris from 1925 to 1930, where he earned a living as a writer. In 1927, he married Aase Christoffersen (1900–1938) and they settled in Saint-Leu-la-Forêt, just outside of Paris, the same year. In 1930, he moved back to Sweden and later achieved a major breakthrough with *Romanen om Olof*, a partially autobiographical novel series in four parts. The novel depicts what it was like to grow up as a young man in Northern Sweden during the 1910s. Johnson himself grew up in Björkelund, near Boden, in Norrbotten County, where his childhood home has been preserved. The experiences and environments described in the novel series were new to literature at that time, but the series also represents his most consistent attempt at using internal monologue; almost everything in the plot is described from Olof's consciousness at the time it occurs. Jan Troell filmed the work under the title *Här har du ditt liv*.

Eyvind Johnson was deeply troubled by the rise of Nazism and Fascism in Europe during the 1930s. In 1934, he contributed to the anti-fascist publication "Mänsklighet," and his anti-Nazi stance was also reflected in the book reviews he wrote for the newspaper "Arbetet" from 1932 to 1939. During the Spanish Civil War, he became actively involved in the movement that sought to support the Republican side. He was one of the initiators of the collection "Till Madrid," which featured contributions from twenty-five authors expressing solidarity with the Republican cause.

During World War II, Eyvind Johnson served as editor of the Norwegian resistance newspaper "Håndslag" from 1942 to 1945, and also wrote actively for the journal of "Samfundet Nordens Frihet." He was strongly critical of Sweden's neutrality policy. Johnson's strong opposition to Nazism is reflected in his novel "Nattövning" (1938), which features Swedish Nazis, as well as in the three novels about Krilon, where world politics are depicted allegorically, with characters inspired by "Nordens Frihet."
