Harry Edmund Martinson, born May 6, 1904, in Jämshög, Blekinge province, and died February 11, 1978, in Solna (at the time residing in Sollentuna), was a Swedish author. Martinson was a member of the Swedish Academy (chair no. 15) from 1949 and received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1974, together with Eyvind Johnson – the last time a Nobel Prize in Literature has been shared between two individuals.

== Biography ==

=== Background === Martinson lost his parents at a young age. He was only six years old when his father, Martin Olofsson, died of pulmonary tuberculosis in 1910, and his mother emigrated to Oregon, USA, a year later. She settled in Portland, and the orphaned Harry and his siblings were placed in foster homes – a fate that somewhat mirrored that of Viktor Rydberg, an author who, like Rudyard Kipling, Joseph Conrad, and Leo Tolstoy, was one of Martinson's literary influences. He attended elementary school for six years, including at Alltidhult School in Blekinge. Martinson spent a short time in an orphanage in Gothenburg and at the Seamen's Training Center in Karlskrona. Later, he worked at Jonsereds Fabriker in the industrial town of Jonsered.

At the age of sixteen, he went to sea and sailed around the world, including to Brazil and India, but due to a lung condition, he had to disembark permanently in 1927. During his years as a sailor and afterwards, he often traveled on foot. Both the loss of his parents and his experiences as a sailor and vagrant left a clear mark on his writing. His concept of the "World Nomad," presented in an essay in the early 1930s, influenced much of his poetry.

During his years as an unemployed seaman, Martinson met the writer Moa Martinson, whom he married in 1929. The marriage was turbulent but artistically inspiring for both of them. Martinson sometimes fled from her small farm in Sorunda parish where they lived and embarked on new journeys on foot, but he returned until a final breakdown in 1939 and their divorce in 1941. Martinson volunteered in the Finnish Winter War. He was married to Ingrid Lindcrantz from 1942.

=== The Nobel Prize and the Final Years === In 1974, Harry Martinson was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature, with the citation: "for a literary authorship which, through its sources in observation and empathy, has created images of man's conflict and joy." He shared the prize with Eyvind Johnson. During the award ceremony, Karl Ragnar Gierow emphasized in his speech that Johnson and Martinson represented "a group of proletarian writers or working-class poets who, on a broad front, entered our literature not to destroy and plunder, but to enrich it with their talents." The awarding of the prize was appreciated by the reading public, but it was heavily criticized from several quarters in the Swedish press (especially by critics who were politically left-leaning), primarily because the Swedish Academy had appointed two of its own members as recipients.

The increasingly harsh criticism from some parts of the critical establishment, including criticism that he himself was a member of the Swedish Academy when he received the Nobel Prize, along with his own declining health, ultimately contributed to him taking his own life in 1978. The details surrounding his death are described in Lars Gyllensten's memoirs, where the suicide is depicted as seppuku (a form of ritual suicide).

Harry Martinson is buried at Silverdal Cemetery in Sollentuna parish.

== Literary Works and Reception ==

Martinson debuted on August 27, 1927, with the poem "Sanningssökare" in the newspaper Arbetaren. Around the same time, he met Artur Lundkvist, who introduced him to the poetry of the American Carl Sandburg, which became a significant literary influence. From 1927 to 1928, Martinson had poems published in various publications including Arbetare-Kuriren, Eldaren, Sjömannen, Ny Tid, and Brand.

Martinson contributed to the anthology "Fem unga" in 1929, and that same year marked his book debut with the collection of poems "Spökskepp." He achieved a major breakthrough with the poetry collection "Nomad" in 1931. With the travelogues "Resor utan mål" (1932) and "Kap Farväl!" (1933), he also gained significant recognition as a prose writer. He was praised for his concise and boldly associative language. Anders Österling hailed Martinson as "one of the greatest natural talents ever to emerge in Swedish literature." However, the expressionistic poetry collection "Natur" (1934) received a lukewarm reception, leading Martinson to focus solely on publishing prose books for eleven years.

The autobiographical novel "Nässlorna blomma" (1935) became another major success and was followed by "Vägen ut" (1936). In the following years, he published a series of smaller books that ranged between delicate observations of nature and sharp critiques of society: "Svärmare och harkrank" (1937), "Midsommardalen" (1938), and "Det enkla och det svåra" (1939). The book "Verklighet till döds" (1940) was dominated by social commentary, depicting the author's experiences as a volunteer in the Finnish Winter War. Notable works in his oeuvre include the poetry collection "Passad" (1945) and the novel "Vägen till Klockrike" (1948).
