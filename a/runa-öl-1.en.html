The Karlevisten stone stands on the island of Öland and is one of the oldest runestone inscriptions found in Sweden. It was erected to commemorate a man named Sibbe, who was a chieftain from Denmark. It is believed that he died while traveling through the area and was buried there by his companions.

The text on the stone is unique because it is written in a script known as "plowscript." This means that one must tilt their head or read in different directions to understand the order of the characters, much like reading a winding snake. The inscription tells us that Sibbe was a powerful leader and that he was buried in a nearby burial mound.

Researchers have carefully studied the stone to understand its meaning. Because runestones are the only texts written by people during the Viking Age, they are extremely important for learning about how people lived, what names they had, and where they came from over a thousand years ago.

Full text

The Karlevi Stone (inscription Öl 1) is Öland's oldest runestone, erected around the year 1000.

== Description == The stone is a 130-centimeter-high glacial erratic of gray quartz porphyry, likely originating from the area west of Oskarshamn. It stands in its original location, now a field in the Karlevi village wetlands in the parish of Vickleby, near Kalmarsund, and was erected around the year 1000, probably in the late 9th century, in memory of a Danish chieftain, Sibbe the Good or Sibbe the Wise, son of Foldar. A drawing from the 17th century shows that it stood between two now-cultivated burial mounds. The inscription is somewhat unusual because it is written in a form of plow script where the reading direction changes with each line, but maintains a left-to-right orientation by inverting the runes when the reading direction changes, making it appear as if written on a winding snake. According to the inscription, the deceased was buried in one of the mounds. The fact that the stone stands far away on a desolate shore suggests that Sibbe died during a journey and was laid to rest on land by his retinue. Some believe that the stone was erected by Danes who were returning home after participating in the Battle of Fýrisvellir near Uppsala around the year 985. The philologist Sven Söderberg concludes that the inscription dates to the late 10th century; the stone was erected in memory of a Danish chieftain who was laid to rest next to it, and the inscription was carved by a Norwegian skald who was part of the Danish chieftain's retinue.

Translation into modern Swedish:

This stone was erected in memory of Sibbe the Wise, son of Foldr, but his companions placed it on the island [this memorial]. A hero whose life, as everyone knew, was marked by great deeds, lies hidden in this burial mound. No more virtuous and powerful sea warrior will ever rule over the land in the Danish kingdom. The inscription consists of a shorter prose section that names the chieftain: "This stone was erected in memory of Sibbe [the] Wise, son of Foldr, but his companions placed it on the island [this memorial]." Then there is a longer section in the form of an elaborate poetic verse:

Hidden lies he whose deeds were greatest, as most knew—greatest deeds of warrior Trud in this burial mound. It is said that Righteous-Vidur will rule with strength in Denmark, Endil, destroyer of Jormungandr, over the land. Which translates as: "Hidden lies he whose deeds were greatest, as most knew, 'Trud's warrior' in this mound. No more righteous and powerful 'Vagn-Vidur' will rule over the vast lands of the sea king in Denmark." The poetic verse is written in the meter dróttkvætt, and the poem on the Karlevistenen, along with the Sigtunadosa and runic inscriptions from Bryggen in Bergen, is one of the few verses in this meter that have been preserved in their original form. The poem contains several kennings. Trud is the name of a goddess, and "Trud's warrior" means chieftain or leader.

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary, native sources from the Viking Age – they were carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they describe what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some interpretations are uncertain – these are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
