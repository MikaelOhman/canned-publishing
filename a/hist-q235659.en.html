Gunilla Bielke, born June 25, 1568, and died June 25, 1597, was the Queen of Sweden between 1585 and 1592, through her marriage to Johan III. She served as Johan's advisor and, through him, exerted a well-documented influence on Swedish politics.

== Biography == Gunilla Bielke was the daughter of Margareta Axelsdotter (Posse) and Johan Axelsson (Bielke) of Häradssäter and Räfvelsta, a member of the royal council and governor of Östergötland. She became an orphan at a young age and, at the age of ten, was sent to the royal court for her upbringing. In 1582, she served as a lady-in-waiting to Queen Katarina Jagellonica.

About a year and a half after Katarina Jagellonica's death in 1583, the king began looking for a new queen, and his choice fell on the sixteen-year-old Gunilla Johansdotter Bielke. The king's siblings, both Duke Karl and Cecilia, Anna, Elisabet, and especially Katarina, tried in every way to persuade him to abandon this relationship and instead choose a queen from a foreign royal house.

According to Johan's account, his eldest sister Katarina was the one who "most used her words and protested about this," even claiming "among other insulting remarks, that the devil had torn many pairs of shoes before the Swedish queen reached her final position." In a letter to his sister Elisabet, in which he complained about this, he added: "He who wants to be well regarded by us should not provoke us into anger without reason. Otherwise, we will find ways to ensure that they regret it, and that what goes around comes around."

Johan remained steadfast. As a reason for not choosing a foreign princess, he stated that his children would be less politically influenced by an alliance with a foreign royal house, but also that one could not trust portraits during marriage negotiations, and therefore it was safer to "choose someone from within the country who had actually been seen." He also pointed out that even his father had married members of the Swedish nobility, that a foreign marriage would cost time and money, and that he wanted to get married quickly. He also argued that a domestic subject could not demand the same things as a foreign princess but would instead be grateful, obedient, and willing to serve, as expected of a wife.

Gunilla Bielke herself opposed the marriage. She had previously agreed to marry Per Jonsson (Liljesparre), and when the king announced his choice, she firmly refused his proposal. According to tradition, he was so upset by this that he slapped her in the face with his glove and left. Gunilla Bielke's relatives disagreed, believing that a connection to the royal house was an opportunity not to be missed, and they pressured her with pleas and threats until she finally gave her consent.

The wedding took place in Västerås on February 21, 1585. It was followed by Gunilla Bielke's coronation the next day. The wedding and coronation were very lavish, and Johan was particularly careful to ensure that they were magnificent due to criticism of Gunilla Bielke's status and rank. Special coronation regalia were made for the occasion. Among the guests were the heir apparent Sigismund and his daughter Anna, widowed Queen Katarina Stenbock, and the king's sister, Duchess Sofia of Saxe-Lauenburg, with her son Gustav, and members of the nobility related to the royal house. Only Sofia attended the wedding from Johan's siblings. This caused a schism between the siblings, and Johan financially punished Elisabet and Cecilia, among other things.

The conflict with his brother Karl was particularly difficult. In response to the invitation to the wedding, the duke wrote: "However, we would rather have wished that Your Majesty had chosen a higher and more noble marriage, but since this has been pleasing to Your Majesty, we wish you happiness and God's blessing, and pray that it may benefit Your Majesty, Your Majesty's dear children, land, and kingdom." The ducal couple further thanked him for the invitation. "But," they wrote, "since time is too short for us and we have not heard of such a royal celebration before, we could not afford to attend this important event. We therefore kindly request that Your Majesty excuse us."

The ceremony was officiated in the cathedral by Archbishop Andreas Laurentii Björnram and was followed by tournaments, for which Duke Sigismund had sent out invitations, where the newly crowned queen presented the prizes. Ericus Jacobi Skinnerus had written a Latin celebratory poem in hexameter for the wedding.

=== Queen ===

The marriage had an influence on the king. Queen Gunilla, who was staunchly Protestant, gradually cooled Johan's enthusiasm for Catholic doctrine, although he never abandoned his dreams of imposing the Catholic Church's liturgy on the Swedish church. Bielke is believed to have influenced Johan III and his policies in a Protestant direction. She intervened several times in favor of persecuted anti-liturgy advocates.
