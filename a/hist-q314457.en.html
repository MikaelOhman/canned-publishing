Magnus Eriksson, born in either April or May 1316 and died on December 1, 1374 (drowning), was King of Sweden from 1319 to 1364, King of Norway from 1319 to 1355 as Magnus VII, and King of Scania from 1332 to 1360. He was the son of Duke Eric Magnusson and the Norwegian princess Ingeborg Hakansdotter, and a grandson of Magnus Ladulås. He married Blanche of Namur in 1335.

Magnus Eriksson reigned for 44 years and 222 days, making him the second-longest reigning monarch in Swedish history, surpassed only by the current King Carl XVI Gustaf. He and his son Haakon (co-regent) were the last Swedish kings from the Bjälbo dynasty (also known as the Folkung dynasty).

== The Regency Period ==

Magnus Eriksson, grandson of King Magnus Ladulås, son of Duke Eric Magnusson and Ingeborg, the daughter of the Norwegian king Håkon Magnusson, was born in April or May 1316. Only three years old, he was proclaimed king of Norway after his grandfather Håkon's death (1319), and on July 8th of the same year, following the establishment of a union treaty, the Oslo Treaty of 1319, between the two kingdoms, he was also elected king of Sweden. The so-called "Freedom Charter," which has been called "Sweden's Magna Carta," was drawn up in connection with his election as King of Sweden in 1319, at a meeting held in Uppsala in 1319.

Initially, Magnus' mother, Duchess Ingeborg, had a significant influence on the government in both kingdoms. Ingeborg was present in Oslo when the union treaty was signed, and the treaty mentions her in a way that could be interpreted as granting her certain powers. After Magnus ascended to the throne, she became a member of the Swedish Council of State (Riksrådet). She received Axvalls castle and its associated lands for her maintenance and held court at Varberg Castle, where her son was raised. Thus, she held a geographically strategic position, and at Varberg Castle she surrounded herself with former advisors to Eric Magnusson, such as the Danish commander Knut Porse and the van Kyren family from Holstein. However, suspicion and conflict quickly arose between Ingeborg and the other members of the Council of State. The Lord High Constable (Drotsen) Mats Kettilmundsson was forced to resign and was replaced by Knut Jonsson, the district judge (lagman) of Östergötland. At a council meeting in Skara in the summer of 1322, the nobles of the Council of State pledged to put aside old disputes from the civil war and that no one would enter into legal agreements with Ingeborg without the consent of the entire council.

In an attempt to avoid Danish involvement in Swedish politics, the Council of State sought to maintain good relations with the Danish King Eric Menved. However, Ingeborg and her circle maintained contact with Eric Menved's enemies in Denmark and formed alliances with his opponents in Germany. In the summer of 1321, a treaty was signed between the heir apparent Magnus and Henry II of Mecklenburg. The treaty included a marriage agreement between Magnus' younger sister Eufemia and Duke Albert of Mecklenburg, as well as a promise of mutual assistance in the event of an attack from Denmark. Secretly, they also agreed on a military attack against Scania. Such an attack was prepared in 1322 by Knut Porse, but the plans ultimately failed.

During the years 1323–1326, the Council of State managed to buy Ingeborg's castles of Axevall, Varberg, and Hunehals, and they removed her from her position on the Council of State, which was then led by Lord High Constable Knut Jonsson. This example was followed in 1323 by the Norwegians, who appointed Herr Erling Vidkunsson as Regent (Riksföreståndare). The union between the two kingdoms was subsequently limited to a defense alliance, according to the terms of the union treaty. A peace treaty was signed with the Republic of Novgorod in 1323, ending a long period of strained relations.

== After Ascension to the Throne ==

Magnus came of age in either 1331 or 1332, and in 1335 he appointed his marshal and his chancellor. The marshal, Håkan Mattsson, is referred to as "marskalk" (marshal) in Latin documents: "haquinum mattesson marskalcum" and "haquinus mattysson marscalcus," on February 28th, in connection with a will.

On May 18, 1335, King Magnus Eriksson appointed Nils Abjörnsson as his chancellor. A letter mentions his councilors and closest advisors: Knut Jonsson (of the Aspenäsätten family), Knut Magnusson (a lawman), Lars Ulfsson (of Ama), Gustaf Tunasson (of Vingätten), Sune Jonsson (of Båt), Greger Magnusson, Gisle Elinesson (Sparre of Aspnäs), Karl Näskonungsson, Ulf Abjörnsson (Sparre), Knut Folkesson, Karl Bengtsson (Örnfot), Karl Tukesson Läma, Anund Sture, Ulf Filipsson (Ulv), Karl Orestesson (Färla), Ulf Gudmarsson (Ulvåsaätten), Harald Karlsson (Stubbe), Tyrgils Klemensson (Färla), Gustav Nilsson (Bielke), Inge Kettilbjörnsson (Gren), Håkan Mattsson the younger, Peter Jonsson (Bååt), Sigge Magnusson (of Håkan Tunesson's family).

Magnus Eriksson was crowned in Stockholm in 1336.

Scania and Blekinge were under the rule of Count John of Holstein, and his administration there, with the help of German officials, had caused strong discontent. A delegation from Scania, led by Bishop Karl Eriksen of Lund, came to Kalmar in 1332 and declared that they would rather be ruled from Sweden. The count began negotiations with the Swedes, and it was agreed that the Swedish king would redeem the fiefdom for 34,000 marks of silver. Shortly thereafter, Magnus traveled to Scania, where he was acclaimed at the assembly in Lund in June 1332. Valdemar Atterdag wanted to restore order in Denmark after the internal conflicts there.
