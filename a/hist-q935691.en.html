Laurentius Petri Gothus (Lars Petersson), born in 1529 or 1530 in Söderköping, Östergötland (hence the surname), and died on February 12, 1579, in Uppsala, was a Swedish archbishop and hymn writer. He is also known as Laurentius Petri the Younger, to distinguish him from his father-in-law, Laurentius Petri Nericius.

== Biography == Laurentius Petri Gothus studied at the University of Wittenberg from around 1546, where he became a student of Philipp Melanchthon and received his master's degree in 1558 with the help of Duke Erik (later Eric XIV). Along with Olaus Petri, he was one of the most important hymn writers of his time; he also wrote poetry, including a Latin poem about the Goths, "Strategema Gothici Exercitus adversus Darium" in 1559, which later inspired Gothicism. After returning home in 1561, Laurentius Petri Gothus was appointed court preacher. From 1566, he was a professor of Greek at Uppsala University. He was one of the key editors of the hymn book of 1567 and the edition of 1572. In 1574, he was appointed archbishop, despite not receiving a majority vote from the electoral college; the king appointed him in December after he accepted the king's proposal, which would lead to an attempt to re-Catholicize Sweden; this mainly took liturgical forms and faced strong opposition. He thus moved closer to Catholicism and allowed himself to be ordained in the Catholic manner (anointed in July 1575). Laurentius Petri Gothus strongly supported the reformist efforts expressed in "Johan Nova ordinanta et liturgia," whose preface he likely wrote, and which he defended at the Diet of 1577. However, the following year, he broke with the liturgical movement and published "Contra novas papistarum machinationes." In 1576, he had a new hymn book published, and in the following year, Luther's Small Catechism. He was married to Margareta Larsdotter, daughter of his predecessor, Laurentius Petri Nericius. They had two daughters. His nephew, Laurentius Paulinus Gothus, became archbishop in 1637.

== Hymns == "A Sinful Man" (appears in the "Göteborgspsalmboken" of 1650 and 1695, number 251; in the 1937 edition, number 254). Available on Wikisource. "The Whole World Laments" (1695, number 399). He is also represented in the Danish "Psalmebog for Kirke og Hjem."
