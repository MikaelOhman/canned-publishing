Nils Gustaf Dalén, born November 30, 1869, in the parish of Stenstorp, Skaraborg County, and died December 9, 1937, in the parish of Lidingö, Stockholm County, was a Swedish civil engineer, inventor, and business leader. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1912 and elected to the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1913.

Gustaf Dalén was employed as a development engineer at the company Aga (Aktiebolaget Gasaccumulator) in 1906 and, after three years, was appointed CEO of the company. In a short period, Gustaf Dalén led Aga to become a world leader, primarily in lighthouses that became known as "Aga lights." Aga's breakthrough in the global market came with a major order for lighthouses in 1911 for the Panama Canal. A tragic accident occurred during a test in 1912 involving acetylene gas cylinders used to test maximum pressure resistance; the cylinders exploded, severely burning him and causing him to lose his sight in both eyes. Despite this, he continued as the company's undisputed leader until his death in 1937. Shortly after the accident in 1912, it was announced that Gustaf Dalén had been awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics. Dalén donated a significant portion of the prize money to Aga employees through an extra week's salary and also established a fund at Chalmers University of Technology.

== Family == Gustaf Dalén's parents were Anders Johansson and Lovisa Andersdotter, a farming family at Skräddaregården in Stenstorp, a small village located along the Western Main Line railway between Falköping and Skövde. Gustaf Dalén grew up with the surname Johansson but later changed it to Dalén, following the example of his older brothers. The name was likely taken from the region of Dala, where the family lived for a year during the 1860s, although before Gustaf was born. His mother ensured that all her children had the opportunity to pursue higher education; one brother became a provost, and another, Albin, became an ophthalmologist. In 1901, Gustaf Dalén married Elma Persson (1872–1946) from Borgunda, and they had four children who reached adulthood: two daughters and two sons:

  • Maja (1904–1995), law graduate, member of the county council, married Silfverstolpe
  • Gunnar (1905–1970), civil engineer
  • Anders (1907–1994), physician
  • Inga-Lisa (1910–2006), married Keen

(A daughter, Greta Maria, was born and died in 1902; see the National Archives.)

== Biography ==

Gustaf Dalén grew up with four brothers and one sister. Unlike his siblings, he showed more aptitude for practical skills than academic pursuits, and at a young age, he was involved in creating various complex inventions. After completing elementary school, he attended a folk high school and agricultural college in Stenstorp, where he received training in dairy management and gardening. In the years following his education, he worked on the family farm, demonstrating both technical ingenuity and entrepreneurial spirit. He started a seed business and a beekeeping operation, and also ran a small dairy on the farm. He was also involved in building and repairing various agricultural machines. Over time, he became increasingly interested in more advanced mechanical engineering and natural sciences.

In 1892, he traveled to Stockholm to showcase one of his inventions to Gustaf de Laval (one of the leading inventors of the time who worked with separators and small steam turbines), a device that could easily determine the fat content of milk, allowing farmers to be paid by the dairy based on the milk's fat percentage. This meeting with Laval proved crucial for his career path, as Laval advised him to pursue a solid technical education. After a period of self-study and private tutoring, he was accepted into Chalmers Technical Institute in Gothenburg, where he earned a degree in engineering in 1896 (Chalmers received university status in 1937). As a young engineer, he was fascinated by the bicycle as a means of transportation and had specific ideas about how to improve its braking system. He even brought his bicycle to a photo shoot in a studio in 1895.

After graduating from Chalmers, he went to Switzerland to study for a year at the Polytechnic Institute in Zurich, where Professor Aurel Stodola (1859–1942) worked in the mechanical engineering department, specializing in turbines. The Polytechnic was one of the leading technical institutions in Europe at the time, and Dalén primarily studied various types of steam turbines. During his visit to Stockholm in 1892, Dalén had been promised a job with Gustaf de Laval, which likely motivated him to pursue further education in turbine technology. However, when Dalén returned to Sweden, Gustaf de Laval's company was facing significant financial difficulties and could not offer him employment.

Upon returning to Sweden in 1897, he worked in Gothenburg on experiments involving a hot air turbine, as well as related air compressors and pumps. This work continued at Gustaf de Laval's Steam Turbine AB in Saltsjö-Järla, Nacka. At the same time, he was working at Dalén & Celsing, a company he had founded with a fellow student from Chalmers.
