Sofia Vasa, born October 29, 1547, and died March 17, 1611, was a Swedish princess and Duchess of Saxe-Lauenburg. She was the fourth daughter of Gustav Vasa and Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). She is buried in Strängnäs.

== Biography == Like her sisters, Sofia likely learned German as a child, in addition to household chores and public performances. After her mother's death in 1551, she was placed under the care of Kristina Gyllenstierna, and later by her aunts, Märta Eriksdotter Leionhufvud, sometimes called "Queen Märta," and Brita, before her father remarried to Katarina Stenbock. In 1556, she, like her sisters, had her portrait painted, poems praising her virtues were written by the court poet Henricus Mollerus, and she was provided with a dowry of 100,000 daler to be presented on the political marriage market.

During the Nordic Seven Years' War, Dowager Queen Dorothea of Denmark proposed a peace treaty through a marital alliance between Sofia and the younger brother of Frederick II. However, these plans did not materialize and did not have the support of the Danish monarch. On July 4, 1568, Sofia married Duke Magnus II of Saxe-Lauenburg, nephew of Gustav Vasa's first wife, Katarina of Saxe-Lauenburg. The wedding was held at the same time as Sofia's half-brother, Erik XIV, married Karin Månsdotter.

On March 24, 1566, Erik XIV spoke with Sofia about a marriage to Magnus, but she refused. Two days later, it was noted that she had agreed to the marriage. It is likely that she was pressured into accepting the marriage against her will. The marriage was probably arranged by Erik because he needed support from Magnus in preparation for his own planned marriage to Karin Månsdotter, whom Magnus had pledged to defend during negotiations. At this time, Erik's relationship with his sisters Sofia and Elisabet was strained, and he wrote in January 1567: "Some scoundrels provoked me into great anger and claimed to have been sent by my sisters."

There are indications that Sofia tried to postpone the wedding, perhaps hoping for a change in the political situation given the dissatisfaction with Erik's marriage to Karin Månsdotter. Erik strongly reprimanded her in a letter for refusing to prepare for the wedding and ordered others to make purchases on her behalf if she did not do so herself. The wedding did not take place in July 1567 as originally planned, as Erik had his first wedding with Karin Månsdotter. After his first marriage to Karin, when Erik realized that an official wedding and coronation were necessary for her acceptance, he resumed the wedding plans for Sofia. He announced that there would be a double wedding between her and Magnus, and between him and Karin. She was also informed that her life annuity would remain in Sweden instead of Saxe-Lauenburg, meaning the couple would reside in Sweden.

Sofia wore crimson velvet at the wedding in Stockholm. In the procession to the church for the ceremony, she walked behind Karin and Katarina Stenbock, along with Elisabet, which was considered inappropriate given her higher status than Karin's. Neither Sofia nor Elisabet attended the coronation or the tournament. During the coup that led to Erik XIV's deposition, Sofia, Elisabet, and Katarina Stenbock were taken by Magnus in a boat from Stockholm to Johan in Uppsala. Johan later claimed that Erik had planned to murder him and send them to Russia as hostages in exchange for Johan's wife.

=== Marriage === The ducal couple received Ekolsund and Vänngarn as feudal holdings from Johan III. In 1597, Sofia also held several parishes in the districts of Lagunda and Håbo. The marriage was unhappy – Duke Magnus was rough and brutal and engaged in domestic violence. He is described as one of the most notorious members of his lineage, a typical example of a harsh German Landsknecht officer. The couple had six children, but only one survived. In 1571, Magnus pawned Sofia's jewelry to finance an attempt to conquer Saxony-Lauenburg. In 1575, Johan III granted Magnus the castle fiefdom of Sonnenburg on Ösel in order to get him out of the country, but he was expelled from there due to mistreatment of the inhabitants.

Sofia acted as a mediator to smooth over her husband's abuse of power against the residents of their feudal holdings, and she constantly requested more money because Magnus quickly depleted their resources. Magnus committed many abuses. For example, he imprisoned the goldsmith Evert van Dortt when he visited Sofia and forced him at gunpoint to hand over some gold objects that belonged to her sister, Elisabet, for which Magnus was convicted of theft. In 1576, Magnus had a serious conflict with Johan because he refused to allow his peasants to pay taxes or perform their feudal duties. In August 1576, Duke Karl summoned Sofia, Elisabet, and Cecilia to a family council at Gripsholm, likely to discuss her situation. Sofia requested funds and support for Magnus to attack Saxony-Lauenburg, but Johan and Karl refused because "His Royal Highness does not behave towards Her Royal Highness as a Christian should."
