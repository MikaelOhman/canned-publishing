In Sweden, antibiotic resistance continued to increase during the year 2025.

TL;DRMore bacteria in Sweden are becoming resistant to antibiotics, making it more difficult to treat illnesses. Of particular concern is that certain dangerous bacteria are now beginning to spread outside of hospitals.
In plain language

A new report from the Swedish Public Health Agency and the National Veterinary Institute (SVA) shows that an increasing number of bacteria in Sweden are no longer killed by antibiotics. This is particularly concerning with a type of bacteria called ESBL-CARBA, as it is difficult to treat with currently available medications.

Previously, these dangerous bacteria were mainly found in people who were hospitalized or receiving care. However, the study now shows that more people who have not been in healthcare settings are becoming infected. This indicates that the bacteria are beginning to spread more freely within the community.

Despite this worrying trend of antibiotic resistance, sales of antibiotics decreased during 2024. Sweden and other Nordic countries still have relatively low levels of antibiotic use and resistance compared to other countries worldwide.

A new annual report from Folkhälsomyndigheten and SVA shows that antibiotic resistance in Sweden continues to increase. The increase in bacteria that produce ESBL-CARBA is particularly concerning because they are resistant to most antibiotics.

Bacteria that produce ESBL-CARBA are primarily found in patients within healthcare and care settings. However, the report Swedres-Svarm now shows an increasing number of cases in the general population among people who have never been treated in Swedish healthcare facilities. This increase in ESBL-CARBA is problematic because treatment options for infections caused by these bacteria are limited. The fact that there are now signs of community spread of these bacteria is serious.

"It is important to prioritize work on preventing infections, preventing the spread of infection, and responsible use of antibiotics," says Hanna Billström, investigator at Folkhälsomyndigheten. "Our ongoing efforts can slow down the increasing prevalence of antibiotic resistance."

The Swedres-Svarm report is published annually by Folkhälsomyndigheten and the National Veterinary Institute (SVA) and contains statistics on antibiotic sales and antibiotic resistance in bacteria from both humans and animals. After 25 printed reports, reporting will now transition to a digital format, which means new interactive ways to present and retrieve data.

The report shows that antibiotic sales continued to decrease in 2024. From an international perspective, Sweden and the rest of Northern Europe have low levels of both antibiotic resistance and antibiotic use.
