Full text

The Hovgårdssten, also known as the Håkansten, is a runestone located below the ruins of Hovgård on Adelsö island in Adelsö parish and Ekerö municipality in Uppland. The runestone, which can be dated to before 1080, has the designation U 11. It is situated on the shoreline of Lake Mälaren, and across the water, one can see Birka.

== Inscription == Transliteration of the runic inscription:

raþ | þu : runaʀ : ret : lit : rista : toliʀ : bry[t]i : i roþ : kunuki : toliʀ : a(u)k : gyla : litu : ris… …- : þaun : hion : eftiʀ …k : merki srni… haku(n) · (b)aþ : rista Normalization to Old Swedish:

Rað þu runaʀ. Rett let Toliʀ bryti i roði kunungi. Toliʀ ok Gylla letu ris[ta] …, þaun hion æftiʀ [si]k(?) mærki … Hakon bað rista. Translation to modern Swedish:

Read the runes! Tolir, a royal official in Hovgård, had them carved for the king. Tolir and Gyla both had these runes carved as a memorial ... Håkon ordered it to be carved.

Interpretation: Tolir was a "bryte," meaning he was the king's bailiff or governor at Hovgård, and Gyla or Gylla was his wife. Håkon has been identified with Håkan Röde, who is believed to have co-ruled with Inge the Elder in the late 10th century. Håkon may also have been the Norwegian jarl (earl) Håkon Ivarsson, who challenged Harald Hardrada in the First Norse-Swedish War.

== The Stone ==

The inscription is carved on a bedrock stone composed of fine-grained gray granite. The block measures 2.75 meters wide and 1.4 meters deep, with a maximum height of 1.4 meters. The runic inscriptions are 7-8 centimeters high. The southern side of the block is smooth but has a shallow and unclear inscription due to surface scorching from fires used to burn wood and brush. As early as the 17th century, some of the runes and ornamentation on the right edge were missing. Elsewhere, there are cracks, and thin flakes have broken off.

Around the year 1065, this seven-ton stone block was transported here to the busy harbor. The runestone's visible surface was positioned so that everyone arriving by sea could see this impressive inscription, read the runes, and be aware of the royal estate. Today, the water level of Lake Mälaren is five to six meters lower due to land uplift, and remnants of an ancient quay are located near the stone. This means it was originally placed directly at the shoreline. From the runestone, a road leads up to Adelsö Church and the adjacent burial ground with several imposing royal mounds, extending into the ancient harbor bay.

How certain are we about this?

Runestones are the only contemporary and original sources from the Viking Age – they were carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes the names, family relationships, and journeys mentioned strong evidence. However, the limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they describe what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some interpretations are uncertain – such readings are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
