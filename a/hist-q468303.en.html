Anders Gustaf Ekeberg, born January 16, 1767, in Stockholm, and died February 11, 1813, in Uppsala, was a Swedish chemist who discovered the element tantalum in 1802.

== Biography == Anders Gustaf Ekeberg was the son of shipbuilder Joseph Eric Ekeberg (brother of Karl Gustaf Ekeberg) and Hedvig Ulrica Kilberg, and belonged to the Ekeberg family, which originated from Djursholm. During his childhood, a cold weakened his hearing, a disability that would later limit his career opportunities.

He initially received private tutoring at the Söderåkra vicarage in Kalmar, then studied in Västervik, and was enrolled at Uppsala University in 1784. There, he received help and support from his uncle and Johan Låstbom, and was taught by Carl Peter Thunberg. He defended his dissertation "pro exercitio" in 1787 and "pro gradu" the following year, and was promoted to Master of Philosophy in 1788. He then undertook a study trip to Greifswald and Berlin, for which he received a scholarship, and shortly after returning home in 1794, he became an auditor at the Bergskollegium (Mining Bureau) and a docent in chemistry at Uppsala. In 1799, he became an adjunct professor and laboratory instructor in chemistry at Uppsala. He was elected as member number 294 of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1799. Two years later, he lost sight in one eye due to an explosion.

Most of Ekeberg's scientific papers were published in the proceedings of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. Together with Pehr von Afzelius, he proposed the Swedish names for oxygen, hydrogen, and nitrogen in the brochure "Försök till svensk nomenklatur för chemien, lämpad efter de sednaste upptäckterne" (Attempt at a Swedish nomenclature for chemistry, adapted to the latest discoveries) in 1795. The same year, he published an article in the "Litteraturtidning." He published both works anonymously, probably to avoid conflict with his superior professor Johan Afzelius. Among his teaching activities was presiding over Jöns Jacob Berzelius's dissertation in 1800. Ekeberg made several scientific discoveries, including a method for producing a type of very strong, clear, and transparent porcelain. However, he kept its composition secret, and the secret went with him to the grave.

He also produced literary works, among which "Ode öfver nordiska kriget" (Ode on the Nordic War) in 1788 and "Tal öfver freden mellan Sverige och Ryssland" (Speech on the peace between Sweden and Russia) in 1790 are the most well-known.

Anders Gustaf Ekeberg remained unmarried.
