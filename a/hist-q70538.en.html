Carl Wilhelm Scheele, born December 9th (according to the old calendar; December 19th according to the new calendar) 1742 in Stralsund, Swedish Pomerania, and died May 21, 1786 in Köping, was a Swedish apothecary and one of the most prominent chemists of 18th-century Europe.

He was born as a Swedish subject, the seventh of eleven children. His parents were Joachim Christian Scheele, a merchant in Stralsund from an old and respected German family, and Margareta Eleonora Warnecross. Scheele is primarily known for his numerous discoveries of individual substances. Among these are several elements that were later identified as chemical elements, such as oxygen and chlorine. He is best remembered today for his experiments with what was then called "dephlogisticated air," i.e., oxygen. However, this discovery was just one in a long series of discoveries of both organic and inorganic chemical substances. The discovery was likely made several years before Joseph Priestley's discovery of the same substance. Priestley, however, published his findings before Scheele. Both scientists' work preceded Antoine Lavoisier, who is sometimes mentioned in older literature as one of the discoverers of oxygen.

== Early Life and Education == Carl Wilhelm Scheele came from an old German family. As a child, he was quiet and reserved, and had little interest in playing with other children. His greatest joy was in tinkering and assembling small objects. He soon developed a passion for chemistry, and to pursue this interest, he decided to become a pharmacist, which at the time was almost the only way to gain access to a chemical laboratory.

As early as 1757, when he was just 14 years old, he was sent to Gothenburg to become an apprentice at the pharmacy "Enhörningen," owned by M. A. Bauch. His older brother had previously worked there but had since passed away. It was there that he acquired the knowledge of chemical analysis that would later make him one of Europe's most prominent chemists. The pharmacist praised him highly, but worried that the young man might overexert himself. After completing his six-year apprenticeship, Scheele remained in Gothenburg for another two years.

In 1765, he moved to Malmö, where he became a journeyman at the pharmacy "Fläkta Örn," owned by P.M. Kjellström, a man who was highly regarded in his profession and particularly interested in chemistry. Scheele continued to develop during his time in Scania (the region of Sweden where Malmö is located). During this period, he came into contact with Anders Jahan Retzius, then a docent in chemistry and natural history at Lund University. In 1768, both Retzius and Scheele moved to Stockholm, where Scheele worked at the pharmacy "Korpen." He probably believed that the capital city would offer more opportunities for scientific research and development. However, this was not the case. Scheele failed to connect with Stockholm's chemists and was not allowed to work in the laboratory; instead, he worked in the compounding area, where medicines were prepared. In 1770, Scheele moved to Uppsala and the pharmacy "Uplands Wapen." In Uppsala, he met Johan Gottlieb Gahn, Torbern Bergman's favorite student and a professor of chemistry. Scheele quickly convinced Gahn of his skills as a chemist, and it was through Gahn that he made contact with Bergman. He collaborated with Bergman until Bergman's death, and it was largely thanks to Bergman's support that Scheele gained access to the scientific community. As an apprentice pharmacist, Scheele did not automatically have access to the rooms where scientific research was primarily conducted in 18th-century Sweden.

== Scientific and Technical Achievements == After completing his apprenticeship and journeyman training, Scheele was granted the privilege of running the pharmacy in Köping. Most of his works were published as a series of articles in the "Kungliga Vetenskapsakademiens Handlingar" (Proceedings of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences), and he is credited with discovering a wide range of both organic and inorganic chemical substances. His discovery of the element that would later be named oxygen, made between 1772 and 1773, was published in 1778 in his only book, "Chemische Abhandlung von der Luft und dem Feuer" (Chemical Treatise on Air and Fire). However, Scheele himself did not use this term, which was introduced by the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier. In some cases, Scheele's discoveries were made independently and almost simultaneously with those of others; for example, oxygen with Priestley and nitrogen with Rutherford and Priestley. Scheele was a proponent of the phlogiston theory. Scheele discovered that certain elements, such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and arsenic, have different oxidation states. He interpreted this as variations in the amount of phlogiston present.

Scheele did not discover any elements in the true sense of the word; he would not have understood what the term meant. However, he was the first to analyze and describe a number of substances that were later identified by other chemists as elements. These include chlorine (1774), manganese (1774), molybdenum (1778), tungsten (1781), and barium. He was also the first to identify several substances that would later be considered composite molecules, including hydrogen fluoride and silicofluoric acid (published in 1771), hydrocyanic acid (published in 1782), glycerol (published in 1783), malic acid and citric acid (published in 1785), tartaric acid, mucic acid, etc.
