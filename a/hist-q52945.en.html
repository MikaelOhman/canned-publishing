Erik XIV, born on December 13, 1533, at Tre Kronor castle, and died on February 26, 1577, at Örbyhus castle, was the King of Sweden between 1560 and 1568. As king, he placed northern Estonia under his protection, which was one of the causes of the Nordic Seven Years' War with Denmark-Norway. A period of mental illness began with the Sture murders in 1567, and although Erik XIV later recovered, this contributed to him being deposed and succeeded by his brother John III the following year. John III initially kept him imprisoned but likely eventually ordered his death through poisoning.

Erik XIV was the son of Gustav Vasa and Catherine of Saxe-Lauenburg. Through his mother, he descended from both the House of Erik and the Bjälbo dynasty. Catherine of Saxe-Lauenburg was a descendant of Ingeborg Birgersdotter, daughter of Birger Jarl and Princess Ingeborg Eriksdotter of Sweden. Ingeborg Birgersdotter married Duke John I of Saxe-Lauenburg in 1270. Erik was also the half-brother of John III and Charles IX, and the uncle of Sigismund and Gustav II Adolf. He married Karin Månsdotter (1550–1612) in July 1568.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Youth === Erik XIV was born at Tre Kronor castle at 9 p.m. on Saturday, December 13, 1533. Before he was two years old, he lost his mother, and before he turned three, he had a stepmother, Margaret Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). Erik received an excellent education. His first teacher was the learned German, Georg Norman, whose services were soon needed elsewhere in the Swedish government. He was replaced by the French Calvinist Dionysius Beurreus, who also taught Erik's brother John, and who seemed to be appreciated by both of them. Olaus Canuti Helsingius also served as the prince's tutor.

Erik had reached the pinnacle of education for his time: he possessed excellent knowledge of foreign languages and mathematics, was well-versed in history, and wrote with a style and precision that revealed his intimate familiarity with rhetoric and logic. He was also knowledgeable in astrology. Furthermore, he was a master of chivalrous pursuits, sang and played the lute beautifully, and could draw skillfully. Prominent negative traits of Erik were his choleric and suspicious nature, which was also evident in both his father and his brothers.

King Gustav wrote his will in 1557 and divided the kingdom into hereditary duchies for his sons. Johan had already been appointed Duke of Finland the year before, and the sons Magnus and Karl now received their respective duchies. From the time Erik began to appear publicly, he was called the "designated king" (but after the Diet in Stockholm in 1560, he became Sweden's "hereditary king"). In 1557, Erik, as Duke, received Kalmar and the province of Kronoberg, along with Öland, as fiefdoms (to which Tjust was added in 1559), and subsequently held his court in Kalmar.

The opinions of the cautious and conservative old king were sometimes challenged by his quick-witted and educated eldest son, who wanted to modernize the kingdom and was not as frugal as the king. However, the king was also very concerned about Duke Johan's reckless and self-serving policies towards Livonia, Russia, and Poland. The king became truly enraged by the handling of a major scandal of the time, the "Vadstena affair." The scandal occurred after the wedding in 1559 between the dukes' sister, Princess Katarina Gustavsdotter Vasa, and Count Edzard II of East Frisia, during Christmas celebrations at Vadstena Castle. The groom's brother, Count Johan, was caught visiting the bride's sister, Princess Cecilia, in her bedroom, was arrested, and thrown into the tower. The king was furious, especially with Duke Erik himself, for making a public matter out of an event that should have remained within the family.

As King Gustav began to age, the heirs had to plan for the future. Duke Erik needed to find a suitable future queen. His grandmother, Katarina of Braunschweig-Wolfenbüttel, Dowager Duchess of Saxe-Lauenburg, as well as Dionysius Beurreus and Jöran Persson, advised him to seek his bride from foreign royal houses rather than the Swedish nobility. The best match on the continent was Elizabeth, daughter of the English King Henry VIII, who was crowned Queen Elizabeth I in 1558. His brother, Duke Johan, wanted to marry Katarina Jagellonica, sister of the Polish King Sigismund II Augustus. The brothers supported each other against the resistance of old King Gustav, and Johan traveled to England with Sten Eriksson Leijonhuvud as part of an elaborate courtship delegation. The delegation returned in April 1560 with the recommendation that the heir himself should make a courtship journey in person. The king's and the estates' approval was obtained, and Duke Erik could begin preparations for the trip. In mid-August, he set out towards Älvsborg, where the fleet had gathered for the crossing, but the plans were suddenly canceled because King Gustav's health deteriorated rapidly. The king died on September 29, 1560.
