Inge the Elder, also known as Inge Stenkilsson, died around 1110 and was King of Sweden from at least 1080 to around 1084, and again from approximately 1088–1110. He was the son of Stenkil, and his mother may have been a daughter of King Emund the Old. According to the Hervarar saga, he co-ruled with his brother Halsten, and a papal letter was addressed to him and a king "A," which has been interpreted as either Halsten or Håkan Röde. However, other sources offer conflicting information regarding the order in which these three kings ruled.

It is said that Inge was deposed by the Swedes around 1084 because he refused to participate in pagan rituals at the Allthing (a general assembly), and he was replaced by Blot-Sven. Inge reportedly attempted to Christianize the Swedes, a move that was not well received. Around the year 1087, Inge is said to have killed his rival, Sven, who was also his brother-in-law, burned down the pagan temple in Uppsala, and regained power over all of Sweden. == Land Holdings in Vreta == According to a record from the 16th century, Inge founded Vreta monastery and had the Vreta church built. According to this record, his wife, Helena, also donated approximately twenty farms, most of which were located nearby. The papal letter on this subject mentions only Inge as the Swedish king: "Ingo gloriosus Suetonum Rex" – "Inge, the glorious King of the Swedes."

== The Three Kings' Meeting in Kungahälla == According to several Icelandic sources, King Inge the Elder attended the so-called meeting of the three kings in 1101 in Kungahälla (Kungälv), where King Eric Ejegod of Denmark, King Magnus Barefoot of Norway, and Inge met to establish a lasting peace, apparently after conflicts between Inge and Magnus. To solidify the peace, Inge's daughter Margaret was married to King Magnus at this meeting, and she subsequently received the name Margaret the Peaceful. The earliest source, the Norwegian monk Theodoricus' "Historia de antiquitate regum Norvagiensium," mentions a peace agreement between "the king of the Swedes" Inge and King Magnus, which was confirmed by the marriage and included certain disputed areas of Dalsland as part of Margaret's dowry. The meeting is also described by others, including Snorre Sturlasson, who provides additional details about the war and mentions that King Eric participated in the meeting.

Of the kings present at the meeting, Snorre describes Inge as the most imposing, with a broad rear end that earned him the nickname "Inge the Broad-Backed" in Norway. Theodoricus describes him as "blameless." According to the royal genealogy in the Westrogothic Law, he was said to be courageous and law-abiding.

== Inge's Death == According to the Prose Chronicle, Inge is said to have fought in Scania and ruled the region for three years before being murdered in his bed. Johannes Magnus' claim that he fought against a Russian prince named Valdemar or Jarizlav is even more uncertain. The dates of Inge's death and abdication from the throne are uncertain. Sources mention various dates ranging from 1103 to 1118, but it is not certain whether these two events occurred simultaneously, although the "Hervararsaga" states that "Inge ruled the kingdom until his dying day and died of a sudden illness."

== The Stenkils Chapel Tomb == According to recent research, Inge is buried in the Stenkils chapel tomb within Vreta Abbey Church. This tomb, also known by that name regardless of family affiliation, was built by the king's son, Ragnvald, who died before his father. It has been dated to the early 12th century. Beneath the monument and floor are skeletons in several stone sarcophagi belonging to tall men, specifically measuring approximately 6 feet 3 inches (1.98 meters) and 6 feet 7 inches (2.02 meters) in height. This aligns with the information that members of the Stenkils family were known for their height.

== Family == Inge was married to Helena, who may have been the same person as "Maer" or "mö," the sister of Blot-Sven. According to a 15th-century account, he was also married to Ragnhild of Tälje. Children:

  • Kristina (d. 1122), married to Grand Prince Mstislav I of Kiev (1076–1132)
  • Ragnvald, likely died before his father, but some historians identify him with King Ragnvald Knaphövde
  • Margaret, known as Fredkulla, married to Magnus Barefoot of Norway and Nils of Denmark
  • Katarina, married to Björn Haraldsson Järnsida (d. 1134); their daughter Kristina became the Queen of Sweden.
