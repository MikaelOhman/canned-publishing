Alexander Roslin, born July 15, 1718, in Malmö, and died July 5, 1793, in Paris, was a Swedish portrait painter. From the 1750s, he primarily worked in Paris.

In Paris, Roslin gained great renown and was elected to the French Academy of Fine Arts. His early portraits are executed in light and cool colors and show the influence of Jean-Marc Nattier, such as the portrait of "Friherrinnan de Neubourg-Cromière" (1756). During the 1760s, there is a tendency towards a more formal and vibrant style of painting, exemplified by the portraits of his wife, "Damen med slöjan" (The Lady with the Veil) (1768), and "Brukspatron John Jennings, hans bror och svägerska" (Mr. John Jennings, his brother and sister-in-law) (1769).

Roslin was primarily a masterful portrayer of fabrics and skin tones, but he could also paint psychologically insightful portraits. His work demonstrated a combination of sensitivity to the tastes of the time and great skill. This combination proved very successful with the Parisian aristocracy.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life ===

Alexander Roslin was the son of Hans Roslin, a naval physician, and Katarina Wertmüller. His father, also named Hans Roslin, was a doctor from a Stockholm family who had been "Gouvernements-Medicus" (provincial physician) in Skåne since 1714 but lost his official positions in 1718 due to economic hardship. He then supported himself as a town physician and through private commissions. His mother was the cousin of Johan Ulrik Wertmüller, father of the artist Adolf Ulrik Wertmüller, making them cousins.

Alexander Roslin's family originated from Stockholm. His oldest known ancestor was Bengt Mattsson Roselin (died 1653), who successfully worked as a translator from Russian. Bengt Mattsson Roselin first served as a clerk in the royal chancellery (1614) and then as a translator of the Russian language. He died after August 15, 1653, in Stockholm, and was buried on November 30, 1653, in Storkyrkan (Stockholm Cathedral), where he owned a burial plot. He was often referred to as Bengt Ryssetolk ("Bengt the Russian Interpreter") and "it seems he received land grants in Ingermanland" for his services. His sons adopted the surname Roselin (Roslin). One son, Hans Roslin, a councilman in Stockholm, became the grandfather of Alexander Roslin. Both of Bengt Mattsson Roselin's sons added the prefix "Ros-" to their surnames. The eldest son, Johan Bengtsson Roselin (Johan Rosenlindt) (1617–1657), followed his father's profession and was ennobled as Rosenlindt in 1650, a name that continues with the family in Finland. Johan Roselin was murdered in Poland in 1657 during a diplomatic mission. The connection between "Ryss" (Russian) and "Roslagen" (a region in Sweden) suggests a linguistic link, as Russia was once colonized from Roslagen. Hans Roslin the elder, Bengt Mattsson Roselin's youngest son, adopted the surname Roslin. He first worked as a wig maker and later (1683) became a councilman in Stockholm. He would become Alexander's grandfather, and his son, Hans Roslin the younger, was Alexander's father.

Due to his father's assignments, the family moved around southern Sweden. They lived for a time in Kristianstad and Karlskrona, where Hans Roslin was appointed naval physician. When Alexander was nineteen years old, his father died in 1737. The young Alexander found it natural to train as a shipbuilder in the naval city of Karlskrona, given the family's limited financial resources. He received his first artistic training from Lars Ehrenbill (1697–1747), a naval draftsman. Ehrenbill recognized Roslin's artistic talent and advised him to continue his education in Stockholm. With an unusual aptitude for drawing and painting, Roslin learned from naval captain Lars Ehrenbill in Karlskrona and then practiced miniature painting. The plan was for him to become a ship's draughtsman. In 1737, Roslin returned to Stockholm, where he became an apprentice to the court painter Georg Engelhard Schröder (1684–1750) at the age of sixteen, remaining with him until 1741. Schröder was the court portraitist for Frederick William I of Prussia (1688–1740). We don't know exactly why Roslin focused on portraits, but from the beginning, he took a practical approach to his career choice, recognizing that a skilled portrait painter would always have demand for their work.

Roslin stayed with the portrait painter Schröder for five years, from the age of eighteen to twenty-three. His training there provided a solid foundation in craftsmanship. After this, Roslin considered himself fully trained but realized that the competition in Stockholm was fierce and the client base limited. He began painting larger portraits in oil. His former teacher, Lars Ehrenbill, encouraged him to move to Gothenburg in 1741, but no records of his work there have been found. In 1742, he painted two works for Hasslövs Church in Halland: "The Crucifixion" and "The Resurrection." He remained in Skåne until 1745, where he received his first significant commissions from members of the Skånska aristocracy, including families like Coyet (Trolle-Ljungby Castle) and Lewenhaupt (Övedskloster). Everywhere he went, he was busy with portrait painting.
