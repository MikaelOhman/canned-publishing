The Vasa era is a period in Swedish history during which the House of Vasa held the throne of Sweden. It began in 1523 with Gustav Vasa and ended when Queen Christina abdicated in 1654. This period followed the Union period (also known as the late Middle Ages) from 1389 to 1521. The era is typically divided into two sub-periods: the earlier Vasa era (1521–1611) and the later Vasa era (1611–1654).

The Vasa era began when Gustav Vasa was elected Regent of Sweden in 1521. The subsequent liberation war led to Sweden's secession from the Kalmar Union. The period following Gustav Vasa's rise to power was characterized by the Reformation in Sweden, several wars and uprisings, and the centralization of state power through reforms. Furthermore, power became increasingly concentrated in the hands of the king. During this time, Gustav Vasa closed monasteries and reformed the church.

Much of what was built during this period involved renovations of medieval structures, such as Örebro Castle, Kalmar Castle, Västerås Castle, Läckö Castle, and Nyköping Castle. During this period, art began to be used as a political tool.

== Earlier Vasa Era ==

In Swedish historical literature, the earlier Vasa era refers to the period from 1521 to 1611, during which Gustav Vasa and his sons Erik XIV, Johan III, and Karl IX, along with Johan III's son Sigismund, were the rulers of Sweden.

== Later Vasa Era ==

In Swedish history, the later Vasa era refers to the period from 1611 to 1654, during which Gustav II Adolf and his daughter Christina ruled Sweden.
