In plain language

The government wants to change the laws governing independent schools, preschools, and after-school care centers. They aim to prevent companies from solely seeking quick profits within the education system. From now on, those operating these institutions must demonstrate a long-term plan and financial stability.

A key new rule prohibits transferring funds from the school to other purposes, for example, if the owner changes or someone wants to sell the business. This means that money received from the municipality must be used for the children's education and activities. If the funds are misused, the municipality can demand their return.

To ensure compliance, only companies registered as legal entities will be allowed to operate schools. The Swedish Schools Inspectorate, the agency responsible for monitoring school quality, will also gain more authority. They can now issue fines for non-compliance and have the right to speak with students under 16 years old to assess their educational experience.

Full text

The proposal includes suggested amendments to the Education Act (2010:800), which would tighten the conditions for private operators of independent schools, preschools, and after-school care centers. The aim is to improve the education system by promoting the long-term stability of private operators and preventing unserious or unsuitable actors with short-term profit interests from entering the field. The proposals include the following:

  • A ban on transferring value will be introduced in three situations: during new establishment, changes in ownership, and when a supervisory order is issued. This ban ensures that funds remain within the operations, and requirements for separate accounting will make it possible to verify compliance with the ban.
  • Stricter requirements for the long-term stability and financial soundness of private operators will be introduced and monitored both during the application process for approval and during supervision.
  • In certain cases, private operators may be required to repay subsidies received from municipalities, particularly if the funds have been used in a manner that constitutes a crime. Municipalities should be able to recover these subsidies in similar situations.
  • Only legal entities will be allowed to operate as private providers within the education system.
  • A private operator intending to close a school unit must notify the relevant municipality.
  • The Swedish Schools Inspectorate and municipalities will receive expanded powers related to supervision. A system of sanction fees will be introduced, including for violations of the ban on transferring value. During inspections and quality reviews, under certain conditions, the Schools Inspectorate may have conversations with students under 16 years of age without the presence of their guardians.
