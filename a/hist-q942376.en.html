Gustaf Trolle.

Gustaf Eriksson Trolle, born in September 1488 and died in the summer of 1535, was a Swedish clergyman and Archbishop of Uppsala from 1515 to 1521. He was one of those responsible for the Stockholm Bloodbath.

== Biography == Gustaf Trolle was the son of Erik Trolle and Ingeborg Filipsdotter (Tott). After a couple of years of studies at the University of Cologne and in Rome, Trolle was appointed provost of the Diocese of Linköping in 1513, and a year later, Archbishop of the Archdiocese of Uppsala, succeeding Jakob Ulvsson. Like his predecessors, Trolle initially advocated for a union-friendly and council-constitutionalist policy. However, as early as 1515, he came into conflict with the Regent Sten Sture the Younger. Despite attempts at reconciliation, Sten Sture besieged Trolle's castle, Almarestäket, from the autumn of 1516. After capturing the castle in 1517, Trolle was taken prisoner and deposed. The castle was demolished according to a decision by the national assembly, and the documents related to this decision later became evidence in the Stockholm Bloodbath. After Sten Sture's death during Denmark's King Christian II's conquest of Sweden in the autumn of 1520, Trolle was reinstated as Archbishop. He officiated at King Christian's coronation in Uppsala Cathedral, and the festivities were interrupted by the Stockholm Bloodbath, in which Gustaf Trolle played a controversial role. For example, the king used Trolle's demands for compensation to brand Trolle's opponents as heretics and have them executed. As early as 1521, Trolle was forced to flee to Denmark after several uprisings in Småland and Dalarna. He remained there and continued to fight for Christian's cause. In 1534, he became Bishop of Odense, but was fatally injured in the Battle of Öxnebjerg on the island of Fyn on June 11th, and died a few weeks later. He was buried in the cathedral of Schleswig. Despite Trolle's high position and support from the Pope, Gustav Vasa refused to recognize him as Archbishop and denounced Trolle as a traitor. The pressure from Rome contributed to Gustav Vasa breaking with the Vatican, initiating the Reformation, and introducing Protestantism in Sweden.
