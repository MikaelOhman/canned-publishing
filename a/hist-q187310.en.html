Carl Olof Larsson, born May 28, 1853, in Gamla Stan (Old Town) in Stockholm, and died January 22, 1919, in Falun, Dalarna, was a Swedish artist. Larsson is one of the most famous Swedish artists and perhaps the most beloved. He is considered one of Sweden's most important watercolor painters. He is known for his watercolors depicting idyllic family life. Larsson was married to the artist Karin Larsson.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood ===

Carl Larsson came from humble beginnings and was born at Prästgatan 78 in Gamla Stan, Stockholm. During his childhood, his parents lived in Ladugårdslandet, initially on Grevgatan and later on Grev Magnigatan. When he was thirteen years old, his teacher encouraged him to apply for a place as a student at the Royal Swedish Academy of Fine Arts. Carl Larsson studied at the Academy from 1869 to 1876. To earn a living, he had to work while studying. He supported himself by working, among other things, as a retoucher for a photographer.

=== Illustrator ===

From 1872 to 1876, he frequently collaborated as a graphic artist for the satirical magazine *Kasper*. In 1876, Carl Larsson was awarded a medal for his painting "Sten Sture the Elder liberates Queen Kristina of Denmark from Vadstena Abbey." He illustrated works such as Hans Christian Andersen's fairy tales (1875–1876), Topelius' *The Field-Surgeon's Tales* (1883–1884), and Anna Maria Lenngren's poems (1884). Even after his initial successes, he occasionally worked as an illustrator, including for Elias Sehlstedt's poems (1893) and Viktor Rydberg's *Singoalla* (1894). "Before he went to Paris in 1877, he needed a way to support himself, so he created illustrations for Asbjørnsen and Moe's Norwegian folktales. He received a few crowns for each completed illustration. The commission came from the publisher Hjalmar Linnström, but his publishing house went bankrupt, so no book was produced. At the bankruptcy auction, Albert Bonnier acquired the illustrations and they ended up in his publishing archive."

Albert Bonnier acquired the translation rights, manuscripts, and illustrations at the bankruptcy auction, and then nothing more happened. As mentioned earlier, Carl Larsson had previously illustrated works such as Hans Christian Andersen's fairy tales. Asbjørnsen and Moe's folktales appealed to Carl Larsson's imagination. Larsson became acquainted with Asbjørnsen and Moe's stories through the Swedish edition published between 1868 and 1875. However, it wasn't until 1927, several years after Larsson's death, that the book "Folksagor" by Asbjørnsen and Moe with illustrations by Carl Larsson was published. Carl Larsson had also been commissioned to illustrate four collections of "Tales" written by Richard Gustafsson, which were published between 1874 and 1882. Richard Gustafsson founded the satirical magazine *Kasper* in 1869 and served as its editor until 1910. One of the well-known contributors to the magazine *Kasper* was, as mentioned earlier, Carl Larsson, who was one of the illustrators for the publication.

In 1873, Carl Larsson began a relationship with Wilhelmina Holmgren, who was 13 years older and also studying at the Academy of Fine Arts. She played a significant role in his artistic development and career. Their mutual friend, art critic Georg Nordensvan, stated in his biography of the artist that "she created him." They had two children together, both of whom died shortly after birth. Wilhelmina died in childbirth in 1877 during the delivery of their second child.

Carl Larsson moved to Paris in 1877. During his time there, he lived in poverty. After returning to Stockholm and supporting himself for two years through illustration work, he traveled back to Paris in 1880 and began painting. In a letter dated March 1881, art professor Fredrik Wilhelm Scholander advised him to put aside all eccentricity and "paint a study, completely simple, so that you must be ashamed of its lack of originality, while at the same time you feel that it looks like a piece of reality." This advice gave purpose and meaning to Larsson's stay in Paris. The small village of Grez-sur-Loing, near Fontainebleau south of Paris, became his home during his Parisian period. He developed his own style there, and watercolor painting became his specialty.

=== Grez-sur-Loing and Marriage ===

Grez-sur-Loing was a small, idyllic place where a painter could easily find inspiration: gardens, young fruit trees, abundant flowers and herbs, beaches with terraces and trellises, old pavilions and docks, a river with small waterfalls, men in wooden shoes, and girls enjoying the sunshine – all readily available subjects. Larsson quickly adapted to the soft light of northern France, and his technique naturally became elegant and effortless, complemented by a personal touch that soon caught the attention of French observers. In 1883, Larsson married the artist Karin Bergöö, and their growing family soon became a common subject for intimate portraits, including "The Bride" (1883), "Studio Idyll," and "Little Suzanne" (1885). The first Grez watercolors, including "October" and "November" (both at Gothenburg Museum of Art), which he submitted to the art salon in 1883, earned him third prize.
