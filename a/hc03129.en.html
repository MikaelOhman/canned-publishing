In plain language

The government proposes creating a new crime specifically for assisting in the transportation of stolen goods out of Sweden. This crime is called "export handling." Individuals convicted of a serious offense could face a prison sentence of up to six years. Attempting to commit this crime will also be illegal.

The proposal also increases the responsibility of the Swedish Customs Administration (Tullverket), the agency responsible for controlling goods at the border. They will be able to assist prosecutors with investigations and will have greater authority to prevent crime. Customs officials will be able to initiate investigations in a manner similar to that of the police.

There are also proposals that would allow authorities working to combat crime to retain seized items for use in training or for evidentiary examination. If seized items are dangerous or difficult to store, they can be destroyed more quickly than previously allowed.

The government also wants to change the rules regarding penalties for smuggling narcotics intended for resale. Most of these new regulations are intended to come into effect in September 2025, but a minor amendment to a law regarding taxable goods will take effect on July 1, 2025.

Full text

The proposal suggests introducing a new crime, "export handling," which involves facilitating the illegal export of goods. The proposed penalty for "export handling" is a maximum prison sentence of two years, and for aggravated "export handling," a minimum of six months and a maximum of six years. The proposal also includes criminalizing attempts, preparations, and incitement to both "export handling" and aggravated "export handling."

The government proposes that this new crime should be included in the core responsibilities of the Customs Agency.

In addition, the government proposes the following:

  • A special penalty scale will be introduced for drug smuggling of a normal degree if the drugs are intended for sale.
  • Employees of the Customs Agency, designated by the authority, should be able to assist prosecutors in investigations regarding independent confiscation. A customs officer, like a police officer, should be able to initiate such an investigation.
  • Law enforcement agencies should be allowed to retain confiscated property for training purposes or for forensic analysis.
  • Property that has been seized may, under certain circumstances, be destroyed prematurely, for example, if its storage poses serious security risks.
  • A correction is proposed for an incorrect reference in the law (1998:506) regarding the movement and control of certain goods subject to excise tax.

The amendment to the law regarding the movement and control of certain goods subject to excise tax, which is of a corrective nature, is proposed to take effect on July 1, 2025, and the other changes on September 1, 2025.
