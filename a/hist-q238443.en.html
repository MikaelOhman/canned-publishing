Ellen Karolina Sofia Key (pronounced [kej]), born December 11, 1849, in the parish of Gladhammar near Västervik, and died April 25, 1926, at her home, Strand, on the southern slope of Omberg overlooking Lake Vättern, was a Swedish author, educator, and feminist ideologue. She is primarily known as an early feminist and sexologist.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Ellen Key was born in the parish of Gladhammar near Västervik. She was the daughter of Emil Key (1822–1892), a member of parliament, and Sofia Ottiliana Posse (1824–1884), a countess from the noble family Posse, who lived at Sundsholms Manor in southern Tjust. Her mother was a cousin of Prime Minister Arvid Posse. She received her early education at home from governesses, and later attended confirmation classes with the Åhlin sisters and Rossanderska School for Girls in Stockholm. Otherwise, she was largely self-educated, drawing knowledge from the extensive library at her parents' manor house.

From a young age, she served as her father's secretary and, during the winter parliamentary sessions, acted as hostess at his meetings with politicians and cultural figures in their apartment in Stockholm. According to family lore, the Swedish Key family descends from James MacKay, who came to Sweden from Scotland during the Thirty Years' War.

=== Early Career and Writing === In the 1870s, Key established a Sunday school in her hometown for local children. After Sundsholm farm was lost due to her father's bankruptcy, she supported herself as a teacher at Anna Whitlock's school, which she had helped found. At the same time, she gave lectures at the Stockholm Workers' Institute and other venues, primarily on literature and history. She gained national attention as an opinion leader when, in 1889, she lectured on freedom of speech and the press at the student association Verdandi.

Key is known as both a writer and an educator. As a writer, she has published works including a biography of her father, several feminist articles, books on folk education and the labor movement, contemporary political pamphlets, and literary analyses. The work that has received the most attention is "Barnets århundrade" (1900), which has been translated into 26 languages and explores Key's views on schools and upbringing. Other notable works include "Skönhet för alla" (1899), her aesthetic manifesto; "Lifslinjer I-III" (1903–1906), a summary of her life philosophy; and "Missbrukad kvinnokraft" (1896), in which she, from a distinct feminist perspective, criticized what she saw as excesses within the women's movement.

Since 2025, "Barnets århundrade" has been part of Sweden's cultural canon.

=== Ellen Key's Ideology and Life ===

Ellen Key's ideological foundation was individualism, empiricism, and humanism. She garnered both great admiration and sharp criticism. The criticisms stemmed from her distance from Christianity, which was seen as morally corrupting, her fight for women's rights, her allegedly "unpatriotic" stance on peace issues, and what was considered to be a legitimization of eugenics. However, she can also be credited with helping society place children's best interests at the forefront. Key had a significant impact on the education system, rejecting authoritarian pedagogy and segregated instruction, influenced by Rousseau.

She was one of the most influential writers of her time, even beyond Sweden's borders. She was friends with and introduced Swedish readers to authors such as Romain Rolland, Rainer Maria Rilke, Martin Buber, and Lou Andreas Salomé. During the 19th century, she associated with prominent playwrights of the era, including Victoria Benedictsson, Signe Sohlman, and Anne Charlotte Leffler. Together with mathematician Sofja Kovalevskaja, they formed an important part of the radical literary circle of the 1880s. During the same period, her close friendships began with people like Verner von Heidenstam, Richard Bergh, Hanna Pauli, and Oscar Levertin. Several of these were part of a close-knit group called "Juntan," immortalized in Hanna Pauli's painting "Friends," completed in 1907. Her friendly relationship with August Strindberg eventually turned into a bitter rivalry, manifested, among other things, in Strindberg's novel "Svarta fanor," where Key was portrayed under the name Hanna Paj as an intriguing and unsympathetic character. The critic Vitalis Norström wrote the book "Ellen Key's Third Reich" in 1902, which is a study of radicalism and a critique of the entire cultural-radical project.

Ellen Key also associated with American architect and modern thinker Frank Lloyd Wright, although they never met in person; their communication was facilitated by Martha Cheney Borthwick (Wright's partner from 1909 to 1914). These two moved to Europe in an attempt to avoid attention in the United States and Wright's wife, Catherine Wright, who did not want a divorce. Wright also helped introduce Key to the United States, and Borthwick served as a translator into English. Borthwick abandoned her children to live with Wright, a fact that gave Ellen Key insight into the consequences of "free love."

Key's influence on Wright can be seen in the design of his own home, Taliesin, built in 1913.
