Charles XIII, previously known as Duke Charles, was born on September 26th (Old Style) / October 7th (New Style) 1748 at Kungshuset, Riddarholmen in Stockholm, and died on February 5, 1818 at Stockholm Palace. He reigned as King of Sweden from 1809 and of Norway from 1814 until his death. He was also the Duke of Södermanland. In Norway, he was known as Charles XIII during his lifetime, but has sometimes been referred to as Charles II afterwards.

Charles XIII was the son of King Adolf Frederick and Queen Louisa Ulrika, brother to Gustav III, and cousin to Catherine II of Russia. He married his cousin Hedwig Elisabeth Charlotte of Holstein-Gottorp on July 7, 1774 in the Palace Church at Stockholm Palace; her father was the younger brother of Adolf Frederick.

== Royal Title ==

Charles XIII's full title in Swedish was: "Carl with God's Grace, King of Sweden, Norway, Goths and Vandals, etc. etc. etc. Duke of Schleswig Holstein, Stormarn and Dithmarschen, Count of Oldenburg and Delmenhorst, etc. etc."

The titulature of princes often ended with "etc." to indicate that they also held less significant titles. The number of concluding "etc." could be up to three. They were often written as "&c.", using the ligature &, representing "et" from the Latin word "and".

== Biography ==

At the time of Charles' birth, complications arose, and it was thanks to the intervention of the surgeon Herman Schützercrantz that the delivery was successful. As a thank you, Schützercrantz received an annual pension. At his baptism, Charles was named Sweden's Grand Admiral.

During his upbringing, Charles was the sibling closest to Gustav III; he sided with his brother in conflicts with their mother and helped Gustav III during the coup of 1772, after which he was appointed Duke of Södermanland.

Charles served as an admiral during Gustav III's Russian War, and under his command, the fleet achieved some successes. He was also appointed to lead the land army when Gustav III returned to southern Sweden to deal with the Danish invasion, and he successfully managed the Anjalaförbundet alliance. He remained loyal to the king when, in 1789, the latter pushed through the Act of Union and Security during the Diet. He was the target of the conspiracy of 1789, which aimed to place him on the throne, but the planned coup was called off after he refused to participate. Later, he maintained close ties with leading figures within the opposition, but it is disputed whether he knew about or supported plans to assassinate his brother, Gustav III.

Charles became the nominal regent for his nephew, Gustav IV Adolf, in 1792, but in practice, he delegated power to his favorite, Gustaf Adolf Reuterholm. During the regency, a plot to overthrow Charles was uncovered in 1793, known as the Armfeldt Conspiracy. He relinquished the government when his nephew came of age in 1796.

Charles' only son within marriage, Karl Adolf, lived for only one week. This had significance for the future history of the Holstein-Gottorp dynasty on the Swedish throne. When Charles eventually ascended the throne, after his brother was murdered in 1792 and his nephew, Gustav IV Adolf, was deposed and exiled with his wife and children in 1809, he lacked any heirs. The childless king therefore first adopted his relative, Prince Karl August, also of the House of Oldenburg, but after a short stay in Sweden, he suddenly died. The king then adopted the French Marshal Bernadotte, who had no royal lineage himself, and who became the ancestor of the current royal dynasty.

Gustav III's son, Gustav Adolf, therefore became the new regent, and during his reign, Finland was lost, and Pomerania was temporarily occupied by Napoleon. This led to the deposition of Gustav IV Adolf. Subsequently, Charles XIII had to act as a puppet king and was forced to adopt the Danish Prince Kristian August, who however died of a stroke in 1810, which caused great uproar and strong rumors of poisoning. During the funeral procession for Kristian August through Gamla Stan in Stockholm on June 20, 1810, the Lord Marshal, Axel von Fersen, was murdered at Riddarhustorget, accused by the crowd of having poisoned the Danish prince.

It was necessary to appoint a new Crown Prince. Carl Otto Mörner was sent to France to obtain Emperor Napoleon I's opinion on the issue of succession, but he acted independently and sold the Swedish royal crown to the French Marshal Jean Baptiste Bernadotte. It is said that Charles XIII was shocked at having to adopt a lawyer's son from Pau. However, he soon became satisfied with his new adopted son, who was named Karl Johan, and grandson.

In 1812, Karl Johan took over the regency in practice, after the king had become increasingly older and more tired. In late 1809, Charles XIII suffered a stroke, and during government meetings, he would sometimes fall asleep for the remainder of the meeting, and was not always aware of what he approved or rejected, easily being influenced by other government members.
