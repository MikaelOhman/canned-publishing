Gustav II Adolf, born on December 9th (according to the Gregorian calendar) 1594 at Tre Kronor Castle in Stockholm, and died on November 6th (according to the Gregorian calendar) 1632 in Lützen in the then Holy Roman Empire, was King of Sweden from 1611 until his death. During his reign, Sweden established its position as a major power in Europe, and he led Sweden's entry into the Thirty Years' War, where the country's military strength came to determine the political and religious balance of power in Europe. In 1633, the Swedish Riksdag (parliament) honored him with the title "Gustav Adolf the Great."

Gustav II Adolf collaborated with his Chancellor Axel Oxenstierna to implement several reforms and modernizations of the Swedish government administration, legal system, and economy. Together with Oxenstierna, the king founded several cities and schools throughout the kingdom, including the city of Gothenburg and the Västerås gymnasium (high school), established the Svea and Åbo appellate courts, developed the country's trade and industry, and created an administrative structure that largely remains in place in modern Swedish society. He also reformed the Swedish military by creating a strong and mobile army, based on his experiences from previous wars and Dutch tactics, where infantry, cavalry, and artillery worked together effectively, which later became a model for European armies.

For most of Gustav II Adolf's reign, several wars were fought against Sweden's neighboring countries. He ended the Kalmar War with Denmark-Norway in 1613, during which Sweden was required to pay the Älvsborg ransom. He also waged successful wars with Russia until 1617 and with Poland until 1629, which led to Swedish territorial gains in areas such as Ingria and Livonia. In the summer of 1630, he led Sweden into the Thirty Years' War, siding with the Protestant German states against the superior Catholic armies under Holy Roman Emperor Ferdinand II. He conducted a successful campaign in the Holy Roman Empire and became a central figure uniting Protestants, while also achieving his greatest military victory against the imperial army at the Battle of Breitenfeld in September 1631. The king's confrontation with the imperial army's generalissimo Albrecht von Wallenstein led to his death in the Battle of Lützen on November 6, 1632. After the king's death, both Axel Oxenstierna and the king's generals continued the war in his stead, expanding Sweden's influence and territory in both Scandinavia and Germany until the end of the war in 1648.

Gustav II Adolf's legacy has undergone significant changes throughout history. During the 17th and 19th centuries, he was regarded as a hero for Protestantism and religious freedom, but later he has been criticized for his role as an aggressive conqueror. In the 20th century, the glorification of Gustav II Adolf diminished, but his importance as a military and political leader remained prominent, especially in Germany. Several Lutheran churches in Germany are named after him, as is the Lutheran school Gustavus Adolphus College in the United States. In Sweden, Finland, and Estonia, his memory lives on through several statues and squares, and his death day is celebrated annually as Gustav Adolf Day.

Gustav II Adolf's full title in Swedish was: "We, Gustav Adolph, by the grace of God, King of Sweden, Göta and Vände lands, Grand Duke of Finland, Duke of Estonia and Karelia, Lord over Ingria."

== Childhood and Upbringing (1594–1611) ==

Gustav II Adolf was born at Tre Kronor castle on December 9, 1594, as the son of the then-Duke Charles (later King Charles IX) and his wife in her second marriage, Kristina of Holstein-Gottorp. He was named Gustav after his grandfather, King Gustav Vasa, and Adolf after his maternal grandfather, Duke Adolf of Holstein-Gottorp. He had two younger siblings, Maria Elisabet and Karl Filip, as well as half-sister Katarina Karlsdotter Vasa, half-brother Carl Carlsson Gyllenhielm, and cousin Duke Johan. Through his lineage on his mother's side from the Holstein house, Gustav Adolf also belonged to the circle of German princely families from which the champions of Protestantism emerged, including Philip the Magnanimous of Hesse, Moritz of Saxony, William of Orange, and Moritz of Orange. The young duke mostly grew up at Nyköpingshus in Nyköping, where his father ruled his duchy, which consisted of Värmland, most of Södermanland and Närke, and certain parishes in Västmanland and Västergötland.

The King of Sweden during Gustav Adolf's upbringing was Sigismund, since 1592, the son of Duke Charles' brother, King Johan III, who had also been King of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth since 1587. Sigismund, a Roman Catholic, supported Catholicism in Sweden, which led to the Reformation and the anti-Catholic movement initiated by his grandfather, Gustav Vasa, stagnating during his reign. For this and other reasons, Duke Charles launched a rebellion against Sigismund with the aim of breaking the Swedish-Polish union and completing the Reformation in the country. Initially, Duke Charles faced resistance, but he eventually prevailed.
