Full text

Olof von Dalin (originally named Dahlin, later Dalin), born August 29, 1708, in the vicarage of Vinberg parish in Halland, and died on August 12, 1763, at Drottningholm Palace, was a Swedish poet, prose writer, and historian. The magazine he founded, *Then Swänska Argus*, is considered to mark the beginning of the younger "New Swedish" period.

== Biography ==

=== Olof von Dalin's Background and Upbringing === He came from old clerical families on both his father's and mother's sides. The year after his birth, in 1709, his father, who was the vicar of Vinberg, died, leaving behind a widow and four children. His father had taken his name from his hometown, the parish of Dalstorp in Älvsborg County. It wasn't long before the widow remarried, as was customary at the time, to the successor, Reverend Severin Bökman. It soon became apparent that Olof was an unusually gifted child, and he showed particular talent as a poet and artist. His stepfather personally guided him in his early studies. At the age of 13, Dalin was sent to Lund University, where Dr. Kilian Stobæus and philosopher Andreas Rydelius took care of him. After initially studying medicine, Dalin abandoned this path and devoted himself to the humanities. There, Rydelius became his mentor and also influenced Dalin's intellectual development.

=== Moving to Stockholm === In 1727, Dalin moved to Stockholm, where he was employed as a tutor by Councilor Claes Rålamb, based on Rydelius' recommendation. He gradually gained acceptance into several of Stockholm's prominent families, including that of President Conrad Ribbing. Dalin wrote poetry intended for these circles, as well as for the knightly society "Awazu och Wallasis," founded in 1732. The Grand Master of "Awazu och Wallasis" was Olof von Dalin's best friend, Carl Fredrik Piper. In 1731, Dalin became an extraordinary clerk at the National Archives, and in 1732 he obtained a clerk position at the Chancery Collegium. He used some of his free time for historical research.

=== Then Swänska Argus ===

In December 1732, the first issue of *Then Swänska Argus*, Dalin's moralistic-satirical weekly publication, was anonymously published by the printer B. Schneider. The inspiration for "Argus" came from English periodicals such as those by Richard Steele and Joseph Addison, particularly *The Spectator*. These publications were also imitated and translated in "Argus"' Swedish predecessor, *Sedelärande Mercurius*, which was published by Carl and Edvard Carleson from 1730 to 1731. "Argus" itself used translated material, from *The Spectator* and even more so from the Dutch-French copy *Le Misantrope*. Certain typical characters were drawn from this source: the soldier Hiertskott, the courtier Ehrenmenuet, among others. What was new in Swedish publications was the inclusion of stories about ordinary people who discussed everyday problems in plain language, as well as political satire presented in fairytale form. Another novelty was the publication of letters. These letters served both as a literary narrative device, where Dalin's talent for satire shone through, and as a platform for (actual or fictional) readers to express their opinions.

"Argus" aimed to strike a balance, criticizing what Dalin perceived as the opposing flaws of the time: on one hand, pedantry, and on the other, ostentation and superficiality. In political matters, Dalin was necessarily cautious, but in economic policy, he presented himself as a mercantilist and advocate for protecting domestic industries. His sympathies lay with the "young nobility," and he was close to some of the "dissatisfied" elements within the emerging Hat Party.

In March 1733, despite its caution, the newspaper was banned by the Kanslikollegium (a government board) due to certain criticisms directed at religion, the state, and public morality, particularly comments about the old and new nobility. The ban was lifted four days later by Chancellor Arvid Horn, possibly influenced by the court – Queen Ulrika Eleonora appreciated the publication.

There was great curiosity surrounding the author's identity. One theory suggested that the word "Argus" was an acrostic formed from the names of the contributors (which could have stood for: Abrahamson, Rosenadler, Gyllenborg, Wilde, and Stiernman). At the 1734 Diet (the then-current parliamentary assembly), the Riksdag (Swedish parliament) recommended to the government "an unknown author, who under the name of Argus with published writings both pleased and served the public," although this recommendation faced opposition from the clergy and peasant representatives. It was not until 1735 that it became widely known that Dalin was indeed the same person as the highly regarded, "witty and learned Mr. Argus." At that time, he was presented to the king and queen by Rålamb, who rewarded him with valuable gifts. His childhood friend, Baroness Stina Virding, was also present. In 1737, thanks to the Riksdag's recommendation and the king's favor, Dalin was appointed royal librarian, and at the 1738 Diet, he was granted an additional salary.

=== The 1730s: Dalin, the Polymath === During the 1730s, Dalin continued his work in various fields. Among his works from this decade are particularly noteworthy his memorial poem dedicated to Conrad Ribbing, the lengthy poem he composed for Carl Gustaf Tessin, dedicating it to him in his role as Marshal of the Realm at the Riksdag (parliament) of 1738 under the title "Reflections on Injustice," and the satire "Aprilverk om vå" influenced by Boileau.
