Hjalmar Emil Fredrik Söderberg, born July 2, 1869, in Stockholm, and died October 14, 1941, in Copenhagen, was a Swedish author, writer, and translator. His literary work is considered one of the most significant in Sweden during the 20th century. It was controversial during his lifetime, but has since gained increasing recognition from both readers and literary scholars.

Söderberg wrote poetry, short stories, essays, literary and theater reviews, novels, and plays, as well as critical commentaries on contemporary society and several works on religious history. He is also known for his vivid portrayals of Stockholm. Among his most acclaimed works are *Martin Bircks ungdom* (Martin Birck's Youth), *Doktor Glas*, and *Den allvarsamma leken* (The Earnest Play). The first is a coming-of-age novel with autobiographical elements, and even in the latter – a deeply moving story about relationships – the personal aspects are evident. *Doktor Glas* is a moral philosophical tale centered around a murder. Both of these later books have been adapted into films multiple times.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Education ===

Hjalmar Söderberg was born at Grev Turegatan 47 A – now number 35 on Östermalm in Stockholm. He was the son of Fredrik Söderberg (1833–1900), a treasurer at the Kammarkollegiet, and Emilia (1836–92), née Brander; both parents were from Stockholm and married in 1865. Söderberg grew up at Majorsgatan 10 and 12 with his sister Frida (1866-1926; who died unmarried). The family home was a well-ordered civil servant's residence in Ladugårdslandet, later renamed Östermalm. At the Norra Latin secondary school, he had classmates including Bo Bergman and Carl G. Laurin, who became lifelong friends. There was also the literary society "Concordia." Another source of inspiration for him was the Stockholm Reading Room with its books. However, Söderberg did not have a strong inclination towards academic studies. After graduating from high school, he studied at Uppsala University, but without much success. He only spent a few months there during the autumn semester of 1890.

=== Career as a Civil Servant and Journalist ===

After working for several years at the General Customs Board, Söderberg began his career as a journalist. In the autumn of 1891, he sought employment in Kristianstad. There, in December 1891, he replaced the cultural journalist Emil Kléen at Kristianstadsbladet. He stayed there until April 1892. His time in Kristianstad was cut short because his mother suddenly fell ill, and Söderberg immediately returned to his hometown. Back in Stockholm, and after his mother's death, he began writing reviews as a freelancer for Dagens Nyheter and Ord & Bild. From 1897, he was recruited as a regular contributor to the circle around Verner von Heidenstam, who had taken over Svenska Dagbladet with financial support from Ernest Thiel. There, Söderberg primarily worked as a theater critic and columnist until 1908. By the time he debuted as a novelist, he had already made a name for himself as a journalist and writer.

=== Family Life === From January 18, 1899, to February 15, 1917, Hjalmar Söderberg was married to Märta Abenius (1871–1932), who was the daughter of Carl Johan Abenius and Augusta Maria Elliot. As newlyweds, the couple lived at Villagatan 6. Together, they had three children: Dora, born in 1899 at Folkungagatan 95; Tom, born in 1900 at Styrmansgatan 51; and Mikael (Michael), born in 1903 at Observatoriegatan 6.

During the years 1901–07, when the family lived on Observatoriegatan, Söderberg rented a "writing studio" at Rådmansgatan 73 from 1905 to 1908 so that he could have peace and quiet for work. The home and the writing studio were located on parallel streets, with Kungstensgatan in between. At both of his addresses, which were marked with plaques, Söderberg wrote short stories for "Främlingarna," and he worked on the novel "Doktor Glas." Several short stories from "Det mörknar öfver vägen" were also written here, as well as the play "Gertrud." This play is a love drama. It has been filmed by the Danish director Carl Th. Dreyer. "Gertrud" is based on the author's own painful experiences. From this period comes the phrase "the lust of the flesh and the soul's unyielding solitude."

=== Crisis in the Marriage – Moving to Copenhagen === After 1906, Söderberg lived intermittently in Copenhagen at various addresses, including Poste restante København. In August 1907, Söderberg was in Skåne, where he met the Danish woman Emilie Voss (1876–1957) in Mölle. They fell in love, and three years later, on November 6, 1910, their daughter Betty was born in Munich, where they had traveled secretly. In February 1917, Söderberg obtained a divorce from his first wife, Märta, partly because of changes to the marriage laws in Sweden in November 1915. On August 23, 1917, Hjalmar Söderberg and Emilie Voss married at the City Hall in Copenhagen. However, it was not until January 1919 that it was recorded in the Swedish population registry that Söderberg had "moved from Sweden to abroad, Denmark/Själland/Copenhagen."
