Nicodemus Tessin the Younger, born May 23, 1654, in Nyköping, and died April 10, 1728, in Stockholm, was a Swedish architect and count. He is known as the castle architect behind Stockholm Palace and was one of the great architects of the Baroque era in Sweden. He served as the city architect of Stockholm from 1681 to 1715. On June 21, 1697, he was appointed superintendent; in 1701, he became a court marshal; and in 1717, an over-court marshal. For his contributions, he was elevated to the rank of baron and count.

== Biography == Nicodemus Tessin the Younger was born in Nyköping as the son of Nicodemus Tessin the Elder and Maria Persdotter Swahn. He was presented at baptism by Dowager Queen Maria Eleonora. Tessin attended the German School in Stockholm, where he studied architecture under his father. He showed early talent and gained recognition for an ode to Charles XI that was published. In 1664, he enrolled at Uppsala University with the Stockholm nation, where he studied mathematics, French, and Italian. From 1671, he came under the patronage of Dowager Queen Hedvig Eleonora, which allowed him to travel to Italy for studies in 1673. He was accompanied by Marquis del Monte, and they traveled through Germany to Verona, and then spent a significant amount of time in Rome, where Queen Christina arranged his education. He studied under Lorenzo Bernini, as well as Carlo Fontana and Giovanni Pietro Bellori. As the Renaissance demanded, he received training in the aesthetics of antiquity, particularly Roman art. He returned to Sweden in 1678 with an unparalleled education, but was then sent to France and Louis XIV along with diplomat Daniel Cronström. In France, he received inspiration and guidance from Charles Le Brun, André Le Nôtre, and Jean Bérain. For a short time, he stayed in England with Charles II, where he met Christopher Wren. Several drawings from these travels have been preserved.

In 1680, Tessin was appointed as the head of Queen Hedwig Eleonora's building projects and also became a chamberlain. From 1687 to 1688, he traveled again to France and Italy to gather inspiration for the interior design of Drottningholm Palace, where he met Burchard Precht. This journey is documented in a diary. By that time, he had already succeeded his father as the royal architect and also became the city architect in Stockholm, the latter position shared with his half-brother, Abraham Winantz. On June 21, 1697, he was appointed as the chief superintendent, in 1701 as the marshal of the court, and in 1705 as the senior master of ceremonies. For his contributions, he was elevated to the ranks of baron and count.

Over time, his administrative ambitions took precedence. In 1714, he became chancellor at Lund University and, in connection with this appointment, he resigned from his position as city architect. Charles XII appointed him as a royal councilor and member of the Judicial Revision Committee in 1712. His political career created enemies for him, and after Charles XII's death, he joined the Holstein faction and sought an alliance with Russia, which led to him falling out of favor in 1726 and being dismissed by Frederick I. He died in 1728 and was given a simple, private burial in Stockholm Cathedral.

Nicodemus Tessin the Younger was the father of Carl Gustaf Tessin from his first marriage to Countess Hedwig Eleonora Stenbock. She died in 1714, and Tessin remarried in 1716 to Baroness Maria Barbro Horn af Marienborg, who died in 1725; his second wife was a lady-in-waiting and known for her involvement in political intrigues at court.
