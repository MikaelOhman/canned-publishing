Results from the European Central Bank's survey on consumer expectations – May 2026.

Source ECB Read the original at ECB →2026-06-26
TL;DRA survey reveals that consumers expect lower price increases for goods and services in the future, but they remain concerned about high unemployment rates.
In plain language

The European Central Bank has investigated what ordinary people think about the economy. Most people believe that inflation, which means how much prices increase, will decrease over the next year. However, there is still more uncertainty in the economy than there was a few years ago.

People with lower incomes often feel more worried about high prices than those who earn a lot of money. Younger people also tend to believe that prices will rise less than older people do. At the same time, many are hoping for their salaries to increase slightly over the coming year.

Regarding jobs, the picture is mixed. Some hope for better economic growth, but many still believe that more people will be unemployed in the future. However, there is a significant difference in what different groups think; those with lower incomes expect more unemployment than those with higher incomes.

When it comes to housing and loans, many believe that house prices will increase slightly next year. The interest rate on mortgages, which is the cost of borrowing money for your home, is expected to remain at roughly the same level as before.

Full text

June 26, 2026

Compared to April 2026:

In May, the median perceived inflation rate over the past 12 months remained unchanged at 4.0%. The median expectations for inflation over the next 12 months decreased to 3.5%, compared to 4.0% in April. Expectations for inflation in three years remained constant at 2.9%, and inflation expectations for five years also remained unchanged at 2.4%. Uncertainty surrounding inflation expectations for the next 12 months decreased, but still remains at a higher level than before the outbreak of the conflict in the Middle East. Respondents in lower income brackets continued to report, on average, higher perceptions and expectations regarding inflation compared to those in higher income brackets. Younger respondents (ages 18-34) continued to report lower perceptions and expectations regarding inflation compared to older respondents (ages 35-54 and 55-70).

Consumer expectations for nominal income increases over the next 12 months increased to 1.0%, compared to 0.8% in April. At the same time, the perceived nominal increase in consumption over the past 12 months rose to 5.4%, compared to 5.3% in April. Expectations for nominal increases in consumption over the next 12 months decreased to 3.8%, compared to 4.3% in April, with respondents in the three lowest income brackets expecting slightly higher consumption increases than those in the two highest groups.

Expectations regarding economic growth over the next 12 months increased to -1.7%, compared to -2.2% in April. In contrast, expectations for unemployment over the next 12 months rose to 11.3%, compared to 11.2% in April. As observed in previous months, households with lower incomes expected the highest level of unemployment (13.7%) over the next 12 months, while households with higher incomes anticipated the lowest level (9.5%). Consumers continued to expect that future unemployment would be slightly higher than the perceived current unemployment rate (10.7%), suggesting a largely stable labor market.

In May, consumers expected the price of their homes to increase by 3.6% over the next 12 months, compared to 3.7% in April. As in previous months, expectations for increases in housing prices were higher among households with the lowest incomes (4.1%) than among those with the highest incomes (3.4%). Expectations regarding mortgage interest rates over the next 12 months remained unchanged since March and stayed at 4.9%. Similar to previous months, households with lower incomes expected the highest mortgage interest rates (5.6%) over the next 12 months, while households with higher incomes anticipated the lowest rates (4.4%). The proportion of households reporting a tightening (compared to those reporting an easing) in access to credit over the past 12 months increased further in May, reaching the highest level since February 2024. At the same time, the proportion of households expecting stricter lending conditions (compared to those expecting an easing) over the next 12 months decreased.

The publication of the CES results for June is scheduled for July 24, 2026.
