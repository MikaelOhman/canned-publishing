Lovisa Ulrika of Prussia (German: Luise Ulrike), born July 24, 1720, in Berlin, and died on July 16, 1782, at Svartsjö Castle, was Queen of Sweden from 1751 to 1771. She was the daughter of Frederick William I of Prussia and Sophie Dorothea of Hanover, and the sister of Frederick II of Prussia. She was married to King Adolf Frederick. In some contemporary sources, she is also referred to as Ludovica Ulrika and Ludvika Ulrika.

== Princess of Prussia == Lovisa Ulrika was a goddaughter of Queen Ulrika Eleonora, after whom she was named Ulrika, because she had been born shortly after the peace treaty between Sweden and Prussia. She exchanged letters with her godmother and received her portrait in 1728. Lovisa Ulrika was described as beautiful, intelligent, strong-willed, and temperamental. She was closest to her younger brother August Wilhelm, nicknamed "Hulla," and they were both the favorites of their father, along with her sister Sophia.

=== Education === Lovisa Ulrika grew up with the ideals of the French Enlightenment. Her education was heavily influenced by France: her governess was the Huguenot Marthe de Roucoulle, assisted by the lady-in-waiting Mademoiselle de Jaucourt, who in 1734 was replaced by her daughter, Marthe de Montbail; her tutor was Maturin Veyssiére la Croze. Her interest in education did not cause controversy with her father, who opposed her brother Frederick's educational pursuits. As an adult, she lived at her mother's court and reportedly favored her sister Amalia. Lovisa Ulrika got along well with her brother Frederick and shared his interests in education. At her mother's and brother's courts, she came into contact with classical scholars and Enlightenment figures, including the naturalist Maupertuis in 1740 and Voltaire in 1743, with whom she later corresponded.

=== Marriage Proposals === Lovisa Ulrika was the subject of many marriage proposals, but none were realized. In 1732, there were unsuccessful negotiations with England regarding a marriage to one of the two British princes. Other potential matches included Charles III of Spain, Louis IX of Hesse-Darmstadt, and Christian IV of Zweibrücken-Birkenfeld, whose proposal was put forward by Cardinal Fleury and who was a candidate in the Swedish succession election. This proposal was rejected after he lost the election. In 1743, she was appointed coadjutrix in Quedlinburg with the expectation of becoming abbess, a position she reportedly strongly disliked.

When arrangements were being made for the marriage of the Swedish heir apparent, Adolf Frederick, Louise of Denmark was initially the preferred choice, but plans changed in 1743. At this time, an alliance between Sweden, Prussia, and Russia was being planned through marriages: this would be achieved by having the Swedish heir apparent marry a member of the Prussian royal family, and the Russian heir apparent marrying Sophia of Anhalt-Zerbst, who had been selected by Prussia. Consequently, either Lovisa Ulrika or her sister Anna Amalia were considered as potential brides for the Swedish heir apparent. The Swedish envoy in Berlin, Carl Rudenschöld, inspected both Lovisa Ulrika and Anna Amalia and recommended that a proposal be made to the former.

Frederick II described Lovisa Ulrika through his chief minister, von Podewik, to the Swedish negotiators as haughty, temperamental, and scheming, and instead recommended Amalia, whom he described as kind-hearted and more suitable for Sweden. He also claimed that Lovisa Ulrika was unwilling to marry, had a secure income from a convent, and that her mother did not want to be separated from her. In reality, however, both Lovisa Ulrika and her mother were reportedly working to promote her candidacy. The real reason is believed to be that Frederick II wanted to exert influence in Sweden through his sister and considered Anna Amalia to be a more manageable agent for Prussia.

The Swedish government insisted, after consulting with the groom, on Lovisa Ulrika, and on March 1, 1744, Frederick II gave his consent. Lovisa Ulrika was educated about Sweden, advised to avoid any involvement in politics, and converted to Lutheranism on June 28.

== Crown Princess ==

=== Wedding === On July 17, 1744, a wedding by proxy took place in Berlin between Louisa Ulrika and Adolf Frederick, with her beloved brother August William representing the groom. She officially became the Crown Princess of Sweden as a result. She was escorted from Berlin by the Swedish ambassador Carl Gustaf Tessin, Ulla Tessin, and Lotta Sparre. At the estate of Gnatzkow, belonging to C. H. von Bohlen, she met the Governor-General of Swedish Pomerania and the Swedish retinue that would escort her to Sweden. She retained only a few court officials from her Prussian entourage, including Wilhelmine von der Knesebeck, who would serve as her lady-in-waiting for her first two years in Sweden.

Louisa Ulrika inherited the former queen's household staff, which included, in addition to the First Lady of the Court, Hedvig Elisabet Strömfelt, and the Chambermaid, Margareta Magdalena Torstensson, the ladies-in-waiting Maria Siöblad, Ernestine von Griesheim, Henrika Juliana von Liewen, Catharina Charlotta De la Gardie, Lotta Sparre, and the sisters Ulrika and Agneta Strömfelt.
