Karl Knutsson, sometimes referred to as Charles VIII, was born in October 1408 or 1409 and died on May 15, 1470. He served as Swedish Regent from 1438 to 1440, and was King of Sweden from 1448 to 1457, 1464 to 1465, and 1467 to 1470. He also reigned as Charles I, King of Norway, from 1449 to 1450. He was the son of Knut Bonde and Margareta Karlsdotter (Sparre of Tofta).

Karl Knutsson, of the Bonde family, was born in 1408 or 1409 as the son of the knight Knut Bonde and Margareta Karlsdotter. After his father's death in 1413, he was raised by his stepfather, Sten Turesson (Bielke). In 1429, upon his mother's death, he inherited the estate of Fågelvik in Småland, which became his primary residence before he entered politics. In his younger years, Karl Knutsson traveled extensively abroad, learning foreign languages and studying military tactics.

== Chief Justice, Regent, and Ancestor == Following the outbreak of the popular movement led by Engelbrekt Engelbrektsson in 1434, Karl Knutsson was continuously involved in political life. He joined Engelbrekt during the siege of Stegeborg in 1434 and was mentioned that same year as a member of the council. During the subsequent conflicts and negotiations, he gained such a good reputation that the council, in 1435 during the reconciliation with King Eric of Pomerania, proposed him as first choice for Chancellor (Lord High Constable). The king disapproved of this and instead appointed him Marshal. When the uprising resumed due to King Eric's broken promises, Karl Knutsson was elected Chief Justice at the meeting in Arboga in 1436, bypassing Engelbrekt. Although he initially had to share his power with Engelbrekt because the common people clearly expressed their dissatisfaction with the election, after Engelbrekt's murder on April 27, 1436, Karl Knutsson became the sole ruler. When the brave but volatile Erik Puke tried to attract Engelbrekt's supporters and challenge Karl Knutsson's authority, Erik Puke was arrested and sent to Stockholm, where the Chancellor had him executed. In March 1438, when the council believed they had reached a "friendly agreement" with the king, Karl Knutsson resigned from his position as Chief Justice, but in the autumn of the same year, when King Eric was in Visby, a national assembly in Stockholm re-elected Karl Knutsson as Regent.

Chancellor Krister Nilsson (Vasa) worked to maintain the Kalmar Union and bring back Eric. After discontent openly erupted, Karl Knutsson successfully captured and humiliated his rival, but this man's views were still supported by several members of the council. When Duke Christopher of Bavaria was elected king in Denmark, Karl Knutsson felt he could no longer resist the unionist faction. After demanding Finland and Öland as fiefdoms, and later expanding the demand to include the entire Svartsjö province, Karl Knutsson agreed in 1440 to Christopher's appointment as king. However, Karl Knutsson never fully possessed all of these large fiefdoms. Krister Nilsson refused to relinquish Viborg, and in 1441, the king took Åbo Castle away from him. The favor he had initially enjoyed with the king, who had appointed him Chancellor, had ceased, and after 1441, he spent almost all of Christopher's reign primarily in Finland. He only visited Sweden once, in 1446, when he participated in the expedition to Gotland, and at Svartsjö, he celebrated his daughter Kristina's wedding to Erik Eriksson.

== King of Sweden and Norway == As soon as news of Christopher's death (1448) reached Finland, Karl Knutsson went to Sweden. At the subsequent royal election on June 20, 1448, he received the most votes. Although the legal provisions were not fully observed during the election, he was formally proclaimed at Mora Stenar on June 28 and subsequently crowned king. His first attempt at governing, an effort to regain Gotland, failed due to Magnus Gren's negligence and treachery. At the same time, he also ran for the Norwegian crown, and on November 20, 1449, he was elected king by the national party and crowned in Trondheim. Another faction, which dominated southern Norway, had meanwhile proclaimed the Danish King Christian I. Karl Knutsson attempted to assert his authority there through military force, but after only a few weeks, the parties agreed on a ceasefire, and the resolution of the dispute would be postponed until the congress in Halmstad, where the issue of Gotland and other points of contention between Denmark and Sweden would also be decided. At this meeting, held at the beginning of May 1450, the Swedish representatives, contrary to Karl Knutsson's instructions, pledged to persuade him to renounce his claims to Norway. The dispute over Gotland, however, was to be resolved at a subsequent meeting. In addition, a new union act was agreed upon, which would be applied in the event of the death of either or both kings.

Reluctantly, the king had to accept the decisions made at the national assembly in Arboga that same year; however, a conspiracy by some of the representatives to restrict his right to freely dispose of the provinces was discovered by the king before it could come to fruition.
