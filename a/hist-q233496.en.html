Kristina of Holstein-Gottorp, born April 12, 1573, in Kiel, and died December 8, 1625, at Nyköping Castle, was Queen of Sweden through her marriage to Charles IX. She served as regent during the king's absence in 1605 and led the provisional regency government in Sweden for two months in 1611.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Kristina was the daughter of Adolf of Holstein-Gottorp and Kristina of Hesse, and a member of the House of Holstein-Gottorp. In 1586, she received a marriage proposal from Sigismund, then Prince of Sweden, who later became King of Poland and Sweden. The marriage had been suggested by Elizabeth Vasa, but the plans were not carried out. Instead, she married Sigismund's uncle, Duke Charles of Södermanland. Kristina was a cousin to Charles' former wife, Maria of Palatinate. This marriage was also arranged by Elizabeth Vasa. Through the marriage, Charles forged a stronger alliance with German Protestants. The wedding took place on August 27, 1592, in Nyköping. Kristina bore the children Gustav Adolf (later King of Sweden), Maria Elisabeth (married to Duke John of Östergötland), Kristina (who died in infancy), and Charles Philip.

=== Queen === In 1598, Charles seized the Swedish throne from his nephew Sigismund. According to tradition, Kristina encouraged Charles to depose Sigismund as revenge for Sigismund having broken off marriage plans twelve years earlier. Kristina was crowned alongside Charles in Uppsala Cathedral on March 15, 1607. Queen Kristina has been described as strict and stingy; a well-known example is the story that she personally measured out the thread for her ladies-in-waiting. She has also been described as dominant, curt, and strong-willed, and Charles appreciated her competence. He called her "a woman who can handle things," and their relationship was generally considered good. In 1604, she was designated as Sweden's future regent in the event of Charles' death, provided that the heir to the throne was still a minor: Charles IX named her, describing her as "our beloved Queen, our son's loving mother," as guardian for his son and thus the ruler of the kingdom if the son ascended the throne before reaching adulthood. As guardian for both the future king Gustav Adolf and the younger son Karl Filip, she held an important leadership position in both politics and economics. In 1605, she served as acting regent of Sweden while Charles was in Livland. Contemporaries accused her of encouraging Charles' brutality, but this is not considered accurate. However, she was actively involved as a mediator.

=== Dowager Queen ===

After King Charles' death in 1611, she, together with Duke John of Östergötland, led the regency government for her son, Gustav II Adolf, from October 1611 to December of the same year, when he reached his majority. However, she remained regent for her younger son, Karl Filip, and thus ruled over his duchies: Södermanland, Närke, and Värmland.

Kristina exerted some influence over Gustav II Adolf during the early years of his reign. From 1613 to 1615, she opposed his plans to marry her lady-in-waiting, Ebba Brahe, and eventually managed to prevent the marriage. Her motivation was the domestic political complications that a marriage with a member of the Swedish nobility would entail, seeing a negative example in Johan III's marriage to Gunilla Bielke, and preferring the advantages of a dynastic marriage. In 1612, despite protests from the church, she arranged a marriage between her daughter, Maria Elisabet, and her cousin, Johan of Östergötland. She later regretted this marriage, which reportedly severely worsened the already poor mental health of both parties. She also arranged, against the wishes of Chancellor Axel Oxenstierna, the marriage of her stepdaughter, Katarina, to Johan Kasimir in 1615. Karl Filip was described as her favorite child, and she reluctantly agreed to his Russian claim to the throne in 1611. When the project ultimately failed in 1614, she was reportedly relieved.

In Karl Filip's duchy, Kristina remained regent in her capacity as her son's guardian. Her concern for Karl Filip's actual or perceived rights led to several minor disputes with the crown. Kristina continued to manage the ironworks and other industrial properties that Karl had developed in the former duchies of Värmland, Södermanland, and Närke. She was also unusually involved and skilled in these areas, and very interested in mining. The duchy, with Nyköping as its capital, was a center for major grain exports and iron and weapon production stimulated by the eastward foreign policy expansion. This industry was leased to the Dutchman Willem de Besche in 1611. Through her administration of the duchy, she became one of the most important financiers of the Swedish crown during that era.

Kristina prevented Gustav Adolf's attempts to restrict Södertälje's right to engage in foreign trade. She defended the duchy's autonomy against the crown, asserted its unrestricted jurisdiction, and opposed the nobility's military service under the crown. During the Diet, her demands for autonomy led to a conflict in which her spokesperson, Doctor Nils, was arrested. The conflict between the crown and the duchies was eventually resolved.
