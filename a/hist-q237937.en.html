Karin Månsdotter (who styled herself Queen Catherine), born November 6, 1550, in Uppland, and died on September 13, 1612, at Liuksiala royal estate, was the Queen of Sweden from July 4 to September 29, 1568. She was married to King Erik XIV.

== Biography ==

=== Background === Karin was born in Uppland as the daughter of Måns, a landsknecht (a type of mercenary soldier) or stockeknecht (a prisoner forced into military service), and his wife Ingrid. Little is known about her father, but it is clear that Karin's maternal family originated in the parish of Trögd in Uppland, where her three maternal uncles were farmers. Her relatives are first mentioned after her elevation to queen: Anna Nilsdotter in Hammarstorp, who has sometimes been identified as her sister; her maternal uncles Hans Jacobsson in Gnesta, Jakob Jakobsson in Håtuna, and Erik Nilsson in Husby-Sjutolft; and the children of her uncle, as well as the widow Anna Eriksdotter in Sko in Harby in Villberga. Karin first appears on the historical scene when she worked for the musician Gert Cantor, who also ran a tavern and enjoyed the king's favor. In early 1565, when Karin was not yet 15 years old, she met Erik XIV for the first time. Karin moved to Stockholm Castle and initially worked as a chambermaid for Elizabeth Vasa at court, and also cared for the king's illegitimate daughter, Virginia Eriksdotter. Besides Désirée Clary and Queen Silvia, Karin is the only queen Sweden has had who was neither royal nor noble. Much has been written over the years about the seemingly improbable love story between King Erik and the orphaned peasant girl. There is no evidence to support the story that the king discovered Karin while she was selling nuts in the Stockholm marketplace. However, there is considerable evidence suggesting that Erik early on planned to marry his mistress. All indications suggest that the king truly fell deeply in love with the 17-year-younger girl, and his closest advisors noted that she had a calming effect on the king, who suffered from periods of mental illness.

=== Arrival at the Court === At the age of fifteen, Karin was recognized as one of King Erik XIV's mistresses. Like other unmarried nobles of that time, he surrounded himself with several lovers. Before his relationship with Karin, he had a group of mistresses who were collectively known as "the mistress group" and held a recognized position called "Royal Mistress." These women came and went, but Karin was different. She quickly surpassed the others.

Preserved documents from the court show that it didn't take long for her to enjoy the wealth of the king's castle. She was provided with the finest clothes available, had private rooms, and learned proper manners and etiquette. Soon, the king also provided his mistress with her own servants, just like a queen. In 1565, Carl Kammarsven, Anders Hansson, and Gert Cantor's wife Karin were mentioned as her servants, and the following year this was expanded to include Elin Andersdotter, Karin Hansdotter, and Bovik Tattare.

The couple's first child, Sigrid, was born in 1566 and was treated from the beginning as a true princess.

Visitors to Sweden had positive opinions of Karin: "She was beautiful, kind, modest, humble, and devout towards everyone, and she knew how to skillfully adapt to the king's desires," wrote a Danish author. The French ambassador reported to his government: "It is said that she is a very virtuous woman who readily helps anyone in need, and who has saved several innocent people from execution. If she has seen the king upset and angry at someone and has been unable to calm him with her prayers and tears, she has secretly informed the person in danger so that they could stay away for a few days."

=== Queen of Sweden === On July 4, 1568, Karin married the king, and the following day she was crowned Queen of Sweden. However, a secret wedding had already taken place on July 13 of the previous year, officiated by Archbishop Laurentius Petri Nericius and approved by the Council of State. Karin Månsdotter received her queen's insignia at the end of December 1567. This marriage was not viewed favorably by the contemporary ruling elite, as it violated the custom of marriages within social classes. Besides Karl Knutsson's marriage to Kristina Abrahamsdotter a century earlier, this is the only instance in Swedish history where a king married his mistress. As queen, Karin had her own secretary, Thomas Jakobsson, who was believed to be a relative.

Erik, after pressure from the Council of State, had obtained permission to marry whomever he chose, regardless of social status. Nevertheless, the marriage to a woman with Karin's background posed a significant challenge to the nobility, which became one of several reasons why her husband was imprisoned and deposed in 1568–1569. When Erik XIV was overthrown from the Swedish throne, Karin had already given birth to two children: a daughter named Sigrid and a son named Gustav, who was the legitimate heir to the throne.
