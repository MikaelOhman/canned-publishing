Erik Dahlbergh, born Erik Jönsson on October 10, 1625, in Stockholm, and died on January 16, 1703, also in Stockholm. He was buried in the church of Turinge in Södermanland. He was a Swedish count, military figure, architect, and civil servant; he served as War Councilor in 1677, Governor of Jönköping from 1687 to 1693, Field Marshal in 1693, General Governor of Bremen and Verden in 1693, and General Governor of Livland from 1696 to 1702. He was married to Maria Eleonora Drakenhielm (1650–1680), the daughter of Wilhelm Böös Drakenhielm, who served as General Customs Commissioner.

Erik Dahlbergh planned and edited the famous illustrated work *Suecia antiqua et hodierna* (Ancient and Modern Sweden), which contains a large number of copper engravings depicting Swedish castles and cities, many based on Erik Dahlbergh's own drawings.

Among other things, Erik Dahlbergh left behind what is known as a diary, published in 1913, but known much earlier. The book was written retrospectively by Dahlbergh and is therefore more of an autobiography than a diary. The book received considerable attention and has been the basis for much of the historical writing about Dahlbergh. However, later research has shown that Dahlbergh often embellishes and exaggerates his own accomplishments, and that the book contains many inaccuracies. For example, his description of his role in "The Crossing of the Belt" is a post-hoc fabrication that does not align well with other historical sources.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Youth === After initially attending school in Söderköping, Erik Dahlbergh was sent to a writing and arithmetic school in Hamburg at the age of thirteen. In 1641, he was employed as a clerk by Gerdt Rehnsköld, then the general treasurer of Pomerania and Mecklenburg, with whom he was related through his wife. It is said that Rehnsköld initially treated him strictly, but soon gained confidence in him and used him for several important assignments. During this time, Dahlbergh witnessed the military campaign of 1643 and participated in the storming of Friedrichsort. In 1646, he became a chamberlain in Pomerania and that same year accompanied the merchant ships hired by Rehnsköld to transport Swedish troops to Germany. After returning to Germany, he was appointed as a quartermaster in the fortifications in 1647. He received his education in engineering, particularly under the guidance of Colonel Conrad Marderfelt, and soon attracted the attention of people like Count Charles Gustav of Palatinate. In 1650, he was sent to Frankfurt am Main to collect 120,000 Riksdaler (Swedish currency) that the Swabian, Rhenish, and Frankish circles were supposed to pay to the Swedish crown. It took three years to complete this task. During that time, Dahlbergh used the opportunity for studies – particularly in mathematics, fortification techniques, and perspective drawing – as well as travel and practical exercises. He also made many useful acquaintances during this period. Shortly after returning to Sweden, and after discussions about him being given a company in the guard, he undertook another trip to France and Italy, leading a group of young Counts Cronstjerna. During this time, he particularly developed his skills as an architectural draftsman. In Rome, he had the opportunity to see "many shrines and rarities" in the company of Queen Christina. Several times, obstacles arose for his plans to extend the trip to Constantinople and Egypt, and soon he was called back to serve his homeland.

=== Military Career ===

Erik Dahlbergh joined the Swedish forces on July 16, 1656, the day before the Battle of Warsaw began. He was appointed lieutenant general quartermaster in the army by King Charles X Gustav, but shortly thereafter contracted a bout of plague. After recovering, he remained actively engaged.

He oversaw fortifications at Thorn, served as a general adjutant, and distinguished himself during negotiations with Prince George II Rákóczy of Hungary, as well as during reconnaissance missions along the western Bug River and at the fortresses of Brest Litevsky and Frederiksodde, now Fredericia (1657). During the capture of the latter, he led the cavalry and infantry across the water, around the palisade, into the fortress.

Dahlbergh gained his greatest military reputation during the Danish Wars and the campaign across the Belt strait in early 1658, although his role has been downplayed by later historians.

It was Dahlbergh who examined the ice before the crossing. Subsequently, Dahlbergh conducted several reconnaissance missions, including of Copenhagen's fortifications and various landing sites.

At the outbreak of the second Danish War in July 1658, he advised that Copenhagen should be stormed immediately rather than besieged, citing his knowledge of the fortifications, and he promised to lead the assault, using horses and wagons to cross ditches and walls. For a time, he led the sieges of Copenhagen and Kronborg. He also participated in the unsuccessful storming of Copenhagen on February 11, 1659.

In 1660, Dahlbergh became lieutenant colonel in the Södermanland regiment. The regency government during Charles XI's minority apparently did not want to utilize his services. He was not appointed as the king's tutor in mathematics and fortifications, despite reports that Charles X Gustav had intended for him to be.
