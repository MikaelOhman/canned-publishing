Sophia Magdalene of Denmark (Danish: Sophie Magdalene), born July 3, 1746, at Charlottenborg in Copenhagen, and died August 21, 1813, at Ulriksdal Palace, was a Danish princess who was married to King Gustav III and Queen of Sweden from 1771 to 1792. She was the daughter of Frederick V of Denmark and Louise of Great Britain, and the mother of Gustav IV Adolf.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and early years ===

Princess Sophia Magdalene was born on July 3, 1746, at Charlottenborg, a secondary residence of the Danish royal family located near Kongens Nytorv in central Copenhagen. She was the second child and eldest daughter of then Crown Prince Frederick and Princess Louise of Denmark-Norway, and she was named after her grandmother, Queen Sophia Magdalene of Brandenburg-Kulmbach of Denmark. Upon her birth, Sophia Magdalene was eligible to inherit the Danish throne and, as the king's eldest daughter, was often referred to by the title of "princess." She had her own court staff from birth.

Just a month after her birth, her grandfather, King Christian VI, died, and Sophia Magdalene's father ascended the throne as King Frederick V. Her older brother, Prince Christian, passed away in 1747, making her the sole heir to the throne until her younger brother (the future King Christian VII) was born in 1749. Early on, a marriage between Sophia Magdalene and the Swedish heir apparent, Gustav, was proposed by the Swedish Estates. The purpose was to try to ease the old animosity between Denmark and Sweden, which had been rekindled by the Swedish succession crisis of 1743, where Denmark's candidate had lost. Both in Denmark and Sweden, the proposal was met with skepticism from both sets of parents. Sophia Magdalene's mother reportedly feared that Sophia Magdalene would be mistreated by her future mother-in-law, Louisa Ulrika, because Louisa Ulrika was known even in Denmark for her pride, ambition, and hostility towards all things Danish, which she demonstrated to the Danish envoys in Stockholm.

Louisa Ulrika stipulated that Sophia Magdalene should be raised in Sweden as a condition for agreeing to the marriage, but this requirement could not be met due to opposition from Sophia Magdalene's mother. In the spring of 1751, the engagement was officially announced. This pleased Carl Gustaf Tessin but horrified the royal couple, Adolf Frederick and Louisa Ulrika. According to the contemporary Axel von Fersen the Elder, the engagement was a result of French pressure. After her mother's death, the responsibility for Sophia Magdalene's upbringing largely fell to her grandmother, who reportedly provided her with a loving but strictly religious upbringing. Because her father and soon also her brother Christian were considered immoral, she and her sisters were raised even more strictly so that they would not follow their bad example. She was said to have had a close relationship with her siblings and also got along well with her stepmother. However, her father was often brutal and frightened Sophia when he appeared drunk in front of her, for example, by kissing dogs on her, which created a lifelong phobia of dogs. Sophia Magdalene had a French governess, Jeanne Rosselin, and in 1754 she received a tutor, Kai Koch. She studied history, geography, German, French, English, and drawing. Sophia Magdalene was also taught dancing and music by the ballet master Pierre Laurent from the Paris Opera Ballet.

In 1760, negotiations regarding the marriage were resumed by the Danish side. These discussions took place between Denmark and Lovisa Ulrika, as King Adolf Frederick was considered only in a formal capacity. Lovisa Ulrika argued that the engagement had been imposed upon them against their will by Count Tessin and should no longer be valid, and that she planned to arrange a marriage between Gustav and a German princess instead. The Danish ambassador also complained that Lovisa Ulrika treated him with hostility and filled Gustav's mind with prejudices about Denmark. However, Frederick V was determined to fulfill the commitment: "His Danish Majesty could not allow his daughter's interests to be sacrificed for the Swedish queen's prejudices and whims." Nevertheless, the Swedish public generally viewed the prospect positively, as they expected Sofia Magdalena to resemble the previous Danish princess who had been Queen of Sweden: Ulrika Eleonora of Denmark. The Estates demanded that the marriage be completed, and the "hat party" particularly supported it, hoping that Sofia Magdalena would be a more compliant and gentle queen than the haughty and politically influential Lovisa Ulrika. In 1764, reports indicated that Gustav himself was becoming increasingly favorable to the marriage, as he wanted to free himself from his mother, who had begun to view him with disapproval due to his growing influence at court. On October 20th, Gustav personally told Lovisa Ulrika that he freely desired to enter into the marriage. Lovisa Ulrika received this news very poorly, as she was negotiating with her brother and Catherine of Russia to arrange something beneficial for Denmark as compensation for a marriage that would not take place. Finally, on April 3rd, 1766, the engagement was formally declared.
