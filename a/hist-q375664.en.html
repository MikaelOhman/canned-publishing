In plain language

Halsten was the son of King Stenkil and ruled in Sweden approximately between the years 1067 and 1070. Because his reign was short, it is difficult to know exactly when he died, but he is mentioned in several old historical texts and law books.

In older sources, Halsten is described as a good leader who was cheerful and judged fairly in all matters. According to these texts, Sweden declined after his death because his passing meant the country lost a good king.

Halsten had two sons who also became kings later: Filip and Inge the Younger. There are also traces in old letters from the Pope that suggest Halsten may have ruled together with his brother for a period.

Halsten (or Hallsten) Stenkilsson, likely born around 1050 and died in an unknown year, was a Swedish king who ruled Sweden for a short period after 1067, probably until around 1070. Adam of Bremen mentions him in a scholion, stating that he was deposed in favor of Anund Gårdske. He is also mentioned in the royal chronicles of the older Västergötland Law and Hervar Saga, and possibly in a papal letter.

== Biography == The sources agree that Halsten was the son of King Stenkil. The Västergötland Law and Hervar Saga also state that he was the father of both kings Filip and Inge the Younger, and Hervar Saga also claims that he co-reigned with his brother Inge the Elder, which may be supported by a letter from Pope Gregory VII addressed to Inge and a king "A" whom this Inge is said to have ruled alongside. Both are urged to introduce tithes and are called *reges wisigothorum* ("kings of the West Goths," but actually 'Visigothic kings'). This "A" is usually interpreted as Halsten, but it may possibly refer to Håkan Röde. In the list of rulers in the Västergötland Law, he was described as courteous and cheerful, and whenever a legal matter was brought before him, he judged fairly, and "therefore Sweden suffered from his departure and death." However, none of the sources mention when this occurred. He was eventually succeeded on the throne by his sons Filip and Inge.
