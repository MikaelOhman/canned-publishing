TL;DRThe Swedish Tax Agency has collected over half a billion krona in additional taxes from companies that violate the regulations when creating cryptocurrencies (known as "mining"). This is because these companies have attempted to conceal their activities in order to obtain tax benefits they are not entitled to.
In plain language

The Swedish Tax Agency has investigated companies that operate so-called "mining farms," which use powerful computers to create new digital currencies, also known as cryptocurrencies. During the years 2024 to 2026, these inspections resulted in nine different companies being required to pay over half a billion krona in additional taxes and VAT adjustments.

The problem is that many of these companies deliberately provide false information about their activities. They falsely claim to be providing other services in order to obtain benefits they are not legally entitled to. Because it is difficult to see exactly what is happening inside the computer farms, some businesses try to conceal their operations in order to avoid paying the correct taxes and fees.

The Tax Agency emphasizes that these inspections are important for protecting the Swedish tax system. When companies provide false information about their activities, it can lead to money leaving the country and may also result in violations of regulations designed to prevent money laundering (the process of concealing the origin of illegally obtained funds).

Listen Tax Agency press release 2026-06-22 Continued significant tax adjustments in the mining sector The Tax Agency's audits of companies involved in cryptocurrency mining ("mining") show that problems within the industry persist. Audits of these companies from 2024 to 2026 have resulted in total tax adjustments exceeding half a billion Swedish krona.

The Swedish Tax Agency is conducting inspections of cryptocurrency mining facilities to prevent attacks on the tax system. In the most recent review of nine companies, these businesses were assessed with over half a billion krona (approximately $48 million USD). During the previous inspection of data centers from 2020-2023, eighteen companies were assessed with a total of one billion krona (approximately $96 million USD).

"The companies we have inspected often have specific arrangements to conceal their cryptocurrency mining activities, which involves creating new units of digital currencies. The purpose is to obtain tax advantages that the companies are not entitled to," says Patrik Lillqvist, Head of Intelligence at the Tax Agency.

The total adjustments resulting from the inspections conducted from 2024-2026 amount to 529 million krona (approximately $51 million USD), including 57 million krona (approximately $5.5 million USD) in tax penalties and 471 million krona (approximately $45.5 million USD) in VAT adjustments.

"The investigations have been complex, not least due to the difficulty in determining who is actually using the mining equipment and what type of business activities are being conducted in the data centers," says Patrik Lillqvist.

He adds that there are clear incentives for unscrupulous actors to try to misclassify their businesses.

From a VAT perspective, it is crucial how the business is classified. Cryptocurrency mining often falls outside the scope of VAT because there is no clear counterparty in the transaction, which means input VAT cannot be deducted. At the same time, some companies describe their activities as subject to VAT, for example, by claiming that they provide computing power or other services that are subject to VAT.

"Sometimes, companies conceal their mining operations and instead claim to be engaged in VAT-taxable activities. In this way, tax revenues can disappear from the country through incorrect payments, unpaid output VAT, and unreported cryptocurrency assets," says Patrik Lillqvist.

In addition to tax losses, there are other risks because cryptocurrency mining facilities are not covered by anti-money laundering legislation.

The audits conducted between 2024 and 2026 covered nine companies that have been assessed with a total of 529 million Swedish krona in additional taxes. The Swedish Tax Agency's audits of cryptocurrency mining facilities from 2020 to 2023 resulted in 18 companies being reassessed, totaling nearly 1 billion Swedish krona.
