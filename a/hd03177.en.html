Simplified supplier verification during procurement.

TL;DRThe government wants to make it easier for government agencies to verify companies that want to provide services to the state and municipalities. The purpose is to quickly determine whether a company has a criminal record, unpaid taxes, or significant financial problems.
In plain language

The government proposes new regulations that will make it easier to monitor companies participating in procurement processes. A procurement process is when government agencies purchase services or goods from private companies. The new system aims to facilitate coordination of checks to determine if a company has grounds for being excluded from bidding on contracts.

The checks should reveal whether a company has committed crimes, failed to pay its taxes, or has not paid its social security contributions. It should also be possible to identify if a company has significant financial problems, such as having gone bankrupt. This information should be available both when selecting a company to award a contract to and when monitoring ongoing contracts.

The proposal also aims to standardize the rules for different types of systems. This applies to both regular procurements and so-called "choice systems," where private providers are allowed to offer services, such as in healthcare and social care. The goal is to ensure that the rules for when a company should be excluded are the same, regardless of the type of service involved.

If the proposal is approved by the parliament, the new regulations will come into effect on July 1, 2026. The proposal also includes rules on how personal data can be handled and how confidentiality should be maintained during these checks.

Full text

The proposal suggests establishing a system for coordinated record checks to be applied during the procurement and use of choice-based systems. This system should allow procuring authorities and entities to verify whether there are grounds for excluding a supplier due to criminal offenses, unpaid taxes and social security contributions, or due to certain serious financial difficulties (e.g., bankruptcy). Such a supplier check should be possible both when awarding contracts and during the follow-up of agreements. The proposal also includes supplementary confidentiality provisions, provisions on professional secrecy, and provisions on legal support for certain personal data processing related to the information that may emerge from the checks in the criminal record database.

Furthermore, the provisions on excluding suppliers in the Act (2016:1145) on Public Procurement (LOU) should largely also apply to the exclusion of applicants in the Act (2008:962) on Choice-Based Systems. A procuring authority should, with a few exceptions, be able to exclude an applicant based on the directive-driven exclusion grounds that follow from LOU. These changes mean that the grounds for when a supplier can be excluded will not differ depending on whether the service in question is procured or provided within a choice-based system.

The proposed amendments to the law should come into force on July 1, 2026.
