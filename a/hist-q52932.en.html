Frederick I (Friedrich of Hesse), born April 17 according to the Julian calendar; April 28 according to the Gregorian calendar; 1676, died March 25 according to the Julian calendar; April 5 according to the Gregorian calendar; 1751, was King of Sweden from 1720 to 1751. From 1730, he was the ruling Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel in Germany until his death. Frederick was successful as a field marshal in the War of the Spanish Succession on the side of the allies against France. He became a widower without children in 1705, and then proposed to Ulrika Eleonora, the heir to the Swedish throne, whom he married. She became Queen after Charles XII's death, but abdicated in 1720 in favor of her husband, who found himself well suited to the role of constitutional monarch during the Age of Liberty.

Frederick introduced the House of Brabant into Sweden as a unique occurrence, and its Hessian branch could have become the Swedish royal dynasty, but he died without his own heir to the throne within that line. Through Eufemia of Sweden, he descended from King Magnus Ladulås through eleven generations.

== Crown Prince Frederick of Hesse == Frederick I was born in Kassel as the son of Landgrave Charles I of Hesse-Kassel and Maria Amalia of Courland. He received a well-rounded education for his time, made trips to the Netherlands, Switzerland, Italy, Denmark, and Germany during his youth, and presided over the government in 1699 during his father's visit to Italy. On May 31, 1700, he married his cousin, Louise Dorothea of Prussia. His wife fell ill with smallpox and died in 1705 after a childless marriage. After becoming a widower, Frederick began carefully looking among the royal houses of Europe for a new wife, and his attention turned to Ulrika Eleonora, who was perfectly suited to him because she was the only surviving sibling of Charles XII and was expected to succeed him. Frederick had greater ambitions than simply being Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel; he wanted to become king or at least have significant influence over a larger kingdom.

Shortly after the outbreak of the War of the Spanish Succession (1701), Frederick was sent by his father at the head of a support force to the Netherlands, where he served as a general and distinguished himself through personal bravery on the side of the coalition until its end with the Peace of Utrecht (1713). Frederick served under figures such as Eugene of Savoy and the Duke of Marlborough. During the war, Frederick sustained injuries, including being shot in the chest and receiving several cuts to the head from a saber. Three of Frederick's brothers were killed in the same war.

As early as 1710, Frederick began negotiations for a marriage with the Swedish princess Ulrika Eleonora. Her brother, Charles XII, took a long time to give his consent, but in 1714 he finally agreed, and Frederick traveled to Stockholm, where the wedding was celebrated on March 24, 1715.

As a member of the royal family through marriage, Frederick participated in the campaign against Norway in 1716 and sustained a serious wound at the Battle of Hølands prestegård. The same year, he was appointed Generalissimus (Supreme Commander) of the Swedish armed forces and followed his brother-in-law, King Charles XII, on his campaign against Fredriksten in 1718. Among historians who believe that Charles XII was assassinated at Fredriksten, Frederick is often cited as the one behind the fatal shot fired at his brother-in-law. To support his wife's claim to the throne, he had Georg Heinrich von Görtz, the main supporter of Duke Karl Friedrich of Holstein-Gottorp, arrested after Charles XII's sudden death. He also lifted the siege of the fortress and, through intense lobbying, persuaded the military command to conditionally approve Ulrika Eleonora's accession to the throne. After the estates formally elected Ulrika Eleonora as queen, defeating her nephew and rival, the aforementioned Karl Friedrich of Holstein-Gottorp, she wanted to elevate her husband to co-regent, but failed to achieve this at the 1719 Diet. Frederick had to settle for being granted the title of "Royal Highness" by the estates.

However, it soon became apparent that Frederick held considerable influence over the government due to the queen's unlimited affection and trust in him. The title of Generalissimus, which had previously been an honorary title, now became a supreme command over the kingdom's armed forces, with significant influence on military affairs and promotions. He was primarily responsible for leading the defense against Russian incursions. He also supported the decision to cede Bremen and Verden to Hanover and part of Pomerania to Prussia in order to gain their support (especially from England) against Tsar Peter I. Furthermore, he played a major role, and a conflict between him and the Chancellor Gustav Cronhielm led to the latter's unfortunate dismissal. The experience of the heir apparent's significant actual influence and the queen's autocratic behavior led the estates to support Ulrika Eleonora's plan to elevate Frederick to the throne the following year. The condition was that the queen herself would renounce the crown; however, if she outlived her husband, she would resume her rights. The right of succession to the throne remained with her male descendants. On March 24, 1720, Frederick I was elected King of Sweden and crowned in Stockholm on May 3 of the same year. This elevation was accompanied by further restrictions on royal power.
