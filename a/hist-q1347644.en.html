Laurentius Andreæ, the Latinized name of Lars Andersson, was born in the 1470s and died on April 14, 1552. He was a Swedish priest, archdeacon of Strängnäs, and an Evangelical Lutheran reformer.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Laurentius Andreæ studied in Skara and Uppsala, where he earned his bachelor's degree. He then continued his studies at the universities of Rostock and Leipzig, obtaining his master's degree in 1498. He likely studied law, as he styled himself "notarius publicus with apostolic authority," a title uncommon in Sweden. He visited Rome three times, the last time in 1509, where he attempted to regain Birgittahuset, which the Pope had seized, and become its leader. However, this position was given to Petrus Magni, another important figure in Swedish Reformation history.

=== Archdeacon of Strängnäs === Upon returning to Sweden, Laurentius Andreæ joined the cathedral chapter of the Diocese of Strängnäs and quickly advanced. After Bishop Matthias Gregersson Lillies' execution in 1520, Laurentius became the archdeacon, effectively the administrator of the diocese (while the dean served as a religious representative). The fact that he was not promoted to bishop by either Christian II or the Pope likely further distanced this nationalistic man from the hierarchical system, whose negative aspects he had witnessed in Rome. Laurentius took young deacon Olaus Petri under his wing and found in his preaching a religious foundation for a new national church policy.

=== Secretary to King Gustav Vasa ===

At the Diet of Strängnäs in 1523, Laurentius Andreæ met the newly elected King Gustav Vasa, who quickly recognized in the respected archdeacon a man capable of handling the complex religious issues. Tradition holds that it was Laurentius who introduced Gustav Vasa to Olaus Petri, and the king soon realized the advantages he could gain from Lutheran teachings. He readily employed both clerics, appointing Laurentius as his secretary. With astute understanding of human nature, Laurentius always ensured that the king appeared decisive, but "Master Lars" was instrumental behind nearly all significant religious endeavors. Thanks to his personal knowledge of the Roman Curia, negotiations with it were concluded as early as spring 1524, meaning Sweden broke its ties with the papacy earlier than any other country. It is also likely that Laurentius drafted the remarkable governmental proposal at the Diet of Vadstena in 1524, the first preserved document of its kind, which granted the king powers exceeding those of the Middle Ages. In a famous letter dated February 21, 1524, to Vadstena Abbey, he articulated the Protestant concept of the church on behalf of the king and provided theological justification for the confiscation of silver from churches. The background was that representatives of the abbey had asked Laurentius to defend their interests. He defended the state's actions by arguing that the church consisted of the community of believers, i.e., the Christian people, and that the church's money belonged to the people and should be used for the common good. According to Laurentius, it was sacrilegious to spare the church's funds when the people were in danger. In the letter, he also officially recommended studying Martin Luther's writings. He urged the monks to always test any foreign doctrine ("peregrina doctrina"), whether from Luther or anyone else, against the Bible. As a sign of Gustav Vasa's trust, Laurentius was appointed Archdeacon of Uppsala in 1524, along with his position in Strängnäs, and he also received a seat on the council and the income from the Virmo parish in Finland. It is noteworthy that Gustav Vasa's defense of the confiscation of silver did not include any references to theological arguments.

During Gustav Vasa's struggle against Bishop Hans Brask of Linköping, the archbishops, monastic orders, and the entire Roman Catholic Church system from 1524 to 1527, Laurentius worked behind the scenes as a cautious but determined force. He was also one of the driving forces behind the publication in 1526 of the New Testament of the Bible in Swedish. In the aforementioned letter to the monks of Vadstena, his desire is evident not to radically overturn things, but to preserve continuity with the past as much as possible, and not to completely transfer power over the church from the Pope to the King, as was done in Germany. This is evidenced by the Västerås Recess from the Diet of Västerås in 1527, where Laurentius achieved significant success. In 1528, he oversaw the consecration of bishops, which preserved the apostolic succession ("successio apostolica") for the Church of Sweden. From this year also dates his only known printed work, "En kort undervisning om trona och goda gärningar" (A Short Instruction on Faith and Good Works) (1528), which explains the Lutheran position in simple terms. As Archdeacon of the Archdiocese, he presided over the Örebro Council in 1529, which formed the ecclesiastical complement to the Västerås Diet; undoubtedly, he, along with Olaus Petri, led the decisions, which included maintaining all "Catholic" ceremonies but giving them an "evangelical" interpretation.
