Elisabet Vasa, born on April 4, 1549, at the royal estate in Kungsör, and died on November 19, 1597, in Stockholm, was a Swedish princess and Duchess of Mecklenburg, the youngest daughter of Gustav Vasa and Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud), and married to Duke Christopher of Mecklenburg.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Elisabet received a good education and was described as being very musical. She was considered one of the "most accomplished" princesses in Europe, and many plans were made for a politically advantageous marriage, including with the future Grand Duke Francis I of Tuscany. In 1562, she became engaged to Duke Christopher of Mecklenburg, but he ended up in long-term Polish captivity, and Elisabet's brother, Johan III, broke off the engagement. During the Nordic Seven Years' War, Elisabet is said to have shown generosity towards captured Danish and German officers in Sweden and tried to alleviate their imprisonment.

=== Early Conflict and Marriage Proposals === Elisabet maintained a lively correspondence with her siblings. During the reign of Johan III, she was often asked to act as an informant regarding Johan's actions towards his other siblings, particularly Karl. The correspondence could be political and beneficial to her brother Karl, whom she was close to.

In the autumn of 1573, a plot against Johan III was prepared, led by Charles de Mornay, who was in contact with Kristina of Denmark and the French diplomat Charles Dançay in Copenhagen. The plan was for Johan to be killed in an assassination attempt by Scottish swordsmen who were scheduled to perform at a banquet held before the departure of the Scottish mercenaries to the Baltic region in October. Karl would then be placed on the throne, and de Mornay would reveal the supposed location of Erik XIV's gold treasure in exchange for better conditions for Erik in prison. However, de Mornay never gave the signal, and the plan was abandoned and revealed in September 1574. It was never clarified who else was involved, but it is documented that the suspected individuals, Hogenskild Bielke, Gustav Banér, and Pontus De la Gardie, often met at Elisabet's residence during this time, and she, along with Cecilia and Karl, were believed to have participated in the conspiracy. Charles de Mornay also revealed during interrogation that one of the measures agreed upon after the coup was to increase Elisabet's dowry to 150,000 riksdaler, so that she could enter into an even more advantageous marriage.

During Johan III's reign, there were conflicts regarding whether Elisabet should marry a Catholic or a Protestant. In 1573, negotiations began for a marriage between Elisabet and the son of the Grand Duke of Tuscany, Francesco. At the same time, Elisabet, with the help of Karl and Katarina, was negotiating a marriage with a German Protestant prince, such as a duke from Pomerania or Cleves, without Johan's knowledge.

In 1574, Elisabet received an offer of marriage from Henry III of France. There are theories that it was Karl who first suggested the marriage while Henry III was King of Poland, in order to gain support for obtaining part of Livonia. According to one theory, this proposal was related to Charles de Mornay's conspiracy in 1573. It is pointed out that the French ambassador was not satisfied with Elisabet's dowry, and that according to the plans of the conspiracy, it was intended to be increased precisely so that she could enter into a marriage of higher status.

Henry III's mother, Catherine de Medici, considered Elisabet suitable because she wanted a royal princess as a daughter-in-law, and also because she saw the match as an alliance that could benefit French influence in Poland. According to contemporary historians, Catherine also wanted a daughter-in-law who was unfamiliar with the language, customs, and traditions of France, so that she would not threaten her position. The French ambassador Charles Dançay in Copenhagen submitted the following report about her during the negotiations for the French marriage:

I am assured that she will be very pleasant, possess good intelligence, be agreeable, have a beautiful figure and posture... everyone praises her great humility; truly, Sire, all who know her praise and honor her virtues. She finds pleasure in playing the harpsichord and plays it better than most; furthermore, she is of a gentle nature and benevolent.

Elisabet is described as beautiful, quick-witted, with a fine figure and good posture, praised for her modesty, and appreciated for her virtues. She could play both the lute and the harpsichord, and "in the eyes of all, she was considered one of the most perfect and virtuous princesses in Europe, and no one had heard anything about even the smallest flaw, either physical or moral."
