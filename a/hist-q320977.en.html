Full text

Knut Eriksson, born no later than the 1140s and died in 1195 or 1196, was King of Sweden from 1167. He was the son of Erik the Holy and Kristina Björnsdotter of Denmark.

Knut Eriksson seized power by first killing Karl Sverkersson (in an attack on Visingsö in 1167). Later, in 1172/1173, he defeated and killed Kol and Burislev, who were half-brothers of Karl Sverkersson (according to some sources) and had declared themselves kings, controlling Östergötland. In 1173, he was recognized as king and reigned for 23 years. One of his significant achievements was his effort to have his father, Erik, canonized. However, this proved impossible because Pope Alexander III was very upset that Stefan, the Archbishop of Uppsala, had been forced to flee for his life when Knut Eriksson took power. Another important action by Knut Eriksson was the resumption of minting coins, which had inexplicably ceased for 150 years.

Knut was married to a Swedish woman, about whose family we only know with certainty that she had a brother named Knut. Based on assumptions and potential misunderstandings in historical accounts, she is often referred to as Cecilia. A papal letter from 1193, preserved in transcript, reveals that during a life-threatening illness, she took a vow of chastity and entered a convent after recovering. King Knut later petitioned the Pope to release her from this vow.

== Biography == After his father, Saint Erik, was killed around 1160, Knut was defeated by his opponents and forced to flee the kingdom. According to a much later chronicle (from the 14th century), he stayed in Norway for three years. However, this information is missing from the Norwegian royal chronicles and likely stems from the chronicler confusing Knut with his son, Erik. Knut returned in 1167 and killed King Karl Sverkersson of Visingsö, according to the West Gothian Law's list of kings. Subsequently, he engaged in battles with Karl Sverkersson's relatives, Kol and Burislev, who claimed the throne with support from Denmark. After his rivals were defeated and killed (around 1172–1173), Knut ruled Sweden unchallenged for twenty-three years, according to the West Gothian Law's list of kings.

From the time of Knut Eriksson dates the first known Swedish trade treaty with another kingdom – in this case, Lübeck. It was concluded through his jarl (earl) Birger Brosa between Knut Eriksson and Duke Henry the Lion of Saxony. Knut also established diplomatic relations with England around 1185 and received, among other things, a suit of armor as a gift from King Henry II of England. According to Sverre's Saga, Knut (and the other Nordic kings) received a letter in 1195 requesting military assistance from the Byzantine Emperor Alexios III Angelos. Knut also maintained good relations with the Norwegian king Sverre Sigurdsson, who married Knut's sister, Margaret, in 1185, but relations with the Danish kings were more strained.

According to medieval annals, Sigtuna was attacked and burned by pirates in 1187 during Knut's reign, according to the Karelian Chronicle (Erikskrönikan). However, archaeological excavations have now confirmed that there is no evidence of a fire layer in Sigtuna that can be linked to this event. According to the same sources, Archbishop Johannes was killed at Almarestäket fortress at the same time.

The oldest surviving original documents issued by a Swedish king are those of Knut, and he was also one of the first, after Karl Sverkersson, to call himself King of Svea and Götaland. Of the nine preserved original documents, eight are related to Viby Monastery near Sigtuna.

There was also extensive minting during Knut's reign, with at least three coins depicting the king with a shield adorned with bars. These may have served as inspiration for Holmger Knutsson's depicted coat of arms on his burial shroud. Knut died of natural causes in 1195 (or 1196) and was buried in Varnhem monastery church, where Magnus Gabriel De la Gardie erected a tombstone over him several centuries later. Overall, Knut's long reign can be seen as the period when the Swedish state began to establish itself according to continental models, with a written central administration and documented diplomatic relations with other kingdoms. However, the sources do not provide information on how this achievement should be attributed between King Knut and his jarl Birger Brosa. He was succeeded as king by his predecessor's son, Sverker II (Sverker den yngre). Children:

Three sons, whose names are unknown, were killed in the Battle of Älgarås in 1205. Erik, King of Sweden from 1208 to 1216. Sigrid, married to Magnus Broka.
