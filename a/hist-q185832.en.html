Emanuel Swedenborg ([eˈmaːnuɛl ˈsvêːdɛnˌbɔrj]; born Emanuel Swedberg), born January 29, 1688, in the parish of Saint Jacob in Stockholm, and died March 29, 1772, in London, was a Swedish scientist (primarily in the field of natural sciences), philosopher, biblical interpreter, Christian mystic, and theologian. Swedenborg had a diverse career as an inventor and scientist. For most of his life, he published numerous writings, mostly written in Latin.

==Spirituality, Followers, and Critics== In 1744, at the age of 56, he entered a spiritual phase during which he claimed to communicate with angels, spirits, and deceased individuals through dreams and visions, leading to a spiritual awakening. He believed he was chosen by God to write a heavenly doctrine based on reformed Christianity. He asserted that God had opened his eyes, allowing him from that point forward to freely visit heaven and hell and converse with angels, demons, and other spirits. Over the next 28 years, until his death, he wrote and published 18 theological works, the most famous of which is "Heaven and Hell" from 1758, as well as numerous unpublished writings.

Swedenborg's theological writings have elicited a wide range of reactions. Towards the end of his life, smaller groups in Sweden and England were formed to study his writings and the truths they believed were contained within his teachings. Numerous authors were influenced by him, including William Blake, C.J.L. Almqvist, August Strindberg, Charles Baudelaire, Honoré de Balzac, and William Butler Yeats, as well as Carl Jung. The Swedish painter and mystic Ivan Aguéli shared Swedenborg's interpretation of the Bible and was likely the first to recognize a connection between it and Sufism, a relationship later noted by the French Islamic scholar Henry Corbin. The philosopher and psychologist William James also followed Swedenborg's teachings. Within spiritualism, he is considered an important precursor, although Swedenborg himself warned against contacting the spirit world.

There were also many critics, such as the contemporary author Johan Henrik Kellgren, who called Swedenborg "a madman," a view that he shared with much of the establishment even to this day. Johan Henrik Kellgren published, among other things, a satirical poem titled "One Does Not Possess Genius for What One is Mad" in 1787. In 1768, a heresy trial was initiated against Swedenborg's writings, as well as against two men who promoted his ideas. However, the trial did not result in any convictions.

In the 250 years since Swedenborg's death, numerous different interpretations of his theology have been proposed (see: Swedenborgianism), and he has been examined in biographies and psychological studies.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years ===

Swedenborg was the son of Bishop Jesper Swedberg and Sara Behm, daughter of the mining inspector and assessor Albrekt Behm (deceased in 1679), whose mother, Anna Danielsdotter Kröger, belonged to the Bure family. Jesper Swedberg was the great-great-grandson of Otte Persson from Sundborn, mentioned in 1571.

Swedenborg's father, Jesper Swedberg, came from a humble background but, after theological studies and travels abroad, became a priest. Through his sermons as regimental pastor in Stockholm, he gained the favor of King Charles XI, and at Swedenborg's birth, he was the royal chaplain. In 1692, the family moved to Uppsala, where the father obtained a position as professor of theology at Uppsala University. In 1696, Swedenborg's mother died, and the following year, his father remarried Sara Bergia. In 1702, the father was appointed bishop of Skara.

The father was interested in the Pietist movement, a revival movement among Lutherans that aimed to separate the church from the state and emphasized the importance of communicating with God rather than relying solely on faith. Jesper also held more unconventional beliefs about the presence of spirits and angels in everyday life. He believed he was constantly surrounded by evil spirits, against which he was protected by a good angel at his side. Possessed individuals and those speaking in tongues would come to the bishop's residence in Brunsbo, and when something that seemed like the work of the devil occurred within the diocese, the bishop would be called upon to drive out the demon or heal the afflicted through prayer, laying on of hands, and similar means. It was in such an environment that Emanuel grew up. As a boy, his mind was filled with thoughts about God and heavenly things, and his parents believed that angels spoke through him. He could enter a state of deep rapture, so profound and heartfelt that his breathing almost stopped. During these times, he felt bathed in an inner light and saw visions from the spiritual world.

Emanuel completed his studies at Uppsala University, and in 1710, he undertook the "Grand Tour," which was customary at the time, traveling through the Netherlands, France, and Germany before arriving in London, where he stayed for the next four years. At that time, London was a center in Europe for philosophical discussions, freedom of expression, scientific ideas, and discoveries.
