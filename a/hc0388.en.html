A new remedy in the data protection law and the criminal data law.

TL;DRThe government wants to make it easier to obtain a response when the Swedish Authority for Data Protection (IMY) is too slow in handling complaints about personal data. After three months, individuals should be able to demand an update on the status of their complaints.
In plain language

Currently, it can take a long time to receive a response when something is reported to the Swedish Authority for Data Protection (Smyndigheten), which is the agency responsible for ensuring that regulations regarding personal data are followed. The government therefore wants to amend the law to give it more power to obtain answers to its questions.

If three months have passed since you submitted a complaint, you should have the right to request an update from the agency. The agency must then, within two weeks, inform you whether or not they will initiate an investigation. If they do not respond within that time, it is considered that they have declined your request.

If the agency refuses to investigate your complaint, you have the right to appeal the decision to a court called the Administrative Court of Appeal (förvaltningsrätten). If the court believes that the agency should have responded to your question, they can compel the agency to provide you with an answer as soon as possible.

The new rules are intended to come into effect on May 1, 2025. The purpose is to ensure that the agency operates more efficiently and does not allow cases to remain unresolved for too long without providing any information.

Full text

The government proposes introducing a new legal remedy for delayed or slow processing of cases by the supervisory authority (the Swedish Authority for Data Protection) through amendments to the Act on Supplementary Provisions to the EU's General Data Protection Regulation and the Act on Criminal Data. A person who has submitted a complaint to the supervisory authority will, after three months have passed since the complaint was submitted, have the right to request information on whether the supervisory authority intends to initiate an investigation or not. According to the proposal, the supervisory authority then has two weeks to either provide such information or reject the request with a reasoned decision. If the supervisory authority fails to provide a response or make a decision within that timeframe, the request will be deemed rejected. The supervisory authority's decision is proposed to be appealable to the administrative court. This also applies to a rejection decision, which will be considered to have been made if no response is provided within the specified timeframe. The administrative court should, if it considers that the supervisory authority should have the opportunity to provide information on whether an investigation should be initiated or not, order the authority to do so as soon as possible.

The proposed amendments are scheduled to come into force on May 1, 2025.
