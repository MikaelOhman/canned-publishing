In plain language

The Viking Age describes the period when people from Scandinavia became known for their seafaring voyages. During this time, the population of Scandinavia increased, and they developed remarkable skills in shipbuilding. This enabled many to travel for trade, establish new settlements, or conduct raids in areas that are now England, Ireland, and Russia.

However, most people living at that time were ordinary farmers who led peaceful lives and did not always identify as Vikings. We know a great deal about how they lived thanks to ancient texts on runestones, Icelandic sagas, and artifacts discovered through archaeology. Historians and researchers sometimes debate precisely when the Viking Age began and ended because they use different methods. However, most agree that the period lasted until the middle of the 10th century.

Full text

The Viking Age is the final period of the Scandinavian Iron Age. It is characterized by Viking raids and lasted from the late 8th century to the mid-11th century (according to British tradition, from the raid on Lindisfarne in 793 to the Battle of Stamford Bridge in 1066). The term is primarily used when describing the prehistory of Northern Europe, but also appears in British and Irish historical writing. In other parts of Europe, this period is either considered part of the Iron Age or part of the early Middle Ages.

The population of Scandinavia increased significantly during this period, while shipbuilding technology advanced. These factors contributed to the first Viking raids, which were plundering expeditions carried out by Vikings – boat-borne warriors from Scandinavia – throughout Europe. On June 8, 793, Vikings raided the Lindisfarne monastery on the east coast of England, an attack that was soon followed by countless military campaigns, as well as trade and colonization. In the east, large numbers of Scandinavians traveled along the Russian rivers. They came as merchants ("foreigners"), or as recruits to become "Varangians," i.e., soldiers in the army and fleet of the Byzantine Emperor, but also as conquerors. According to tradition, the first Russian state, Kievan Rus', was founded with nobles from the Kingdom of Sweden as its rulers.

However, most Scandinavians during the Viking Age lived a peaceful life as farmers, and probably would not have called themselves Vikings. In Icelandic sagas, the term "norrœnir menn" ("Northmen") is used to refer to the population of Northern Europe. A general Swedish term is "nordmän" (Northerners). Through the preserved literary treasures found in Icelandic manuscripts from the 12th and 13th centuries, as well as inscriptions on runestones, we know many details about the lives of people during the Viking Age.

== Periodization and Time Limits == The Scandinavian prehistory is divided into numerous time periods. The end of the so-called Iron Age has come to be known as the Viking Age. A characteristic feature of the Viking Age is that written sources begin to appear, providing information about internal conditions in Scandinavia. These writings, although limited in scope, complement the archaeological evidence in a significant way.

The term "Viking Age," referring to a specific period in history, was first used by the Danish archaeologist Worsaae in 1873. For a Swedish audience, it was the archaeologist Montelius who used this term in 1877 in his "History of Sweden from Ancient Times to the Present Day." The use of the term then spread to textbooks for elementary schools and was applied to the period from the 9th century to the 10th century.

The time limits of the period have shifted over the years, leading to some conflict between historians, who focus on events documented in writing, and archaeologists. Archaeologists first determine which types of artifacts define a period (for example, everything decorated with the "grip" animal style) and then strive to obtain increasingly precise calendar dating for when these types were first produced. The archaeologists' dating methods are primarily scientific, and their period divisions can sometimes fall within decades for which historians have no records at all.

=== The Beginning of the Viking Age ===

Historians initially placed the beginning of the Viking Age at the first recorded Viking raid, the attack on Lindisfarne in 793. Around 1990, archaeologists, based partly on preliminary dating from the Danish city of Ribe, suggested that the material culture of the Viking Age might have emerged as early as around 750. However, with more accurate dating methods, this idea has been abandoned, which resolved a number of problems related to the chronology of the late Vendel period that arose from attempts to push back the start date of the Viking Age. Today, many historians and archaeologists largely agree that the starting date is approximately 790 for the traditional era.

=== End of the Viking Age === British and Swedish historians linked the end of the period to Harald Hardrada's failed Viking expedition to England in 1066, as a result of his death at the Battle of Stamford Bridge, which marked the end of Viking raids on England and halted the Danegeld payments from England. The year 1050 has also been commonly cited as an end date, and most agree that the period ended around the mid-11th century.

==== Archaeological End Point ==== From an archaeological perspective, there is no universally accepted definition of which societal change or type of artifact defines the beginning of the Scandinavian Middle Ages, and therefore it is unclear when the archaeological Viking Age should end. In preparation for the major Viking exhibition in Florence in 1989/1990, the end of the Viking Age was placed at the years 1103/1104. At this time, all of Scandinavia became its own archbishopric, indicating that it had been integrated into the Western Christian cultural sphere.

==== Viking-like Activities After the Traditional Era === From a broader perspective on Viking activity in the Baltic Sea, it is worth noting that the Wends conducted raiding expeditions against Denmark until the end of the 12th century; the last known attack was the Battle of Falster on December 6.

How certain are we about this?

Our understanding of the Viking Age is primarily based on archaeological evidence (graves, settlements, and artifacts), supplemented by Icelandic sagas written 200-300 years later, terse runestones, and occasional accounts from foreign eyewitnesses. While the broad outlines are well-established, much of the everyday life and specific events are reconstructions rather than certain knowledge.
