A 28-year-old woman with a mental illness was denied forced care and was discharged from the hospital in the middle of the night. Shortly thereafter, Lina Bryant was found dead in the snow in the municipality of Tanum. Now, her mother is demanding change.

June 10, 2026, 18:01

Lina Bryant was 28 years old when she was found lifeless in the snow near a house in the municipality of Tanum on November 21, 2022. Because she was lightly dressed and had injuries, the police initially launched a murder investigation. However, that investigation pointed in a different direction. The young woman had been denied help by the psychiatric services shortly before her death, even though she was in a psychotic state, and relatives had warned about her condition.

Lina Bryant was born in Linköping but grew up in Norway, spoke Norwegian, and lived in the country. The case has received a great deal of attention in Norway through the NRK documentary "The Case of Lina: Dead in the Snow," which is now available on SVT Play. Lina's mother, Camilla Muukki, 55, is Swedish but has lived in Norway for 25 years. She feels that the silence in Sweden regarding her daughter's fate stands in stark contrast to the engagement in the neighboring country.

"In Norway, many people have reacted to the inhumane treatment Lina experienced," she told Aftonbladet.

A Courageous and Creative Young Woman

Camilla Muukki describes her daughter as goal-oriented and full of life, with a strong sense of justice. As a child, Lina was reportedly beaten when she stood up for a lonely girl who was being bullied. In school, she was popular and surrounded by many friends.

She had an artistic side, with drawing, music, and photography, and she sold some of her pictures. She cared deeply about people in vulnerable situations.

At the same time, there was a struggle in the background. School was difficult, and she fell behind, and her plans for a career in law enforcement were thwarted. During the isolation of the pandemic, she withdrew, and the first signs of psychosis appeared.

The Illness Worsened

Lina was admitted to compulsory care in Norway but escaped multiple times. She was under compulsory care, but after a period, she was assessed as being able to manage on her own, with support from a care team that would manage her medication. Camilla Muukki states that she alerted the healthcare system about her concerns but received no response.

The situation worsened. Lina lost her housing and spent periods living in a tent and in homeless shelters. She reportedly suffered from delusions and, on one occasion, said on the phone that someone wanted to kill her. Before her last winter, she returned to the area around Kämpersvik in Sweden, where her family had previously had a summer cottage. In several places in the area, passersby noticed her and reacted to the fact that she seemed confused.

The Last Day

At a sports store outside Grebbestad, staff alerted authorities about a barefoot woman in the cold. Lina was taken to the hospital by ambulance and interacted with staff at an emergency clinic, a psychiatric emergency room, and the general emergency room at Norra Älvsborgs County Hospital.

Despite this, no one assessed that there was a basis for compulsory care. Camilla Muukki says that the staff were instructed to let her daughter leave, and that was in the middle of the night. Lina left the hospital and later died of hypothermia.

According to her mother, Lina was not suicidal. She went out onto a frozen lake and fell through the ice, where she injured herself. She managed to get out of the water and realized how severely hypothermic she had become. Outside a locked house, she considered breaking a window to get inside for warmth, but she didn't.

Afterward, internal investigations pointed to significant mistakes made by both Närhälsan and NU Healthcare. The case was reported under the "Lex Maria" law to the Board of Healthcare and Welfare. Camilla Muukki, who had been on sick leave for long periods, had hoped for an independent review.

Fighting for Change

Camilla Muukki founded the Lina Bryant Memorial Fund, which aims to raise awareness about psychosis and mental health, and to promote a more respectful approach in healthcare. She says that the work is a way for the family to move forward.

"My greatest fear is that the discussion will not happen. What happened to my Lina must never happen again," she says.

Kristofer Wennerström, chief physician at Närhälsan, regrets what happened.

"Our Lex Maria investigation concluded that deficiencies in competence regarding compulsory legislation and the application of this legislation were the most important explanation for what happened," he wrote in a response to Aftonbladet.

Jesper Swärd, chief physician at NU Healthcare, calls it a diagnostic error.

"They incorrectly assessed that she did not meet the criteria for issuing a care certificate according to the law on compulsory psychiatric care," he wrote.
