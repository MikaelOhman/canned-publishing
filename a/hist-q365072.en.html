Sverker the Younger (also known as Sverker Karlsson and Sverker II), born in 1164, died on July 17, 1210, at the Battle of Gestilren (killed in action). He was King of Sweden from 1196 to 1208, son of King Karl Sverkersson and Queen Kristina.

== Biography == When Sverker's father, King Karl, was killed in 1167 on Visingsö by Knut Eriksson in the struggle for the crown, Sverker was taken to his mother Kristina "the White" (hvítaleð) and her family in Denmark for a safer upbringing. When King Knut died around 1196, his sons were too young to be chosen as king, so Sverker returned to Sweden and was elected king with the support of Jarl Birger Brosa. Sverker married the Danish noblewoman Bengta Hvide by 1190 at the latest, and they had at least three children. She died before 1200, when Sverker remarried to Birger Brosa's daughter Ingegärd, and they had a son named Johan. During Sverker's reign, the power struggle between the Sverkerska dynasty and the Erikska dynasty reached its peak. Sverker had close ties to the papacy and promoted the church's policies in Sweden, as well as a strong royal authority, while the Erikska dynasty advocated for a national church, the king's right to appoint bishops, and the traditional regional power and self-determination of the nobility. [Source needed] Danish interests also supported Sverker, who wanted to expand the Danish kingdom. For example, Valdemar Sejr sought to incorporate Västergötland into Denmark by supporting Sverker with Danish troops [source needed], but failed in the Battle of Lena.

=== Church Privileges ===

In the year 1200, Sverker issued the oldest known general church privilege in Sweden through a charter of donation and privileges for Uppsala Cathedral and Archbishop Olof Lambatunga. These privileges included a distinction between secular (civil) law and canon (church) law, meaning that priests could not be tried in civil courts for criminal offenses but only by a cathedral chapter. The Church in Sweden was also exempted from certain royal taxes, effectively becoming an independent estate. In return, the Church offered the written administration necessary during the transition from several regional power centers led by powerful nobles to a more centralized royal authority and legal system, which Sverker sought to establish.

=== Conflicts and Death === King Sverker's reign was controversial, and when Birger Brosa died in 1202, the conflicts between the different noble factions intensified. Knut Eriksson's four sons, who had previously resided at court, went to Norway. When Sverker appointed his underage son Johan as Earl, the Eriksson brothers reacted with a rebellion. Supported by the Norwegian group known as the birkebeiners, they clashed with Sverker's army in the Battle of Älgarås in 1205 in Tiveden. Three of the brothers were killed in the battle, and the surviving Erik Knutsson fled to Norway.

Erik Knutsson returned with a Swedish-Norwegian army and met King Sverker at the Battle of Lena on January 31, 1208. Sverker had Danish support, and his father-in-law, Ebbe Sunesson Hvide, led the Danish auxiliary force, which medieval sources claimed consisted of as many as 18,000 men (likely a significant exaggeration). However, Erik defeated Sverker's army and was elected King of Sweden, while Sverker fled with his family and Archbishop Valerius to Denmark.

Pope Innocentius III supported Sverker against Erik and the noble faction with a papal letter in 1209, which prevented Erik from being officially crowned king in Sweden. In a final attempt to regain the kingdom, Sverker gathered an army and met Erik and the Folkungar at the long-unidentified location of Gestilren in July 1210, where Sverker was killed. The Westgötalagen royal genealogy mentions that it was the Folkungar who killed King Sverker, and that their leader, Folke Jarl, also died there. Sverker was buried in his family's burial church in Alvastra.

== Marriages and Children == With Bengta (married c. 1180):

  • Helena, married to Earl Sune Folkesson (of the Bjälboätten), a lawman in Västergötland. One of their descendants was Gustav Vasa.
  • Karl (died 1198).
  • Kristina (died 1252), married to Henrik Burwin II of Mecklenburg (also considered to be the daughter of King Karl I).
  • Margareta (born 1192), married to Wizlaw I of Rügen.

With Ingegärd (married 1200):

  • Johan (1201–1222), King of Sweden in 1216 (not to be confused with Jon Jarl).
  • Ingrid, abbess in Vreta.
