Appropriate safety and radiation protection requirements for the extraction and processing of nuclear materials.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the regulations regarding the extraction and processing of nuclear materials such as uranium. The goal is to ensure that safety requirements are aligned with the actual risks and that the work resembles mining operations for other metals.
In plain language

The government has proposed a change to the law regarding nuclear technology activities. Currently, locations where nuclear materials are extracted and processed are classified as nuclear facilities. The new proposal suggests that these locations should instead be considered part of nuclear technology activities, but not as nuclear facilities themselves.

The purpose of this change is to make the regulations for radiation protection and safety more appropriate for the specific risks associated with this type of work. This proposal will ensure that the extraction and processing of uranium are treated in a similar manner to other metals and minerals when applying for permits to begin operations.

The proposal also includes changes to other laws. The government wants the law regarding liability and compensation for radiological accidents to not apply to accidents occurring during the extraction and processing of nuclear materials. Furthermore, waste from this type of activity should no longer be considered a residual product according to the law on financing nuclear residual products.

If the parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new regulations will come into effect on July 15, 2026.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes to the Nuclear Activities Act (1984:3), which would mean that a facility for the extraction of nuclear materials or for processing to concentrate extracted materials would no longer be considered a nuclear facility. Extraction and processing for subsequent concentration of nuclear materials would instead constitute nuclear activities. These changes are intended to create a more appropriate regulatory framework for the extraction and processing of nuclear materials, where radiation protection and safety requirements are adapted to the risks associated with the activity. The proposals mean that the extraction and processing of uranium will, at a general level, be treated in the same way as other metals and minerals within the overall licensing process.

It is also proposed to make consequential amendments to the Act (2010:950) on liability and compensation for radiological accidents, which would mean that the Act would not apply to radiological accidents that may occur in connection with the extraction of nuclear materials with subsequent processing for concentration of the extracted material. Furthermore, it is proposed to amend the Act (2006:647) on the financing of nuclear waste, so that waste from a nuclear activity related to the extraction and processing of nuclear materials is not considered waste.

The proposed amendments to the law are intended to come into force on July 15, 2026.
