Fredrika Bremer (born August 17, 1801, at Tuorla in the parish of Piki near Åbo; died December 31, 1865, at Årsta Manor in the parish of Österhaninge, Stockholm County) was a Swedish author, women's rights advocate, and philanthropist.

Fredrika Bremer is considered the foremost pioneer of the Swedish women's movement, and as an author, she introduced the realistic novel to Sweden. Her novel *Hertha* sparked public debate and ultimately led to the Riksdag (Swedish Parliament) passing a law in 1858 that allowed unmarried women to apply for legal majority (married women did not gain legal majority until the law of 1921). During her lifetime, she was internationally recognized, and her books were sold throughout Europe. She was also appreciated in Australia.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood and Early Years ===

Fredrika Bremer's family history traces back to Bremen, where a merchant named Isac Bremer (1683–1713) moved before settling in Västerås. He married Anna Hult, born in 1687. Their son, Jacob Bremer (1711–1785), relocated to Finland (then part of Sweden) and became a shipowner in Åbo. In his second marriage to Ulrika Fredrika Salonius, born [date unknown], their son Carl Fredric was born. Carl Fredric Bremer, an industrialist and merchant, and his wife Birgitta Charlotta Hollström (1777–1855), were the parents of Fredrika, who was the second youngest of seven children.

Fredrika Bremer grew up in Stockholm, where the family moved in 1805. Her father had been one of Sweden's most successful businessmen, making the family very wealthy. They purchased not only a grand apartment in the capital but also the prestigious estate of Årsta Manor. They also owned Nynäs farm in Ösmo parish and lived there while the dilapidated manor house was being renovated. As was common in affluent families, the children were educated by a governess at home.

Her father was authoritarian and stern, but emotionally driven and unpredictable towards his family, while her mother was a socialite with a French upbringing. From the age of eight, Fredrika Bremer began writing French verse. As a child, she often reacted rebelliously, which was seen as indicative of an artistic temperament. Her mother's upbringing was very strict; every morning, Fredrika and her sisters were required to kneel before her and kiss her hand, and they were expected to eat minimally in order to become gracefully "ethereal," as their future depended on being suitably married off. Unmarried women were considered minors at the time, and it was deemed unacceptable for them to support themselves through paid work.

When the daughters expressed a desire to go out for walks, their mother would respond that such activities were not appropriate for young girls unless they were accompanied by older individuals. If they wanted exercise, they were instructed to stand behind a chair, hold onto the backrest, and jump up and down 200 times. Bremer once attempted this but burst into tears when she lost her patience. Life was better in the countryside, where they could move freely in the manor park and pick flowers, fruit, and berries. However, the lively girl often displeased her parents, and she also felt unattractive to her mother.

Later in life, Fredrika stated that she realized her mother's failings as a parent stemmed from being bound by the prejudices of her time. Nevertheless, she felt deeply unhappy. She never received affection or even words of encouragement from either her father or mother. As she later wrote, she was "excluded from the Garden of Eden of childhood." The education she received at home, she herself described as "of the usual kind," providing "superficial talents, superficial knowledge, moral platitudes in the head, and vanity in the heart." She began to grieve that she was a girl. "To become something for Sweden," she wrote later, "I prayed with burning passion when I was thirteen years old."

In 1813, when the Swedish army moved into Germany, the Södermanland Regiment was stationed at Årsta. The atmosphere was charged with war fever, and the twelve-year-old girl yearned to join the conflict. She devised elaborate plans to disguise herself and earn glory and fame. On a couple of occasions, she even snuck out of the house and walked towards Stockholm, hoping that someone would take her there and then to Germany. However, before she could travel even a quarter of the way, her courage and strength faltered, and she was forced to turn back.

In 1821, Bremer traveled with her family and a servant on a journey to the continent in a covered carriage, a type of educational trip for young ladies and gentlemen. The purpose was for them to learn about languages and continental culture. The family visited Germany, Switzerland, France, and the Netherlands.

While staying in Paris for an extended period, she gained insight into the city's social activities, an experience that she carried with her. After returning home in 1823, she participated in Stockholm's social life or spent her time embroidering, conversing, reading poetry, and other activities deemed suitable for a young lady. "Embroidery on an endless and gray scarf numbed me more and more, that is to say, my vital forces, my desire to live," she wrote in her diary.
