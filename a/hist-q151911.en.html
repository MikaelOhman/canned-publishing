Jöns Jacob Berzelius, known as Jacob Berzelius by himself and his contemporaries, was born on August 20, 1779, in Väversunda sörgård, in the village of Väversunda, southwest of Vadstena. He died on August 7, 1848, in Stockholm. He was a Swedish baron, chemist, and natural scientist who is often called "the father of Swedish chemistry." He discovered the elements cerium, selenium, silicon, and thorium, and two minerals have been named after him: berzelianite and berzeliite. He is also considered the initiator of the chemical notation system that uses abbreviations and parts of element names, such as H2O for water.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Jacob Berzelius was the son of Samuel Berzelius (1744–1783), a colleague (teacher) at the Linköping gymnasium, and Elisabet Dorotea Berzelius, née Sjösteen (1747–1787). The family name originates from the farmstead Bergsätters near Motala; originally, the name was written Bergselius. In 1835, Jacob married Elisabet Johanna (Betty) Poppius (1811–1884) in Stockholm. She was the daughter of Gabriel Poppius, a member of the government.

Jacob's father passed away in 1783, and in 1785, his mother remarried to Anders Ekmarck, a pastor. In 1787, his mother also died, and his stepfather, who soon remarried, had many children to care for. This led to Jacob being sent to live with an uncle to be raised. Even this uncle had a large family. From 1793, Jacob attended the Katedralskolan (cathedral school) in Linköping. He was often misunderstood due to his strong interest in natural sciences. His final report card described him as "a young man of dubious prospects." To support himself, he sometimes had to interrupt his studies. Clas Fredrik Hornstedt was his teacher there and played a significant role in fostering his interest in the natural sciences. In 1796, he began studying at Uppsala University, initially with the intention of becoming a priest, as were many of his ancestors for generations. However, he felt that his knowledge of natural sciences was not useful in this field and instead chose to study medicine.

=== Scientific Career === During his studies, he largely supported himself by teaching others. It was during this time that he began to develop an interest in chemistry. During a summer spent at the Vadstena pharmacy, he learned how to blow glass and make barometers, thermometers, and other instruments. In 1800, he served as a physician for the poor at Medevi spa, where he conducted studies of the spring water, which were later presented as a master's thesis. He also performed the first experiments using electricity as a medical treatment method.

In 1802, he defended his dissertation and became an unpaid lecturer, and subsequently a professor of medicine and pharmacy at the Surgical School (later the Karolinska Institute) from 1807 to 1832. He was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1808 and served as its permanent secretary from 1819 until his death in 1848. On November 10, 1829, Berzelius was made an honorary member of the Royal Society of Literature, History, and Antiquities. He became a member of the Swedish Academy in 1837 (chair number 5). Berzelius was ennobled in 1818 and became a baron in 1835, which is considered to be King Charles XIV John's wedding gift, and in 1841, he received a national award in the form of an annual pension of 2,000 riksdaler banco. Over time, he became a member of 94 intellectual societies. For example, he was elected as a foreign member of the Royal Society in 1813.

=== Discoveries and Contributions to Science === Berzelius is one of the most renowned Swedish scientists ever – only Carl von Linné is considered to have surpassed him – and his work was fundamental to the rise of chemistry during the 19th century. His most valuable contribution was his work on fixed chemical proportions, which involved introducing atomic theory into chemistry. Berzelius advocated for the dissemination of John Dalton's atomic theory, which states that all atoms have a characteristic mass specific to each element. Berzelius's most famous contribution is assigning one- or two-letter symbols to elements. He also proposed several theories regarding the structure of different atoms and discovered several elements: silicon, selenium, cerium, and thorium. In 1818, he published his first table of atomic weights, listing 45 out of the then-known 49 elements. He subsequently improved this table, and in 1826, he published a new one that is very similar to the tables used today.

As early as 1814, he developed the methodology for elemental analysis of organic compounds and also proposed a new mineralogical system, along with his famous electrochemical theory, which states that every chemical compound consists of a positively charged part and a negatively charged part. In 1835, he introduced the important concept of catalysis, which refers to the change that occurs in a reaction due to the presence of a foreign substance.
