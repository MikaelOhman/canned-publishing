Hans Christoph von Königsmarck, born December 12, 1605, in Kötzlin, Prignitz, and died February 20, 1663, in Stockholm, was the Count of Tjust, a German-Swedish military officer, and a Major General in 1640. He served as governor of Bremen-Verden in 1645, became a Lieutenant Field Marshal in 1648, a member of the Swedish Council of State in 1651, and a Field Marshal in 1655. He was ennobled as a Swedish Count in 1651, after which the von Königsmarck family was formally registered at the House of Nobility in 1652.

He was married to Barbara Maria Agata von Leesten (1608–1671), lady-in-waiting to the Duchess of Wolffenbüttel, and father to Conrad Christoph von Königsmarck, Beata Elisabet von Königsmarck, and Otto Wilhelm von Königsmarck.

== Biography == Königsmarck was born at his father's estate, Kötzlin, in the Mark Brandenburg region, as the son of the cavalry master Conrad Königsmarck and his wife, Beatrix Elisabeth von Blumenthal. Even as a child, he served as a page for Duke Frederick Ulrich of Brunswick-Lüneburg. He later entered the service of the Imperial Austrian army in Italy as a cavalry master, but left this position in 1629. When Gustav II Adolf landed in Germany in 1630, Königsmarck joined the Swedish side. Under Lennart Torstenson and Carl Gustaf Wrangel, Königsmarck often commanded an independent mobile corps. Königsmarck also possessed an extraordinary ability to recruit new regiments from the resources he plundered in German territory, and many of his campaigns were specifically designed to provide reinforcements for the main army. However, among the populations of the areas he raided, Königsmarck became notorious, especially because he often took some of the loot for himself. In 1639, he commanded the western wing of the Swedish army, with Westphalia as its primary area of operation.

For Lennart Torstenson, he was an invaluable assistant, playing a crucial role in victories such as the Battle of Jüterbog and the conquest of the Archbishopric of Bremen. He was appointed governor of Bremen, a position he held until his death. Königsmarck also helped to force Johann Georg I of Saxony to agree to a ceasefire.

However, the cooperation between Königsmarck and Carl Gustaf Wrangel was considerably worse. Königsmarck was reluctant to be subordinate to the much younger Wrangel, and after Wrangel took command, Königsmarck almost always operated independently. Perhaps his most famous exploit was the storming of the "Little Side" in Prague in 1648, during which he acquired a vast amount of loot. Königsmarck never participated in Charles X Gustav's Polish War; he was shipwrecked en route to Poland near Danzig. The considerable wealth he had accumulated from the Thirty Years' War and the generosity of Queen Christina made Königsmarck one of Sweden's richest men, with princely estates both in Sweden and his governorship of Bremen.

He died in 1663 from blood poisoning following an operation for a thorn injury.
