Karl Gunnar Myrdal, originally Pettersson, born December 6, 1898, in Skattungbyn, in the then-County of Kopparberg (now part of Gävleborg County), and registered in the parish of Gustafs, died on May 17, 1987, in Danderyd, Stockholm County. He was a Swedish economist, sociologist, and social democratic politician.

He shared the Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel in 1974 with Friedrich von Hayek. Myrdal was a professor of economics at Stockholm University College from 1933 to 1947, and later a professor of international economics at Stockholm University from 1960 to 1967. He served as Minister for Trade from 1945 to 1947 in the Erlander I government and as Executive Secretary of the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe from 1947 to 1957. From 1924, Myrdal was married to Alva Myrdal.

== Early Life ==

=== Childhood === Myrdal was the son of Carl Adolf Pettersson, a builder. His father gradually developed an increasingly conservative worldview. In the book "Childhood" by Gunnar Myrdal's son, Jan Myrdal, it is stated that his father held extreme right-wing views. During his upbringing, the family lived in various places, including Stockholm.

=== High School Years === During high school, Myrdal read the works of Rudolf Kjellén, a right-wing politician and nationalist. Kjellén's geopolitical thinking was one of several influences on his political stance. Rudolf Kjellén profoundly shaped his worldview, something he openly acknowledged. He believed that Kjellén was more important than Karl Marx for the Social Democrats during the construction of the Swedish welfare state. In 1919, he met Alva Myrdal, who would become his lifelong companion.

== Education ==

=== Doctoral Studies at Stockholm University === Gunnar Myrdal initially studied law and obtained a law degree in 1923. During this time, he was involved in student organizations, including the Nationella Ungdomsförbundet and Heimdal. He then switched to studying economics at Stockholm University. His doctoral dissertation, completed in 1927 under the supervision of Gustav Cassel, was titled "The Problem of Price Formation and Change." This dissertation is considered a foundational work for what became known as the "Stockholm School" of economic research. Myrdal's early research largely focused on theories that John Maynard Keynes would later develop further. However, Keynes' theories were heavily criticized, particularly by Friedrich von Hayek, with whom Myrdal would later share the Swedish Central Bank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel.

== Career == Early in his career, Myrdal was interested in methodological questions within the social sciences. In the historical and theoretical work "Science and Politics in Economics" from 1930, he criticized the approach of liberal economists who drew political conclusions directly from what they presented as objectively scientific analyses. Myrdal later developed his own perspective on the "objectivity problem" in the social sciences. He argued that it is important for researchers to explicitly state the value premises that guide their investigations.

=== Professor at the University of Stockholm === In 1933, Myrdal was appointed professor of economics at the University of Stockholm. He served as a professor there for fifteen years, until 1947. At the university, he came into contact with Bertil Ohlin, who was a professor of economics at the School of Economics from 1929 to 1965. Together, they made some of the most important contributions to the Stockholm school of economics. Both Myrdal and Ohlin later received the Swedish Central Bank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel.

Together with his wife, Alva Myrdal, he wrote "Crisis in the Population Question" in 1934. This book had a significant impact on the Swedish debate about family policy in the 1930s.

=== Member of the Swedish Parliament === During this time, Myrdal was also politically active. In 1934, he was elected to the Swedish Parliament as a member of the Social Democrats. He actively worked with leading social democratic politicians to shape Swedish welfare policy, and he participated in the Housing Policy Investigation Committee. He advocated for government intervention to combat unemployment in the 1930s. He also emphasized, along with his wife Alva Myrdal, the importance of equal rights for women.

=== Work in the USA === In 1938, he began a comprehensive study of the situation of African Americans in the United States with the help of the Carnegie Institution of Washington. The book "An American Dilemma," published in 1944 – arguably Myrdal's most significant work – became a classic on race relations and played a role in the American debate. He analyzed the racist practices in the USA, which he believed were in conflict with "The American Creed" – the ideals of freedom, equality, and justice under the law. This was the "American dilemma," which, according to Myrdal, was fundamentally a moral issue.

In this study, he also emerged as an "institutional economist." Myrdal wanted to study non-economic "institutions." This meant that he incorporated not only economic but also historical, sociological, anthropological, political, and other causal relationships into his analysis. Through this, he evolved from a purely theoretical economist to a multidisciplinary scholar.
