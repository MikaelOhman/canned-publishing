Carl Adolph Agardh, born January 23, 1785, in Båstad, and died January 28, 1859, in Karlstad, was a Swedish theologian, mathematician, natural scientist, and political economist. He was the father of Jacob Georg Agardh and Emilia Fröding, the uncle of Mortimer Agardh, and the grandfather of Gustaf Fröding.

== Biography ==

Agardh was born in Båstad, where his father, who came from Schleswig, engaged in trade. In 1799, he became a student and enrolled at Lund University on October 4th – the same day as Esaias Tegnér – and earned a Master of Philosophy degree in 1805. In 1807, he was appointed docent (lecturer) in mathematics, in 1810 as an adjunct professor and demonstrator in botany and economics, and in 1812 as a professor in these subjects, after having been nominated for the professorship in mathematics the previous year. In 1816, he was ordained as a priest and became the vicar of Sankt Peters Klosters parish that same year. He was elected in 1817 as member number 372 of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. From 1831, he held the fourth seat at the Swedish Academy. In 1835, he became bishop of the Karlstad diocese and, in 1837, also a knight-bishop.

Agardh's first work, *Caricographia scanensis* (1806), belongs to the field of botany, the science that he would advance through insightful observations and dedicated research. Subsequently, he published a treatise on a mathematical subject. The university chancellor, Count Lars von Engeström, invited Agardh to become a tutor for one of his sons, which gave Agardh the opportunity to visit Stockholm and get to know Olof Swartz, who was then considered the foremost Swedish botanist and who provided a crucial direction for his studies. At that time, Anders Jahan Retzius was professor of natural history in Lund, and Carl Peter Thunberg was professor in Uppsala. Both were staunch followers of Carl von Linné's school, and their main goal in botany was to incorporate newly discovered flowering plants into the sexual system and to carefully describe their varying forms. In particular, the large group of cryptogams remained in the same disordered state that Linné had left it.

The first impetus for a new direction came from Swartz and Erik Acharius, who chose lichens as their subject of study. Of the two remaining groups of cryptogams, Elias Fries studied fungi, while Agardh focused on algae, and he would primarily gain fame as an algologist and reformer. His work *Dispositio algarum Sueciæ* (1810–1812) still adhered to previous views. In *Species plantarum*, Linné had in 1753 grouped these numerous organisms of the seas and freshwater under the genera *Fucus, Ulva,* and *Conferva*, of which Swedish botanists had identified only 65 species. However, at that time, several works had been published abroad that significantly maintained the old Linnaean classification but greatly increased the number of known algal species, thereby highlighting the importance of a more thorough understanding and a truer perception of these plant organisms.

With the support of this preliminary work, Agardh, after becoming Retzius's successor in 1812, undertook the transformation of algology that made him famous. He published *Algarum decas* in 1812–1815 and *Synopsis algarum Scandinaviæ* in 1817, which was well-received by scholars in other countries. Agardh then decided to describe all previously known genera and species within the entire group of algae, and in 1820–1822, the first volume of *Species algarum* was published, containing all known species of *Fucaceæ, Florideæ,* and *Ulvaceæ*. However, the publication of this work was interrupted, and instead, *Systema algarum* was published in 1824, which includes only the characteristics of genera and species of known algae. "At once," says J.E. Areschoug, "the Agardhian reform was fully accomplished; orders and genera were established, and the species, both those long known and many new ones, were diagnosed." In 1827, Agardh made a trip to Venice and Trieste, primarily for algological purposes, during which he discovered a large number of genera and species. This was followed by the works *Icones algarum europæarum* (Leipzig 1828–1835) and *Species algarum*, vol. II, sectio prior, thus completing his algological works.

However, algology was not the only field in botany where Agardh worked and introduced new ideas. In 1817–1826, Agardh published *Aphorismi botanici* and, in 1825, *Classes plantarum*, in which he attempted to organize plant forms. A contemporary foreign scholar said that "Agardh deserves the credit of being the first who succeeded to a greater extent in establishing natural orders."

Finally, Agardh also addressed the anatomy and physiology of plants, which had been founded by English and Dutch researchers in the 17th century but had been neglected during Linné's period of form and order. However, interest in these branches of science had been revived at that time, and Agardh published numerous writings on these subjects: *Dissertatio de metamorphosi Algarum* (1820), two treatises in French (1829), and his textbook on botany, which was also translated into German. These works presented several new ideas.
