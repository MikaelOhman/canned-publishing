The European Central Bank (ECB) will incorporate portfolios of non-financial credit claims into its framework for eligible collateral and is phasing out temporary measures.

TL;DRThe European Central Bank is making it easier for banks to borrow money by simplifying the rules regarding which types of assets can be used as collateral.
In plain language

The European Central Bank (ECB), the highest banking authority in the Eurozone, has decided on new rules for how banks can use loans as collateral. Previously, there were temporary regulations that made the system complex, but these are now being incorporated into permanent rules. This ensures that all banks across Europe have the same clear rules and conditions.

These rules apply to loans that banks have provided to non-financial companies, i.e., ordinary businesses. Instead of each individual loan being assessed separately, groups of these loans can now be used together as collateral to obtain funds from the central bank. This makes it easier for banks to access liquidity, which is the money needed to operate the bank's business.

Certain types of lower-quality loans can now be included in these groups, provided they are sufficiently diversified. This is done to reduce risks while also making things more convenient for the banks. The old, temporary regulations will be phased out gradually and replaced by this new system, which is planned to be fully implemented technically by early 2027.

Full text

June 25, 2026

The Governing Council of the European Central Bank (ECB) has today decided on the criteria that must be met and the framework for risk control to permanently integrate portfolios of credit claims from non-financial companies into its general collateral framework. This follows the Council's initial communication in November 2024 regarding the gradual phasing out of temporary measures that allowed banks to use a wider range of credit claims from non-financial companies as collateral, and marks the final step in the phase-out of the Temporary Additional Credit Claims (ACC) framework.

Portfolios of credit claims from non-financial companies consist of the loans that banks grant to non-financial companies in the real economy. Instead of being used individually as collateral, these loans are grouped together by counterparty and presented as a whole to obtain liquidity from the Eurosystem.

The eligibility criteria, as well as the management procedures for the credit claims that make up the portfolios of credit claims from non-financial companies, will primarily be harmonized with the existing criteria for individual credit claims, with the exception of the acceptance of a broader range of credit quality levels. This means that certain loans with a credit quality lower than the requirement for individual credit claims may be included, provided they meet diversification criteria within the portfolio, as specified in the applicable risk control framework. The risk control framework complements the eligibility criteria with valuation haircuts and a concentration limit to ensure that the portfolios do not pose a higher risk than the assets already eligible under the general collateral framework.

Until the technical implementation is completed, existing portfolios of credit claims covered by the National Credit Claim System (NFC) will continue to be accepted as valid collateral within the framework of the temporary regulations. Credit claims covered by a public sector guarantee related to COVID-19 under the framework of the temporary regulations will only be eligible until the end of 2026, unless they meet all the requirements of the current general framework. However, national central banks may decide to terminate their temporary ACC systems earlier. The relevant national central banks will inform the parties involved about the details.

Today's decision marks a return to a single list of eligible collateral applicable throughout the euro area, which reduces complexity and ensures equal conditions for all credit institutions. This means that the temporary framework can be discontinued, while counterparties can still access the Eurosystem's lending operations with a broad range of collaterals under the general framework. The technical implementation is currently planned to begin no earlier than November 2027.
