One of his most important contributions was helping to determine the measurements of light waves. That's why a small unit of measurement for light is today called an ångström. He was also the first to investigate the properties of auroras using scientific methods.

Because he made so many important contributions to his research, he received several awards and honors. Today, both a lunar crater and a laboratory in Uppsala bear his name to honor his work.

Anders Jonas Ångström (pronounced [ˈânːdɛʂ ˈjûːnas ˈɔ̂ŋːstrœm]), born August 13, 1814, at Lögdö ironworks in Medelpad, and died June 21, 1874, in Uppsala, was a Swedish physicist and astronomer. He was one of the founders of spectroscopy. Ångström was a professor of physics at Uppsala University from 1858, and also served as the university's rector during the years 1870–1871.

== Biography == Anders Jonas Ångström was the second son of the pastor Johan Ångström and Anna Katarina Tunberg. His older brother, Johan, was a physician and botanist, while his younger brother, Carl Arendt, worked as a mining mechanic. He married Augusta Bedoire (1820–1906), the daughter of a factory owner, in 1845. He was the father of Augusta and Knut Ångström, and the grandfather of the meteorologist Anders Ångström.

After spending his childhood in Lögdö and later Sättna, where his father served as pastor, he attended Härnösand Gymnasium for his secondary education. In 1833, Ångström went to Uppsala University as a student, where he studied mathematics and physics. He financed his studies by working as a tutor. During his time as a student, he was elected curator of Norrlands nation in 1839, and later became an honorary member in 1844. He also belonged to the students' society "Juvenalerna" during the 1840s.

In 1839, Ångström received the highest grade ("laudatur") in mathematics, physics, and astronomy, and he obtained his master's degree under Atterbom on June 15, 1839. His doctoral dissertation, presented that same year to Fredric Rudberg, was titled "On Conic Refraction." In the autumn of the same year, he was appointed as a docent (lecturer) in physics. In 1843, he was appointed as an astronomer at the observatory, a discipline that then also included the Earth's magnetism and temperature.

In 1843, Ångström traveled through Europe, during which time he met Johann von Lamont in Munich. Even at this time, Ångström was already a recognized researcher. As a teacher, he became famous for introducing laboratory work into his teaching. After his predecessor's death in 1858, he was appointed professor of physics.

Ångström served as chairman of the university's finance committee, secretary of the Royal Society of Sciences in Uppsala, and a member of the city council from 1868 to 1873. He was rector of Uppsala University from 1870 to 1871, and he was a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences from 1850, the Society of Science in Uppsala from 1851, the Geographical Society in Lund from 1866, the Royal Society from 1870, the Danish Academy of Sciences from 1873, and the French Academy of Sciences from 1873.

Anders Jonas Ångström is buried in the old cemetery in Uppsala.

== Scientific Contributions == Classical physics had undergone tremendous development since the 18th century, driven by new inventions, theories, and measuring instruments. Consequently, the role and research areas of professors changed; previously, professors were considered to be nearly all-knowing in all disciplines. However, Ångström, despite his broad scientific knowledge, came to symbolize the new era of specialization.

In 1852, in "Optical Investigations" (published by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences), Ångström formulated an absorption law, which was later slightly modified and became known as Kirchhoff's radiation law. "Optical Investigations" became fundamental to spectral analysis and is one of the first works in spectroscopy. He demonstrated, among other things, that an electric spark produces spectral lines characteristic of both the metal in the electrodes and the surrounding gas, and that the spectrum of a chemical compound contains lines characteristic of its constituent elements. Some of his most important discoveries are only mentioned in footnotes, for example, the significance of the solar spectrum for the spectral lines of metals.

In "New Determination of the Wavelengths of Light" from 1863, he determined the spectral lines of various wavelengths. This work led to the adoption of the Ångström unit internationally at a conference in Oxford in 1905. Ångström's most significant work was done with his successor, Robert Thalén. It was published in 1868 under the title "Recherches sur le Spectre Solaire" and is an investigation of the solar spectral lines, specifically the Fraunhofer lines. Interestingly, during this work, it was discovered that the Swedish standard meter was 0.190 mm shorter than the French meter. As a direct consequence, all of Ångström's wavelength values have an error of 0.013%.

In 1867, Ångström was the first to study the spectrum of auroras, and his work on this topic was groundbreaking. He also studied, among other things, heat theory and the Earth's magnetic field.

== Honors and Awards == The unit of length, the ångström (1 Å = 10-10 meters), is named after him. The Ångström Laboratory in Uppsala is also named in his honor. He was the first Swede to receive the Rumford Medal in 1872, and he received several awards from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. The lunar crater Ångström bears his name, as does the asteroid 42487 Ångström.
