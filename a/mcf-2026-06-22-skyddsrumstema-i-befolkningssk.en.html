In plain language

The Swedish Civil Defence Agency, responsible for the country's preparedness, is working on new regulations for how we should protect people during crises. They aim to create a system that functions better in today's context by combining traditional shelters with so-called "protected areas." These are locations adapted to modern needs.

There is broad agreement that clearer rules are needed throughout the country. New regulations, meaning official rules from the agency, will define exactly what these protected areas are and where they must be located. The goal is to make the protection easier to use and more effective for all citizens.

The work is currently underway intensively to make the national defense system more flexible and coordinated. By having clear rules for both shelters and other types of protected locations, the aim is to ensure that society can function as well as possible even if something happens.

The new law regarding shelters and protected spaces was presented, introducing a new type of protective facility and defining it, while also clarifying the requirements for inspection and maintenance of shelters. Simultaneously, extensive work is underway to develop approximately ten new regulations in this area.

On June 16th, representatives from preparedness agencies, civil defense organizations, county administrative boards, regions, municipalities, SKR (a Swedish association), SOS Alarm, and civil society gathered in Stockholm for a meeting of the Civil Defense Council. The focus was on shelters, protected spaces, and how to develop population protection based on today's evolving threat landscape.

A central question was how future protection should be designed. The council emphasized that protected spaces could play a larger role in the future:

– Protected spaces can often be a better alternative. Shelters were built based on yesterday's assessment of the situation – now we have the opportunity to adapt and make protection more accessible to meet today's needs," stated Henrik Larsson from the Swedish Civil Defence Agency.

Henrik Larsson, Swedish Civil Defence Agency

After a visit to a specialized shelter near Hötorget, the council discussed how shelters and protected spaces need to function together in practice:

– It is crucial that we find the right balance between shelters and protected spaces, while also ensuring the functionality of society – especially when it comes to transportation," said Pelle Rytterlund from SL Traffic in Stockholm.
