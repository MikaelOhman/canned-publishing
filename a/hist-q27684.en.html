Peter Forsskål, born January 11, 1732, in Helsingfors, Nyland, and died July 11, 1763, in Jerim, Yemen, was a Swedish naturalist, orientalist, and philosopher, and one of the leading figures of early Swedish liberalism. He is sometimes published under the name Per Forskål.

Peter Forsskål was the son of Johannes Forsskål, who was then the pastor in Helsingfors. In 1740, his father became the pastor in Tegelsmora parish, and the family moved to Uppland. As early as 1742, Forsskål enrolled at Uppsala University, but later continued his studies with his father, who had become a pastor in Stockholm in 1748. In 1751, he received the Guthermuth scholarship and returned to Uppsala for further studies, where Carl von Linné was one of his teachers. He then studied at the University of Göttingen from 1753. Officially, he studied theology, but philosophy and oriental philology were also among the subjects he studied. In 1756, he obtained a master's degree in philosophy there, and upon returning to Sweden, he became a docent in economics at Uppsala University.

At that time, the University of Göttingen was one of Germany's most prominent institutions, but it was also known for its liberal intellectual climate, which was politically possible because it belonged to the kingdom of Hanover, allied with England.

His studies were primarily under the guidance of the philosopher and orientalist Samuel Christian Hollmann, and the theologian and orientalist Johann David Michaelis. He was also inspired by John Locke and Pierre Bayle, and he was a strong critic of Christian von Wolff. Without a doubt, he was the first true Enlightenment philosopher in Sweden.

== A Liberal Pioneer ==

During his time at Uppsala University, Forsskål wrote a political treatise, "Thoughts on Civil Liberties" (1759), which contained an early liberal program advocating for both political and economic freedom, and most importantly, freedom of the press. The book begins with the statement: "Next to life, nothing is more precious to people than liberty." He also writes that the reverence afforded to monarchs has allowed tyrannical kings to play with the lives of their subjects. Referring to England, Forsskål wrote: "Freedom must therefore be met with freedom. A wise government would rather allow its citizens the opportunity to express their dissatisfaction through writing than through weapons, which on one hand promotes enlightenment and on the other, calms and prevents unrest."

He thus expressed a thought that John F. Kennedy articulated 200 years later: "Those who make peaceful revolution impossible will make violent revolution inevitable." From a speech at the White House in 1962.

Forsskål, along with Anders Chydenius, became one of the foremost pioneers of political liberalism in Sweden and Finland. Although censorship was still not abolished, Forsskål managed to obtain approval from censor Niklas von Oelreich. However, after the publication, the council (i.e., the government), which at that time was dominated by the "Hattarna" party with Anders Johan von Höpken as chancellor, intervened. The treatise was confiscated, and Carl von Linné, who was then rector (and Forsskål's teacher), was ordered to collect all copies distributed among students in Uppsala.

Forsskål's pamphlet inspired the new law on freedom of the press that was adopted in Sweden in 1766, the first documented freedom of the press law in the world.

Among those who were inspired by Forsskål was the German philosopher Immanuel Kant. An example of this is the shift that occurred in Kant's philosophical stance between 1756 and 1762, which marked his transition from being a defender of Christian von Wolff to becoming a critic in the spirit of Forsskål. This involved a move away from Wolff's dogmatism towards a more empirical perspective. Inspired by Forsskål, he published a work in 1762 titled: "Die falsche Spitzfindigkeit der vier syllogistischen Figuren," in which Kant heavily relied on Forsskål and further developed his logical ideas.

== Research Trip to Yemen == Forsskål was one of Linnaeus' most important students. In Uppsala, he acquired knowledge in botany that led to his recruitment into the Danish research expedition to the southwestern part of the Arabian Peninsula, which began in January 1761. In Yemen, Forsskål contracted malaria and died on July 11, 1763. The only survivor of the expedition's five participants was Carsten Niebuhr, who returned to Denmark in 1767.

Niebuhr later wrote about Forsskål, among other things: "Forsskål was the most learned man in the entire group, and had he returned alive, he might have been the most learned in all of Europe. He was hardworking, he disregarded all dangers, inconveniences, and sacrifices. His flaws were his eagerness to debate, his self-assuredness, and his impulsiveness."

Forsskål's unique collection of research materials was brought to Copenhagen, and the first documentation from the expedition was published in the 1770s. One of the plants that Forsskål sent back home was given the name *Forsskaolea tenacissima* by Linnaeus, with the additional designations *tenacissima, hispida, adhaerens, uncinata* (hard-headed, wild, stubborn, angular) in memory of Forsskål.
