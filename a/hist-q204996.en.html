Birgitta Birgersdotter, also known as Saint Bridget, was born around 1303 in Uppland and died on July 23, 1373, in Rome within the Papal States. She was a Swedish Catholic writer, theologian, mystic, and the founder of the Bridgettine Order, both for women and men. She was canonized as a saint and, together with Benedict of Nursia, Edith Stein, Catherine of Siena, Cyril, and Methodius, is one of Europe's six patron saints according to the Roman Catholic Church.

Birgitta belonged to the Finsta family and was the daughter of Birger Persson of Finsta, the provincial lawman of Uppland, and Ingeborg Bengtsdotter from the Bjälbo family. She married Ulf Gudmarsson at a young age, with whom she had eight children, including Catherine of Vadstena. After her husband's death, she lived an ascetic life near Alvastra monastery before traveling to Rome around 1349 to advocate for her order and influence the religious and secular leaders of the time through her revelations.

Saint Bridget's most important work is her "Heavenly Revelations" (Revelationes celestes), which gained widespread popularity both during her lifetime and later.

== Biography ==

=== Background and Upbringing === Saint Bridget was born around 1303 as Birgitta Birgersdotter. She belonged to one of the prominent Swedish families of the medieval period, being the daughter of Birger Petersson of Finsta, the district judge (lagman) of Uppland. It is not possible to definitively prove where she was born and raised. Regardless of the arguments for various possibilities regarding her father's ownership of farms, the key question is actually where Birgitta's mother, Ingeborg, lived with her children while her father, the district judge, was away on official duties.

Through both her parents and her husband, Ulf Gudmarsson, she had connections to the political leadership in medieval Sweden. Because her maternal grandfather was a cousin of King Magnus Ladulås, Birgitta was related to the Swedish royal family through her mother. Outside of Sweden, she was also known as the Princess of Närke.

The oldest tradition about Bridget's birth dates back to the 14th century and a chronicle based on materials from the Vadstena monastery, written by Margareta Clausdotter, who was abbess there from 1472 to 1486. It states the following about Birgitta's mother, Ingeborg: "And it must be known that she and her worthy husband lived in Uppland on a farm called Finsta, not far from Uppsala. And there she gave birth to her holy daughter, Saint Bridget, and several of her children, but now they rest together in the Uppsala cathedral in the choir of Saint Catherine."

While there is no definitive evidence in the preserved documents from Swedish medieval times in the Swedish Diplomatarium that Finsta was Birgitta's birthplace, the basis for the hypothesis supported by many historians today – that Birgitta was born and raised on the Sköldnora royal estate in the parish of Fresta in the district of Vallentuna – is also weak. Regarding Birger's ownership of farms in Uppland, the inheritance after him included, among many others, both Finsta and Sköldnora, as well as numerous farms in Södermanland, Östergötland, and Småland. When examining several purchase and exchange documents in the diplomatarium, it appears that the dates of acquisition may be questionable. The Latin terminology used for the properties being sold, exchanged, and bequeathed is also unclear; these are disputed terms, and in each individual case, it is very uncertain which parts of the properties are meant. Furthermore, the materials contain no reference to where the family's main estate was located. Regarding a second argument that would point to Fresta in the district of Vallentuna instead of Finsta in the parish of Skederids as her birthplace, namely the phrase about her origin "in regno Swecie de civitate Frastad" in the papal canonization documents, it is highly uncertain, as noted by the Swedish publisher of the text, whether the Italian writers understood the name correctly; their misunderstandings may also be behind the place being called "civitas," "city," and not "parochia," "parish." However, in wills where Birgitta's grandchildren bequeath the Skällnora (older name for Sköldnora) farm, the farm is referred to as "the true ancestral home."

=== Life in Sweden ===

Even as a child, Birgitta experienced visions. Once, she saw the Virgin Mary placing a crown on her head. Another time, she foresaw Christ suffering and dying on the cross. Both of these themes – deep devotion to Mary and meditation on Christ's suffering – profoundly shaped Birgitta's entire life. When Birgitta was twelve years old, her mother died. Her father felt he could not provide her with the upbringing befitting a girl of her status, so he sent her to his sister-in-law, Katarina Bengtsdotter, at Aspenäs near Lake Sommen in Östergötland.
