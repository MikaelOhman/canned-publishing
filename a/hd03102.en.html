Exchange of information in the supplementary tax report and supplementary procedures for the supplementary tax assessment for companies in large groups.

TL;DRThe government proposes new regulations requiring large companies and international corporations to share tax information with each other. The purpose is to make it easier to verify that large companies are paying the correct amount of tax in different countries.
In plain language

The government intends to amend several laws to comply with new EU regulations known as DAC 9. These regulations require countries to automatically exchange information with each other regarding the amount of tax paid by large companies. This is intended to ensure that large companies do not avoid taxes by shifting profits between countries.

The proposal also includes new methods for working with tax information, based on international agreements from the OECD. This involves authorities being able to exchange information on so-called GloBE information, which is part of a global effort to create a fair minimum tax for large companies.

The proposal also includes changes to the tax process for large corporations. For example, a temporary relief is proposed for companies that receive fines or other penalties, known as sanctions, to facilitate the transition to the new regulations.

Most of the new regulations are proposed to come into effect on May 1, 2026. Certain aspects related to confidentiality and how information is handled will instead come into effect on August 1, 2026. The Parliament must now decide on all of these legislative changes.

Full text

The proposal includes draft provisions for the implementation of Council Directive (EU) 2025/872 of April 14, 2025, amending Directive 2011/16/EU on administrative cooperation in the area of taxation (referred to as DAC 9). This directive is closely linked to the directive on ensuring a global minimum tax level for multinational companies and large national groups in the Union (EU) 2022/2523 (the minimum taxation directive). The minimum taxation directive was implemented into Swedish law through the Act (2023:875) on top-up tax and supplementary provisions in the Fiscal Procedure Act (2011:1244).

Furthermore, the proposal includes draft amendments that are a consequence of the multilateral agreement between competent authorities on the automatic exchange of GloBE information, adopted by the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS), referred to as the Inclusive Framework (IF).

The proposal also includes several amendments aimed at improving and supplementing the tax procedure regarding the top-up tax for companies in large groups, as well as amendments that are a consequence of new administrative guidelines and changes to the GloBE Information Return adopted by the IF.
