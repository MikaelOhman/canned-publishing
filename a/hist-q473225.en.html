Georg Brandt, born June 26, 1694, in Riddarhyttan, Västmanland, and died April 29, 1768, in Stockholm, was a Swedish chemist best known for discovering the element cobalt.

== Biography == Georg Brandt's father, Jürgen Brandt, was a successful pharmacist who came from Femern (the island of Fehmarn) in northern Germany. His mother, Katarina Ysing, belonged to a family that had immigrated from Bremen and owned several industrial plants in central Sweden. She was his wife's third spouse, and there was a significant age difference between them. Katarina Ysing's grandmother was the sister of Börje Olofsson Cronberg, and she belonged to the Bure clan. The father had previously managed the "Örnen" (Raven) pharmacy in Stockholm but sold it and, in 1690, acquired Heds ironworks in Riddarhyttan along with his nephew, Joakim Brandt. There, the father began experimenting with metallurgy and chemistry, and Georg became involved early on. However, it was his younger brother, Carl, who took over the business in Riddarhyttan.

In 1705, Brandt began studying at Uppsala University, but activities there were limited after the fire of 1702. Brandt also received private tutoring in mathematics from A. G. Duhre. From 1714 to 1716, Brandt was a clerk in the legal department of the Bergskollegium (Mining Bureau), but laboratory work had ceased before Brandt was hired. He therefore embarked on a study trip abroad, studying chemistry and medicine for several years in Leiden, and in 1726 he became a Doctor of Medicine in Reims.

Upon his return, he was appointed head of the Bergskollegium's Laboratorium chemicum (Chemical Laboratory) and became a mountain councilor in 1757. He belonged to the school of chemistry founded by Urban Hjärne. Brandt's first significant works are the investigations "De arsenico" and "De semimetallis" (published in "Acta literaria et scientiarum Sueciæ" in 1733 and 1735), through which he concluded that arsenic is a true semi-metal, whose "lime" consists of white arsenic (arsenic acid). He also studied zinc and showed that "galmeja" and "blende" are ores of this metal, and "galitzenstein" is its sulfate. He also conducted investigations into the causes of cold brittleness and red brittleness in iron and demonstrated (in 1756) that sea salt contains the same alkali as soda, but that nitrate, on the other hand, contains a vegetable salt. This last investigation refuted the criticism that had been directed at the Frenchman Henri-Louis Duhamel du Monceau's treatise "Sur la base du sel marin."

Brandt became most famous for his discovery of cobalt. It was long known that there were certain types of rock that were considered silver ores, but which did not yield any metal when melted, but instead produced a poisonous smoke. As early as the mid-15th century, it was discovered that these ores could give glass a beautiful blue color, but detailed knowledge of "coloring cobalt" was lacking until Brandt, in the aforementioned treatise on semi-metals in 1735, showed that the coloring substance contained a metal, which he called cobalt. In the previously known cobalt minerals, this metal was combined with arsenic, but in 1742, Brandt examined an arsenic-free cobalt mineral, which later became known as "linnéite."

He was one of the first to be elected to the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences after its founding. The scientific investigations that Brandt initiated there were continued after his death by Torbern Bergman. Other prominent students of Brandt include Daniel Tilas, Anton von Swab, Axel Fredrik Cronstedt, and Henrik Teofilus Scheffer.

Georg Brandt married Anna Maria Norn in 1714, the daughter of the merchant in Säter, Petter Norn. Their daughter, Catharina Elisabeth, married Samuel Sandel and was the mother of Benjamin and Johan August Sandels.
