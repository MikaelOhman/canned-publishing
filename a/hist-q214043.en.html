John Albert Bauer, born June 4, 1882 in Jönköping, Småland, and died on November 20, 1918 during a shipwreck on Lake Vättern, was a Swedish painter and illustrator. He is known for his illustrations in the early editions of "Among Gnomes and Trolls."

John Bauer was born and raised in Jönköping. At the age of sixteen, he moved to Stockholm to study art and began working as an illustrator. While at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts, he met Esther Ellqvist, who was also a student there, and they eventually married in 1906 in Stockholm.

Early in his career, Bauer made several trips that profoundly influenced his art. He traveled to Lapland, Germany, and Italy. Most of Bauer's work consists of watercolors, but he also painted in oil and created frescoes. His illustrations have had a significant impact on Swedish folklore and storytelling.

At the age of 36, John died along with his wife, Esther Ellqvist-Bauer, and their son, Bengt, when the steamship Per Brahe sank in Lake Vättern near Hästholmen.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years and Education === John Bauer's mother, Emma Charlotta Wadell (1850–1923), was the daughter of a miller from Habo. In 1873, she married Joseph Bauer (1849–1921), who came from Germany. The couple had four children: Hjalmar, Anna, John, and Ernst ("Enne"). John Bauer grew up in Jönköping, near the Östra torget square, where his father ran a successful butcher shop. The family's summer house, Villa Sjövik (now John Bauer's Park), was located by Rocksjön lake, a place Bauer returned to throughout his life. At the age of 16, Bauer moved to Stockholm to study art. He attended the Technical School (Konstfack) and Althin's painting school. Two years later, he was accepted as a student at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts. Together with his wife, Ester, he undertook study trips to Germany and Italy, unlike most of his Swedish contemporaries who traveled to Paris. In 1907, he began illustrating the collection of fairy tales "Bland tomtar och troll," and soon became recognized as Sweden's foremost illustrator of fairy tales. He also created murals, including the fresco "The Saint Martin and the Beggar" in the Odd Fellows hall in Nyköping. His works are represented at Jönköping County Museum, Röhsska Museum, Nationalmuseum, Gothenburg Art Museum, Norrköping Art Museum, Nordic Museum, the Association Archive of Southwestern Götaland, Jamtli, the Printing Museum, and Malmö Museums.

=== The Trip to Lapland === On July 15, 1905, John Bauer traveled to Lapland. His assignment was to illustrate the comprehensive book about Lapland that publisher Carl Adam Victor Lundholm had decided to create. Bauer was the youngest of the illustrators involved in the project. He spent a month in Abisko, trying to connect with the Sami people and working on pencil drawings and watercolors. He dedicated much time to sketching details from tools, clothing, and objects. These details reappear in his fairytale illustrations. During the trip, he took many photographs, which he later used as models for his illustrations.

The trip to Lapland left a lasting impression on Bauer, and it constantly reappeared in his art in the form of folkloric elements. Reindeer and wolf skins are featured in the trolls' clothing, as well as details in the hoods, shoes, and wide belts with curved knives that the trolls wear.

=== Courtship and Marriage === John Bauer met Ester Ellqvist at the Academy of Fine Arts, where they both began their studies in the autumn term of 1900. Their romantic relationship seems to have begun in early 1902. The relationship was characterized by strong passion, encompassing deep love but also much jealousy and ambivalence. On November 18, 1906, John and Ester Ellqvist-Bauer were announced for marriage, and they married on December 18 of the same year.

During their move from Bunn in Småland, John Bauer, aged 36, his wife Ester Ellqvist, and their three-year-old son, Bengt, nicknamed Putte, tragically drowned when the steamship Per Brahe sank during an autumn storm on Lake Vättern near Hästholmen in 1918. Four years later, the Per Brahe was salvaged, and the Bauer family was buried at the Östra cemetery in Jönköping.

== Legacy == In connection with Bauer's centenary in 1982, the postal service issued a series of four stamps and, in 1997, a series of three stamps featuring images from "Among Elves and Trolls." In the National Portrait Collection at Gripsholm Castle, there is a bronze bust depicting Bauer, created by the artist Karl Hultström. The Jönköping County Museum has the largest collection related to John and Ester Bauer. It includes sketches, artworks, letters, and photographs. In 2018, the new permanent exhibition "Following John" opened, allowing younger visitors to learn about the lives of John, Ester, and Putte.
