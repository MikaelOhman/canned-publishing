In plain language

The ECB, which stands for the European Central Bank, is responsible for managing the currency in countries that use the euro. Over the past 15 years, the bank has been forced to employ special and unusual methods to deal with major crises, such as the pandemic and the energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Now, the President, Christine Lagarde, believes that these unusual methods are no longer as necessary.

The reason for this is that the economic rules in Europe have become stronger. Banks are better regulated, and countries have improved systems for managing crises collectively. This means that the bank can focus on its primary mission, which is to maintain price stability by gradually adjusting interest rates, rather than resorting to large, unusual interventions.

Even though the world remains uncertain with conflicts and problems related to energy supply, Lagarde believes that the tools available today make the economy more resilient. This means that the central bank can use its basic methods to address future challenges without needing to rely on such complex solutions as in the past.

Full text

Sintra, June 29, 2026

It is a pleasure to welcome you to the ECB's forum on central bank policy.

Over the past 15 years, the Eurozone has faced an environment characterized by extraordinary pressures that required unconventional solutions.

The sovereign debt crisis brought us close to the effective lower bound for interest rates. The pandemic caused further declines in demand. We responded with asset purchases and refinancing operations to support the economy, as well as with new instruments to manage fragmentation.

Our toolkit has been expanded in other ways as well: forward guidance with criteria for interest rate increases, commitments regarding sequencing, and linkages between different policy instruments.

When the situation turned and Russia cut off our access to natural gas, we responded with a conventional instrument used in an unconventional way: the fastest tightening cycle in our history, where we raised interest rates in steps that we had never used before.

But today, monetary policy is in a different position.

We no longer need to resort to unconventional instruments. Although we have them available, we can now focus on stabilizing inflation using the key interest rate as our primary tool.

We no longer need to act with the same intensity. We can make measured adjustments to interest rates, calibrated according to the shocks we face.

And we no longer need to use complex forms of forward guidance. Our decisions are data-driven and taken meeting by meeting.

Monetary policy has returned to basics. [1] Is that so?

A return to basics does not mean a return to an idealized past, if such a thing even exists in monetary policy.

As Edmund Burke once wrote: "A society without the possibility of change is one that lacks the means of its own conservation." [2]

The same applies to monetary policy. Today's world presents challenges that require us to apply these fundamental principles in new ways and to innovate where our existing frameworks are insufficient.

Several factors have made this return to basics possible.

One is how the world has changed.

Interest rates have moved away from the effective lower bound, partly due to structural factors including increased defense spending. And shocks during this time are more likely to affect the supply side than the demand side. Because these negative supply shocks tend to drive up prices, interest rates have moved away from the effective lower bound. [3]

However, this change also reflects, perhaps more fundamentally, the policy framework that Europe has built in response to these crises. By making the economy more resilient to shocks, this framework has reduced the need for unconventional or forceful policy measures.

The ECB's own tools have reduced the risk of fragmentation, including the Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI). These tools make it less likely that we will see unwarranted changes in the interest rate spreads on government bonds.

They also mean that we can raise interest rates to combat inflation without worrying that the tightening itself will become a source of economic stress.

In addition, the strengthening of the European institutional structure has reduced fragmentation by addressing the relationship between banks and states from both sides.

European banking supervision and the framework for bank resolution have made the banking sector more resilient, so that shocks are less likely to be amplified by the banking system – although we must remain aware that challenges to financial stability may arise in non-bank related financing, where supervision has not yet caught up.

European budgetary frameworks have also evolved significantly, from the European Stability Mechanism (ESM) to Next Generation EU and other common borrowing instruments, which reduces the reliance on national governments.

At the same time, there are reasons to believe that European policy measures are also mitigating the extent to which shocks impact the real economy.

Our strategic analysis showed that inflation expectations have become more anchored around 2%, reflecting the ECB's clear and symmetric objectives, as well as its experience in managing both too-low and too-high inflation. [4] This is crucial for protecting the economy from undesirable, indirect effects of energy shocks.

The transition to renewable energy is also beginning to change how these shocks affect the broader economy. In countries with a higher share of electricity generation from low-carbon sources, such as Portugal and Spain, wholesale electricity prices are increasingly becoming independent of natural gas prices.

This resilience has been clearly evident in recent years.

The failure of Silicon Valley Bank did not destabilize banks within the Eurozone. The Eurozone has also weathered the largest tariff increase from the United States in almost a century, as well as what the International Energy Agency (IEA) describes as the largest oil supply disruption in history.

The costs have been significant, but the economy has not suffered a major setback. Cyclical fluctuations have remained within reasonable bounds, although some of these shocks pose challenges to Europe's long-term growth prospects.

This resilience has allowed monetary policy to focus more directly on its core mission: stabilizing prices through interest rates, without being constrained by fragmentation risks, vulnerabilities in the banking sector, or the effective lower bound on interest rates.

The world in which we apply these fundamental principles is fundamentally different from what it was before.

We are in a tense geopolitical environment where the frequency of major shocks seems to be increasing. And these shocks are also taking on new forms: market access, energy supply, and critical minerals are increasingly being used as weapons.

These types of shocks have two characteristics that challenge our usual analytical models.

First, the economic effects of these shocks depend on strategic reactions that may not follow historical patterns. The introduction of higher US tariffs last year is a clear example.

Most economic models predicted that the euro would weaken against the US dollar, due to expectations of higher interest rates in the US and a smaller US trade deficit. Furthermore, uncertainty was expected to have a significant negative impact on investment. Many observers believed that the EU would react by increasing tariffs on imported goods.
