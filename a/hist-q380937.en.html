Erik Gustaf Geijer, born January 12, 1783, at Ransäter ironworks in Värmland, and died April 23, 1847, in Stockholm, was a Swedish author, Freemason, poet, philosopher, historian, and composer. He was one of the most important aestheticians and thinkers during the Swedish Romantic period. He is considered one of the fathers of Swedish nationalism, and after 1838, he also became an influential advocate for liberalism.

Geijer was a professor of history at Uppsala University from 1817 and in 1824, he became a member of the Swedish Academy. He also served as rector of Uppsala University during the years 1822, 1830, 1836, and 1843–1844. As a representative of the university, he was a member of the clergy in the national assemblies from 1828 to 1830 and from 1840 to 1841. He was also a member of the Götiska förbundet, where he served as editor of its journal *Iduna*, and a member of the Svea Lodge.

Regarding his areas of interest, Geijer himself wrote: "I have engaged with five things – eagerly, if not successfully – philosophy, history, rhetoric, poetry, and music. These are the five fingers on my hand, which I have diligently practiced, and from none of which I wish to relinquish."

Geijer contributed to bringing conservatism into the Swedish political discourse. He also played a role in abolishing slavery in the then-Swedish colony of Saint-Barthélemy, influenced Nathan Söderblom and Einar Billing with his theology, and was a pioneer with his "I-you philosophy." The *Nordisk familjebok* states that it was Geijer who made it possible for liberalism to enter Sweden without the revolutionary upheavals that occurred in France, and that he created a relatively Swedish form of liberalism. Henrik Schück argued that Geijer transformed the study of history in Sweden into a science.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood ===

Erik Gustaf Geijer was born at the estate known as "Geijersgården," located at Ransäters ironworks in Värmland, to Bengt Gustaf Geijer, owner of the ironworks and a member of the Geijer family (great-grandson of the businessman with the same name), and Ulrika Magdalena Geisler. Both his father and mother belonged to old families of miners of German/Austrian origin. Ransäter had been passed down through the Geijer family since Erik Gustaf's grandfather, Jan Gustaf Geijer, purchased the estate in 1759 from his son-in-law. Erik Gustaf was the eldest son in the family, and he himself described his upbringing as very happy.

He received his initial education at home and at Karlstad Gymnasium; a liberal and culturally rich environment, with a deeply religious father. He soon became known for being an exceptionally gifted individual. Through hikes in forests and fields, ice skating trips, and swimming feats, Erik Gustaf developed into a strong and resilient young man. He remained an athlete throughout his life, which instilled in him "a deep-rooted habit of feeling well."

He enrolled at Uppsala University on October 8, 1799, and was taught by professors such as Jacob Fredrik Neikter, Benjamin Höijer, and Daniel Boëthius. However, in his memoirs, he revealed that he hesitated about what career path he wanted to pursue. In 1802, he still had not obtained a degree, much to the disappointment of his family, due to both divided interests and a time-consuming social life. During the summer of 1803, something happened that significantly damaged the reputation of the 20-year-old among his relatives. A friend of the Geijer family had recommended him as a tutor in a prestigious household. However, the father's friend received a letter from the prominent man stating that investigations at the university had revealed that Erik Gustaf Geijer was considered "a young man lacking stability." This harsh judgment likely referred to the fact that he was one of Höijer's most interested students and was therefore suspected of having "revolutionary ideas." "It was," Geijer wrote in his memoirs, "my first experience of what names and reputation could mean. I felt exposed to the entire world. My whole being rebelled against this undeserved notoriety by striving to acquire a better one. So, I took up the pen..."

He published his first work that same year, "Äreminne öfver riksföreståndaren Sten Sture den äldre" (A Memorial to the Regent Sten Sture the Elder), for which he was awarded the Swedish Academy's grand prize. His father had recalled him from the university, but now he was allowed to continue his studies. He finally obtained his first degree (Bachelor of Philosophy) in February 1806, defended his master's thesis in history on June 10th of the same year before Eric Michael Fant with "De ingenio medii ævi," and became a Master on June 12th. Subsequently, he wrote "De stilo historico apud romanos" (On the Historical Style Among the Romans), but was unable to use this work to secure a position as a docent, which he had intended.
