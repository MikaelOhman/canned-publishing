The government has proposed a change to the law regarding who can marry in Sweden. They want to prohibit marriages between cousins and other close relatives. The purpose of this is to protect people, especially women and girls, from coercion and oppression that can occur in so-called honor-related abuse.

The new proposal also means that marriages between cousins that have taken place in other countries will not be recognized as valid in Sweden. The government also wants to prohibit marriages between people who are related in a direct line through siblings. They also want to prevent half-siblings or adopted siblings from being allowed to marry each other.

The reason for the proposal is to counter the isolation of people within their families and to break up criminal networks that are based on family ties. If the proposal is approved and the parliament votes in favor, the new rules will come into effect on July 1, 2026.

Full text

The government's proposal suggests a ban on marriages between first cousins and other close relatives. The purpose is to counter honor-related oppression, coercion, and other pressures related to marriage.

Marriages between first cousins carry a particular risk of honor-related oppression and other forms of lack of freedom, especially for girls and women. Customs associated with such marriages risk contributing to social isolation, as well as maintaining honor structures and family-based criminal networks. The government therefore proposes an absolute ban on marriages between first cousins in Sweden, and that foreign marriages between first cousins should generally not be recognized in Sweden.

The government also proposes a corresponding ban on marriages where one person is a descendant of the other's sibling.

Finally, the government proposes that it should no longer be possible for half-siblings and siblings by adoption to obtain permission to marry.

The proposed changes to the law are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2026.
