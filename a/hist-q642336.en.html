Johan Peter Falck, born November 26, 1732, in Broddetorp, Västergötland, and died March 31, 1774, in Kazan, Russia, was a Swedish botanist, professor, physician, and explorer, and a student of Carl von Linné.

== Name == In the baptismal record for Broddetorp in 1732, Johan Peter Falck is listed as Jean Petter. During his time in Russia, he called himself Ivan Petrovitj (Johan Petersson). In his dissertation from 1762, he used the name Johannes Petrus Falck.

== Biography == Johan Peter Falck was the son of auditor (military judge) Peter Falck and his wife, Beata Winge. He was born at the auditor's residence, Kockstorp, in the parish of Broddetorp. After initial education at Ulricehamn school, he studied at Skara school and gymnasium from 1744. Upon graduating from the gymnasium in 1751, he delivered a farewell oration in verse about Västergötland. He enrolled at Uppsala University and Västgöta nation in 1751. At Uppsala, he studied under Carl von Linné and served as a tutor for Linné's son. In 1762, he defended a practice dissertation under Linné. In 1763, he became responsible for the natural history cabinet of State Councilor Kruse in St. Petersburg, and in 1765, he became a professor of medicine and botany at Collegium medicum in the same city. From 1768 to 1774, he led a scientific expedition in southeastern Russia and parts of Siberia. Depressed and ill, Falck took his own life during a stay in Kazan, now part of the Russian republic of Tatarstan.

No portrait of Falck has been preserved, but he was described by an unknown contemporary as follows: "He was quite tall, straight, strong, and handsome, with an oval, masculine, and attractive face. He usually had a cheerful expression, looked courageous, honest, and aristocratic, was free from servility, friendly, and lovable. In character, he was very honorable, open, and kind." The unknown observer noted that "poverty and melancholy" undermined Falck's "physical health, spiritual vitality, and cheerful disposition."

In 1786, Carl Peter Thunberg named the plant genus *Falckia* after Falck. Falck never married.

== Research == Falck discovered the rare Feather Grass (Stipa pennata), which only grows wild in Västergötland. Linnaeus published this discovery in 1761. At Linnaeus' request, Falck traveled to Gotland in 1760 on a botanical expedition. Many of the specimens he collected there were lost in a shipwreck during his journey to Russia in 1763. Linnaeus and Peter Forsskål unsuccessfully tried to have Falck join the Danish expedition to the Arabian Peninsula in 1761. During his years in St. Petersburg, Falck cultivated the botanical garden and maintained contact with Linnaeus, providing him with news, plants, and seeds. In 1768, Falck was entrusted with one of the scientific expeditions sent by the Russian Academy of Sciences to explore Siberia, which was still largely unknown at the time. For over five years, he traversed southeastern Russia and parts of Siberia. Falck studied the flora and fauna, as well as the economic and cultural conditions, in these areas. Materials were sent to St. Petersburg but also to Linnaeus in Uppsala. Falck's research notes were compiled and published by Johann Gottlieb Georgi in three volumes, "Beyträge zur Topographischen Kenntnis des Russischen Reichs" (St. Petersburg 1785-1786).

== Sources for Falck's Life == The primary sources for information about Falck's life are his travel diary and Georgi's biography (both in "Beyträge"), Falck's letters from Russia, and the letters of his brother, Anders Falck (see bibliography below). Copies of unpublished letters from Falck to contemporary scientists are preserved at the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in Stockholm.
