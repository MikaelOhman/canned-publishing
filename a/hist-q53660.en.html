Per Albin Hansson, born on October 28, 1885, in the parish of Fosie in Kulladal (later becoming part of Malmö), and died on October 6, 1946, in Stockholm, was a Swedish politician (Social Democrat) and advocate for temperance. He served as a member of parliament (lower house) from 1918 to 1946, as a government minister in 1920, 1921–1923, and 1924–1926, as the leader of the Social Democrats from 1925 to 1946, and as Prime Minister from 1932 to 1936 and 1936 to 1946.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life and Political Career === Per Albin Hansson was born in Kulladal, the son of bricklayer Carl Hansson and domestic servant Kjersti Persdotter. Their home consisted of two small rooms and a small kitchen area. The rent was five krona per month. Today (in 2025), his birthplace is a museum, and the street that runs past the house, south of the Mobilia department store in Malmö, is now named Per Albin Hansson's Way. Per Albin had three brothers: Johan Hansson, the oldest; Sigfrid Hansson, who was one year older; and Nils Edvin Hansson, the youngest. He attended Stattenaskolan and was a good student. After completing four years of elementary school, he worked as an errand boy from the age of twelve, and later as a clerk at the cooperative association Pan. At home, he was called Per, but when he was to be confirmed in St. Pauli Church in Malmö, it turned out that another young man named Per Hansson was also being confirmed. The city minister Olsson wanted to distinguish between the two boys and asked if either of them had an additional name. "I am named Per Albin," said Per Albin Hansson. "Then you will be called Per Albin," replied the priest. From then on, his classmates and acquaintances used the double name.

Hansson joined the temperance organization Verdandi around 1901. Although he was only active in the association for a short period, he remained a teetotaler and, in conflict with Hjalmar Branting, voted to include a total alcohol ban in the party's platform ten years later. In 1902, he joined the Socialist Youth Association in Malmö, and in the spring of 1903, he moved to the newly formed Social Democratic Youth Association. In 1904, Hansson became an employee at Arbetet newspaper.

In 1905, he became the first official of the youth association and editor of the magazine Fram, based in Malmö. He was therefore 20 years old when he got his first job in the labor movement, where he would remain for the rest of his life. In parallel with his employment, he was a member of the youth association's central board from 1903 to 1912 and its chairman from 1908 to 1909. At the next national congress, Per Albin Hansson lost to Zeth Höglund, who became the new chairman of the association. Subsequently, Hansson was employed at the party's newspaper Social-Demokraten in Stockholm in 1910. Despite the loss, he remained on the party board, which he had joined in 1908 as chairman of the youth association, and in 1911, he became a member of the party's executive committee. He was also editor-in-chief of Social-Demokraten from 1917 to 1923.

Hansson played a role in forcing the more revolutionary left wing of the party, led by Zeth Höglund, to leave social democracy in 1917. In 1917, Per Albin Hansson gave a speech on the importance of implementing electoral reform before a large audience in Stockholm. The situation was critical, and Sweden was close to civil war because the sitting government did not want to accept demands for universal suffrage [source needed]. The right-wing leader Lindman responded, "The right to vote is not a right for anyone. The only thing that matters is what is best for the state."

In the 1917 parliamentary election, the then 32-year-old Hansson became a member of parliament, and the Social Democrats entered a government for the first time, led by the liberal Nils Edén. This did not prevent Per Albin from continuing to criticize the government for not being radical enough. He demanded, among other things, that Sweden become a republic. The Social Democrats wanted to implement reforms regarding an eight-hour workday and safer working conditions. Edén could not agree to this, and a government crisis ensued. Per Albin Hansson's actions were largely behind the government crisis, as he refused to negotiate, even though Hjalmar Branting wanted him to.

To the dismay of the right wing and King Gustaf V, Per Albin Hansson, who had established himself in the 1910s as an anti-militarist, was appointed Minister of Defence in Hjalmar Brantings first government, the first entirely Social Democratic government, in 1920. He then left his position as editor at the newspaper Social-Demokraten. During Branting's three terms in office between 1920 and 1926, he also implemented disarmament to free up state resources for social reforms. During the 1920s, Per Albin Hansson was also involved in an assault case. When Branting died in 1925, the obvious successor, Fredrik Thorsson, was seriously ill, and Hansson was appointed chairman by the party board. However, Hansson's position was controversial, and Rickard Sandler was appointed Prime Minister. Only in 1927 did Hansson become elected as chairman of the parliamentary group, and on June 8, 1928, he was elected as chairman of the party at the national congress.
