TL;DRSo far in 2026, the Public Health Agency has analyzed 12 samples to check if patients are carrying dangerous infectious diseases such as Ebola. All of these samples have tested negative, meaning that no dangerous infections have been detected.
In plain language

The Public Health Agency of Sweden works around the clock to analyze samples from individuals who may have contagious diseases. They use a specialized high-security laboratory for this purpose. It is the only laboratory in Scandinavia with the highest level of security, which allows them to handle highly dangerous pathogens without risking their spread to other people.

They examine samples from individuals who, for example, have traveled to areas where there is a risk of serious diseases. The goal is to quickly rule out whether a person carries any dangerous pathogens, so that the patient can receive appropriate care and society can be protected. This is important because handling highly contagious diseases is both complex and expensive.

During the years 2023 to 2026, all samples analyzed in this work have been negative. This means that no dangerous pathogens were detected during this period. The number of samples they receive varies depending on the situation regarding disease outbreaks in other countries where Swedes travel.

The Public Health Agency has 24/7 preparedness for analyzing samples from patients to rule out or confirm whether they carry dangerous infectious diseases, such as Ebola. To date, the agency has analyzed 12 preparedness samples in 2026.

Preparedness samples require rapid analysis in a secure laboratory. The Public Health Agency houses the only biosafety level 4 (BSL-4) laboratory in Scandinavia, which is the highest level of containment. Examples of infectious agents in risk class 4 include infections with Ebola virus and Marburg virus.

In the BSL-4 laboratories, analyses and diagnostics are performed on pathogens that cause particularly serious infections in humans, where the spread of infection could have major consequences for society. Infectious diseases in the highest risk class do not normally occur in Sweden, but there is a small risk that travelers from an affected area may bring an infection home.

"When a clinic receives a patient with symptoms after traveling to an area where a dangerous infectious disease is spreading, it is not uncommon for the responsible physician to assess that a sample needs to be sent to the Public Health Agency for analysis," says Johan Aarum, head of the Preparedness and Infectious Disease Diagnostics Unit at the Public Health Agency. "This is often done to rule out whether the illness is caused by the dangerous infectious disease, rather than because it is the most likely diagnosis."

It is crucial for both the patient and society to quickly rule out whether a patient carries an infection that requires high-level isolation care, such as Ebola. The patient needs prompt treatment, and managing infections classified as risk level 4 is extensive and costly for healthcare providers and society.

In total, the Public Health Agency analyzed 14 preparedness samples in 2025. So far, 12 preparedness samples have been analyzed in 2026.

"The number of preparedness samples for infections classified as risk levels 3 and 4 that the Public Health Agency analyzes varies from year to year," says Johan Aarum. "During major outbreaks in locations with travel to and from Sweden, we often receive and analyze more samples than usual."

From 2023 to 2026, all preparedness samples have shown negative results. The last time a preparedness sample was positive was in 2022, when a patient was diagnosed with mpox for the first time in Sweden. In 2020, the Public Health Agency's biosafety laboratory analyzed the first COVID-19 samples in the country.
