Folke Bernadotte af Wisborg, born on January 2, 1895, in Stockholm (registered as living in the parish of Skeppsholmen), and died on September 17, 1948, in Jerusalem (assassinated by the Zionist paramilitary group Stern Gang), was a Swedish army officer, Red Cross leader, and diplomat who held the Luxembourgish noble title of Count af Wisborg. He was the son of Prince Oscar Bernadotte and his wife Ebba Munck af Fulkila, and thus a cousin of Gustaf VI Adolf.

== Childhood ==

Folke Bernadotte described his childhood as happy and harmonious, but his upbringing as strict. His father's marriage had forfeited his descendants' right to the throne, and the home environment resembled upper-middle class rather than extravagant aristocracy. He attended Nya Elementarskolan in Stockholm and passed his final exams in 1913, along with others including Erik C:son Boheman, Herbert Beckman, and Curt Björklund. He performed averagely in school, and it is said that he had dyslexia, a condition common within the family. Subsequently, he underwent cavalry officer training, graduating as an officer in 1915. In 1918, he became a lieutenant in the Livregementets dragoner (Royal Dragoons), in 1930 he was promoted to captain in the Livregementet till häst (Royal Mounted Regiment), and in 1932 he transferred to the reserves. During the years of preparedness, he became a major in 1941. His military career came to an abrupt end in 1927 when Bernadotte was diagnosed with "fragile blood vessels," reportedly a bleeding ulcer – a condition that plagued him for the rest of his life. During his hospital stay, he began to seriously consider important issues and described how he became more understanding of human suffering.

== Family == Folke Bernadotte married Estelle Manville from Pleasantville, New York, on December 1, 1928, in the St. Johns Episcopal Church in Pleasantville. They had met for the first time on the French Riviera, where Bernadotte was staying to recover. At the wedding, the bride wore a Swedish bridal crown made of platinum and rock crystal, and Queen Sofia's veil made of delicate lace. Prince Gustaf Adolf served as the best man, and Prince Sigvard was the groomsman. Over 1,500 guests were invited to the festivities held at the Manville family estate, Hi-Esmaro. This marked the first time a member of a European royal family had married on American soil. Estelle's family's considerable wealth allowed Bernadotte to live a much more comfortable life than he had in his childhood home. == Assignments Before World War II ==

After his military career, Folke Bernadotte continued his work within the royal household. In 1934, he was appointed as the temporary head of the staff for Prince Gustaf Adolf, Duke of Västerbotten, a position he held until 1940. His first major official assignment was as a general commissioner in the Swedish board at the World's Fair in New York in 1939. In connection with the fair, Folke Bernadotte was awarded an honorary doctorate in law from Upsala University in East Orange, New Jersey.

Folke Bernadotte was a successful competitive rider, which naturally led to his involvement in the boards of various equestrian organizations. Like Prince Gustaf Adolf, he was involved in the scouting movement and succeeded Ebbe Lieberath as chairman of the Swedish Scout Federation in November 1937. He served as chairman until he took over Prince Gustaf Adolf's position as chairman of the Swedish Scouting Union in 1947. In 1940, he published "Boys and Scouting," and for his contributions to the scouting movement, he was awarded the Silver Wolf in 1941. He also served as chairman of the Swedish Swimming Association, the Stockholm City Voluntary Rifle Club, and the National Shooting Federation.

In 1943, Folke Bernadotte became vice-chairman of the Swedish Red Cross. The chairman at the time, Prince Carl, Folke Bernadotte's uncle, was 82 years old, so in practice, he took over the responsibility; in 1946, he was formally appointed as chairman.

== The White Buses ==

During the final stages of World War II, Folke Bernadotte led several rescue operations for the Red Cross. In the autumns of 1943 and 1944, he organized prisoner exchanges, in which 11,000 prisoners of war were able to return to their home countries via Sweden. From February 1945, he was in Germany and met with Heinrich Himmler.
