Adjustments to the rules regarding interest deductibility to comply with EU law.

Source Riksdagen Read the original at Riksdagen →2025-09-30
TL;DRThe government wants to change the rules regarding the amount of interest that companies can deduct from their taxes, in order to comply with EU laws. The proposal aims to prevent unusual schemes where companies use debt to reduce their tax burden.
In plain language

The government has proposed a change to the income tax law, which governs how much tax companies and individuals must pay. This concerns the deductibility of interest, meaning the right to deduct the cost of interest on debts when calculating taxes.

Currently, there are rules that limit the amount of interest that can be deducted if there are debts to companies that the individual or entity is part of, known as related entities. The government now wants to align these rules with EU law. The purpose is to prevent companies from creating artificial or fabricated debt relationships solely to reduce their tax burden.

The new rules also apply to companies located within the European Economic Area (EEA), a group of countries that cooperate closely with the EU. If a company uses a debt in a way that is not natural or genuine, the interest should not be deductible for tax purposes.

If the parliament approves the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on January 1, 2026. This means that companies must be aware of their interest deductions and how they manage debts to related companies before the new year begins.

Full text

The proposal suggests that the rules limiting the deductibility of interest expenses, which restrict the right to deduct interest expenses on certain debts to companies within the same group, be aligned with EU law. This means that interest expenses paid to a company within the same group that is located within the European Economic Area (EEA) will not be deductible if the debt relationship is part of an artificial arrangement.

The new provisions are proposed to come into force on January 1, 2026.
