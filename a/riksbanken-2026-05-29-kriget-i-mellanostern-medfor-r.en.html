If the war continues, there is a risk that commodity prices will rise (inflation) and interest rates will increase while the economy weakens. This could make it more difficult for companies and banks to operate normally.

There are also other global issues that could exacerbate the situation, such as high levels of debt in some countries and an increased risk of cyberattacks on computers. These factors can affect the Swedish economy through our international connections with the rest of the world.

In Sweden, banks are stable and have a good capacity to manage difficulties at present. However, there is concern about real estate companies that have large debts and a short timeframe to repay their loans. Households with high levels of debt may also face challenges if interest rates rise, which could leave people with less disposable income for purchases.

The Riksbank, the authority responsible for Sweden's currency and interest rates, assesses that Swedish banks have a good ability to handle disruptions. Therefore, they have decided to maintain their safety buffers at the same level to address future challenges.

The financial system has been functioning well, but uncertainty is high.

Geopolitical uncertainty has been elevated for some time and has increased further due to the war in the Middle East. Despite periods of significant turbulence in financial markets, the global financial system has functioned well during the spring. However, uncertainty remains high, and the longer the war continues, the greater the risk of higher inflation and interest rates combined with a weaker economy. In such a scenario, market sentiment could deteriorate rapidly, leading to higher risk premiums and reduced functionality in financial markets.

In addition to these factors, several global vulnerabilities could exacerbate the situation, such as high and growing public debt in major economies, the expanding non-bank sector, and elevated asset valuations. Furthermore, cyber threats are increasing due to geopolitical tensions and rapid advancements in artificial intelligence, placing greater demands on the preparedness and risk management of financial institutions. If multiple negative events occur simultaneously, it could have serious consequences for global financial stability, with spillover effects for Sweden.

Stress on global financial markets can quickly impact actors within the Swedish financial system. However, so far, they have managed these challenges well, particularly because banks have had good access to funding and liquidity in all currencies. Lending remains subdued, but structural risks related to factors such as the debt levels of real estate companies persist.

Real estate companies still have high levels of debt and now face even shorter interest rate and capital commitment periods. They are also challenged by a weak rental market, and in a more difficult situation, they risk having to take measures to refinance maturing loans and improve their financial position. Longer interest rate and capital commitment periods would therefore strengthen the resilience of the real estate sector.

Household debt levels have decreased in recent years but remain relatively high, while households have short-term interest rate commitments. In a scenario where inflation and interest rates rise, households may need to reduce their consumption, which would reinforce an economic downturn. It is important for the government and parliament to introduce a new income-based tool, such as a debt-to-income ratio limit, to prevent household debt from increasing in a way that is not sustainable in the long term.

Households and businesses continue to have good access to financing, while lending is increasing at a moderate pace. Overall indebtedness among households and businesses, relative to GDP and income, also decreased slightly towards the end of 2025. Taken together, this suggests that the build-up of cyclical systemic risks is limited, while Swedish banks possess strong resilience and flexibility to manage various disruptions. Therefore, the Riksbank is leaving the countercyclical capital buffer rate unchanged at its positive neutral level of 2 percent.
