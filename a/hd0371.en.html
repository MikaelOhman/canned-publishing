Access to documents obtained through searches and seizures, and through copying during a search warrant execution.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the law so that files seized by the police or other authorities during a search warrant are not considered public records. This means that the public will not have the right to access them directly.
In plain language

The government has proposed a new rule in the Act on Freedom of the Press, which governs what documents can be accessed from government agencies. The proposal concerns digital files and recordings that government agencies obtain during searches or when seizing items during criminal investigations.

If an agency takes responsibility for files that have been copied or seized, these files will no longer be considered public documents. A public document is something that anyone has the right to request to see. This change will make it more difficult for individuals to demand access to these specific files.

However, there is an exception in the proposal. If the files become part of an ongoing criminal investigation, they will still be considered public documents. The purpose is to regulate how information collected during police operations should be handled.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rule will come into effect on January 1, 2027.

Full text

The proposal suggests that a recording intended for automated processing, which has been received by an authority as a result of a seized information carrier during an investigation of criminal activity or international legal assistance, or as a copy made during a search of such activities, should not be considered a public document. The same applies to a recording received by an authority because the authority has taken over responsibility for the seized information carrier or the copy from the search. However, if a recording is added to an investigation of criminal activity or other activities within the authority, it will continue to be considered a public document. The change is proposed to be introduced as an addition to section 14 of chapter 2 of the Freedom of the Press Act.

The proposed amendment is to come into force on January 1, 2027.
