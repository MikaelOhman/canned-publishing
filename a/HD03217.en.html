The public has the right to demand that public activities are conducted correctly and responsibly. The government wants to strengthen the ability to hold individuals accountable when mistakes are made.

Therefore, a new, tiered crime is being introduced into the criminal code: abuse of public office.

It will become punishable to deliberately violate a law or regulation while in a public position or assignment – by doing something or failing to do something – in order to gain an unfair advantage for oneself or someone else, or to unfairly disadvantage someone. Serious cases will be prosecuted as aggravated offenses.

Full text

The public has a legitimate expectation that public activities are conducted in a responsible and correct manner, and it is necessary to have an effective system for holding individuals accountable when mistakes are made. The government therefore proposes the introduction of a new, tiered criminal offense in the criminal code, designated as "misuse of public office." This would penalize the intentional violation of laws or regulations when performing a public service, public duty, or other public activity, by taking or failing to take an action to obtain an undue benefit for oneself or for another, or to unfairly disadvantage another. If the offense is serious, it will be punished as "aggravated misuse of public office." The proposed penalty for misuse of public office is a fine or imprisonment of up to two years, and for the aggravated offense, imprisonment of at least one year and six months and up to six years.

To better reflect the seriousness of the crime, the government also proposes to increase the minimum penalty for aggravated official misconduct to imprisonment for one year and six months.

The proposed amendments to the law will take effect on August 1, 2026.

Anyone who intentionally or through negligence, in the exercise of official duties, disregards what applies to the task, shall be convicted of misconduct and sentenced to a fine or imprisonment for a maximum of two years. No one shall be held liable if the act, considering the perpetrator's authority, the task's connection to other official duties, or other circumstances, is considered minor.

If a crime as described in the first paragraph has been committed intentionally and is serious, the person shall be convicted of gross misconduct and sentenced to imprisonment for a minimum of one year and six months and a maximum of six years. When assessing whether the crime is serious, particular consideration shall be given to whether:

  1. The perpetrator has seriously abused their position, or
  2. The act has resulted in significant damage or inconvenience, or a substantial unfair advantage for the public or an individual.

The first and second paragraphs do not apply to members of a legislative body at the state or municipal level with regard to an action.
