Adaptation of certain tax and fee reductions to the EU's rules on state aid.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the rules for certain tax benefits and subsidies to ensure they comply with EU laws on state aid. The proposal includes new ways for companies to reimburse reduced fees for hiring new employees.
In plain language

The government has proposed changes to Swedish tax regulations. The aim is to ensure that the economic benefits provided by the state to companies and individuals comply with EU rules regarding permissible subsidies. Failure to comply with these rules could be considered illegal state aid to individual companies.

An important example is the so-called "growth support" program. This is a reduction in employer contributions that companies receive when they hire their first or second employee. The government proposes that this be changed to a reimbursement of funds. To enable the Swedish Tax Agency to monitor this, companies will also need to submit more information than they do currently.

Changes are also planned for the regional allowance. This is a lower contribution for employers operating in certain parts of northern and western Sweden. The rules will be amended to align with EU regulations for regional support. This also means that the types of businesses eligible for the allowance will change.

Other tax deductions, such as support for residents in rural areas or for the production of renewable energy, will also be affected. For businesses that are eligible for these benefits, the requirements for reporting information will be changed. Most of these new laws are intended to come into effect on January 1, 2026.

Full text

The government's proposal suggests adjustments to Swedish tax legislation so that tax relief measures, which are considered to be of minor significance, are granted in a manner consistent with EU law. This includes the following:

The reduction in employer contributions when a first or second person is hired in a business (so-called "growth support") is proposed to be structured as a right to reimbursement of the portion of employer contributions that corresponds to the current reduction. To enable the Swedish Tax Agency to monitor and report on granted growth support, companies need to provide certain information that they do not currently provide. The so-called "regional deduction" that employers operating in certain specified municipalities in northern Sweden and western Svealand are entitled to when calculating social security contributions is proposed to be aligned with the conditions applicable to regional operating grants under the General Block Exemption Regulation (GBER), which, among other things, requires an adjustment of the eligible sectors. The regional deduction that individual entrepreneurs and individuals who are partners in limited liability companies can deduct when calculating self-employment taxes is also proposed to continue to be granted as a tax relief measure of minor significance, but with certain adjustments to the eligible sectors. The proposal also includes adjustments to the reporting obligations for certain individuals who are entitled to the tax reduction for residents in certain sparsely populated areas (regional tax reduction) or the tax reduction for micro-generation of renewable electricity, and who operate businesses.

The proposed amendments to the law are expected to come into force on January 1, 2026.
