Erik Segersäll (Old Norse: Eiríkr inn sigrsæli Bjarnarson, referred to in Latin as Hericus rex Victor by Adam of Bremen), died in 995, was a Swedish king who ruled from approximately 970 to 995 and likely also governed Denmark for a time. He is one of the first Swedish kings about whom we have definite knowledge, and Sweden's royal chronicles typically begin with him. Erik may have ruled over Svealand, Västergötland, and Östergötland, and he is often considered to be the founder of the city of Sigtuna.

== Reign == The sources that describe Erik Segersäll's reign are primarily Icelandic sagas, Saxo Grammaticus (Gesta Danorum), and Adam of Bremen's chronicle.

=== The Battle of Fyrisvallarna ===

According to Icelandic saga literature (Flatöboken), he co-ruled with his younger brother Olof upon ascending the throne. However, Olof reportedly died young, leaving Erik as the sole king. Later sagas claim that Olof left behind a son named Styrbjörn who claimed his father's right to the throne. Erik refused to recognize this claim but provided Styrbjörn with equipped ships. Styrbjörn sailed towards Denmark and eventually became the leader of the Jomsvikings. Around 985, Styrbjörn, along with his allies, reportedly set out for Sweden to overthrow Erik. Upon arrival, Styrbjörn is said to have burned his ships to prevent his own men from fleeing. Erik and Styrbjörn then met in a three-day battle at Fyrisvallarna. Styrbjörn was killed, and Erik emerged victorious, earning him the epithet "Segersäll" (victorious). According to Snorre, the battle began with Erik throwing a spear at his enemies while shouting, "Odin owns you all."

=== Conquest of Denmark? === Shortly after the Battle of Fýrisvellir, it is said that Erik also drove the Danish king Sven Tveskägg out of Denmark and ruled over this kingdom until his death. In Denmark, he supposedly converted to Christianity sometime between 990 and 992 and was baptized, but shortly thereafter, he reverted to Norse paganism. The sources for these claims are Adam of Bremen and Saxo Grammaticus, with the former stating that he received this information from the Danish king Sven Estridsson. While the details surrounding Adam of Bremen's accounts of the Swedish conquest of Denmark have been questioned, historian Sture Bolin argues that Adam of Bremen's sources are still credible. One of Adam's sources was King Sven Estridsson's oral accounts, which included information about Erik's victory over Sven and his rule in Denmark. Adam also heard from other people that Erik fought against and was defeated by Otto III. Sture Bolin believes that such conflicts almost necessitate that Erik Segersäll ruled in Denmark, and therefore concludes that "Erik's dominion over Denmark is mentioned by two independent sources and must therefore be considered historically substantiated." The Rune Stone known as Eriksstenen, likely erected around the year 995, bears an inscription that Ludwig Wimmer has interpreted as a possible description of an attack on Hedeby carried out by King Sven Tveskägg against the Swedish defenders who had occupied Hedeby after Erik Segersäll's conquest of Denmark.

=== Death === Erik Segersäll died either in the autumn of 994 or the winter of 995 from illness (likely the bubonic plague) at the royal estate in Gamla Uppsala, and was succeeded by his son Olof Skötkonung.

== Marriage ==

Erik Segersäll was married to either Sigrid Storråda, daughter of the West Gothian chieftain Skoglar-Toste, or Świętosława (also known as Gunhild) of Poland, daughter of Duke Mieszko I of Poland.

The sources disagree about who was Erik's queen, and which of these women is considered the most credible depends entirely on how one assesses the reliability of the sources. The Icelandic sources and Saxo Grammaticus in Gesta Danorum claim it was Sigrid Storråda, and she receives considerable attention in their accounts. Thietmar of Merseburg and Adam of Bremen, on the other hand, state that he was married to an unnamed Slavic princess, sister or daughter of Bolesław. Snorre Sturlasson also mentions a Slavic princess named Gunhild. This marriage would have symbolized an alliance between Erik and Bolesław, aimed at the Danish king Harald Bluetooth. However, there is a problem if "Bolesław" refers to the Polish King Bolesław I, as he ruled after Harald Bluetooth's death.

According to the saga of Ingvar the Far-Traveled, Erik was also married to Aud Håkansdotter. He supposedly already had a daughter who became the grandmother of Ingvar. Some historians have also suggested that he might be the father of Holmfrid, whom Olof Skötkonung married to the Norwegian jarl Sven Håkonsson, but Snorre states that she was the daughter of Olof.
