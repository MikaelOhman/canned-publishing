Louise of Sweden (originally Lovisa Josefina Eugenia), born October 31, 1851, in Stockholm, and died March 20, 1926, in Copenhagen, was a Swedish and Norwegian princess, Crown Princess, and Queen of Denmark. She was the daughter of King Charles XV of Sweden and Norway and Queen Louise, and she married the Danish King Frederick VIII in 1869, remaining married until his death in 1912.

Louise was the only surviving child of the royal couple. Although her father repeatedly attempted to change the relevant paragraph in the then-current succession law, Louise was not eligible to inherit the throne due to her gender. In 1869, she married Prince Frederick of Denmark, who later became King. Louise experienced difficulties during her long time as Danish Crown Princess. She eventually became popular among the Danish public but unpopular within the Danish court and royal family. It is said that Louise was troubled by Frederick's private life and infidelity. She has been described as a "good housewife of bourgeois type" and was reportedly strict but caring. The family lived a secluded life and, thanks to the inheritance from her maternal grandparents, had very good financial resources.

Louise finally became Queen in 1906, but her reign lasted only six years. It is said that she remained well-informed and politically up-to-date, but never intervened in politics. Even as Queen, Louise maintained her popularity among the public through her philanthropic endeavors. After Frederick's death, she continued to dedicate herself to her interests in charity and missionary work. She was the mother of King Christian X of Denmark and King Haakon VII of Norway.

== Biography ==

=== Princess ===

Princess Lovisa was born at Stockholm Palace on October 31, 1851. She was the eldest and only surviving child of then Crown Prince Karl and Crown Princess Lovisa. The following year, Lovisa's brother, the long-awaited heir prince Carl Oscar, was born. However, the little prince died in 1854, and Lovisa became an only child at the age of three. It subsequently became clear that her mother would not have any more children due to injuries sustained during childbirth. The mother reportedly offered Crown Prince Karl a divorce, which he refused. Thus, Lovisa remained the royal couple's only child.

Her father became king upon the death of Oscar I in 1859, when Lovisa was seven years old. Due to the then-current succession laws, Lovisa was not eligible to inherit the throne because of her gender. Her father, Karl XV, repeatedly tried to change the clause in the constitution to make it possible for Lovisa to become heir to the throne, but without success. Since his brother, Crown Prince Oscar, who had several sons starting in 1858, was already next in line, it was not deemed necessary.

As the royal couple's only child, Lovisa was very cherished by her parents. She was said to resemble her father in personality and her mother in appearance, and she was described as lively and cheerful. During her time in Sweden, she was commonly called "Sessan," a nickname for "Princess."

During her father's reign, children's balls were often held for Sessan at the palace in Stockholm, which were the highlight of all society's children's balls, and to which her cousins, Crown Prince Oscar's sons, were often invited.

In 1862, Lovisa took swimming lessons together with her mother from Nancy Edberg, a pioneer in women's swimming, which led to increased acceptance of women learning to swim. Hilda Elfving was her teacher between 1857 and 1860. She also had an English governess for a time, "Miss Brown." She was confirmed on Easter Sunday in 1868 in the newly built palace chapel next to Ulriksdal Palace.

=== Engagement and marriage ===

The question of Princess Louise's future marriage became a subject of speculation early on. The most popular candidate for a match was the Danish Crown Prince Frederick, who was the eldest son of King Christian IX and Queen Louise of Denmark. Such a marriage was considered desirable for several reasons. For various reasons, there wasn't much sympathy between the Danish and Swedish royal houses. After the death of the Danish King Frederick VII in November 1863, some people wanted either Karl XV or Prince Oscar to ascend the Danish throne. However, this did not happen, and Christian IX became the King of Denmark. The Danish-German War of 1864 dealt a blow to political Scandinavianism, as Sweden-Norway had been unwilling to provide military support to Denmark, despite the initial promise of such support from the Swedish King Karl XV. After 1864, both royal houses began discussing a symbolic reconciliation between the two countries through an arranged marriage between Princess Louise and Crown Prince Frederick.

However, both parties had reservations. Karl XV was critical of Christian IX's character and abilities to be a suitable king, but he still wanted his daughter Louise to marry the Danish Crown Prince Frederick. The Danish royal family also had some reservations about the alliance. Princess Louise was not considered beautiful, and her future mother-in-law, Queen Louise, feared that her personality would not fit in with the Danish royal family.
