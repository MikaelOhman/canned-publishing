Lennart Torstenson (who sometimes wrote his name as Linnardt Torstenson), born August 17, 1603, in Forstena, and died April 7, 1651, in Stockholm, was a Swedish count and military figure. He became a lieutenant colonel in 1628, a colonel in 1630, a general in 1632, a member of the Council of State (riksråd) and field marshal in 1641. Furthermore, he served as governor-general of Västergötland, Dalsland, Värmland, and Halland. He is considered one of the most successful military commanders of his time, and also one of Sweden's greatest military leaders of all time.

== Biography ==

=== Early life and career ===

Lennart Torstenson was born at Forstena farm in the parish of Västra Tunhem, Västergötland. His father, Torsten Lennartsson, was the governor of Älvsborg Castle and a brother of Field Marshal Anders Lennartsson of the Forstena family. Torstenson's parents were forced into exile by Duke Charles, and Torstenson was raised by relatives, including Councilor Bo Ribbing from 1616. At the age of 15, he was employed as a chamberlain in the service of Gustav II Adolf. During this period, he accompanied military campaigns to Livland, where he served as the king's armor bearer and witnessed the capture of Riga in 1621. At the age of 20, he made the customary trip to foreign countries, which was then considered a necessary part of the education for young nobles. In 1623, he participated in a military study group led by Gustaf Horn, who traveled to the Netherlands to study warfare. As a lieutenant in Frans Bernhard von Thurn's hovregemente (now Svea Livgarde), he participated in the Battle of Wallhof in January 1626.

During the period of Gustav Adolf's war history known as the Prussian campaigns (1626–1629), Torstenson received his actual military training. In 1628, he became a major in Gustav Horn's northern regiment, and in 1629, commander of the Hälsinge regiment that was separated from it. A turning point in his development came with his appointment in 1630 as colonel of artillery, an arm for which the era of Gustav Adolf and Torstenson marked a new epoch. The new organization of the artillery was a decisive factor in Gustav Adolf's victories. Under Torstenson's leadership and supervision, these changes were implemented. As head of the artillery, Torstenson also significantly contributed to the victories at the Battle of Breitenfeld (1631) and the Battle of Lech (1632), as well as in the capture of numerous fortresses. In 1632, Torstenson was appointed "General of Artillery," but later that same year, he was captured by the enemy during fighting near Alte Veste and held prisoner in a damp prison in Ingolstadt until 1633, where the foundation for his severe illness (rheumatism and gout) was laid, which plagued him for the rest of his life.

Shortly after his release, he contributed to the capture of the fortress of Landsberg in Bavaria, but returned home to Sweden later that same year with Gustav Adolf's remains. During the funeral procession, Torstenson carried the Swedish national banner. In 1634, he was appointed National Master of the Armory. During the subsequent period of the Thirty Years' War, under the overall command of Johan Banér (1634–1641), Torstenson was initially assigned to lead the war in Prussia as Jakob De la Gardie's closest advisor, a conflict that seemed imminent from Poland. However, after the armistice with Poland in 1635, he was called back to the German theater of war to assist the besieged supreme commander, with whom he joined forces at the end of 1635. Banér and Torstenson together saved Sweden's position in Germany under desperate circumstances. Torstenson played a significant role in the Battle of Wittstock in 1636, in the energetic defense of Pomerania in 1637 and 1638, in the Battle of Chemnitz, and in the invasion of Bohemia in 1639. In 1640, he was granted leave to return to Sweden due to his deteriorating health, but he did not arrive until the spring of 1641. He was already considering requesting a discharge due to his illness. Immediately after his return, he was appointed Councilor (April 1641). In the same year, 1641, Lennart Torstenson received through a charter of donation, full ownership of Norra Ljusterö in the northern archipelago of Stockholm.

=== Commander-in-Chief in Germany ===

Torstensson had only been home for a few weeks when news arrived of Banér's death. With great reluctance, he now assumed the position that had become vacant, a role that public opinion within the government and among the people considered him suitable for. On August 31, 1641, he was appointed Field Marshal and Governor-General of Pomerania. Despite being ready to set sail earlier, he remained at home due to illness well into September. Torstensson's command, from 1641 to 1645, was a successful period in Sweden's military history.

During the years he held command, his ailments were so overwhelming that he repeatedly requested to be relieved of duty. In 1643, he wrote to Axel Oxenstierna: "There are times when I can, for necessity's sake, sit on horseback for an hour or a few, but even then, I am unable to control the horse, and often, out of impatience, I wish for death rather than life."
