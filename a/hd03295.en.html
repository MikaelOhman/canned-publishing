Preventative measures within the social services system to protect children and young people when there is a lack of cooperation.

TL;DRA new law is being proposed to protect children and young people who are not receiving the support they need from their parents or guardians. This would allow social services to take action even without consent, for example, by imposing fines or implementing monitoring measures if necessary.
In plain language

The government wants to introduce a new law that will assist social services when children and young people are in danger, but their guardians are unwilling to cooperate. If parents refuse to allow the child to receive help, the social services department can still decide on interventions. Parents who violate these decisions may be fined, a penalty known as a "fine."

For young people who fail to comply with specific regulations, the police may use electronic monitoring to track their location. If a young person does not follow these rules, the police may temporarily take them to another location. To make this legal, the regulations regarding how the police handle personal data will also be amended.

Parents participating in certain interventions through social services or the new law may be eligible for temporary parental leave benefits from the state. Additionally, young people who commit crimes for the first time will receive a special meeting, known as a "serious conversation," if the crime is serious enough to potentially lead to imprisonment.

Most of the new regulations are planned to come into effect on January 2, 2027. Only the regulations regarding electronic monitoring have a different date, which will be determined later by the government.

Full text

The proposal suggests a new law regarding interventions within the social services when consent is lacking. The aim of this proposal is to prevent and stop children and young people from being harmed.

According to the new law, the social welfare board should have the authority to decide on interventions for caregivers, children, and young people when these interventions cannot be provided voluntarily. The board should also be able to set specific conditions for them. A decision regarding interventions for a caregiver may include a financial penalty. A specific condition for a child or young person requiring them to be present at their residence during certain times may be enforced through electronic monitoring. The police should temporarily have the authority to take custody of a child or young person who does not comply with such a specific condition. To enable the processing of personal data resulting from such detentions, a new registration law for the Police Authority is also proposed.

The proposal also suggests that a parent may be eligible for temporary parental leave when participating in certain interventions under the Social Services Act (2025:400) or the new law regarding interventions within social services when consent is lacking.

Furthermore, it is proposed that the social welfare board should hold a special meeting with a child or young person if it is their first time being suspected of a crime that could result in imprisonment (a serious conversation).

The proposals, except those concerning electronic monitoring, are suggested to come into effect on January 2, 2027. The provisions regarding electronic monitoring are proposed to come into effect on the date determined by the government.
