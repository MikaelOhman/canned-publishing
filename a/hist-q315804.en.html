Olaus Magnus (Latinized from Olof Månsson), born in October 1490 in Skänninge and died on August 1, 1557, in Rome, was a Swedish priest, titular archbishop of the Catholic Archdiocese of Uppsala, as well as a diplomat, humanist, ethnologist, and cartographer. Olaus Magnus and his brother Johannes are considered the first Swedish humanists. They are credited with increasing interest in Scandinavia on the continent and enhancing the reputation of the Nordic peoples.

== Biography ==

=== Years in Sweden === Olaus Magnus's parents were Måns Petersson, a burgher from Linköping, and Kristina. He was the brother of the Swedish Catholic Archbishop Johannes Magnus. He attended schools in Linköping and Västerås, studied in Rostock, and likely Cologne; he earned his bachelor's degree in 1514 and became a master. Olaus Magnus traveled through northern Sweden from 1518 to 1519, where he sold indulgences and gained knowledge about the local culture. The journey was commissioned by Arcimboldus, an apostolic commissioner representing the papal curia, and also had political motives as the coast had been ravaged by the Danes. After serving as a canon in Uppsala and Linköping, he resided in Stockholm in 1520 and witnessed the Stockholm Bloodbath. Around the same time, he was appointed pastor there. He was appointed Provost of Strängnäs in 1523 and simultaneously tasked with traveling to Rome to convey Sweden's requests regarding the appointment of certain offices, particularly to secure papal approval for his brother as archbishop. He informed the Vatican about the religious developments in the country; Olaus Petri had already been preaching Luther's teachings for three years. The year before Gustav Vasa began showing tendencies towards implementing liturgical reforms at the Church Council in Örebro in 1529, he had tasked Olaus Magnus with negotiating improved trade relations with the Netherlands, which included several Hanseatic cities and was ruled by the Habsburgs. However, when the Reformation was introduced in 1527, Olaus Magnus, along with his brother, was forced into exile and went to Danzig because of strained relations with Rome.

=== Exile ===

In Danzig, he was inspired by his Portuguese friend Damianus à Goes to study the cultural history and curiosities of his homeland, and received financial support for this from Bishop Johannes Dantiscus. He may have met Copernicus there.

He traveled to Venice in 1539, where he stayed with Patriarch Hieronimo Quirino, who was interested in Olaus Magnus's studies of Sweden. Thanks to the patriarch's generosity and his own good memory, Olaus Magnus created the map "Carta marina" around 1540, which depicts Scandinavia.

His brother Johannes Magnus died in 1544, and on October 26th of the same year, Olaus Magnus was appointed Archbishop of Uppsala by Pope Paul III. He participated as a Swedish delegate at the Council of Trent in 1545, with the aim of reintroducing Catholicism to Sweden, but remained in Rome. There, he was appointed by the Pope as head of the Bridgettine Order, where he also resided from 1549. He established a printing press at the Bridgettine Order and published his brother's and his own major historical works there.

Olaus died on August 1, 1557, and was buried in Santa Maria dell'Anima in Rome, the German national church, where Hadrianus VI's monument still stands, but Olaus's monument probably disappeared during renovations in 1774.[1] A Latin inscription carved in marble remains in the church, commemorating Olaus Magnus.

== Research == Olaus Magnus is primarily known for his patriotic portrayal of Scandinavia (including Russia and Greenland), where he combines ethnology, history, and cartography with legends and folklore. His "Historia de gentibus septentrionalibus" (History of the Northern Peoples), along with letters and chronicles about Saint Bridget and her daughter, demonstrates a lively storytelling ability, but like his brother, it often lacks critical analysis of sources and sometimes contains inaccuracies.

"Historia" is a literary continuation of his work on the map "Carta marina." He blends his own observations from his journey through Norrland, along with guesses and legends, with classical historical writings by authors such as Saxo and Jordanes, in an indiscriminate but convincing manner. When he describes the Gothic script (runestones), he assumes they were created by giants from ancient times, and he recounts incredible sea creatures that inhabit the Bothnian Sea, about Sami people who ski, female hunters who surpass men in skill, and men with large wreaths on their heads toasting at parties. A strong emphasis is placed on highlighting the Nordic people's martial superiority, its natural resources such as mining, and the people's innate courage.

One can sense a certain longing for home when he talks about the fish markets in Venice, which despite their varied offerings lack certain types of fish whose names are impossible to convey using Greek or Latin.

Even during his lifetime, "Historia" aroused curiosity and interest. It was printed in most major cities, and in Antwerp and Amsterdam, it appeared in several new editions during the 17th century. Olaus Magnus's description of Scandinavia is also considered a source of inspiration for Olof Rudbeck the Elder's historical research. However, it was not translated.
