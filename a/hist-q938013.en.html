Karl Filip (Carl Philip), born on April 22, 1601, at Reval Castle in present-day Estonia, and died on January 25, 1622, in Narva, was a Swedish prince of the Vasa dynasty. He held the titles of Duke of Södermanland, Närke, and Värmland, and was the son of Karl IX and Kristina of Holstein-Gottorp, as well as the younger brother of Gustav II Adolf.

He secretly married Elisabet Ribbing on March 5, 1620. After his death in 1622, they had a daughter named Elisabet Carlsdotter (Gyllenhielm).

== Biography == Karl Filip is primarily known for having, for a time, been one of the candidates to become Tsar of Russia. A Russian envoy was sent in 1611 from Moscow to Stockholm with this offer to the Swedish king. The city of Novgorod, which was under Swedish protection, had nominated Karl Filip as their candidate. The election of the Tsar took place amidst turbulent circumstances in Moscow around the turn of the year 1612–1613 (the Time of Troubles). When Karl Filip arrived at Viborg Castle in the summer of 1613, it was already too late, and he had to return home. It was not Karl Filip, but a candidate put forward at the last minute, Mikhail Romanov, who ultimately won. The Romanov dynasty would remain on the Russian throne until 1917.

Karl Filip's candidacy was consistently opposed by his mother, Dowager Queen Kristina, who considered him too young.

After completing his studies, Karl Filip traveled to Denmark, Germany, and France from 1617 to 1618. The dukedom of Karl Filip had been established by his father through a charter in 1609. Due to his minority, the Dowager Queen was appointed as his guardian, and she fiercely defended the Duke's rights against Gustav II Adolf, which particularly in 1617 led to intense conflicts between Gustav Adolf on one hand, and the Dowager Queen and Karl Filip on the other.

During this time, Sweden was involved in the Russian civil war, and Swedish power in the Baltic region was consolidated. Military successes were achieved, including Jacob De la Gardie's capture of Novgorod.

Karl Filip also participated in the Swedish campaigns in the Baltic region that Gustav II Adolf initiated in 1621. In the summer of 1621, the siege of Riga began. Faced with the threat of a Swedish assault, the city surrendered in mid-September. For Karl Filip, the joy of victory would be short-lived. Shortly after the capture of Riga, he fell ill with dysentery and was taken to the city of Narva for care and rest. His condition worsened, and on the night of January 25th, the prince died. A letter from Gustav Adolf to his sister Katarina revealed that his brother deeply mourned him. He was buried on November 19, 1622.

== Other Information == The town of Filipstad in the province of Värmland is named after Karl Filip. Since 1993, there has been an academic society in Uppsala that keeps the memory of Prince Carl Philip alive: The Society Karl Filip of Vasa.
