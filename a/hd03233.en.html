New regulations to combat fraud and other deceptive practices through electronic communications.

TL;DRNew regulations are being proposed to prevent fraud through SMS and other digital messages. Companies that manage your communications should be able to block messages that appear to be malicious.
In plain language

The government wants to amend the law on electronic communications to make it more difficult to digitally defraud people. The proposal means that companies that provide messaging services, such as SMS services, will have a greater responsibility to prevent fraud.

If a company sees technical information about a message that suggests it is a scam or an attempt to mislead people, they should not forward the message. It is important to know that companies are not allowed to read the actual content of your messages to check this; they can only look at technical information about the traffic.

To be able to do this work, companies may process certain information about the messages, but only for as long as it is necessary to stop the fraud. After that, the information must be deleted immediately. The government or another authority will determine exactly which rules will apply to this work.

The new rules are part of an effort within the EU to create safer digital communication. If the proposal is approved, the new laws will come into effect on August 1, 2026.

Full text

The proposal includes measures aimed at preventing electronic communication services from being used to commit fraud or mislead recipients in other ways.

It is proposed that providers of publicly available, number-based, interpersonal communication services should refrain from transmitting an electronic message if the traffic data suggests that the message is being sent as part of a fraud or with the intention of, in some other way, misleading a wider group of recipients. Measures taken in accordance with this obligation must not involve a review or other processing of the content of an electronic message. Furthermore, it is proposed that the government or the authority designated by the government should be authorized to issue regulations related to these proposals. These provisions partially constitute a new implementation of the EU's Code of Electronic Communications.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into force on August 1, 2026.

Prop. 2025/26:233

Government Proposal: The Riksdag (Swedish Parliament) adopts the government's proposal for an amendment to the Act (2022:482) on Electronic Communications. 3

Prop. 2025/26:233 2 Proposed Act Amending the Act (2022:482) on Electronic Communications

Hereby prescribed is, with respect to the Act (2022:482) on Electronic Communications:

  1. That section 9, chapter 27, shall have the following wording;
  2. That two new sections, chapter 4, section 12, and chapter 9, section 6a, of the following wording, shall be introduced.

Current Wording Proposed Wording

Chapter 4, Section 12 A provider of a generally available, number-based, interpersonal communication service shall refrain from transmitting an electronic message if traffic data indicates that the message is being transmitted as part of a fraud. The same applies if traffic data indicates that the message is being transmitted with the intention of misleading a wider group of recipients in some other way. The government or the authority designated by the government may issue regulations on the measures to be taken to fulfill the obligation in the first paragraph.

Chapter 9, Section 6a Traffic data may be processed if the data is necessary for measures required to fulfill the obligation in chapter 4, section 12. The data may only be processed to the extent and for the duration necessary for the purpose. It shall then be immediately erased. The government or the authority designated by the government may issue regulations on:

  1. Which data may be processed according to the first paragraph, and
  2. How long the data may be processed before it must be erased.

27 § In addition to what is stated in sections 1-3 and 31, no one other than the users involved may access or otherwise process data in an electronic message transmitted in a general electronic communications network or with a generally available electronic communication service, or traffic data related to this message, unless one of the users has consented to the processing. This does not preclude:

  1. Storage that is automatic, intermediate, and temporary, if it is necessary for the transmission of an electronic message, or such storage to the extent that it is necessary for the operation of an electronic communications network or an electronic communication service;
  2. That others gain access to the content of an electronic message, if the content is publicly available, if access is granted only to improve the further transmission to other recipients, and if the information about who requested the information is erased; or
  3. That someone in a radio receiver intercepts or otherwise, by using such a receiver, gains access to a radio-transmitted electronic message that is not intended for that person or for the public.

The first paragraph does not apply when a provider of number-independent interpersonal communication services processes data in accordance with Regulation (EU) 2021/1232. This law shall enter into force on August 1, 2026.
