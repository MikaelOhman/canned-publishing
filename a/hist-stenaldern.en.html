Full text

The Stone Age is the period in human history that begins with the creation of tools, starting over 2 million years ago. It is also the first epoch according to Thomsen's three-period system for the prehistory of the Old World. The name comes from the fact that most of the cutting tools used during this period were made of stone. Humans also used other materials, such as wood, plant fibers, leather, and bone. However, these are relatively perishable and rarely survive from that time.

The Stone Age is typically divided into three or four parts:

  • Paleolithic
  • Mesolithic
  • Neolithic
  • Sometimes, a "Copper Stone Age" is included before the Bronze Age begins:
  • Chalcolithic

A simpler division is between the hunter-gatherer Stone Age (the earlier Stone Age) for the period when humans lived by hunting and fishing, and the agricultural Stone Age (the later Stone Age) for the period when agriculture became humanity's primary focus. However, for Scandinavia, this corresponds to the division of Mesolithic versus Neolithic, because there are almost no Paleolithic remains, i.e., no earlier Stone Age finds. This is due to the impact of ice ages on the landscape.

Some also believe that after the Paleolithic period comes (depending on the region and how development unfolded):

  • The Neolithic (later Stone Age, agricultural Stone Age) follows directly after the Paleolithic in Western Asia, where agriculture was invented around 10000 BC.
  • The Mesolithic (middle Stone Age) follows the Paleolithic in Northern Europe, representing a change in lifestyle compared to the previous ice age, but not one that involves the introduction of agriculture.
  • The Epipaleolithic (Greek "epi" means "on") follows the Paleolithic in regions where neither agriculture is introduced nor are there significant changes in living conditions with the end of the ice age. This includes large parts of Africa.

== Paleolithic ==

The Paleolithic era, meaning "Old Stone Age," is the first period in human history. The Stone Age begins when the ancestors of modern humans (so-called hominids) start creating stone tools. This occurred approximately 2,300,000 years ago, and the oldest known evidence comes from Olduvai Gorge in East Africa. This stone tool technology is called the Oldowan culture, named after the location where the first evidence was found.

It's not yet clear exactly which species of hominid created the first stone tools. *Homo habilis*, the first species in our own genus *Homo*, is a strong candidate, but it cannot be ruled out that australopithecines could also have made simple stone tools. The most famous australopithecus is "Lucy," who lived much earlier. Lucy is a find of an individual of the species *Australopithecus afarensis* found during excavations in Hadar in the Afar region of Ethiopia.

The first humans to arrive in Europe were of the species *Homo erectus*, bringing with them a Stone Age culture from Africa. This culture is usually called the Acheulean culture, and the tools are of the same type as those found at Olduvai. *Homo erectus* emerged in Africa sometime between 1,600,000 and 1,000,000 years ago. Finds on Java date back approximately 900,000 years. At Ban Mae Tha in Thailand, finds dating back about 700,000 years have been discovered, and there are also older finds from Zhoukoudian in China, dating between 460,000 and 230,000 years.

Even the later Neanderthals lived in a relatively well-developed Stone Age culture, which is called the Mousterian culture after a site in southern France, Le Moustier. Neanderthals emerged in Europe approximately 400,000 years ago. Elements of the Mousterian culture are shared with modern humans (*Homo sapiens*). This applies especially to what is known as the Levallois technique, which was used by both Neanderthals and modern humans. However, there are many significant differences between the Neanderthal Mousterian culture and the Aurignacian culture of modern humans. For example, Neanderthals use a flake technology, while *Homo sapiens* had a fully developed blade technology at this time. The first humans of the species *Homo sapiens* are often called Cro-Magnon people, after a site in Dordogne, France. It is believed that the Cro-Magnons were the first to make real, sewn clothing. Cro-Magnons arrived in Europe approximately 40,000 years ago.

== Mesolithic Period ==

The society that emerged after the Paleolithic period is called the "Mesolithic," which means "middle Stone Age." In regions where there was no inland ice (such as in Africa), the term "Epipaleolithic" is often used instead. In Africa, large mammals also persisted, so the transition wasn't as dramatic. In Sweden, this period is often referred to as the "Early Stone Age," because there were no human inhabitants during the Ice Age.

During the last ice age (Weichsel glaciation), people primarily subsisted by hunting large tundra-dwelling mammals: mammoths, giant deer, and woolly rhinoceroses. As the ice retreated, these large tundra animals began to die out, both due to extensive hunting by humans and significant climate changes. How certain are we about this?

The Stone Age lacks any written sources – everything is based on archaeological findings (tools, settlements, graves). We can date these finds and get a general idea of how people lived, but names, languages, beliefs, and specific events remain unknown or are qualified guesses.
