Carl Gustaf Rehnskiöld (who wrote his name Rehnschiöldt), born August 6, 1651, in Stralsund, and died January 29, 1722, at the Läggesta inn near Mariefred, was a Swedish count and military figure. He served as King Charles XII's teacher of military tactics and closest military advisor, and also acted as deputy commander-in-chief of the Carolinian army, an army to which he contributed significantly in its training and development.

Rehnskiöld grew up in Swedish Pomerania and studied at Lund University under the philosopher Samuel von Pufendorf. In 1673, he entered Swedish military service and participated in the battles of Halmstad, Lund, and Landskrona during the Scanian War, where he was promoted to colonel and general adjutant. After the war, he served as a regimental commander, tutor to Duke Frederick IV during the Palatine Succession War, and governor of Scania.

During the Great Northern War, Rehnskiöld held a key position in the operational leadership of the Carolinian army, planning battles before the landing at Humlebæk and the engagements at Narva, Düna, and Kliszów. At the Battle of Fraustadt in 1706, he independently commanded an army and defeated a Saxon-Russian force led by Field Marshal Schulenburg. For his achievements, Rehnskiöld was promoted to field marshal and ennobled as a count. During Charles XII's campaign against Russia, Rehnskiöld commanded at the Battle of Holowczyn and the assault on Veprik, where he was seriously wounded. After Charles XII was shot in the foot, Rehnskiöld took command of the Swedish army at the Battle of Poltava in 1709, where the Swedes suffered a decisive defeat.

After the battle, Rehnskiöld became a prisoner of war in Russia and spent several years in Moscow, together with Count Carl Piper, running an administrative office to assist other Swedish prisoners of war. Rehnskiöld was released in 1718 and subsequently arrived at the siege of Fredriksten shortly before Charles XII was killed. Afterward, Rehnskiöld served as commander in western Sweden and died in 1722 from complications related to an old shrapnel wound.

== Childhood and upbringing (1651-1676) ==

Rehnskiöld was born on August 6, 1651, in Stralsund, Swedish Pomerania, as the son of Gerdt Antoniison Rehnskiöld (1610–1658), originally Keffenbrinck, a government councilor in Pomerania, and Birgitta Torskeskål (died 1655), from Västergötland, who was the niece of Baron Johan Adler Salvius. The Keffenbrinck family originated from Westphalia, and their ancestral seat was Rehne Castle in the northern part of the Münsterland province. Gerdt Rehnskiöld entered the Chamber College as a clerk and later became King Gustav II Adolf's representative in the administrative staff during the campaign in Germany. Thanks to his efforts in supplying the Swedish troops during the Thirty Years' War, he was naturalized as a Swedish nobleman in 1639 by Queen Christina and adopted the name Rehnskiöld after his family's ancestral seat. He was also granted the estates of Griebenow, Willershusen, and Hohenwarth in Pomerania, as well as the Stensättra estate in Södermanland. In 1640, the Rehnskiöld family was registered at the House of Nobility under number 270.

Carl Gustaf Rehnskiöld was the eighth of eleven children born to the Rehnskiöld couple. After his father's death in 1658, Carl Gustaf had two brothers, including Axel Rehnskiöld, and two sisters remaining. The government councilor Philip Christoff von der Lancken and the lord chancellor Joachim Cuhn von Owstien, who were colleagues of Gerdt Rehnskiöld, were appointed as guardians for the five siblings. The siblings suffered from financial difficulties, partly due to their father's economic problems during the latter part of his life, and partly due to inheritance disputes between the siblings and Gerdt Rehnskiöld's third wife, Anna Catharina Gärffelt. The guardians had granted her Birgitta Rehnskiöld's family jewelry and 14,000 Riksdaler from the inheritance. This led to the siblings complaining about their guardians' way of providing for them and writing letters of complaint to the Swedish government. Carl Gustaf Rehnskiöld's brother-in-law, Anders Appelman, later became more actively involved in caring for the siblings and contributed financial resources to Carl Gustaf's and his brothers' continued education. Carl Gustaf Rehnskiöld received private tutoring from various teachers and later attended Lund University when he was 20 years old. There, his main subjects were theology, history, languages, and philosophy, and he attended lectures by the historian and philosopher Samuel von Pufendorf, who noticed the talented Rehnskiöld and gave him individual lessons. This teacher inspired him so much that he transcribed Pufendorf's work "Einleitung zur Historie der vornehmsten Reichen und Staaten in Europa" (published in Frankfurt in 1682), added Pufendorf's personal comments to the manuscript, and kept the writing with him for a long time.

Rehnskiöld entered Swedish military service at the age of 22, and in 1673 he received an officer commission when he joined the Närke-Värmland regiment as a ensign in Captain Reinhold Anrep's company. The following year, through a transfer, he obtained a lieutenant commission in the Queen's Life Regiment of Horse. In July 1675, he was transferred to the Uppland regiment, and on February 12, 1676, he became an officer.
