Maria Eleanor of Brandenburg, born November 11, 1599, in Königsberg, and died March 18, 1655, in Stockholm, was Queen of Sweden. She was the daughter of Elector John Sigismund of Brandenburg and Anna of Prussia, and the sister of Elector George William of Brandenburg; her younger sister Catherine became the ruling Princess of Transylvania. She married Gustav II Adolf of Sweden on November 25, 1620. Maria Eleanor was the mother of Queen Christina but did not serve as regent for her daughter.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life ===

The marriage was arranged for political reasons. Sweden at the time desired an alliance with Brandenburg-Prussia against Poland, while German Protestants favored an alliance with Sweden against the Catholic Counter-Reformation. Gustav Adolf's mother, Kristina, also worked to secure a dynastic marriage and opposed his plans to marry Ebba Brahe. In July 1615, Kristina announced that Gustav Adolf's affair with Ebba Brahe was over, and the same month, diplomat Hieronymus von Birckholtz proposed the marriage to Maria Eleonora's father, Johan Sigismund, in Küstrin. Johan Sigismund was initially positive but postponed the decision: to inherit Prussia from his father-in-law, he needed permission from the overlord of Prussia, who was also the monarch of Poland, against whom the marriage was intended as an alliance. However, Maria Eleonora's mother, Anna, and her brother, the heir apparent Georg Wilhelm, opposed the marriage: Georg Wilhelm did not want to risk Prussia and preferred to marry her off to England. In 1618, Johan Sigismund inherited Prussia, which became part of Brandenburg, but he died in 1619, and Georg Wilhelm became regent.

In 1619, Gustav Adolf visited Berlin incognito with his brother-in-law, Count Palatine Johann Kasimir. He had the opportunity to meet Maria Eleonora and her sisters, who were all in favor of the marriage at that time, but her mother and brother still opposed it. Gustav Adolf then embarked on a tour of Germany. Upon his return to Berlin in June 1620, Maria Eleonora's mother, Anna, had changed her mind. The reason is unknown, but it was suggested that she wanted to provoke Sigismund of Poland to give Prussia to her younger son instead, and that she disapproved of Georg Wilhelm's pro-Polish stance. Anna herself claimed that she was swayed by Gustav Adolf's and Maria Eleonora's love for each other. It is said that Anna was charmed by Gustav Adolf, and when Georg Wilhelm protested, she sent Maria Eleonora away from him to Braunschweig. Axel Oxenstierna, who arrived in Berlin at the head of a large delegation in the autumn, was supposed to take the princess to Sweden with her mother without any further complications.

The plan was immediately put into action. After three days at the court of Braunschweig, Axel Oxenstierna left with Maria Eleonora and her sister. Meanwhile, Duchess Anna had prepared for the journey. The Duke had refused to provide her with funds, but she gained access to the ducal treasury through a secret passage. Thus, the marriage was arranged by Anna without a dowry, travel expenses, or wedding attire from Brandenburg, except for what Anna managed to take as collateral from the treasury, including a gilded chalice adorned with sapphires and 12 silver goblets. On October 7, 1620, Anna traveled with her two daughters, Maria Eleonora and Katarina, to Kalmar in Sweden.

=== Queen === Maria Eleonora married Gustav Adolf on November 25th in Stockholm, and was crowned at the Storkyrkan cathedral on November 28th, 1620. During the first few years of their marriage, both her mother Anna and her sister Katarina remained in Sweden; they did not return to Germany until August 1624.

Queen Maria Eleonora was called the most beautiful queen in Europe, but she was also considered hysterical, unstable, and overly emotional. The French legation secretary Charles Ogier described her in 1634 as: "the most beautiful and radiant woman we have ever seen. We were completely dazzled by her beauty and charmed by the gentle expression of her face and her regal bearing." In 1639, Axel Oxenstierna said about her emotional life that she: "is weak by nature, very susceptible to emotions, which in Her Majesty's case are excessive." She was interested in music, theater, art, and architecture, and enjoyed luxury and beautiful objects. She brought a German court chapel to Sweden, often listened to performances by students, and played the lute herself. Jacob Heinrich Elbfas was her court painter, and she also supported David Klöker Ehrenstrahl, the stonemason Jost Henne, and the sculptor Conrad Weber. She was not only interested in architecture but also created drawings for castles, which she would have had built if there had been money available. She also kept animals, such as monkeys, as companions. It is often mentioned that she employed many so-called "dwarfs" in her household staff, which was common at the time, including the court jester Elisabet. Despite all her interests and a large court, she often complained of loneliness and boredom. Maria Eleonora never took an interest in politics or academic subjects. However, she is not considered to have been unintelligent: she spelled and wrote poorly, but this was not uncommon among women of that era.
