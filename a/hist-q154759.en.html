Sven Anders Hedin, born February 19, 1865, in Stockholm, and died November 26, 1952, in Stockholm, was a Swedish explorer, author, and member of the Swedish Academy. His significant expeditions in Central Asia made him perhaps the most famous Swede of his time. On July 1, 1902, he became the last person in Sweden to be ennobled. In 1913, he was elected a member of the Swedish Academy. During three extensive expeditions between 1893 and 1909, Hedin mapped vast areas of Tibet and other parts of Central Asia, which were previously blank spaces on the map, and solved the mystery of Lake Lop Nor. He mapped 170,000 square kilometers in Tibet alone. Despite using primitive methods to calculate distances, largely based on counting the number of steps his camel took, his maps later proved to contain only minor inaccuracies. Hedin was a strong supporter of Germany and viewed Russia with suspicion. He supported Germany during World War I and never wavered from this position, despite the changing political circumstances in Germany. Hedin's support for Hitler and Nazi Germany tarnished his reputation. Hedin never married and had no children, so his noble lineage died out with him.

== Biography ==

Sven Hedin was born in Stockholm, where his father, Ludvig Hedin, served as the city architect. His mother's name was Anna Berlin. The Hedin family has been known since the mid-17th century. The family's ancestor lived in the village of Hidingsta in the parish of Norrbyås, 1.5 miles south of Örebro. His son, Lars, adopted the surname Hedin after the village. Lars Hedin worked as a royal tax collector. Hedin's grandfather, Sven Anders Hedin, was a chancellor, and his great-grandfather, Sven Anders Hedin, was a court physician. His sister, Alma Hedin, a well-known philanthropist, sometimes served as his secretary.

Hedin studied at Stockholm University under Waldemar Brøgger and earned a Bachelor of Arts degree from Uppsala University in 1888, after studying geology, mineralogy, zoology, and Latin. From 1889 to 1890, he studied at the University of Berlin with Ferdinand von Richthofen. He then studied in Halle an der Saale with Alfred Kirchhoff and earned a doctorate in philosophy from the University of Halle in 1892.

After returning from his second Central Asian expedition, Hedin was ennobled on July 1, 1902, retaining his original name.

In 1905, he was elected a member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, in 1909 to the Military Science Academy, in 1910 to the Royal Physiographic Society in Lund, in 1913 to the Swedish Academy, and in 1922 to the Royal Scientific Society in Uppsala. In 1940, he became an honorary member of the Royal Society of Arts and Sciences in Gothenburg.

Hedin was involved in what is known as the "Strindberg Feud," when August Strindberg attacked a large part of the Swedish establishment in 1910. Strindberg questioned many of Hedin's discoveries in Asia and claimed that these had been made much earlier, arguing that Hedin was not the first Swede to travel through the Gobi Desert. An example was Gustaf Mannerheim, a Finnish-Swedish officer in the Russian army who led a 14,000 km expedition in Asia and China from 1906 to 1908. The conflict largely continued until Strindberg's death in the spring of 1912.

After his last expedition in 1935 and until his death, Hedin lived at Norr Mälarstrand 66 in Stockholm, where he had owned an apartment since 1931. The property owner decorated two walls near the entrance with maps of Asia. These maps were colored and raised in relief. Hedin is buried in the Adolf Fredriks Cemetery in Stockholm. Adolf Fredriks Church also has a memorial plaque dedicated to him.

Large archives related to Hedin are now held at the National Archives. Objects and photographs he brought back from his expeditions are managed by the Swedish Museum of World Culture.

== Travels and Expeditions == At the age of 15, Hedin witnessed the triumphant return to Stockholm of Adolf Erik Nordenskiöld and the crew of the Vega expedition after their journey through the Northeast Passage. He was deeply moved by the spectacle of thousands of people welcoming polar explorer Nordenskiöld's ship, "Vega," and its crew to Stockholm. This event inspired Hedin to decide that he would become an explorer.

=== First Journey in Persia === In May 1885, Hedin graduated from Beskowska School in Stockholm. He had been offered a position as a tutor in Baku for a Swedish boy whose father worked there as an engineer for the Nobel brothers, Ludvig and Robert. In August, he began his journey to Baku via Helsinki and St. Petersburg, traveling by train to Vladikavkaz and then by horse to Tbilisi before the final leg to Baku on the Caspian Sea. In Baku, Hedin began studying Asian languages. He had already learned German and English in school, but now he also studied Persian and Tatar. Later in life, he also learned Kyrgyz, Mongolian, Tibetan, and could understand Chinese to some extent. While in Baku, he made plans with a friend, Baki Khanov, to travel through Persia. In April 1886, they left Baku by boat for the northern coast of Persia and rode across the Elburz Mountains to Teh.
