Stenkil was king of Sweden from 1060 to 1066. He is described as a man who was very popular with the people, especially in Västergötland, and he was known for being an excellent archer.

Stenkil was Christian and actively worked to spread the religion. He helped build up the power of the church in Sigtuna. However, he was cautious about forcing changes, such as burning down old temples, because he wanted people to choose Christianity voluntarily.

In older accounts, he is described in various ways; some say that he was a large and heavy man who enjoyed drinking and being calm. After his death, there were conflicts over who would be the next king, but his lineage continued to rule for a long time.

Full text

Stenkil, born around 1030 and died around 1066, was King of Sweden from approximately 1060 to 1066. His name originates from the word "kittel" (meaning cauldron), not "kil" (wedge). His possible marriage to an unnamed daughter of King Emund the Old may have been crucial for his ascension to the throne.

== Biography == Stenkil's origins are unknown. The Westrogothic law code's royal genealogy states that Stenkil loved the people of Västergötland more than any other region in his kingdom, and that they were happy with him. The genealogy also says that he was a "mighty archer." The Hervarar saga recounts that during the reign of his predecessor, King Emund, Stenkil was a jarl (earl) in Svealand. He is described as a devout man and an advocate for Christianity, and to support missionary work in Svealand, he established a bishopric in Sigtuna. The Hervarar saga further states that he "died of illness in Svealand," approximately at the same time that the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada died in the Battle of Stamford Bridge in England in 1066.

From Adam of Bremen, we know that he belonged to the circle around King Emund the Old. However, Adam was uncertain about Stenkil's connection to Emund and wrote in his chronicle that he did not know whether Stenkil was Emund's nephew (syskonbarn) or possibly grandson, or a stepson. In another part of Adam's text, however, Stenkil is called Emund's nephew without any reservations.

Stenkil was reportedly the only person around Emund who supported Adalvard the Elder, sent from Hamburg-Bremen, when the king, influenced by Osmundus, opposed them. After Stenkil became king, he continued to support the Bremen bishopric.

When a new delegation of bishops led by Bishop Adalvard the Younger in Sigtuna (later also bishop in Skara) and Egino in Dalby attempted a few years later to try again to Christianize Uppland by burning the pagan temple in Uppsala, believing that the entire population would then convert to Christianity, Stenkil stopped them. Stenkil himself was a Christian but managed to persuade the bishops to abandon this plan, fearing that he would be overthrown and those who had converted would revert to paganism.

According to Snorre Sturluson's Heimskringla, Stenkil supported the Norwegian jarl (earl) Håkon Ivarsson (possibly identical to Håkan Röde) in his resistance against the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada, and entrusted the rule of Västergötland and Värmland to him. However, when fighting broke out in Västergötland between the jarl and King Harald, Stenkil was conspicuously absent.

Morkinskinna describes him as being fat, heavy, and fond of drinking, as well as enjoying peace and quiet in his own home. According to Knytlingasagan, he unsuccessfully attacked Sven Estridsson.

After Stenkil's death, Sweden was plagued by internal conflicts between two claimants to the throne, both named Erik. His most significant successors on the throne were his sons Halsten and Inge, and later two of his grandsons also ascended to the throne. This royal lineage is therefore called the Stenkil dynasty.
