In plain language

The government has proposed new laws that will come into effect on July 1, 2025. The purpose is to strengthen the protection for women and children who are experiencing violence or abuse. The aim is to make it easier to issue restraining orders, which are decisions that prohibit a person from contacting or approaching another person.

One of the major changes is that it will be possible to issue a restraining order even if someone is wrongly monitoring another person. The police should also be able to use restraining orders to protect those who are testifying in a trial. When assessing whether a person is dangerous, more factors than just previous convictions will be considered.

The proposal also means that a restraining order can apply to a larger area than it does currently. If a person has committed a serious crime in a close relationship, there should be a stronger expectation that a restraining order will be issued immediately. Furthermore, the penalties will be stricter for those who violate these rules or commit crimes against children.

To implement this, the government proposes changes to several laws, including the Criminal Code and the Cohabitation Act. These changes include providing more legal assistance to children and granting the police the right to search a home in order to issue a restraining order.

Full text

Contact bans are an important protective measure, primarily for women and children who are victims of violence and other abuses in close relationships. The legislation needs to be amended to make it possible to issue more contact bans, strengthen its crime prevention function, and provide real protection for those at risk.

The government proposes, among other things, the following:

  • Unreasonable surveillance should be introduced as a new basis for issuing a contact ban.
  • Contact bans should be able to be issued to protect victims and witnesses in legal proceedings.
  • Factors other than previous criminal activity should be given greater weight in the risk assessment.
  • A presumption for a contact ban should be introduced after serious offenses in close relationships.
  • A particularly extensive contact ban should be able to be issued as a first-line measure.
  • Expanded and particularly extensive contact bans should be able to cover larger geographical areas.
  • Searches should be permitted to enforce decisions regarding contact bans.
  • The right to legal assistance for children should be expanded.
  • The penalties for serious offenses against children and certain violations of contact bans should be increased.

The legislative changes are proposed to come into effect on July 1, 2025.
