(before ennoblement in 1757: Carl Linnaeus, Carolus Linnaeus), born May 13, 1707, in Råshult, Stenbrohults parish, Småland, and died January 10, 1778, in Uppsala, was a Swedish botanist, physician, geologist, and zoologist who laid the foundation for modern biological nomenclature and modern taxonomy, which groups plants and animals. Many of his writings were published in Latin, and therefore his Latin name is rendered as Carolus Linnaeus (Carolus a Linné after 1761).

Linnaeus was born in the Swedish countryside. He received most of his higher education at Uppsala University, where he began lecturing on botany in 1730. Between 1735 and 1738, he resided in the Netherlands to continue his studies. In 1735, he became a medical doctor in Harderwijk. For most of his time in the Netherlands, he worked as a physician at Hartecamp, near Haarlem, with George Clifford III as his patron. He published the first edition of Systema Naturae in the Netherlands. Linnaeus then returned to Sweden. From 1738 to 1741, he worked as a practicing physician in Stockholm. In June 1739, he founded the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences together with five other scientists. From 1741 to 1778, he served as a professor of medicine and botany at Uppsala University. In the 1740s, Linnaeus was sent on several expeditions throughout the country to collect and classify plants and animals. In the 1750s and 60s, he continued to collect and classify animals, plants, and minerals while also publishing numerous works. Linnaeus' scientific work is considered to have contributed to the foundations of modern ecology. At the time of his death, he had become one of Europe's most celebrated scientists. He is known as the father of modern taxonomy. The main part of Linnaeus' herbarium is preserved at the Linnean Society in London. In botany, the author abbreviation "L." is used to indicate Linnaeus as the authority for plant names. In older publications, the abbreviation "Linn." may sometimes be found. According to the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature, Linnaeus' remains are the lectotype for the species *Homo sapiens*, because Linnaeus' description of the species was based on himself.

Carl Linnaeus was born on May 13, 1707, in the parsonage of Råshult, in the parish of Stenbrohult in Småland, as the eldest son of the pastor and later vicar, Nils Linnaeus (originally Nils Ingemarsson), (1674–1748), and Christina Brodersonia (1688–1733).

His siblings were Anna Maria Linnæa, Sofia Juliana Linnæa, Samuel Linnæus, and Emerentia Linnæa. On his father's side, the family consisted of a series of farmers and priests, and Nils was an amateur botanist and pastor in Stenbrohult. Christina was the daughter of the parish vicar Samuel Broderzonius. Linnaeus' great-grandmother, Johanne Pedersdotter, was burned as a witch in Stavanger. One year after Linnaeus was born, his grandfather Samuel Broderzonius died, and his father succeeded him as vicar. The family moved to the parsonage of Stenbrohult, where his younger brother Samuel Linnaeus was born in 1718. From an early age, Linnaeus showed an interest in plants, especially flowers. He quickly learned the names of garden plants and soon received a plot of land from his father, where he could grow them. Conversations between father and son often revolved around the care of the family's garden, which was one of the best in Småland. Their relationship was warm and close. Linnaeus wrote about himself that "when the boy was restless and could not be calmed in any other way, he would immediately become quiet as soon as he was given a flower to hold." Linnaeus described the garden, which contained many foreign plants, as having "inspired my passion for herbs with an endless love." Linnaeus was born at a time when it was relatively common not to have surnames. His father's original name was Nils Ingemarsson, where Ingemarsson was a patronymic and not a family name. It was also common for those studying to become priests to adopt a more refined name, such as one that sounded Latin. Two of his father's uncles were simple farm boys, and when they began studying to become priests, they adopted the name Tiliander, inspired by a large linden tree growing on the edge of the farm where they had grown up in Jonsboda in the parish of Vittaryd in Småland. Linden is called *Tilia* in Latin. When Linnaeus' father was studying to become a priest, he followed his relatives' example and took his name after the linden tree on the farm, but chose to latinize the Swedish word "lind" (linden) and changed it to Linnæus. Thus, his son's name became Carl Nilsson Linnæus.

== Early Education == His father taught the young Linnaeus religion, geography, and basic Latin from an early age. When Linnaeus was seven years old, a tutor was hired. The parents chose Johan Telander, the son of a cavalry officer. Linnaeus did not like him. In his autobiography, he wrote that Telander "was more suited to suppress a child's talents than to develop them." After two years of private tutoring, he was sent to the elementary school in Växjö in 1716. Instead of studying, he would go out to the countryside to look for plants.
