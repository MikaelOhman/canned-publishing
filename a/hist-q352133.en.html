Anund Jakob ruled as King of Sweden from the year 1022 to approximately 1050. He was the son of Olof Skötkonung and reigned for a long period, making him one of the longest-reigning kings during the Viking Age. He had a double name to signify that he was a Christian king who was also accepted by the Swedish people.

During his reign as king, Anund Jakob was very active in foreign policy. He sent soldiers to help his relatives and neighbors in wars, including in Norway and against the Danish King Canute the Great. He also had coins minted in the city of Sigtuna, which served as a way to demonstrate his power and control over the region.

Anund Jakob was also known as "Kolbränna" (meaning "charcoal burner") because he was very strict when it came to law and order. If someone broke the law, he could have their house burned down to ashes. There is still a large stone bearing his name remaining in nature, which serves as a reminder of his time as king.

Full text

Anund Jacob (often referred to as "Anund Kolbränna" and sometimes as "Emund gamle" in the West Gothian law), born sometime between 1007 and 1009, according to Snorre Sturlasson on July 24th ("St. James' Day"), died around 1050, was King of Sweden from 1022. He was the son of Olof Skötkonung and Estrid. Adam of Bremen explained the double name by stating that while the Swedes accepted a Christian king, they did not want one with only a Christian name. Anund co-reigned with his father for two years before his father's death. He was a brother-in-law to both Jaroslav I of Kiev and Saint Olaf, and he provided them with military support. Anund Jacob was one of the longest-reigning monarchs in Sweden during the Viking Age and the Middle Ages, ruling for approximately 28 years.

== Biography == According to Snorre Sturlasson's Heimskringla, Anund Jacob may have ascended the throne during his father's lifetime, after the latter was deposed due to overly adventurous foreign policy. However, this account contains many unlikely elements, and other Icelandic sources state that he only became king upon his father's death.

Anund Jacob had ambitions in foreign policy and sent troops to support Saint Olaf of Norway in his struggle against the Danish King Canute the Great at the Battle of Helgeå, which Canute won. He also provided assistance during Olaf's unsuccessful attempt to regain the throne in 1030. Furthermore, he is said to have supported Olaf's son, Magnus the Good, during conflicts in Norway. During Anund Jacob's reign, a famous expedition eastward, known as the Ingvarståget, was organized. He also appears to have promoted the Hamburg-Bremen diocese, judging by the accounts of Adam of Bremen.

According to the Westrogothic Law (where he is called "Æmunðær colbrænnæ"), Anund Jacob maintained law and order with unusual strictness, including burning down the houses of those who broke the law. This earned him the nickname "Kolbränna" (meaning "coal burner").

Like his father, Anund Jacob minted coins bearing his name in Sigtuna, until around 1030. Towards the end of his reign, coins were also minted with the name Canute, which is sometimes interpreted as indicating that the Danish King Canute the Great declared himself king over "part of the Swedes" in 1027. However, it is usually considered that these coins were copies based on a Danish model; similarly, coins have been found attributed to Olaf Skötkonung that are actually English in origin. After Anund Jacob, Swedish minting ceased completely and was not resumed for another century. In any case, by 1029, Anund Jacob had control over the Swedes.

Near the old Frisegård farm near Annestad, about half a mile northeast of Främmestad, there is a standing stone approximately four meters high, known as "King Ane's Stone," which Birger Nerman associated with Anund Jacob.
