Eugénie, officially Eugenia Charlotte Eugenia Augusta Amalia Albertine of the House of Bernadotte, born April 24, 1830, at Stockholm Palace, and died April 23, 1889, in Stockholm, was a princess of Sweden and Norway. She was the daughter of King Oscar I and Queen Josephine. She was also known as a composer and artist. In her published works, she often used the pseudonym "E******" in various forms.

== Biography == Princess Eugénie, like her siblings, was raised under the supervision of Christina Ulrika Taube. She was reportedly strongly influenced by the court chaplain, Dr. J. G. Lundberg, and her lady-in-waiting, Karen Anker, both described as dominant personalities who may have contributed to Eugénie's own submissive nature.

As a child, she sometimes felt lonely and later said that she often wished she had been born a boy, like her brothers. During the royal children's childhood, one of their teachers contracted tuberculosis; however, he was not dismissed because the risk of infection was not understood at the time, and it is possible that several of the children's health deteriorated as a result.

Eugénie was very interested in art and is known for her drawings depicting life at court. One of her favorite subjects was her second-youngest brother, Gustaf, with whom she had a particularly close relationship. While she drew, he played music, and they often did this together, especially at Tullgarn Palace, where she would depict him playing music with the ladies-in-waiting. They corresponded even when they were only separated for a day, and during her brother's time as a student at Uppsala University, he wrote to her saying that he missed their correspondence even when they saw each other every day.

In 1852, much of the royal family became ill during a visit to Norway after spending time at a spa in Germany: "beloved brother" Prince Gustaf died of typhoid fever, and Eugénie's health deteriorated significantly afterward. During the winters, she was forced to stay in specially heated rooms in the palace. She suffered several bouts of pneumonia before developing tuberculosis, which required her to spend much of her time indoors. One of her doctors was Gabriel Branting, father of the later Prime Minister Hjalmar Branting. From the early 1860s onward, she spent summers at Villa Fridhem in Västerhejde on the island of Gotland, a house she had built after spending previous summers on the island since 1859.

Eugénie was unmarried and sickly throughout her adult life. She believed that this gave her the opportunity to live an independent life. When the Swedish Parliament decided in 1858 that adult, unmarried women could apply to be declared legally competent, she was one of the first women in the country to do so.

She was described as deeply religious and interested in various Christian denominations. She was also interested in painting, sculpting, and composing music; she composed pieces, wrote poems, modeled sculptures, and painted watercolors depicting the royal family's life during both ordinary days and celebrations.

She published a book titled "Swedish Princesses" and translated a book from German (1864), and participated in the Stockholm Art and Industry Exhibition in 1866. She founded a sewing circle and was friends with Lina Sandell, a hymn writer and theologian. However, her most remembered contributions were in social work.

As the only daughter, she inherited many of her mother, Queen Josephine's, jewels, which she later sold and donated the proceeds to charitable causes. However, she kept what became known as the "camée set" and bequeathed it in her will to her youngest nephew, Prince Eugen.

In the 1870s, she initiated the "Friends of the Lapp Mission," which later built a memorial church in her honor in Lannavaara. In 1882, she founded the "Nordic Society for the Prevention of Scientific Animal Cruelty."

Her own poor health made her interested in healthcare, and she had, among other things, Eugeniahemmet (Eugénie Home) built in Stockholm, a facility for terminally ill children. She also established children's homes and Gotland's home for the incurably sick in Visby. Princess Eugénie died peacefully at 00:45 on April 23, 1889, at Stockholm Palace. She is represented by a watercolor and some drawings at the National Museum in Stockholm.

== From the Will == The princess's servant received a lifetime pension. Prince Oscar Bernadotte inherited the property Villa Fridhem with its furnishings. The children's home at Fridtorp received a donation of 240,000 Swedish krona. Other donations: Eugeniahemmet: 20,000 krona The association "Friends of the Lapska Mission": 20,000 krona The City Mission in Visby: 12,000 krona Gotland's hospice for terminally ill patients: 20,000 krona The Nordic Association for combating scientific animal cruelty: 6,000 krona
