Full text

The Kylversten stone, with the identification mark G 88, is a runestone grave marker dating from the 4th century AD. This slab, originating from the parish of Stånga on Gotland, is now housed at the Swedish National Historical Museum in Stockholm. Its inventory number is 13436.

== The Grave Marker == The stone was discovered in an Iron Age burial ground near the farm Kylver in 1903, during an excavation led by headmaster Hans Hansson in Gamleby. The burial ground included a mound, a couple of standing stones, some cairns, and three to four grave chambers located close to the surface. Inside one of these chambers, which consisted of four roofless and floorless rooms, the stone was found in the middle compartment, facing a wall in the northeastern corner of the fire pit. The type of grave, along with a bronze fitting that remained from the previously looted grave goods, were crucial for dating the stone.

== The Inscription == The inscription is carved on the inside of a limestone slab that once belonged to a sarcophagus. It is therefore believed to have had some kind of magical function, possibly to bind the deceased to the grave. The Kylver Stone is one of the few runic inscriptions where the entire Elder Futhark alphabet is inscribed, in the order f u þ a r k g w h n i j p e/ï R/z s t b e m l ŋ d o. The Futhark is considered to be the oldest alphabet in the world. In addition to the Futhark, the palindrome "s u e u s" is inscribed (note that the 's' rune has a later form), a word believed to have magical significance, but the meaning of "sueus" is unknown. The inscription also forms the basis for Sigurd Agrell's now highly questioned Ur-Futhark theory.

At the end of the runic sequence, there is a symbol resembling a fir tree. This "fir tree" is undoubtedly a 'T' rune with six vertical strokes (bistavar) in repeating succession. Each rune in the Elder Futhark had its own name, and the rune 'T' represented the god Tyr. This rune with six double bistavar meant that when Tyr was invoked, the magical power was amplified six times.

It is also possible that the "fir tree" is a linden rune, a so-called branch rune, which shows the entire runic alphabet or serves as a key to deciphering runic ciphers. On a linden rune, the number of branches to the left of the central stroke indicates its family affiliation, while the number of branches to the right indicates the rune's order within that group. The "fir tree" has six branches on the left side and eight on the right, indicating families 1, 2, and 3 on the left side, and all eight runes from those families on the right side.

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary, native sources from the Viking Age – they were carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes the names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they describe what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some readings are uncertain – such readings are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
