Tightening of regulations regarding invasive alien species.

TL;DRThe government wants to make it more difficult to introduce species that damage the environment into Sweden. If someone intentionally or through gross negligence introduces such species, they may face fines or imprisonment.
In plain language

The government has proposed new laws to protect nature from invasive alien species. These are species that do not belong here and can harm our environment. The proposal means that Sweden will have its own list of species that are prohibited from being imported into the country.

If someone knowingly or through gross negligence imports such a species from another EU country, they may be punished. The penalty may be a fine or imprisonment of up to two years. It will also be a requirement to report to the Swedish Customs Agency when importing such a species from another EU country.

The Swedish Customs Agency, the authority that controls goods at the border, will be given more power. They will be able to ensure that everyone complies with the rules, and in some cases, they may take possession of animals or plants that are prohibited. This is to ensure that these dangerous species do not enter Sweden.

The new rules are intended to come into effect on May 1, 2026. The proposal involves changes to two different laws, namely the Environmental Code and the Internal Border Law.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes aimed at strengthening the regulations regarding invasive alien species in preparation for the introduction of a national list of such species. According to the proposal, anyone who intentionally or through gross negligence introduces an invasive alien species into Sweden from another EU country, in violation of a national import ban, may be sentenced to a fine or imprisonment of up to two years. Furthermore, the proposal states that anyone who imports such a species from another EU country must report the species to the Swedish Customs Administration. The Swedish Customs Administration will also have the authority to verify that the reporting obligation is fulfilled and that the import ban is observed, and in certain situations, to confiscate an invasive alien species.

The proposed amendments are scheduled to come into force on May 1, 2026.
