TL;DRThe government wants to amend aviation laws to make it easier to punish offenses that threaten safety. The proposal includes stricter regulations regarding disruptive passenger behavior and clearer definitions of what constitutes a crime.
In plain language

The government has proposed changes to the aviation law and the environmental code. The aim is to make it easier to impose sanctions, which refers to penalties or fines, when regulations within the aviation sector are not followed. This includes regulations originating from the EU.

A key proposal addresses the issue of public intoxication. It would allow individuals who work in critical safety roles at an airport, but not inside the aircraft itself, to be prosecuted for public intoxication if they are under the influence of alcohol. The proposal also aims to clarify what constitutes a serious offense in cases of reckless behavior in air traffic.

The government also wants to be able to mandate that fines be paid for violations of common EU regulations. Furthermore, they want to be able to determine who is responsible for reporting incidents that occur within the aviation sector. This is intended to help ensure the safety of air travel.

If the parliament approves the proposal, the new regulations will come into effect on January 1, 2026. The proposal also includes minor amendments to the environmental code, which is the law that protects our nature and environment.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes to the Aviation Act (2010:500) and the Environmental Code. The criminal provisions of the Aviation Act are proposed to also cover violations of regulations in EU regulations. Recklessness in air traffic that is not considered serious should be grounds for liability, and it will be clarified when a crime should be classified as serious. Individuals who perform a task of significant importance to aviation safety while under the influence of alcohol, outside of an aircraft, should, to a greater extent than under the current regulations, be convicted of being intoxicated while flying. The provisions regarding fines should be consolidated in the Aviation Act. The government should be authorized to issue regulations stating that anyone who violates a provision in an EU regulation with common provisions in the field of civil aviation should be subject to fines. Furthermore, the government should be able to delegate the authority to issue regulations regarding who is obligated to submit incident reports. A consequential amendment is proposed for the Environmental Code. According to the proposal, the amendments to the law should come into effect on January 1, 2026.
