Johan Henric Kellgren, born December 1, 1751 (according to church records) in the parish of Floby, Skaraborg County, and died April 20, 1795, in Stockholm, was a Swedish lyric poet, critic, newspaper editor, and docent since 1774 in Latin literature.

== Biography == Kellgren was the son of Jonas Kellgren, a pastor in Floby, Västergötland, and his wife, Christina Elisabeth von Wulff. With excellent grades from Skara gymnasium, where he studied for five years, he was sent to the Royal Academy in Åbo in 1768. In 1772, he earned a Master of Philosophy degree, and in 1774, he was appointed as a docent in literature. Kellgren was a member of the Aurora Society in Åbo, founded in 1770 – a Finnish branch of Utile Dulci – and anonymously published several poems in its journal (1773–1776). The poem "Mina Löjen" laid the foundation for his fame.

In 1777, he moved to Stockholm, where he had been hired as a private tutor in the household of Lieutenant General, later Field Marshal, Johan August Meijerfeldt. He now showcased his poetic talent with poems that appeared in Carl Christoffer Gjörwell's "Samlaren" and were awarded prizes by the Royal Academy of Literature, being included in its records ("En ynglings inträde i verlden"). On October 29, 1778, he began publishing the newspaper "Stockholms-Posten" with his friend, secretary Carl Peter Lenngren, becoming Sweden's first theater critic. He became known both as a poet and as a witty and sharp polemicist in the spirit of the Enlightenment.

Johan Henric Kellgren attracted the attention of King Gustav III by writing the prologue for a play performed at Drottningholm before the birth of the heir to the throne. As a result, he received an artist's pension from the king, which was further increased when he was first appointed as the royal librarian and then as the royal secretary. In 1777, Kellgren became both a member and secretary of the renowned literary society Utile Dulci. In 1785, he was appointed, along with Abraham Niclas Clewberg, as a royal censor. At the founding of the Swedish Academy in 1786, he was one of the thirteen members appointed by the king. Through a drawing of lots, he became its first director. He was elected member number 111 of the Royal Musical Academy on April 18, 1788.

Kellgren had a naturally weak constitution, which was further weakened by excessive work, and he died unmarried from consumption in Stockholm on April 20, 1795. In his last two years of life, he devoted himself – enduring great suffering – primarily to organizing his writings.

Among Kellgren's poems are love lyrics such as "Den nya skapelsen" and satires, for example, "Man äger ej snille för det man är galen" (1787) and "Dumboms lefverne" (1791), where the character "Salig Dumbom" is the main figure. It is believed that "Dumbom" refers to Per Enbom. Kellgren also wrote the preface to Bellman's "Fredmans epistlar," despite his previous aversion to him, which he expressed in the poem "Mina löjen" (1777).

Anacreon, where is your praise? Another has stolen your lyre and, quick in the intoxication of revelry, has learned to please Priapus. When he spoke jokingly about the "Swedish Homerus," who "snored in every line," he was referring to Erik Skjöldebrand.

In "The Enemies of Light," Kellgren argues for upholding Enlightenment ideals, even though "things are going wrong in the south," meaning that people have begun to abuse these ideas as a result of the French Revolution. He aligned himself philosophically with Voltaire and the French view of art.

Kellgren co-wrote the librettos for the operas *Gustaf Wasa* (1786) and *Gustaf Adolph and Ebba Brahe* (1788) with Gustav III. He also engaged in Sweden's first major literary feud with Thomas Thorild, between 1782 and 1792. During and after this conflict, his taste developed as he became acquainted with the writings of Jens Baggesen, Friedrich Gottlieb Klopstock, and he began to take an interest in literature from other countries besides France, particularly the newly emerging Danish literature.

Johan Henric Kellgren was buried in the cemetery of St. James' Church, where a memorial stone was later placed in the wall. Johan Tobias Sergel immortalized his features in a medallion.
