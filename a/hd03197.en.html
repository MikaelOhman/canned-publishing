TL;DRThe government wants to change the grading system in schools to make it more fair and to prevent grades from being too high. The new system will use both grades and national tests to demonstrate how well a student is performing.
In plain language

The government has proposed changes to the education law. The goal is to present students' knowledge in a more fair way and to prevent grades from being too high without students actually possessing the corresponding knowledge. The proposal introduces a new grading scale with ten steps, from 1 to 10, in primary and secondary education.

To determine a student's overall performance, a so-called "merit value" will be used. This value will be based on both students' grades and the results of new national standardized tests. These tests will be administered in primary school, special schools, secondary schools, and in adult education programs run by the municipalities. The tests are intended to be graded in a central location to ensure consistency for all students.

There are also new rules for admission to different programs and courses. To be eligible for a national program in secondary school, a minimum merit value of 4 is required. To obtain a secondary school diploma, a minimum average of 4 is required, along with a total of at least 2,250 points. When applying to higher education institutions, grades alone will no longer be the sole basis for consideration; the new merit value will be used instead.

Most of these changes are planned to take effect on July 1, 2028. However, for adult education programs run by the municipalities, a later date applies: January 1, 2031.

Full text

The proposal includes suggested amendments to the Education Act (2010:800), introducing a new, more equitable grading system and a new model for calculating merit points. The purpose is to more accurately reflect students' subject knowledge and counteract grade inflation. The proposals include the following:

  • A new model for calculating students' merit points will be developed, consisting of the applicant's grade point average and the results of national standardized tests.
  • A grading scale with ten levels, from 1 to 10, will be introduced in compulsory schools and in voluntary programs at the upper secondary level, without a strict passing grade. Teachers must have assessment discussions when assigning final grades in a subject. Municipalities must submit grade information to an authority.
  • National standardized tests will be introduced in primary school, special schools, upper secondary schools, and municipal adult education programs at the upper secondary level. The tests should be graded centrally.
  • To be eligible for a national program in upper secondary school, a student must have a merit point score of at least 4 from primary school or equivalent.
  • A high school diploma will be awarded if a student averages a grade of 4 or higher on at least 2,250 points, including a grade of 4 in the final project.
  • Merit points will replace grades as a selection criterion for applications to higher education.

The changes to the Education Act will primarily come into effect on July 1, 2028. However, for municipal adult education programs, the changes will come into effect on January 1, 2031.
