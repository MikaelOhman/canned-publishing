The decision to remove the content is directed at the owners of the platform, such as those who operate social media or other digital services. The owners of the service must remove the harmful content as quickly as possible, and no later than one hour after receiving notification. Full text

The recruitment of children and young people by criminal networks to commit serious crimes is a major social problem. Recruitment is increasingly taking place online and through the use of digital services. The government therefore proposes a new law that would allow a competent authority to issue orders requiring the removal or inaccessibility of recruitment content. An order for removal should be directed to the hosting service provider, i.e., the entity that provides the platform where the content is located. The content must be removed or made inaccessible as soon as possible, and no later than one hour from the time the hosting service provider receives the order. A penalty fee may be charged to a hosting service provider that violates its obligations under the law. The purpose of the law is to counter online recruitment, particularly of children and young people, into crime, and to combat the phenomenon of "crime as a service," where crimes are advertised digitally on a service market.

The new law and the proposed amendment are scheduled to come into effect on July 15, 2026.
