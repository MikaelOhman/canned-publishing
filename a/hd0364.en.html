TL;DRSweden aims to introduce new laws to protect individuals who speak out about important issues in society. The purpose is to prevent individuals or companies from using costly legal proceedings to silence critics.
In plain language

The government proposes new regulations that will come into effect on May 1, 2026. These regulations stem from a decision within the EU and aim to protect individuals participating in public discourse. They address so-called "abuse of legal proceedings," which occurs when someone initiates legal action solely to intimidate or prevent others from expressing their opinions.

To facilitate easier defense in these cases, the government proposes that courts should expedite proceedings. This may also involve requiring the party initiating the legal process to provide a security deposit, meaning funds set aside to cover costs if they lose the case. This measure aims to prevent costly lawsuits from being used as a weapon against journalists or others engaged in debate.

The government also intends to introduce a new type of penalty in court, known as a "default judgment sanction." This means that a court can make a decision against an individual or company if they fail to answer the court's questions or follow its instructions. This allows the legal process to proceed even if one party refuses to cooperate.

The proposal involves changes to several important laws, including the Code of Civil Procedure and the Code of Enforcement. The goal is to strengthen freedom of expression, which is the right to speak and disseminate information, and to ensure that legal disputes are handled efficiently.

Full text

This proposal outlines the legislative changes necessary to implement the European Parliament and Council Directive (EU) 2024/1069 on the protection of persons participating in public debate against manifestly unfounded claims or abuse of legal proceedings (strategic lawsuits to stifle public debate).

The proposal suggests introducing new provisions into Swedish law to comply with the directive's requirements regarding, among other things, swift proceedings, requiring security for legal costs, and the jurisdiction of courts in certain cases. These provisions are proposed to be included in a new law on the protection of participation in public debate, applicable in cross-border disputes. The proposal also suggests introducing a new default judgment sanction, which would allow a court, in certain cases, to issue a default judgment against a party that has failed to respond to court orders. These proposals aim to strengthen freedom of expression and access to information and contribute to a more efficient handling of legal disputes.

The proposed legislative changes are scheduled to come into effect on May 1, 2026.
