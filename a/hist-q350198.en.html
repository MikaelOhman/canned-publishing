Olof Rudbeck the Elder, born September 13 [source needed] 1630 in Västerås (baptized December 12), died September 17, 1702, in Uppsala, was a Swedish polymath.

Olof Rudbeck made the first significant Swedish medical discovery with the lymphatic vascular system, which earned him worldwide recognition at just 23 years old. When he was only 20 years old, he discovered the fine vessels that contain lymph and which were later named lymphatic vessels. This is considered the first major scientific discovery in Sweden. He also established a botanical garden near Svartbäcken in Uppsala, published a catalog of its plants, and took students from the classroom into nature.

In addition to medicine and botany, Rudbeck was knowledgeable in everything from astronomy to pyrotechnics, and he was also a skilled builder.

However, his name is also associated with his glorification and overly imaginative depiction of Sweden's greatness in the work "Atlantica," which portrayed Sweden as the dwelling place of the gods and the origin of all major peoples. This tradition of historical writing, known as Gothicism, Rudbeck was one of its most prominent advocates, and he pushed it further than anyone else.

== Early life and family == Olof Rudbeck was the son of Johannes Rudbeckius (the Elder), the bishop of Västerås, and his second wife, Magdalena Hising, and the father of, among others, Olof Rudbeck the Younger (1660–1740). He married Vendela Lohrman on June 24, 1655. She was the daughter of Tomas Lohrman, the city attorney in Uppsala, and Helena Gustafsdotter, and the sister of the architect and doctor Gustaf Lohrman, who was ennobled as Lohreman.

== Discoverer of the lymphatic system ==

Olof Rudbeck attended the school and gymnasium in Västerås before becoming a student at Uppsala University in 1648. There, he chose medicine as his major and attended lectures by Franks and Olaus Stenius. He researched the finer details of bodily structures and discovered the importance of the lymphatic system for blood circulation, a significant advancement for physiology. He called the lymphatic vessels "vasa serosa." It is possible that he had previously read Asellius's work on the same subject, "de lactibus," (the milk veins), published in 1627.

News of his discovery spread to the court, and during Queen Kristina's visit to Uppsala in April 1652, Rudbeck performed a dissection where he demonstrated the lymphatic vessels to the queen and several prominent figures in the kingdom. Shortly thereafter, he visited her in Stockholm and presented her with two drawings that he had created, depicting the lymphatic vessels.

On May 22, 1652, he defended his dissertation "De circulatione sanguinis" (On the Circulation of Blood) before Olaus Stenius, where he developed Harvey's then-little-known theory of blood circulation based on his own experiments. At the beginning of summer 1653, he published "Nova exercitatio anatomica exhibens ductus hepaticos aquosos et vasa glandularum serosa" (A New Anatomical Study Exhibiting Hepatic Ducts and Lymphatic Vessels of Glands), in which he presented his findings on the lymphatic vessel system, based on dissections of more than 400 animal bodies.

His desire to continue his studies in the Netherlands was heard by those in power. The Queen granted him 800 Riksdaler (Swedish currency), Axel Oxenstierna gave him 200, and the former promised him a professorship upon his return. In June 1653, he embarked on the journey and spent most of his time in Leiden, where he attended lectures by van Hoorne and Hemsterhuis.

During his stay, he defended his discovery of the lymphatic vessels against the Dane Thomas Bartholin, who claimed to have preceded him with the discovery. This dispute, in which Rudbeck participated with two writings, lasted for a long time and was eventually resolved in Rudbeck's favor.

== The Botanical Garden is Founded == Rudbeck's reputation was already so well-established that he received several attractive offers, but a professorship in Uppsala was his ultimate goal. He returned home in July 1654, bringing with him models, drawings, and most importantly, seeds and plants. In the Netherlands, anatomical studies, his first love, had been challenged by botany, a field to which he would dedicate himself with passionate devotion from that point forward.

The immediate result of his journey was the establishment of a botanical garden in Uppsala, a desire of the university that Rudbeck had already proposed to Axel Oxenstierna before his trip abroad. He found strong support in Oxenstierna's successor as university chancellor, Magnus Gabriel De la Gardie, who appointed him as an adjunct professor of medicine in 1655 and arranged for an annual grant for the garden in the new budget that same year. Thus, Uppsala Botanical Garden could be inaugurated that very year, becoming the first of its kind in the country. Several plants were cultivated there for the first time in Sweden, including potatoes.

Through significant personal sacrifices, Rudbeck brought the garden to a point where he was able to publish his first "Catalogus plantarum" (a catalog of all the species in the botanical garden) as early as 1658; four years later, he generously donated the entire establishment to the university.
