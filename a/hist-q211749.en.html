Full text

Desideria, born Bernhardine Eugénie Désirée Clary on November 8, 1777, in Marseille, and died on December 17, 1860, in Stockholm, was Princess of Pontecorvo from 1806 and Queen of Sweden and Norway during the Swedish-Norwegian union from 1818 to 1844. She was married to Jean Baptiste Bernadotte, later King Charles XIV John, and mother of Oscar I. Upon her arrival in Sweden, her name was officially translated to Desideria, a name she herself did not use.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Désirée Clary was born as the youngest of nine children to François Clary (1725–1794) and his wife, Françoise Rose Somis (1737–1815), daughter of an army captain. The Somis family came from Pinerolo in Turin, Piedmont. Her father, a wealthy silk merchant in Marseille – he exported French manufactured goods and imported products from Italy and the Orient – made his family home on what is now Rue des Phocéens available as a gathering place for the Bonaparte brothers who had been driven out of Corsica. The Clary family can be traced back to a Jacques Clary from Marseille, married in 1690 and Désirée's great-grandfather. She, like her sister Julie Clary, attended a convent school, but both were brought home by their father when the revolution broke out in 1789. They then received their education at home after French convent schools were abolished the following year.

After her father's death in 1794, it came to the authorities' attention that he had applied for nobility before the revolution. This led to the arrest of her older brother and guardian, Etienne, in place of her father, and he risked execution. However, because Etienne had a friendship with Robespierre's younger brother, Augustin Robespierre, he was released but soon arrested again. This time, Etienne's wife approached the representative Albitte to plead for her husband's life. She brought Désirée along, but the young girl fell asleep in the waiting room. Etienne's wife didn't want to wake her and then forgot about her when she succeeded in her request and immediately went to meet her husband, who was now free. Désirée was later found sleeping in the waiting room by a certain Joseph Bonaparte, who gentlemanly escorted her home; and that same year, they became engaged. Through Joseph, Désirée met his younger brother – Napoleon. He soon developed an affection for the younger of the Clary sisters, Désirée, and suggested that she instead become engaged to him, with Joseph marrying her sister Julie, a proposal that all involved found agreeable.

Julie married Joseph Bonaparte in 1794; he would later become King of Naples and King of Spain. Both families hoped for a marriage between Napoleon and Désirée as well, and the two became engaged that same year. From 1795 to 1797, Désirée lived with her sister and brother-in-law in Genoa, where Joseph had a diplomatic mission, and they lived their social lives within the Bonaparte family circle. However, during the autumn of 1795, Désirée learned that Napoleon had fallen in love with someone else. When he informed her of his intention to break off the engagement in a letter on September 6th of the same year, Désirée released him from their marriage vows. Instead, Napoleon married Joséphine de Beauharnais on March 9, 1796. Nevertheless, Napoleon and Désirée remained friends. She had a loyal ally in him who, as a successful military leader and politician, ensured that she received privileges. In turn, she played an important mediating role between the future emperor and her future husband, Jean Baptiste Bernadotte.

=== Marriage === In 1797, Désirée moved to Rome with her sister Julie and Julie's husband, where Joseph was the ambassador. She became engaged to General Leonard Duphot there. The engagement was more or less arranged by Napoleon, who wanted to arrange a suitable marriage for her. Duphot already had a partner and an illegitimate four-year-old son, but Désirée's dowry and her status as Napoleon's sister-in-law made her an asset for his career. Désirée herself was reportedly not enthusiastic about the plans, but she experienced some pressure and seemed to have given in to her family's wishes, eventually agreeing. The wedding was scheduled for December 30, 1797. However, two days before the planned ceremony, an anti-French uprising broke out in Rome outside Palazzo Corsini, where they lived. In the unrest, Désirée's fiancé, Duphot, was killed. Désirée then settled in Paris with Julie and Joseph, where she mingled with the Bonaparte family, who still liked her. They blamed Napoleon for breaking his engagement to her in favor of Josephine, whom they disliked. Désirée also expressed negative opinions about Josephine, describing her as a cunning older courtesan with a well-deserved bad reputation, but she reportedly did not show the same hostility that Napoleon's family openly displayed.

During this time, Désirée received a marriage proposal from General Junot, but she declined because he only made his request through an intermediary. In Paris, she then met the French general Jean Baptiste Bernadotte. They fell in love and soon became engaged, and their wedding took place on August 17, 1798.
