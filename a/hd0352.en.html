Tax liability for representatives – new rules regarding exemptions and scope of responsibility.

Source Riksdagen Read the original at Riksdagen →2025-11-25
TL;DRNew regulations are being proposed to protect individuals who lead companies from being personally liable for paying the company's outstanding taxes. It will be easier to avoid having to pay if it feels unfair, and individuals will have more time to resolve the situation.
In plain language

The government wants to amend the law on tax procedures. Currently, individuals who represent a company, such as a CEO or board member, can be held personally liable for paying the company's taxes and fees if the company fails to do so. The new rules clarify when an individual may be exempt from this personal liability if it is deemed unfair.

The proposal also introduces a mechanism called "temporary relief." This means that an individual who is at risk of having to pay the company's taxes can apply for a temporary postponement of the payment decision. If temporary relief is granted, the individual receives an additional two months from the original payment deadline.

During these two months, the deadline for the Swedish Tax Agency to decide whether the individual must pay is extended. This gives the individual an opportunity to address the situation before a final decision is made. The purpose is to create more predictability for those who lead legal entities, which is a formal term for companies or organizations.

If the proposal is approved by the Parliament, the new rules are planned to come into effect on June 30, 2026. The proposal also includes amendments to other laws related to the control of certain goods and regulations regarding confidentiality, i.e., what can and cannot be disclosed.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes to the rules regarding representative liability in the tax procedure law (2011:1244). The provision regarding exemption from payment obligations is amended in the proposal such that a representative should be fully or partially exempt from the payment obligation if it is unfair for the representative to be liable for the tax or fee of the legal entity. The exemption ground is clarified through a number of circumstances that should be particularly considered when assessing whether the payment obligation is unfair. A provision for a grace period is also introduced. A representative who may become liable for the tax or fee of a legal entity should be able to apply for a grace period of two months from the original payment deadline for the tax or fee covered by the application, and the payment should not become due until the end of the grace period. The grace period means that the assessment of the representative's obligation to personally be liable for the legal entity's unpaid taxes and fees is postponed from the original due date of the tax or fee to the time when the grace period expires. The amendments to the law are proposed to come into force on June 30, 2026.

The Government's proposal:

  2. The Parliament adopts the Government's proposal for an amendment to the law (1998:506) on the transfer and control of certain goods subject to excise tax.
  3. The Parliament adopts the Government's proposal for an amendment to the public access and secrecy law (2009:400).
