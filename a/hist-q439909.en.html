Carl Gustaf Wrangel, born December 13, 1613 (according to parish records) at the old estate of Skokloster Castle, and died on June 25, 1676, at Spyker Castle in Swedish Rügen, was a Swedish count, military officer, major general in 1638, member of the Council of State in 1646, field marshal in 1646, governor-general of Pomerania in 1648, and Lord Marshal in 1664. He was the son of Herman Wrangel and Margareta (daughter of Moritz) Grip, half-brother to Wolmar Wrangel af Lindeberg, and married to Anna Margareta von Haugwitz. Wrangel had Skokloster Castle built in Uppland, Gripenbergs Castle in Småland, and Wrangelska Palace in Stockholm.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years ===

Wrangel was born in 1613 at the old estate of Skokloster. He initially received his education at home, and then from 1625 to 1626 at Collegium Skytteanum in Stockholm. At the age of 14, he accompanied his father to the war in Prussia, and after returning to Sweden in 1630, he spent a couple of years on an educational journey to Leiden, where he studied shipbuilding and navigation, and to Paris. At the age of 19, he joined the Swedish troops in Germany and participated in the Battle of Lützen in 1632.

=== Career ===

Wrangel was appointed lieutenant colonel in Bengt Bagge's recruited infantry regiment in Elbingen in 1633. In 1634, he became a lieutenant colonel in Joachim Moltke's recruited cavalry regiment, stationed in Pomerania, but joined Johan Banér's army in 1635 and distinguished himself in the Battle of Lüdershausen. He participated in subsequent battles, was promoted to colonel in the Livregementet till häst (Royal Horse Regiment) in 1636, and accompanied the army during operations against Torgau that same year. There, he came into conflict with Banér and returned to Vorpommern, where he served under his father's command in defending the province. In 1638, despite Johan Banér's objections, he was promoted to major general and nominally appointed commander of the Dalregementet (Dalarna Regiment). Shortly after, he returned to Sweden to oversee recruitment but soon returned to Germany and participated in battles in the following years. After Johan Banér's death in 1641, he was one of four major generals who led the Swedish forces until Lennart Torstenson became supreme commander. In 1642, he went to Sweden, from where he brought reinforcements back to Lennart Torstenson's army in Germany and subsequently served as his chief of staff. He made a significant contribution in the Battle of Leipzig, participated in the attack on Denmark in 1643, and after Claes Fleming's death in 1644, took command of the fleet stationed in Kiel. He successfully led the fleet out of the Kiel Bay and defeated the Danish fleet in the naval battle at Femern on October 13th, and in 1645, he was appointed commander-in-chief of the entire Swedish fleet.

The same year, he was appointed national treasurer but in 1646, he took command of the Swedish forces in Germany, initially under the supervision of Lennart Torstenson. That same year, he became a field marshal, governor general of Pomerania, and member of the council of state. Together with Henri de la Tour d'Auvergne de Turenne, he operated in Bavaria but in 1647, he advanced alone towards Bohemia, demonstrating a cautious but well-planned strategy. In 1648, he reconnected with Turenne and defeated the imperial forces in the Battle of Zusmarshausen. The same year, Charles X Gustav replaced him as commander-in-chief in Germany, but he remained governor general of Pomerania until his death.

When Wrangel captured Fredriksodde in 1657, he was rewarded with the title of national admiral and subsequently led the right wing of the army during the crossing of the Little Belt in 1658. Wrangel also played a crucial role in the Battle of Öresund.

Wrangel's successes extended beyond the military, particularly during the 1650s and 1660s. In 1648, he became chancellor of the University of Greifswald. In 1651, he was granted the title of Count over Salmis in the Keksholms län region of then-Eastern Finland. The county was later transferred to Sölvesborg in Blekinge in 1665. With these military and civil appointments, Wrangel's need to establish a stately field marshal's court grew, which could practically correspond to his status in the great powers of Sweden and Europe. Wrangel and his wife dedicated significant resources to creating an appropriate environment for representation by acquiring art, handicrafts, textiles, books, and weapons from Europe and other parts of the world. The construction of Skoklosters Castle, which began in 1654, was an important part of the effort to demonstrate power and immortalize the family's memory. Wrangel's motto reflects these same ideas: "Non est mortale quod opto" – "It is not the perishable that I seek."

=== The Later Years === In the later years of his life, Wrangel suffered from poor health, which prevented him from participating in military campaigns in Germany. Lorenzo Magalotti described him during a meeting in 1674: "Field Marshal Count Carl Gustaf Wrangel is tall and has a handsome but proud appearance. Gout and kidney stones have severely affected him, and his condition is such that he is almost unable to continue leading troops in war due to the frailty of his body. He risks suffering a stroke at any time. His poor health makes him think about death and the afterlife, and he increasingly leaves matters in the hands of the clergy." The Swedish defeat at Fehrbellin in 1675 had significant consequences for Sweden.
