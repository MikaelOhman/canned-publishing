TL;DRGovernment spending on social security programs, such as child benefits and sick pay, represents a smaller portion of Sweden's economy than it has in recent years. This is due to the fact that fewer children are being born and that fewer people are taking sick leave from their jobs.
In plain language

In the year 2025, a total of 255.5 billion Swedish kronor was paid out through the social security system. This represents 3.9 percent of the country's total economy, which is the lowest level since 1980. While some costs are increasing due to higher prices in society, the overall costs for many other types of support are decreasing.

One reason for the decrease in costs is that fewer children are being born, resulting in less money being allocated to child benefits and parental leave. Additionally, expenditures on sick pay have decreased because fewer people are taking sick leave from their jobs.

The social security system is managed by Försäkringskassan and provides financial support to citizens. It includes payments for families with children, individuals who are ill, and those in need of housing assistance. Currently, the costs associated with housing assistance are also decreasing as a temporary increase implemented during the pandemic has been removed.

June 9, 2026

In 2025, social security payments amounted to 3.9 percent of GDP. This is the lowest figure since 1980. "Despite inflation increasing the costs for many benefits, overall spending has decreased, partly because fewer children are being born and sickness absence is decreasing," says Ulrik Lidwall, an analyst at the Swedish Social Insurance Agency.

In 2025, a total of SEK 255.5 billion was paid out from social security, which represents 3.9 percent of GDP, and is the lowest figure since 1980. The expenses are largely unchanged between 2024 and 2025, but because the economy and GDP have grown during those years, the share of social security in GDP has decreased. This information comes from the Swedish Social Insurance Agency's annual publication "Social Security in Figures," which is being released today.

"The proportion of social security in the Swedish economy is influenced by political decisions and changes in the economy and population. For example, expenses for certain benefits increase when inflation raises the base amount," says Ulrik Lidwall. "This applies to parental leave, sick pay, and disability allowances."

"At the same time, we see that expenses for child benefit and temporary parental leave are decreasing as fewer children are born. We also see a decrease in expenses related to sickness absence and sick pay. Expenses for housing benefits are also decreasing because fewer people receive them, and the temporary increase during the pandemic is being phased out," says Ulrik Lidwall.

This annual publication summarizes and describes the benefits included in the Swedish social security system and administered by the Swedish Social Insurance Agency.
