In plain language

The government has proposed a plan to make it easier to conduct legal proceedings in Sweden. The goal is to make the courts more efficient and to reduce the burden on victims and witnesses involved in trials.

A key part of the proposal is to allow statements made during initial interrogations or in witness testimonies to be used as evidence in criminal cases more readily than is currently possible. This could help to uncover the truth without requiring everyone to recount the same information multiple times.

The government also wants to remove certain rules in the appellate courts and the Supreme Court, known as "rules of evidence." In addition, a new rule regarding confidentiality is proposed, meaning that certain information held by the Swedish Judicial Administration must be protected to prevent its dissemination.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposal, the new rules will come into effect on July 1, 2026. The proposal involves changes to several laws, including the Code of Judicial Procedure and the Public Access and Secrecy Act.

Full text

This proposal includes suggestions aimed at strengthening legal certainty, increasing the efficiency of the judicial process, and reducing the burden on victims and witnesses participating in legal proceedings. The government proposes, among other things, the following:

  • Statements made during initial interrogations and witness testimonies should be admissible as evidence in criminal trials to a greater extent than currently.
  • The so-called "rules of deference" in the Court of Appeal and the Supreme Court should be abolished.
  • A new confidentiality provision to protect information about coercive measures held by the Swedish Judicial Administration should be introduced.

The proposed amendments to the law are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2026.
