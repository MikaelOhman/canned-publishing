In plain language

Many people in Sweden are currently struggling to pay off their loans. This is partly because it has become much easier to quickly obtain short-term loans online. These loans often have very high interest rates, making borrowing money expensive.

There are companies known as consumer credit institutions. These companies are not traditional banks and, therefore, have lower requirements regarding the amount of capital they need to hold in reserve. The people who borrow money from these institutions are often young people with low incomes. These individuals are more likely to receive reminders for unpaid bills and are more likely to end up with the debt collection agency, Kronofogden, which is the government agency responsible for debt collection.

To improve the situation, the government is proposing new laws. The aim is to repeal an old law and instead ensure that only approved credit institutions, which function as banks, are allowed to offer loans to individuals. This is intended to make borrowing money safer and create the same rules for everyone who offers credit.

The new regulations are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2025. The government has sent the proposal to the Parliament (Riksdagen) for them to decide whether the legislative changes should be implemented. The proposal also includes changes to several other laws related to banking activities and how money is monitored to prevent crime.

Full text

A contributing factor to the problem of over-indebtedness among some Swedish households is the rapid growth of the consumer loan market in recent years. This trend is driven by technological advancements. Easier access to loans online has increased in recent years, contributing to unhealthy consumption patterns.

Consumer credit institutions are companies that operate in the business of providing consumer credit and have lower requirements for capital and organization than banks. Consumer credit institutions' loans differ from those offered by banks in several ways. They typically have significantly higher interest rates than banks. Their borrowers are largely young people with low incomes. Furthermore, borrowers from consumer credit institutions are much more likely than those from banks to receive payment reminders, debt collection demands, and have debts registered with the Swedish Enforcement Agency.

To strengthen consumer protection, combat over-indebtedness, and create a more level playing field for actors in the consumer credit market, the proposal suggests:

  • The law on certain activities related to consumer credit should be repealed.
  • Only a Swedish or foreign credit institution should be allowed to operate a business with the purpose of providing or brokering credit to consumers.

The proposed legislative changes are scheduled to come into effect on July 1, 2025.

Proposal for a Parliamentary Decision

The government's proposal:

  1. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law repealing the Act (2014:275) concerning certain activities involving consumer credit.
  2. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Credit Reporting Act (1973:1173).
  3. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (1992:160) concerning foreign branches, etc.
  4. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (2004:297) concerning banking and financial services.
  5. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (2009:400) on public access and secrecy.
  6. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (2016:1024) concerning activities involving mortgage credit.
  7. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (2023:714) concerning the acquisition and management of distressed credit agreements.
  8. The Parliament shall adopt the government's proposed law amending the Act (2025:000) amending the Act (2017:630) concerning measures to counter money laundering and the financing of terrorism.
