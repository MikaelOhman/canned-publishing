Full text

Birka was a Swedish Viking-era trading center and settlement located in Lake Mälaren, established in the mid-8th century and inhabited until the end of the 9th century. This settlement, which grew over time, is often referred to as "Sweden's first town." These types of concentrated settlements are sometimes called Viking towns.

Birka primarily served as an important trading center on the island of Björkö in Lake Mälaren, within the municipality of Ekerö. Together with the nearby Hovgården on Adelsö, which includes a royal estate, Birka has been designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

== Etymology == The first part of the name Birka is believed to be identical to the tree name birch, in Old Norse: *biork*, *biark*, *biærk*, *birk* and others, and is therefore a derivative, meaning something like "Birka" or possibly a modification of an existing "Björkö" (*Birkau?), which is still the name of the place today. Previously, it was believed that the name had a connection to *biärk*, which instead relates to trade.

== History ==

=== 8th Century: The Founding of Birka === Birka was an important trading center in the Svea kingdom during the late Vendel era and the Viking Age, and it can arguably be considered Sweden's first city. Since the Middle Ages, Björkö island in Lake Mälaren, located in the parish of Adelsö, Uppland, has been identified as this trading place. The only written source from the time when Birka existed is Archbishop Rimbert's "Vita Anskarii," dating to around 870, which is a biography of Ansgar. Ansgar himself visited Birka twice. In Ansgar's biography, Birka is mentioned as "Birca," believed to be a Latinization of the Old Norse name "Bjǫrkey" – Björkö. Birka is also mentioned by Adam of Bremen around 1070, but at that time the place was deserted. The urban layout of Björkö shows strong similarities to Hedeby in present-day northern Germany, but parallels can also be found with the Norwegian settlement of Kaupang.

It is unknown which prehistoric king had Birka established around the year 750 and what extent of power he held. A royal estate, Hovgården, was built on nearby Adelsö, from where the king could oversee the trade. It is estimated that around 700-1000 people lived in Birka when the city was at its peak. Merchants from near and far sought out the city with valuable goods for trade. Initially, goods came from Arab countries, later from Southern and Western Europe. The transactions involved barter, where domestic goods such as iron, furs, horns, and amber were exchanged for silver coins. These coins were usually melted down and made into jewelry, in addition to exclusive luxury items like silk, glass, and spices being traded.

Today, there are approximately 3000 visible graves on Björkö. Several different types of graves exist, containing either cremation or skeletal burials. The rich finds from the excavations reveal a society with a strong hierarchy and significant differences between social classes and genders.

=== 9th Century: Christian Mission ===

In the year 829, the Benedictine monk Ansgar was sent to what is now Sweden because the Svea king Björn had requested a Christian missionary. Upon arrival, Ansgar first went to the royal estate at Hovgården on Adelsö to obtain the king's permission to work in Birka. There, Ansgar founded a Christian community. In 832, Gautbert, a relative of Ebo, was appointed as the mission bishop for the Svea realm and founded a church in Birka. The number of believers reportedly increased during Gautbert's early tenure, but the pagan population forced Gautbert to flee the kingdom. In 852, Ansgar returned and found his community dissolved, but he re-established it and built a chapel on land donated by King Olof. Two church bells were sent to Birka by the abbot of Fulda (who died in 856), but it is unknown whether these were ever used. Erimbert, Gautbert's nephew, took over the missionary work, which was limited to Birka and ceased after Ansgar's death.

The presence of cross-shaped jewelry in graves indicates that some people were Christian until their deaths, but the Svea people also continued to worship their old Norse gods. Ansgar's contribution to the Christianization of Sweden was not lasting. When Archbishop Unni of Bremen arrived in Birka in 936, he found no trace of the mission. The bishop died during his visit. According to Adam of Bremen, King Ring was then the Svea king. Based on his writings, some historians have concluded that the kings of Birka were no longer residing on Adelsö at the time of King Ring.

As a memorial to Ansgar and his missionary work, the large stone cross, known as the Ansgarkorset, was erected in 1834 on the hilltop within the old fortress on Björkö. The design, resembling a Celtic cross, is considered misleading because it associates it with missionaries from the British Isles, who were Ansgar's "competitors" in Christianizing Scandinavia.

=== 10th Century: Birka is Abandoned === Birka was abandoned at the end of the 10th century for reasons that remain unknown. There are several theories about why this happened: one theory suggests that trade shifted to Sigtuna, while another proposes that Birka became isolated due to land uplift. This means that boats from the Baltic Sea could no longer reach Birka through the inlet near present-day Södertälje, a viewpoint that contradicts the common theory of a relocation to Sigtuna.
