Nelly Sachs, born Leonie Sachs on December 10, 1891, in Schöneberg, Berlin, and died on May 12, 1970, in Stockholm, was a German-Swedish author of Jewish origin who settled in Sweden in 1940. She was an author who, through modernist prose and poetry, depicted the Jewish experience of oppression under Nazism. In 1966, she was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature.

== Biography ==

=== Childhood === Nelly Sachs grew up in Berlin, in a wealthy Jewish family, as the only daughter of Margarete Sachs and the manufacturer William Sachs. Her widowed grandmother also lived in the home, so two generations of women shaped Nelly Sachs' upbringing. As a young girl, Sachs turned to her father, who had artistic interests and could play the piano for his dancing daughter. She came to view dance as her "innermost element," which would continue to influence her poetic work in many ways. The popular Berlin dancer Niddy Impekoven was an important source of inspiration. Between 1897 and 1900, Sachs studied at the Dorotheen-Schule in Berlin. From 1903 until 1908, she continued her studies at the Privatschule von Helene Aubert, a private school in the German capital.

=== Early Writing ===

Nelly Sachs' literary ambitions emerged early in her life, and she sought guidance from writers like Selma Lagerlöf and Hermann Hesse to help her with her writing. She debuted not as a poet, but as a prose writer in 1921 with the collection "Legenden und Erzählungen," which was characterized by a medieval worldview with strong elements of myth and folklore. From 1910, she also wrote sonnets, which she sent to Selma Lagerlöf for evaluation in 1923. However, it wasn't until 1929 that she decided to publish her first poems in the Berlin newspaper "Vossische Zeitung." Following her father's death the next year, a series of poems followed, in which she explored her grief. Approximately 300 poems were written before her exile to Sweden, some of which were published in German newspapers.

Later, Sachs completely distanced herself from this early work and insisted that her true debut occurred in exile with "In den Wohnungen des Todes" in 1947. When the literary scholar Walter Berendsohn compiled her bibliography for her 75th birthday in 1966, she explicitly requested that "all of her work before 1940 be excluded." Therefore, this material has only received greater attention from researchers in recent years.

=== Life in Nazi Germany ===

Nelly Sachs grew up in a secular environment, in a family that was assimilated into German society and had little connection to specific Jewish traditions. However, after the Nazis seized power in Germany in 1933, the situation for Jews in the country became increasingly precarious, and early works by Nelly Sachs were burned during book burnings throughout Germany in May 1933.

The short, dense prose text "Leben unter Bedrohung" (Life Under Threat), written after her escape to Sweden, provides a chilling picture of life in Berlin under terror. The experiences depicted likely stem from visits by SA and SS soldiers during the forced sale of the house on Lessingstraße in 1939. For a time, Sachs lost her ability to speak. "For five days, I lived speechless through a witch trial. My voice had fled to the fish. It fled, without regard for the other limbs, which stood in terror. The voice fled because it no longer had an answer, and 'speaking' was forbidden." After these experiences, her dreamy worldview, full of melancholy and otherworldly longing, became impossible to maintain. Her identity as a human being and poet was fundamentally changing, and one outward sign was that, starting on January 1, 1939, like all Jewish women in Germany, she was forced to adopt the name Sara. Ultimately, escape seemed like the only way out.

=== Flight to Sweden, 1940s === In June-July 1939, Gudrun Harlan, a friend of Nelly Sachs, traveled to Sweden to persuade the elderly Selma Lagerlöf to write a letter of recommendation to the Swedish government, requesting that Sachs and her mother be granted permission to reside in Sweden. The short sentence that would become the refugees' salvation read: "It is important to me that Miss Sachs be received in Sweden." On May 16th, mother and daughter flew on one of the last flights from Tempelhof Airport in Berlin to Bromma Airport in Stockholm. They brought with them five marks, a thermos of fennel tea for the ailing mother, and a brown suitcase filled with clothes, manuscripts, photographs, and a few personal items such as the father's medals, a music box, and Nelly Sachs' collection of bookmarks. All indications suggest that she narrowly escaped certain death at the last possible moment.
