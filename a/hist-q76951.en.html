Full text

George de Hevesy, originally named György Hevesy, was born on August 1, 1885, in Budapest, and died on July 5, 1966, in Freiburg im Breisgau. He was a Hungarian-Swedish chemist who received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1943. He developed methods for tracing metabolic processes in living organisms using radioactive isotopes of elements naturally present in those organisms. This made him one of the founders of radiochemistry.

Together with Dirk Coster, he discovered the element hafnium in Copenhagen in 1923. From 1926 to 1934, he was a professor at the University of Freiburg. After the Nazi seizure of power, he fled to Copenhagen, where he worked at the Institute of Theoretical Physics under the direction of Niels Bohr. The institute had become a refuge for physicists of Jewish origin, and German physicists Max von Laue and James Franck had deposited their Nobel Prize medals there so that the Nazis would not confiscate them. When Germany invaded Denmark in 1940, de Hevesy dissolved the medals in aqua regia (nitric acid and hydrochloric acid), preventing their discovery. After the war, the gold was extracted and used to create replacement medals. De Hevesy himself fled to Sweden in 1943 and worked at Stockholm University until 1961. In 1942, he was elected as foreign member number 806 of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, and later transferred as member number 965 among the Swedish members.

== Legacy == The asteroid 10444 de Hevesy is named after him.
