Gustav III, born January 13 (O.S.) / January 24 (N.S.) 1746 at Kungshuset on Riddarholmen in Stockholm, and died March 29, 1792 at Stockholm Palace, was King of Sweden from 1771 until his death.

He was the son of Adolf Frederick and Louisa Ulrika, brother to Charles XIII, father to Gustav IV Adolph, and cousin to Catherine II of Russia. Due to his great interest in culture, he is sometimes called the "Theatre King." He founded institutions such as the Royal Swedish Academy of Music and the Swedish Academy.

In 1772, he carried out a coup d'état (a state coup, or as he himself called it: a revolution) during which members of the Council of State were imprisoned. The coup was carried out with the help of military troops, and Sweden's first political parties, the Hats and the Caps, were dissolved. This put an end to the party conflicts and corruption that had previously characterized Swedish foreign and domestic policy. Through the new form of government from 1772, political power was once again divided between the king and the parliament. In the same year, Gustav III also abolished torture as a method of interrogation, and the torture chamber known as Rosenkammaren was permanently closed. Gustav III also enacted a freedom of the press law in 1774, which restricted the more liberal law from 1766.

In 1775, he announced the charter for Marstrand, transforming it into an island with almost complete self-governance. Marstrand received privileges and was transformed into the free port of Marstrand, modeled after Italian examples. The free port ceased to exist in 1794. After the Diet (parliament) of 1778–1779, the economy and criminal code were liberalized. For example, the death penalty was restricted, and a law against infanticide was introduced. In the early 1780s, limited religious freedom was established through the Toleration Edict and the Jewish Regulations, which allowed Catholics and Jews to settle in the kingdom under certain conditions and with limited civil rights.

In 1784, France ceded the Caribbean island of Saint-Barthélemy to Sweden, in exchange for trade rights in Gothenburg (see Franska tomten). Gustav III's intention was to use the island to strengthen the Swedish treasury. Since the island was not suitable for agriculture, it was converted into a free port in 1785.

In the union and security act from the Diet of 1789, the king's power was expanded, but it also led to a leveling of social distinctions in Sweden, and from that point on, most positions in the kingdom were open to commoners as well. The law also restored the right to hunt for tenant farmers (landowners) who had lost it in 1647.

His war against Russia from 1788–1790 ended without any territorial changes after the second Battle of Svensksund, which was a major victory against the Russian fleet. The peace treaty at Värälä marked an end to Russia's involvement in Swedish politics.

Gustav was a personal friend of the French royal family. He became involved in the resistance against the French Revolution and introduced strict censorship of the press. His policies made him unpopular among parts of the nobility, and a conspiracy was formed to overthrow him. He was shot during a masked ball at the Stockholm Opera on March 16, 1792, by the perpetrator Jacob Johan Anckarström, and died two weeks later from blood poisoning.

== Royal Title ==

Gustav III's full title in Swedish was: Gustaf, by the Grace of God, King of Sweden, Goths and Vendes, etc., Heir to Norway, Duke of Schleswig Holstein, Stormarn and Dithmarschen, Count of Oldenburg and Delmenhorst, etc.

== The Regency Period == Gustav III, the eldest son of Adolf Frederick and Louisa Ulrika, was born in Kungshuset, Riddarholmen in Stockholm on January 24, 1746 (New Style; January 13, Old Style). He was introduced to the ideas of the French Enlightenment early on through his close surroundings – primarily his mother, but also tutors, attendants, and teachers. Gustav's development was negatively affected by the discord between his parents and teachers, as well as the interference of the Riksdag (Swedish Parliament) in his upbringing. With the new environment provided by the Estates (the four orders of Swedish society) in 1756 (Carl Fredrik Scheffer became his governor), the royal couple openly expressed their dissatisfaction, even in the presence of their son, and the young prince heard harsh words about the powerful estates of the realm. Gustav was forced, according to the demands of the Estates, to marry Sophia Magdalena of Denmark (1766), but this caused a conflict with his mother, who had developed a dislike for the Danish princess. Even as Crown Prince, he participated in the leadership of the court party, not without envy from his mother's side, and increasingly became the hope of those Swedes who were constitutionally and nationally minded. He actively participated in the negotiations between the united court and "hat" parties and the French ambassador that preceded the Riksdag of 1769, and he significantly contributed to persuading Adolf Frederick to renounce the crown during the December Crisis of 1768 in order to force a convocation of an extraordinary Riksdag and improve the system of government. In 1766, Crown Prince Gustav married the Danish Princess Sophia Magdalena (1746–1813). The wedding ceremony took place on October 1 at Christiansborg Palace in Copenhagen without the groom's presence. On her journey to Stockholm, Sophia Magdalena was met a few days later by the Crown Prince in Helsingborg, where they traveled together.
