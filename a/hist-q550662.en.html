Johannes Magnus, born March 19, 1488, in Linköping, and died on March 22, 1544, in Rome, was a Swedish priest, Archbishop of the Diocese of Uppsala (1523–1531, 1533–1544), official, theologian, genealogist, and historian.

He is considered the last ruling Catholic Archbishop of the Diocese of Uppsala to serve on Swedish soil, before he traveled to Rome as a result of the ongoing Reformation in Sweden. In connection with this journey, he delegated responsibility for the Diocese of Uppsala to the Bishop of Linköping, Hans Brask.

== Biography == Johannes Magnus was born in 1488 as the son of Måns Petersson, a citizen of Linköping, and Kristina, and the brother of Olaus Magnus. He studied at the Cathedral School in Skara, where he was appointed canon in 1506. Later, he studied at several European universities, including theology under the future Pope Hadrian VI in Leuven, and eventually became a Doctor of Theology at the University of Perugia, likely in 1520.

In his youth, he served as Sten Sture the Younger's representative in Rome. In 1523, he was elected Archbishop of Uppsala, but did not receive the approval of the new Pope Clement VII until May 6, 1524, a year after his appointment, with the condition that the Pope wanted to investigate the fate of his predecessor. The truth was also that Johannes Magnus did not take a clear stance in the conflict against Lutheranism; he was a good friend of Gustav Vasa, and the prospect of a national church appealed to his patriotism. In 1524, Brask demanded that he introduce the Inquisition, but Johannes Magnus defended his fellow countrymen and the king. It is said that Gustav Vasa exploited him mercilessly, using his uncertain position in both camps to push through his own agenda. Therefore, Johannes Magnus was the first entrusted with translating the Bible, in order not to arouse suspicion in Rome about the Reformation, but the work never came to fruition because he went to Lübeck on a political mission. Instead, Olaus Petri carried out this task. In 1526, he negotiated, allegedly without authorization, a marriage between Gustav Vasa and the Polish princess Hedvig, which is believed to have been a failed strategy in an attempt to preserve Catholicism as the state religion in Sweden.

After this final defeat, he lived in exile. On July 27, 1533, he was nevertheless ordained as Archbishop in Rome, but by that time Laurentius Petri Nericius had already been appointed Protestant Archbishop. For the rest of his life, Johannes Magnus devoted himself to historical research, living without personal means with his brother Olaus Magni in Venice and Rome, relying on the generosity of others. He died there in the spring of 1544 and was buried in St. Peter's Basilica in Rome.

After Johannes' death, his brother had his two major works published: "Historia de omnibus Gothorum Sveonumque regibus" (History of all Kings of the Goths and Swedes) in 1554, and "Historia metropolitanæ ecclesiæ Upsalensis" (History of the Metropolitan Church of Uppsala) in 1557. The former, in particular, received great international attention and was printed in several editions. It also played a significant role for historians of the Age of Great Powers when they wanted to glorify Swedish antiquity. Johannes Magnus identifies Sweden as the homeland of the Goths and extends the Swedish regnal list all the way back to Magog in the Old Testament.

At the time of Johannes Magnus' death, his brother was appointed his successor as Archbishop, but remained in exile in Rome due to political and religious developments.

== Works ==

Johannes Magnus's work, *Historia de omnibus Gothorum Sveonumque regibus* (The History of All the Kings of Gotland and Sweden), was first published after Johannes's death. His brother, Olaus, oversaw the printing in 1554 and dedicated it to the sons of King Gustav. The book became a great success and was reprinted several times. Johannes Magnus constructed extensive royal genealogies with the intention of extending his people's history further back in time. Many of the figures described can be contradicted by other sources and are considered legendary kings of entirely fictional origin. The book's imaginative historical writing is one reason why both Eric XIV and Charles IX adopted such high regnal numbers. The self-image of the Vasa kings and the main ideology of Sweden's era of great power, Gothicism, found their manifesto here. The book had a significant influence on Swedish perceptions of history during the 16th, 17th, and early 18th centuries.

The first Swedish translation of Johannes Magnus's major work was published in 1620 by Ericus Schroderus. A second Swedish translation, titled *Goternas och svearnas historia* (The History of the Goths and Swedes), was published in 2018 by Michaelisgillet in collaboration with the Royal Academy of Letters. It was translated by Professor Kurt Johannesson, specializing in rhetoric. The edition is typographically aligned with the original Latin text and includes a comprehensive scholarly commentary by Professor Hans Helander, specializing in Latin. The main volume contains 723 pages in a large format, while the commentary volume has 376 pages.
