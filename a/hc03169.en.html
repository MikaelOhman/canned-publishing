The government has proposed changes to the planning and building law. If the proposal is approved on December 1, 2025, it will be easier to carry out certain work on your property without needing to apply for a building permit first. This includes building larger extensions or altering the facade of single-family homes.

For those living in areas that do not have a detailed development plan (a map that specifies what can be built), the proposal suggests that it will be possible to build more structures without a permit. This could include smaller buildings on the property, such as a storage shed or a guest house. However, there are exceptions for areas that are important for national defense or areas that are particularly valuable for the environment.

It will also be easier to change the appearance of your house without having to consult with the municipality. You should be able to replace facade materials, repaint, or install solar panels and skylights without needing a building permit. The rules regarding the use of attics and the establishment of small businesses in older buildings will also become more flexible.

The proposal also includes changes to several other laws, such as the environmental code and regulations regarding signage. The purpose of all these changes is to reduce bureaucratic hurdles and make it easier for individuals to develop their properties.

Full text

The proposal suggests changes to the planning and building code, aiming for a new, simpler, more efficient, and more appropriate regulatory framework for building permits, etc. The proposals involve expanding the number of activities that can be carried out without requiring a building permit and that can be allowed without restrictions in detailed development plans and zoning regulations. This aims to create more housing and increase property owners' ability to decide how their property should be developed. Furthermore, it is proposed that the requirement for a permit should be area-differentiated. More and larger projects should be allowed without a permit in areas not covered by a detailed development plan, such as the construction of ancillary buildings and complementary residential buildings. However, a permit requirement is proposed for projects located near borders, within areas of national interest for total defense, and within particularly valuable areas. Some other simplifications proposed include:

  • Larger extensions can be carried out without requiring a building permit on all buildings.
  • For single-family and two-family homes, the requirement for a building permit for facade changes is eliminated, which simplifies activities such as repainting, replacing facade materials, and installing solar panels or skylights.
  • Building permits can be granted for attic conversions in residential buildings, even if they conflict with attic conversion prohibitions in detailed development plans.
  • Extensions that do not require a permit should be allowed to be carried out simultaneously with the construction of a single-family or two-family home.
  • The requirement for compliance with the approved plan is eliminated fifteen years after the implementation period of the detailed development plan has expired.
  • An existing building outside of a detailed development plan area can be renovated with one or more rooms up to a certain size without requiring a permit.

The proposed changes to the law are scheduled to come into effect on December 1, 2025.
