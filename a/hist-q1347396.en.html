Nils Dacke, born around 1510 in the parish of Vissefjärda, Småland, and died in August 1543 on the meadow of Ollatorpet near Flaka in Torsås, Kalmar County, was a Småland farmer and rebellion leader.

Nils Dacke became known as the leader of a peasant uprising in Småland, the so-called Dackefejden (Dacke Rebellion). Along with his supporters, Nils Dacke opposed, among other things, the tax burden in Småland, and, as Catholics, the introduction of Lutheran Christianity in the area. King Gustav Vasa confiscated church silver and other valuables that the Småland farmers had accumulated over centuries – all under the king's efforts to centralize power in Stockholm.

The rebellion ended in defeat. Dacke's men were defeated because the rebels lacked the economic resources and political influence that Gustav Vasa possessed, and Dacke was shot by the bailiff's men.

== Biography == Nils Dacke was likely born around 1510 in the parish of Vissefjärda in Småland. The first mention of Dacke in Swedish history is in 1536, when he allegedly killed a bailiff during a series of minor disturbances in Småland. At this time, Dacke was reportedly in his thirties and had at least one son. The name of the son is unknown. After this, he fled from justice for a period before eventually surrendering to the court, where he was sentenced to pay a fine of 200 marks, a very large sum at that time. With the help of relatives in his home area in Södra Möre district, he managed to collect the money. He then settled in a newly built farm near the Lyckebyån river on the border with Blekinge.

== The Dackefejden (Dacke Rebellion) ==

A few years later, Dacke's name reappeared in history. This time, he was the leader of a group of peasants from the region who had decided to fight against the local bailiff's oppression of the farmers in the area (according to historian Lars-Olof Larsson, this initial attack clearly bore the hallmarks of revenge). A couple of months later, Dacke found himself in a position of power he hadn't anticipated, leading the peasants of Småland. After concluding a peace agreement with Gustav Vasa on November 8, 1542, instead of marching towards Stockholm with his troops, it appears that Dacke attempted to fulfill the uprising's slogan, "old and ancient," by reintroducing border trade with Denmark as lord of Kronoberg, restoring pre-Reformation religious services in the churches, and more.

In the spring of 1543, the king's troops attacked again, violating the agreed-upon ceasefire, and achieved a crushing victory after learning how to counter the peasants' tactics. According to tradition, Nils Dacke was seriously wounded. Both of his legs were shot through during the decisive battle on the ice over Lake Hjorten in Virserum parish. However, his men managed to get him to safety, and he evaded the king's troops for two months. Dacke finally died in August 1543 at Ollatorpets meadow near the farm he had used at the beginning of the uprising, near Flaka in Torsås, now Gullabo parish. All this was in Södra Möre district, Kalmar County, near the then-national border with Blekinge, Denmark. He was shot by the bailiff's men, who were allegedly led by one of his former lieutenants. A memorial stone has been erected at the site. After the uprising, his body was posthumously displayed and placed in Kalmar, including his head on a pole near the gallows. According to unclear sources, Gustav Vasa had Dacke's son starved to death in a prison cell, out of fear of the son's symbolic value and a potential new uprising.

According to one myth, the person who was killed may not have been Dacke himself, but someone who resembled him and who was also shot in both legs. The real Dacke is said to have fled to Germany and returned to Sweden during the reign of Johan III, where he was then betrayed, captured, and paraded through the streets of Stockholm with a copper crown on his head. After that, he is said to have been placed in a hospital and died during the plague year of 1580. For example, Per Brahe stated that it was later revealed that the man whose body was drawn through Kalmar was not the real Dacke.

== Legacy ==

In the regions where the Dacke Rebellion took place, and especially in southeastern Småland, Nils Dacke is still a well-known name. Statues in his memory have been erected in various places in the district of Södra Möre and in Virserum – where a decisive battle during the Dacke Rebellion took place during Holy Week in 1543 – and elsewhere. Streets, companies, sports clubs (e.g., the speedway club Dackarna), schools, scout districts, and other organizations, as well as a long list of institutions, are named after him. On his farm, Södra Lindön in the parish of Vissefjärda, a memorial stone was erected in 1985. The inscription reads: "Here, on the royal estate S Lindön, lived Nils Dacke until he shot the cruel and intolerable bailiff Inge Andersson in the spring of 1536.
