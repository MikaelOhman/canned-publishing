This article is about Field Marshal Herman Wrangel. For other people with the same name, see Herman Wrangel (disambiguation).

Herman Wrangel, born in either 1584 or 1587, and died on December 11, 1643, was a Swedish military figure of Baltic German origin. He was appointed Field Marshal in 1621, Councilor of the Realm in 1630, and Governor-General of Livonia in 1643. He was the father of Carl Gustaf Wrangel.

== Biography ==

=== Early Years === Wrangel accompanied the Polish army during the war in the Baltic provinces, and from 1608 he served in the Swedish army as a captain with the Östgöta cavalry regiment and in 1612 with the King's Lifeguard. In 1611, he received Sko Abbey, which had fallen into disrepair, along with its stone building, which would become his manor house at Skokloster, as a gift from Charles IX. The following year, he married Margareta Grip at Tre Kronor Castle in Stockholm, and the wedding was hosted by the newly crowned Gustav II Adolf. In 1613, the couple's first son was born at Skokloster and was named Carl Gustaf, who would become a famous field marshal, Marshal of the Realm, and Count. From 1612 to 1621, he served in the castle guard at Kalmar Castle, but also participated in the Russo-Swedish War and carried out other assignments, such as when, in 1619, as a colonel, he received Älvsborg from the Danes on behalf of Sweden. In 1621, he was knighted and appointed Field Marshal during the siege of Riga; the following year, he also participated in the campaign against the Poles. In 1623, he replaced Peder Mikaelsson Hammarskiöld as governor of Öland, but was transferred to Jönköping Castle in 1625.

=== Prussian Campaigns 1626–29 === He participated with distinction in the Prussian campaigns of 1626–29, serving as Swedish commandant in Marienburg and, in the king's absence, as commander of the army under Axel Oxenstierna. In 1629, when Potocki attempted to prevent him from sending 8,000 men to relieve the besieged Strasbourg in West Prussia, Wrangel decisively defeated him at Gorzno, resulting in 2,000 Polish casualties (killed and wounded).

During one of the Prussian campaigns, Wrangel and Åke Tott, both known for their fiery tempers, challenged each other to a duel. When the king announced at the designated meeting place that whoever survived the duel would be punished according to the law – meaning execution – they both declined the challenge.

=== Councilor, Castle Commander, and General Governor === In the spring of 1630, Wrangel became a member of the Royal Council and shortly after the king's departure for Germany that same year, he was given command of Älvsborg and Jönköping castles, along with the troops stationed nearby to protect the border regions in case of a Danish attack. He caused considerable trouble for the Swedish government when, in 1631, he attacked Pros Knudsen, a Danish diplomatic agent in Stockholm, whom he suspected of being a favored rival for the hand of Margareta Stenbock. In 1632, Wrangel was appointed Vice General Governor of Prussia and in 1635, he was one of the Swedish representatives during negotiations in Stuhmsdorf. After the treaty was concluded, Wrangel's regiments were transferred to Pomerania.

=== In the War of 1636-37 === In the new year of 1636, a Swedish regiment was formed under his command near the Oder River. However, this eastern detachment hardly lived up to the expectations placed upon it. It was undoubtedly an error that it was not directly subordinate to Johan Banér, whose strategic intentions were not always understood by Wrangel. Conversely, Wrangel's initiative was hampered by support missions sent to Banér.

In the autumn of 1636, Wrangel launched a campaign from Pomerania, which led to the capture of Berlin and Frankfurt am Main, but otherwise yielded little result. The army had to retreat back to Pomerania due to superior enemy forces. In July 1637, Banér joined Wrangel after successfully maneuvering out of the enemy's trap during his famous retreat from Torgau.

=== Rivalry with Banér === Later, Banér was stationed in a fortified camp near Stettin. Wrangel was similarly positioned near Anklam to prevent the enemy from crossing the Peene River.

The relationship between Banér and Wrangel was strained, primarily due to Wrangel's actions. As a result of Wrangel's unauthorized behavior, Banér soon requested his resignation and that command be given to Wrangel, because "two commanders for the army here is too much."

Wrangel's dismissal had already been issued in July 1637, but he remained in place. Although Banér frequently expressed his dissatisfaction with Wrangel's negligence and that of his subordinates, it can be said that he showed tolerance towards Wrangel and was more inclined to cooperate with him than Wrangel was with Banér.

For a time, there was concern within the army that Wrangel might become a leader for the nobility of Estonia and Livonia in their opposition to the Swedish crown. This dissatisfaction was significant in the autumn of 1637 due to Axel Oxenstierna's stern reprimand delivered to the Estonian representatives regarding the Estonian nobility's disrespectful attitude towards the government. However, these fears proved unfounded, as Wrangel stated in the presence of several high-ranking Swedish officers that he never wanted to return to Sweden and would instead withdraw to the Baltic provinces.
