Fraud attempts increase in connection with tax refunds

Source Skatteverket Read the original at Skatteverket →2026-06-15
TL;DRScammers often impersonate representatives from the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket) to try and steal your personal information and banking details. Be cautious of emails, text messages, or phone calls that ask for passwords or account information.
In plain language

There is an increase in fraud attempts where individuals impersonate employees of the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket). These fraudsters try to obtain your personal information by sending fake emails, text messages, or making phone calls. This often happens around tax filing season or when you receive information about your final tax bill.

If someone calls claiming to be from the Skatteverket without you having initiated contact, hang up immediately. The Skatteverket may call you if you have asked them a question or if there is something unclear about your tax return, but they will never ask for your passwords or bank card details over the phone.

Fraudsters may send links to fake websites where they want you to enter your account information in order to supposedly receive a tax refund. They might also ask you to open files that are claimed to contain tips on deductions. The Skatteverket never requests this type of information via email or text message; they only use secure online services where you log in with your personal identification.

It is common for scammers to impersonate the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket) and attempt to gain access to personal information through methods such as phishing emails, fake text messages, letters, and phone calls. Attempts at online fraud are particularly prevalent during tax season and when final tax assessments are sent out.

"If someone calls claiming to be from the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket) without you having previously contacted them via email, we recommend hanging up. The Tax Agency may call you if you have sent us an inquiry by email or if there are uncertainties in your tax return. However, we never ask for account details, passwords, or bank card information," says Anders Ågren, Head of Communications at the Swedish Tax Agency.

These scam attempts may include requests to provide information about bank account numbers or credit card numbers, or instructions to open an attached file that supposedly contains tips on tax deductions or similar information. There may also be links to a website where you are prompted to provide account details in order to receive a tax refund.

"The Swedish Tax Agency never asks for your account details via email or SMS. We only do so through our online services, which you access using your electronic identification," says Anders Ågren, Head of Communications at the Swedish Tax Agency.
