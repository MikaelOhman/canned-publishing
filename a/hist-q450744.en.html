Hedvig Charlotta Nordenflycht, born November 28, 1718, in Stockholm, and died June 29, 1763, in the parish of Skoklosters, was a Swedish author. She was the daughter of Anders Andersson Nordbohm (ennobled under the name Nordenflycht), who served as a chamberlain at the Chamber Collegium, and Christina Rosin. She is sometimes considered to be Sweden's first feminist and is regarded as one of the foremost Swedish poets of the 18th century. She is also unique in that she was one of the first Swedish women to support herself through her writing.

**Upbringing and Marriage**

Nordenflycht grew up in Stockholm, but in 1731 moved to the family's estate, Viby, in the parish of Harbo, Uppsala County. After a promise made on her father's deathbed in 1734, she became engaged to her teacher, Johan Tideman, who died in 1737 before the marriage could take place. Nordenflycht was greatly influenced by Tideman's sharp intellect, but he was an advocate of the Enlightenment-era rationalism and skepticism that Nordenflycht would later reject. [citation needed] Shortly after Tideman's death, she met the priest Jacob Fabricius and married him in 1741. Their marriage proved to be short but also happy. Her husband fell ill and died only nine months after their wedding. As a result of this, Nordenflycht moved from Karlskrona, where the couple had relocated when Fabricius was appointed as a naval chaplain, to a small cottage on Lidingö. Her life was marked by grief, and she described her home with the words: "The entire room, adorned with sad paintings, testified to my longing, and a cymbal with a few birds were the only signs of joy..."

**Literary Works**

Nordenflycht gained fame with her collection of poems, "Den sörgande turtur-dufwan" (1743), written in mourning after the death of her husband. From 1744 to 1750, she published four volumes titled "Qwinligit Tankespel." She closely followed the debates of her time, and many of her works are characterized as opinion pieces on political, philosophical, and religious issues, as well as on women's freedom and rights. In addition to poetry, Nordenflycht also wrote rococo poems (such as "Dorillas kväde"). Among her works, "Fruentimrets försvar" (1761) is particularly noteworthy, in which Nordenflycht defends the female gender against Rousseau's criticism of women's role in the theater. Another important work is "Fruentimbers Plikt at upöfwa deras Wett, 1741," where she criticizes the oppression of women and advocates for women's right to education.

Nordenflycht's most widely read poem, "Öfver en Hyacint" (1762), is a declaration of love to a younger man named Johan Fischerström, a physiocrat and economist, who became her last love. The later poems deal with the romantic drama between these three individuals: Nordenflycht, Fischerström, and Countess Cathérine Charlotte De la Gardie, which unfolded during the winter of 1762–63. According to accounts of her death, "...abandoned by him whose affection she believed she possessed, she threw herself into the stream flowing past her home. She was rescued alive but died three days later, on June 28, 1763," at the age of 44.
