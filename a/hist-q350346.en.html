This article is about Adam Ludwig Lewenhaupt, a Swedish count and general. For other people with this name, or for other meanings, see Adam Ludvig Lewenhaupt (disambiguation).

Adam Ludwig Lewenhaupt, born April 15, 1659, in the field camp outside Copenhagen, and died February 12, 1719, in Moscow, was a Swedish count, member of the council of state, and general. He was married to his cousin Brita Dorothea Lewenhaupt (1663–1730) and owned Johannesberg Castle in Gottröra.

== Biography == Lewenhaupt was born in 1659 in the Swedish camp near Copenhagen, son of Count Ludvig Wierich Lewenhaupt and Countess Charlotte zu Hohenlohe-Neuenstein und Gleichen. They lived at Charlottenborg Castle on the Motala River. He became an orphan at the age of nine, but was raised by Per Brahe the Younger. Lewenhaupt studied at Lund University. He originally wanted to become a diplomat, but at the age of 25, he realized that this was not possible for him, and he was recruited into the army by Nils Bielke.

=== The Turkish Wars === After his education, he was recruited by Nils Bielke (who was then in Bavarian service) and participated in a war against the Ottoman Empire, a conflict that began in 1684. He fought primarily for the Catholic League. He later participated in the War of the Palatine Succession against France, but soon returned to Sweden. During this war, he achieved the rank of colonel.

=== The Great Northern War === Charles XII recruited him shortly after the outbreak of the Great Northern War, and Lewenhaupt was appointed commander of the Livonian front. On March 19, 1703, he achieved victory against the Russians at Saladen, although this victory had no strategic significance. He won another victory at Jakobstadt on July 26, 1704. Again, this victory lacked strategic importance, but it forced the Russians to postpone further operations until autumn.

On July 16, 1705, the Battle of Gemauerthof took place. An army, commanded by Lewenhaupt and consisting of over 7,000 Swedish soldiers, defeated a Russian force led by Boris Sheremetyev. The Russians numbered over 12,000 men. This victory also had no strategic importance. A few weeks later, Russia occupied all of Courland, forcing Lewenhaupt to withdraw.

=== The Russian Campaign === Throughout the spring of 1708, the Swedish army remained stationary in Belarus, preparing for that year's campaign. King Charles XII ordered Lewenhaupt in 1708 to assemble 12,000 soldiers along with provisions for three months, which he did. He departed from Jelgava (in present-day Latvia) at the end of June 1708.

However, the journey on the poor and swampy roads in present-day Belarus was extremely slow. The entire army barely moved a mile per day. Lewenhaupt had difficulty determining the King's location. He also did not receive any information that the campaign against Moscow had been called off; he only learned this at the encampment near Mohilev.

Meanwhile, Charles XII had been active since the beginning of June, and on July 4th, he defeated a large Russian force in the Battle of Holowczyn. This was followed by a month-long wait in Mogilyov before the main army advanced towards Smolensk. A shortage of supplies then forced it to march southwards to Severien in mid-September. Charles XII, believing that Lewenhaupt's army was much closer to the main force than it actually was, put Lewenhaupt's troops in a very vulnerable position. A large Russian force was detached to attack Lewenhaupt's smaller unit. Lewenhaupt attempted to rejoin the King, but was instead pursued near Lesna, in present-day Belarus. The Russian army was led by Peter the Great. After eight hours of fighting, it became clear that the Russians had won a decisive victory. Lewenhaupt continued southwards. After the battle, he had ordered the march to continue towards Propoisk during the night. All supplies were transported across a bridge, and confusion ensued. Soldiers looted the supply wagons and became intoxicated. Lewenhaupt was forced to leave thousands of soldiers in the forests (a detachment of 1500 men went missing and reappeared in Riga a few months later), while the rest followed the general to the King's main army. Due to all the confusion, Lewenhaupt was forced to burn the supplies. After the burning of the supplies, the already dire food situation in the Swedish camp worsened. It was cold, and it was very difficult to obtain food, as the Russians had burned their own villages. With the remaining infantry mounted on horses, a rapid march southwards was undertaken to rejoin the main army. When they finally reunited with the King and the rest of the Swedish army at Kostenitji, near Starodub, on October 10, 1708, only 5,000 men remained, along with barely any supplies. This exacerbated the famine situation.

By the spring of 1709, the Swedes had arrived in Little Russia, part of present-day Ukraine, and among other things, stormed Veprik. The thaw had begun, but the rain made it difficult for the Swedes to advance. On the other hand, it was easier to obtain food, as Ukraine was not as devastated as Belarus.

=== The Battle of Poltava === By the summer, the Tsar had decided to fight against the Swedes. After Lesna, Lewenhaupt had not held any command position.
