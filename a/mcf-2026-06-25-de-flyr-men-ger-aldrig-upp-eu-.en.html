Full text

To support Ukraine's internally displaced people, Sweden is once again deploying its RescEU Shelter resources, providing emergency housing to Ukraine. The Swedish Civil Defence Agency is managing the deployment and has recently completed training for local actors in Ukraine on how to use the emergency shelters.

Training on assembling emergency shelters in Ukraine:

The last time Sweden's RescEU Shelter was activated for Ukraine was at the start of the full-scale invasion in 2022. Between 2022 and 2025, there have been ten activations of the RescEU Shelter program. In total, this has involved 3,000 emergency shelters with accompanying materials.

Since 2022, many of these emergency shelters have been damaged by Russian drones and missiles. Therefore, the resource is now being activated for the eleventh time. This time, the EU is sending 250 new emergency shelters that can provide housing for 1,250 people.

"Many people do not want to leave Ukraine but need to flee from the front lines to safer areas within the country," says Gunilla Fallqvist, project manager at the section for civil support to Ukraine. "Therefore, more temporary emergency shelters are needed that can serve as transit accommodations or be used for other purposes."

An important part of this effort is ensuring that local actors have the knowledge and skills to manage the emergency shelters.

Anders Olsson, who normally works as a duty officer at the Skaraborg Fire Department, also works as an operational staff member for the Swedish Civil Defence Agency. He, along with five other people, has been in Ukraine training both the Red Cross and representatives from regions near the front lines.

"We were a team of six people who trained them on how to build and adapt these shelters so that they can, in turn, train others," says Anders.

During the training, a demonstration house was built to showcase different solutions – for example, how units can be combined or winterized.

"A new feature of these emergency shelters is that by building an insulated shell with framing, they will now be better able to withstand the winter," says Anders.

The solution involving insulation was developed in a short period during the autumn of 2022 to enable its use during the winter months.

Anders has extensive experience in both firefighting and international assignments. His first international mission came through a request at the fire station, marking the beginning of a long series of deployments, including missions in Africa and after the tsunami in Thailand.

– It was somewhat by chance that I went on my first mission with the Rescue Agency. However, I had previously participated in operations with the Swedish Armed Forces, so I had a general idea of what it involved.

What has motivated him to continue is the feeling of contributing to something larger than himself.

– Being part of Sweden's humanitarian efforts is like playing for the national team. It's the highest level. You gain perspective – and you realize that there isn't much to complain about at home.

The mission in Ukraine differs from his previous experiences. There is a sense of proximity, both geographically and culturally.

– It feels as if it could have been us. Ukrainians have quite similar perspectives to ours, which makes it feel closer.

What has made the strongest impression on him are the people he has met and the feeling that it's not just about Ukraine's future.

– Their attitude, their perseverance, and their ability to find solutions. There is no thought of giving up. They stand up for what is right and wrong. And many feel that they are doing it not only for themselves but also for us others.

The RescEU Shelter is part of a long-term support effort where both equipment and knowledge play crucial roles. By training local actors, the conditions are created to quickly build shelters when new needs arise. Project manager Gunilla Fallqvist emphasizes that the initiative must be continuously strengthened to meet critical needs.

"It feels both meaningful and important to contribute to supporting the people of Ukraine in the difficult situation they are facing. At the same time, we are working intensively to explore further support in the form of temporary emergency housing, as well as strengthening our efforts with water, sanitation, and hygiene to best meet their needs," concludes Gunilla Fallqvist.
