Olaus Petri (Latinized from Olof Pettersson), also known during his studies as Phase, and later as "Master Olof," was born on January 6, 1493, in Örebro, and died on April 19, 1552, in Stockholm. He was a Swedish reformer, humanist, and historian. He is particularly known for having translated the New Testament (in 1526) into Swedish together with Laurentius Andreæ, and this translation became part of Gustav Vasa's Bible.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Olaus Petri was born in Örebro, the son of the blacksmith Peter Olofsson and his wife Kristina Larsdotter. After studying at the Örebro grammar school and in Uppsala, Olaus Petri continued his studies in Germany. He spent a short time in Leipzig in the autumn of 1516 before enrolling at the University of Wittenberg under the name "Phase." There, he was profoundly influenced by Martin Luther and received his Master of Philosophy degree on February 10, 1518. His brother, Laurentius Petri Nericius (Lars Pettersson from Närke), likely studied with him for a shorter period.

He returned to Sweden in the autumn of 1518, becoming chancellor to Bishop Mattias Gregersson (Lillie) in Strängnäs and being ordained as a deacon by him in 1520. After the Stockholm Bloodbath, during which Mattias Gregersson was among the first victims, and which Olaus Petri likely witnessed, he worked as a teacher at the cathedral school in Strängnäs. He quickly attracted attention and concern with his lectures on the Bible. He gained a staunch supporter in his superior, Laurentius Andreæ, who introduced him to Gustav Vasa. In 1524, Gustav Vasa summoned Olaus Petri to Stockholm, where he served as the city's secretary. There, he participated in legal proceedings. He was disturbed by seeing people forced to confess under torture using various instruments. Olaus Petri strongly opposed these methods of torture – 250 years before torture was abolished in Sweden. He stated, "No one should be judged based on a confession obtained through pain and suffering, for often people confess to things that never happened simply to escape the torment."

Upon arriving in Stockholm, he was also granted permission to preach in the city church, which became significant for his work promoting the Reformation. Despite strong opposition, the Reformation surprisingly gained momentum quickly in Stockholm. Olaus Petri's preaching was described as straightforward and free from theological jargon. His most prominent biographer noted, "One can sense how new ideas flowed like a healthy sea breeze through the old worldview." He also avoided religious hatred and attacks against those with different views – despite facing intense hostility from Catholics himself. Olaus Petri's brother, Laurentius Petri, recounts this: "It was considered that the best and most beloved person was the one who could shout the loudest about Lutherans, heretics, blasphemers, betrayers of God, and any other derogatory terms someone could imagine. One would say they wanted to throw them into an old bathhouse, another into an old barn, a third so many loads of wood, tar, peat, bark, etc."

In 1525, Olaus Petri broke decisively with Catholic church law by marrying Kristina, a citizen's daughter from Stockholm. They had two children: their daughter Elisabeth, born in 1526, and their son Reginald, born in 1527. The marriage caused a stir. Bishop Hans Brask immediately wrote to the king, urging him to "for God's sake, intervene in this matter." However, Gustav Vasa defended Petri's decision.

=== Author and Chancellor === In 1526, Olaus Petri also emerged as an author, beginning his literary career with "Een nyttwgh wnderwijsning," Sweden's first reformatory work. The same year, he published "Thet Nyia testamentit på Swensko," which was primarily a translation by Olaus Petri. In 1527, he followed up with "Swar påå tolf spörsmål," directed at Peder Galle, and the polemical work "Swar uppå itt ochristerlighit sendebreff" against Paulus Heliæ. At the Riksdag of Västerås in 1527, Olaus Petri was present, and by that time, the Reformation had gained widespread support among the city's population. 1528 proved to be Olaus Petri's most productive year. He began it with a sermon at Gustav Vasa's coronation and also authored the religious works "Een liten boock om sacramenten," "Een liten boock i huilko closterleffwerne forclarat warder," and "Om Gudz ordh och menniskios bodh och stadhgar." In the following years, "Een handbock på swensko" (1529), "Een lijten postilla" (1530), which also includes "En lijten chatechismus," and "Then swenska messan" (1531) were published. A hymn book, "Swenska songer eller wijsor," which Olaus Petri is said to have published in 1526, does not survive but is known through a reprint in 1530 and was released in an expanded edition in 1536. As Stockholm's city secretary, he recorded the events from 1524 to 1529 in "Tenkiebock."

In 1531, Olaus Petri was appointed chancellor by Gustav Vasa, but soon incurred the king's displeasure. Both the king and Olaus Petri were very independent individuals who found it difficult to agree. It is believed that the political tactics and intrigue did not suit Petri's personality. He was relieved of his duties in 1533. Gustav Vasa remarked that Olaus Petri was as suitable for chancellor as "a donkey is for playing the lute."
