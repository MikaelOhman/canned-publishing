He is particularly known for his research on lichens. Due to the significant work he did in this field, he is often called the "father of lichenology." His studies were so important that several plants and one insect have been named after him as a mark of honor.

Acharius was also a skilled draftsman and artist. He contributed illustrations to scientific books about plants. He is often considered the last student of the renowned naturalist Carl von Linné.

Erik Acharius, born on October 10, 1757, in Gävle, and died on August 14, 1819, in Vadstena, was a Swedish botanist, physician, naturalist, and artist.

== Biography == Acharius was the son of Johan Eric Acharius, "Treasurer and Controller" at the Customs Office in Gävle, and Catharina Margaretha Hagtorn. Initially, Acharius received private tutoring until he entered the gymnasium in Gävle in 1770. In 1773, he became a student at Uppsala University, where he studied for several years. He studied natural history and medicine at Uppsala University and Lund University. In 1776, he defended his dissertation "De Planta Aphyteia" as the last student under Linnaeus's supervision. He then resided in Stockholm, studying natural sciences and medicine, and became a draughtsman at the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1778. The author abbreviation "Ach." can be used for Erik Acharius when referring to a scientific name in botany.

In 1782, Acharius completed his medical examinations in Lund under Rosenblad's supervision. In 1785, he became the town physician in Landskrona, in 1789 the provincial physician in Vadstena, and in 1795 the director of the new spa in Vadstena, receiving a professorship in 1803. Acharius became a member of the Royal Physiographic Society in Lund in 1795, of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 1796, and of the Linnean Society in London in 1801. In 1809, he was appointed Knight of the Order of Vasa, and in 1810, a member of the Royal Scientific Society in Uppsala. The spa in Vadstena was established according to his proposal and design. As an illustrator, Acharius contributed to Johan Peter Westring's work "Svenska lafvarnes färghistoria" (1805) and Carl Peter Thunberg's "Flora Capensis."

Acharius's interest in botany attracted Linnaeus's attention and favor. He became particularly renowned for his groundbreaking study of the class Lichenes, lichens (which are now not considered a distinct class), among the cryptogams. He developed a new classification system for them, added new genera and species, and defined their characteristics. He has been called the "father of lichenology" and published several highly valuable works in this field: "Lichenographiæ Sueciæ prodromus" (1798); "Methodus lichenum" (1803); "Lichenographia universalis" (1810); "Synopsis methodica lichenum" (1814), as well as a large number of smaller articles in the publications of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, the Journal of Agricultural Sciences, and other periodicals. A plant family (Achariaceae) and a genus within that family, *Acharia*, along with several species such as *Rosa acharii* and *Conferva acharii*, and an insect, *Tortrix achariana*, have been named after him. He is often referred to as Carl Linnaeus's last student.
