Improved regulations regarding the taxation of forests.

TL;DRThe government wants to change the rules regarding taxes on forestry to make it easier to protect nature. The proposal aims to simplify the process of saving money for conservation efforts and to ensure that the regulations comply with EU law.
In plain language

The government has proposed changes to tax regulations for forestry, aiming to make it more attractive for forest owners to set aside land for nature conservation. One of the proposals involves the possibility of forest owners receiving reimbursements for funds used in nature conservation after ten years.

It will also be possible to create a special account called a "nature conservation account." By using such an account, forest owners can spread their costs over several years, a process known as "amortization." This means that the tax burden will not be as high at once when receiving compensation for nature conservation efforts.

To comply with EU regulations, the government proposes that these accounts should be managed by banks in other EU and EES countries. This means that forest owners will be able to use banks from other European countries to manage their forestry accounts. The possibility of deducting forestry-related expenses for tax purposes may also be extended to properties within the EU.

If the Parliament votes in favor of the proposals, the new regulations are planned to come into effect on April 1, 2026. The proposal also includes the removal of an old law regarding taxes on interest for forestry accounts.

Full text

This proposal contains a number of suggestions aimed at simplifying and improving tax rules related to forestry, increasing incentives for formal nature conservation, and adapting tax rules to EU law.

The government proposes a more efficient way to tax compensation and payments made for nature conservation purposes within the framework of the compensation fund system. The proposal means that contributions to the compensation fund for land defined as nature conservation land can be reversed in the tenth tax year after the tax year in which the deduction was made. The government also proposes that a self-employed individual's compensation for nature conservation agreements can be spread over time, allowing the individual to deduct amounts deposited into a special account at a credit institution. This account should be called a "nature conservation account." The government further proposes that the law on tax on interest on forestry account funds, etc., be repealed.

In order to adapt the tax rules for forestry accounts, forestry damage accounts, and the new nature conservation account to EU law, the government proposes that these accounts be provided by a foreign credit institution domiciled in a state within the European Economic Area (EEA) or by a branch of a foreign credit institution in a state within the EEA. An additional proposal to adapt the tax rules to EU law is to expand the possibility of forestry deductions in certain cases to include properties within the EEA.

The new provisions are proposed to enter into force on April 1, 2026.
