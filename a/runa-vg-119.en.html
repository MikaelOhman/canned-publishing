In plain language

The Sparlösa stone stands near the Sparlösa church in Västergötland. It is a large stone with both images and text carved into it. The stone was created during the Viking Age, approximately 1200 years ago. It is considered one of the most famous runestones in all of Sweden.

The stone has had quite an eventful history. For a long time, it was embedded within a church wall and divided into two pieces. In the 1930s, it was removed and reassembled. Because exposure to air damages the stone, it has been moved into a protective building near the church so that visitors can see it.

The sides of the stone feature various images, such as birds, a ship, and a rider. There are also texts written in runes, which is how people wrote in the past. Part of the text is damaged, but researchers have been able to decipher that a man named Gisle created the stone to commemorate his brother Gunnar.

Runestones are important because they are the only surviving texts from the Viking Age. Even though the text on the stone is short, it helps us learn more about the names and family relationships of the people who lived in those times.

Full text

The Sparlösastone, with the identification mark Vg 119, is located near the church of Sparlösa in the parish and district of Viste, Västergötland. It is Sweden's second most famous runestone, after the Rökstone. The stone, which stands 1.77 meters tall, was mostly carved in the 9th century, but also bears a short inscription added in the 10th century.

== History == Like the Rökstone, the Sparlösastone dates back to around the year 800 and is primarily inscribed with runic inscriptions of the "short twig" type, but it also contains some older Norse runes. The Sparlösastone came to the attention of researchers in 1669 when Elias Brenner, Johan Hadorph, and Petrus Törnewall found it embedded in the southern wall of the then-existing church of Sparlösa. A drawing of the stone in the wall from that time is the oldest known document about the stone. During a renovation of the choir in 1685, the stone was split into two pieces, which were then individually built into the walls. In 1848, the old church was demolished and replaced with the current Sparlösa church, where the two halves of the stone were re-embedded in the walls. The halves were removed from the wall again in 1937. At that time, it was discovered that there were inscriptions on the previously hidden sides. The pieces were rejoined, and the stone was placed on display in the Sparlösa churchyard later that same year. However, due to modern air pollution, the stone began to deteriorate rapidly, requiring it to be taken for conservation. In 1982, the runestone was installed in a protective wooden structure near the church, where it can still be viewed today.

== Sides of the Stone ==

The stone has inscriptions on all four sides. One side is a purely pictorial representation, while the other three contain both runes and images. It is difficult to determine the correct order in which the sides should be read, partly because the stone has significant damage that makes the text incoherent, and partly because interpreting the images requires knowledge of events or stories that are completely unknown today.

The designations "north," "east," "south," and "west" used in this article refer to the stone's current orientation. This does not indicate how the stone may have been originally oriented, but it describes the order in which the sides are arranged without highlighting any particular side as the first.

On the north side, there is an image of two birds entangled with two snakes. The upper bird resembles an owl, and the lower one a crane. One of the snakes appears to be attacking the owl. To the left of the image, there are a long line and two short lines of text, and to the right, two lines. Above the image, there are four horizontal lines of text whose reading order is unclear.

On the east side, there are three lines of text, two spiral shapes, and something described as part of a male head.

On the south side, there are images of an elaborate building, a ship with two birds, a spotted animal resembling a lion with a cub, and a rider with a drawn sword and a deer-like animal behind him.

On the west side, there is a line of text with extremely high characters. Along the right edge and extending upwards along the top part of this side, there is also a line of text that was added later. At the bottom, there is a man carrying a beam decorated with crosses.

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary primary sources from the Viking Age – carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they state what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some readings are uncertain – these are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
