Johan III, born December 20, 1537, at Stegeborg Castle, and died November 17, 1592, at Tre Kronor Castle in Stockholm, was King of Sweden from 1569 to 1592. He seized power in the autumn of 1568 by imprisoning his brother, Erik XIV, and campaigning against him for the remainder of that year. While some historians date his reign from the takeover in 1568, it was only in January 1569 that the Swedish Parliament officially decided on the change of government. In Lars Ericson Wolke's biography of Johan (2004), it is also suggested that Erik may have been murdered eight years later at Johan's command.

He ended the Nordic Seven Years' War, but Sweden subsequently became involved in the Nordic Twenty-Five Years' War with Russia, ultimately achieving some territorial gains. He worked to foster closer relations with Poland, successfully securing the Polish throne for his son and heir, Sigismund.

Johan III was interested in religion and culture. During his reign, he opposed the growing Lutheran tendencies within the Swedish Church, which were influenced by Duke Karl, and instead advocated for a reunification with the Catholic Church and the Pope in Rome. This included initiatives such as the establishment of the Catholic Royal College (Collegium regium Stockholmense), support for the remnants of the Catholic monastic system, and funding for Catholic Jesuit missions in Sweden.

Johan III was the son of Queen Margaret and King Gustav Vasa. He was the brother of Karl IX and Magnus, and the half-brother of Erik XIV. He was married on October 4, 1562, to Katarina Jagellonica (1526–1583), with whom he had a son, Sigismund, and a daughter, Anna Vasa. He remarried on February 21, 1585, to Gunilla Johansdotter (Bielke) (1568–1597).

== Childhood and Youth == Johan III was born at Stegeborg Castle on December 20, 1537, as the son of Gustav Vasa and his second wife, Margareta Eriksdotter (Leijonhufvud). He frequently caused his father displeasure, particularly when, as hereditary Duke of Egentliga Finland (since June 27, 1556), he sought to act behind King Gustav's back and interfere in the affairs of Livonia. He also collaborated with his brother Erik, traveling to London on his behalf for a courtship mission, while Erik protected Johan's interests in Livland. During this time, he also began a relationship with the lady-in-waiting Karin Hansdotter, with whom he lived together between 1556 and 1561, and they had four illegitimate children during those years.

=== The Break with Brother Erik === When his brother Erik XIV became king and held power within the royal authority, a rift soon developed between the brothers. With great reluctance, Johan signed the Articles of Arboga in 1561, which limited his power. The conflict became open when, against Erik's wishes, Johan married Princess Katarina Jagellonica on October 4, 1562, in Vilnius. She was the younger sister of Sigismund II Augustus, King of Poland, with whom Erik was at war. Shortly after the wedding, the two brothers-in-law reached an agreement: Johan would receive seven fortified castles in Livland as collateral from the Polish king in exchange for a prepayment of 120,000 daler. Erik considered this agreement to be in direct violation of the Articles of Arboga – which it was – and as soon as he learned about it, he demanded that Johan relinquish the Livonian castles. When the Duke refused and also gave an evasive answer to the king's demand that he clearly state whether he wished to remain loyal to Sweden or Poland, he was summoned in April 1563 to appear in Sweden to answer charges of treasonous plans.

=== Imprisonment === Since Johan did not comply with the demands, he was sentenced in June 1563 by the representatives gathered in Stockholm to be guilty of treason, resulting in loss of life, property, and inheritance rights to the kingdom. To enforce the sentence, a significant military force was assembled. Johan, who was ill-prepared for conflict, was confined in Åbo Castle. He defended himself there for several weeks with 1200 men, and subsequently surrendered on August 12, 1563, under promise of princely imprisonment. He was transported to Sweden and imprisoned at Gripsholm Castle, accompanied by his wife. As Johan and his wife were being taken to Gripsholm by ship, they passed Söderport, where the executions of 30 of Johan's supporters took place. Johan remained a prisoner for more than four years. However, the imprisonment was relatively lenient, especially compared to what Erik would later experience; the couple, for example, received a large delivery of books. Johan, who was fond of books, spent much of his time studying and conversing with his wife. Their three children were born during the imprisonment: Elisabet, also known as Isabella, in 1564 (who died at the age of two), Sigismund in 1566, and Anna in May 1568.
