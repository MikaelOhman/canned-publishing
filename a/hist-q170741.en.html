Karl Manne Georg Siegbahn, born December 3, 1886, in Örebro, and died September 26, 1978, in Stockholm, was a Swedish physicist who received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1924 "for his discoveries and research in the field of X-ray spectroscopy."

== Biography == Manne Siegbahn was born on December 3, 1886, in Örebro. He was the son of station inspector Georg Siegbahn and Emma Zetterberg. He passed his secondary school leaving exam in Stockholm in 1906 and began studying at the Department of Physics at Lund University that same year. During his studies, he became a research assistant under Professor Janne Rydberg, and he published articles on topics including alternating current arcs, and designed instruments for studying alternating currents. In 1911, he defended his doctoral dissertation "Magnetische Feldmessung" and shortly thereafter became a docent (associate professor) in physics. In 1915, he was appointed acting professor of physics after Janne Rydberg was forced to take leave from his professorship due to illness, and in 1920, he was appointed as the permanent professor after Rydberg retired in 1919. However, in 1922, he left Lund to become a professor at Uppsala University after the then-professor Gustaf Granqvist had died. The reason for this move was that he wanted to take advantage of the larger facilities and greater equipment budget available at Uppsala. He was married to Karin Siegbahn, née Högbom, and was the father of Bo and Kai Siegbahn. His remains are located in the Engelbrekt columbarium in Stockholm.

== X-ray Emission Spectroscopy ==

In 1914, Siegbahn began publishing his first reports on x-ray spectra. He used a spectrometer similar to the one that the Englishman Henry Moseley had used to find the relationship between the wavelengths of different elements and their position in the periodic table. Shortly thereafter, he designed a more precise spectrometer, which was built by the caretaker A. L. Petersen. Using this new spectrometer, Siegbahn and his doctoral student Ivar Malmer discovered that the spectral lines that Moseley had identified were composed of multiple components. This discovery spurred Siegbahn to improve the spectrometer in order to make more accurate measurements and discover even more components. In addition, he began using x-ray tubes, vacuum pumps, and tubes for crystallographic studies of minerals. With these instruments, Siegbahn was able to gain a nearly complete understanding of the energy levels of electrons within atoms.

The empirical discovery that spectral lines contain multiple components received a theoretical explanation from the German physicist Arnold Sommerfeld. To achieve this, Sommerfeld used Niels Bohr's atomic model in conjunction with Albert Einstein's theory of relativity. The verification of Sommerfeld's calculations was performed by Siegbahn at Lund University because it was the only institution that had sufficiently precise spectrometers.

== Awards == In 1924, Siegbahn received the Nobel Prize in Physics for his "discoveries and research in X-ray spectroscopy." He was awarded the Hughes Medal in 1934. In 1936, he was appointed as a professor of experimental physics in Stockholm, and the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences decided to establish an institute with him as its director. Parts of this institute later became the Manne Siegbahn Laboratory at Stockholm University. Manne Siegbahn became a member of the Physiological Society in Lund in 1916, and in 1922 he joined the Scientific Society in Uppsala and the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. From 1923 to 1961, he was a member of the Nobel Committee for Physics at the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. He was elected to the Royal Institute of Technology in 1941 and to the Military Science Academy in 1946. In 1957, he became an honorary doctor of technology at the Royal Institute of Technology.

=== Trivia === He was a distant descendant of the Hungarian Árpád dynasty, which conquered and founded the Kingdom of Hungary.
