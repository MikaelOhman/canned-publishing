Gustav Eriksson Vasa the Younger, born on January 28, 1568, and died in February 1607 in Kasjin, Russia, was a Swedish prince, the legitimate son of King Erik XIV and Karin Månsdotter, and grandson of Gustav Vasa.

After his father's deposition in 1568, Gustav was held captive with his family, but in 1575 he was exiled by his uncle Johan III, who feared that supporters of the deposed king would raise him as a claimant to the throne. In the following years, he lived in exile in Poland, the Holy Roman Empire, and Russia, where he converted to Catholicism and was repeatedly used in international power struggles by figures such as Ivan IV and Boris Godunov. He declined, for example, an offer of marriage to Godunov's daughter Xenia Borisovna, and a vassal kingdom over Estonia and Livonia.

In his final years, Gustav lived in Kasjin, where he devoted himself to alchemy. He died impoverished and was buried outside the city, but his grave was rediscovered in 1610 by Field Marshal Jakob De la Gardie.

== Biography == Gustav was born out of wedlock but legitimized through his parents' marriage. Like his sister, he was placed under the care of their French governess, Johanna d'Herboville. Initially, they lived with their parents in the prison after his father's deposition, but they were moved in 1573. King Johan III feared that supporters of the deposed Erik XIV in Sweden might use Gustav to carry out plans for his reinstatement. Therefore, in 1575, Gustav was sent into exile. In his own words, addressed to his son, King Sigismund in Poland, Johan was determined "not to let the feathers on that bird [i.e., Gustav] grow too long."

In the years that followed, he lived in poverty in Poland, where he reportedly studied at educational institutions run by Jesuits in Braunsberg, Thorn, and Vilnius, and converted to Catholicism at one of them.

Gustav was unwillingly drawn into political intrigues from a young age. One such intrigue originated from Swedish conspirators in France, while another came from Tsar Ivan IV, who, in connection with Johan III's envoy in Poland, Anders Lorich, attempted to persuade Gustav to claim the Swedish throne. The Tsar's death in 1584 thwarted the plan, and Lorich was executed in Sweden that same year. In 1585, the issue of whether Gustav should be assassinated was discussed in the Swedish Council of State.

In 1586, he reportedly visited Rome but later resided in Moravia and Silesia under the protection of Emperor Rudolf II. He finally had the opportunity to see his mother, Karin Månsdotter, in 1596, when they met in Reval. They found it difficult to communicate at that time because Gustav had almost forgotten how to speak Swedish. At that point, Gustav was living in Thorn again.

=== Russia === In the year 1600, he left that city to travel to Russia, where the then-tsar Boris Godunov, like his predecessor Ivan IV, wanted to use him as a tool for political intrigue. The tsar's intention was to use Gustav as a means against his uncle Charles IX in Sweden. Boris even fabricated a threatening letter from Gustav to King Charles, which he had copied and circulated in Estonia and Livonia – the two countries he desired to control. Boris offered his exiled guest command of Russian troops to drive out Swedes and Poles from the Baltic provinces. In return, Gustav would become a Russian vassal king over Estonia and Livonia and be allowed to marry the tsar's only daughter, Xenia – provided that he converted to the Orthodox faith. However, the exiled prince refused to agree to this.

Despite this, Boris promised Gustav freedom, but the prince's situation remained difficult. The tsar made it clear to him that he had fallen out of favor, and when Gustav requested permission to leave Russia, Boris placed his house under guard. Over time, however, the tsar's attitude softened somewhat, and he granted Gustav a small, impoverished town near Moscow as a fiefdom. There, Gustav devoted himself to alchemical studies and experiments. His knowledge of chemistry was significant for that time, and he enjoyed great respect among scholars in Germany and Poland. However, he lacked political ambition.

Gustav also refused attempts by Boris' successor, Dimitrij, to use him as a political tool. A year after the latter's downfall, in February 1607, Gustav Eriksson Vasa died in the small town of Kasjin in central Russia. The Russian tsar Vasily IV of Russia sent money for Gustav's funeral, but the town commandant kept the money for himself. This resulted in Gustav being buried a short distance outside the city in a birch grove.

During the Time of Troubles in Russia, a Swedish army under Jakob De la Gardie marched into Moscow in March 1610, even entering the Kremlin where Tsar Vasily IV received him. De la Gardie then rode on horseback the few miles to Kasjin, where he managed to find Gustav's grave after some searching; it was overgrown and neglected at the time. As far as is known, this was the first Swedish visit to the grave, and probably also the last.
