Carl Michael Bellman, born February 4, 1740 (according to parish records) in the Maria Magdalena parish of Stockholm, and died February 11, 1795 in the Klara parish of Stockholm, was a Swedish poet. His most famous works are the collections of poems known as "Fredman's Letters" and "Fredman's Songs."

Bellman is often considered one of Sweden's national poets and has been called "the Anacreon of the North," referring to the Bacchanalian aspects of his work. His poetry has been very important for later Swedish literature, and his significance in that field has been compared to William Shakespeare's influence on English-language literature.

Bellman's output was exceptionally rich and varied. It included poems, songs, parodies of secret societies, plays, translations, and religious poetry. Among the figures in his songs are the clockmaker Fredman, the musician Movitz, Corporal Mollberg, and the prostitute Ulla Winblad, as well as characters drawn from biblical, Greek, Roman, and Norse mythology. He often worked with contrasting levels of style and genre mixing within a single work, so that what initially seemed like a pastoral could turn into a drinking party and sexual encounter.

Like other song writers, Bellman often borrowed melodies for his songs from popular tunes of the time. These came from sources such as opera and operetta, dance music, and folk songs, which he adapted to suit his purposes. In some cases, such as "Arise, you god of night," no original melody has been found. Whether Bellman himself composed the melody or whether the original melody has simply been forgotten is a question that cannot be answered.

== Biography ==

=== Early Life === Carl Michael Bellman was the son of Johan Arendt Bellman the younger (1707–1765), who served as a secretary in the castle chancellery, and Catharina Hermonia (1717–1765), who was the daughter of the pastor of Maria parish, Michael Hermonius, and his wife Kristina Arosell. He was the grandson of Johan Arendt Bellman the elder (1664–1709), a professor of Latin, and his noble wife Catharina Elisabet Daurer (1688–1709), who was the daughter of Jakob Daurer (1649–1713), secretary in the government overseeing Queen Christina's estates, and his wife Catharina von Santen (1665–1743). Bellman's parents lived in the affluent household of the latter couple at the time of his birth.

Bellman was born in the large estate of Stora Daurerska, which included a baroque garden and a spacious orangery, located on Hornsgatan in Stockholm. However, following the inheritance settlement after his grandmother's mother passed away, the family moved in 1744 to the nearby Lilla Daurerska house on Bellmansgatan (then called Björngårdsbrunnsgränd).

Bellman was the oldest of fifteen children (of whom only eight survived childhood; in his autobiography, Bellman states that his mother gave birth to 21 children, but this may include later miscarriages). Among those who served as godparents at his baptism was Carl Gyllenborg, the Chancellor, whose name he also bore.

Bellman received his education at home from private tutors. The first tutor was Anders Carl Rutström, followed by Petrus Norman, who likely became the subject of parody in Fredmans sång 63. Several other tutors followed. Bellman seems to have struggled with geometry and physics. According to his own account in the "Levernebeskrivningen," a problematic source written more for effect than accuracy, during a severe illness, possibly malaria, he spontaneously composed verses and sang them to the great astonishment of his parents. As a result, he was "appointed" as a student to a tutor named Clas Ludvig Ennes; whom he later helped secure a position with the late King. Ennes taught Bellman how to play the cister, an instrument in the lute family. Under Ennes' supervision, Bellman also translated religious and moral texts, including passages from the pietistic "Neues geistreiches Gesangbuch." It is possible that he also learned some of the poetic meters that Bellman would later use from his tutor, as Ennes was skilled in metrics but otherwise rather mediocre.

=== Early Career and Debut === In December 1757, Bellman was hired for a trial period at the Bank of Sweden (Rikets ständers bank), despite the fact that he wasn't particularly strong in mathematics. In November of the following year, he enrolled as a student at Uppsala University. The exact reason for this is unclear, as he already had one foot in the door at the national bank and didn't return after his Christmas break. Nevertheless, in Uppsala, he made new friends, including Henric Brandel and Johan Wellander. In January 1759, he took an entrance exam for a position at the Bank of Sweden, demonstrating knowledge of French and German, but also continuing to struggle with mathematics. Therefore, his hiring was likely due as much to connections as it was to skills. That summer, he became an extra permanent employee at the bank – an unpaid position. Bellman continued to live with his parents and supported himself through loans.
