In plain language

Starting July 1st, new regulations will come into effect to reduce VAT fraud. The aim is to make it more difficult for companies to engage in tax evasion and to create a more secure system for both Swedish and European businesses.

The Tax Agency (Skatteverket) now has greater opportunities to verify companies that wish to register for VAT. This may mean that business owners need to visit a Tax Agency office to prove their identity. If anything seems incorrect, the Tax Agency can also refuse or revoke a company's right to use VAT.

It will also be easier to prevent unreliable companies from conducting business with other countries in the EU. The Tax Agency can now mark incorrect numbers as invalid within a common European VAT system. Furthermore, the Tax Agency can suspend payments if they suspect that information provided by a company is inaccurate.

Listen New regulations are coming into effect on July 1st to make it more difficult to commit VAT fraud. These changes aim to strengthen trust in both trade and taxation in Sweden and the EU.

"For businesses, these regulations mean that the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket) will have greater opportunities to conduct checks when a company registers for VAT. In some cases, this may involve you needing to visit one of our offices to verify your identity. It will also become possible, in certain situations, to deny or revoke a VAT registration if something is not correct," says Johanna Arrhén, coordinator at the Tax Agency's department for combating economic crime.

Another change is that the Tax Agency can mark a VAT number as invalid within the EU's common VAT system, VIES. This makes it easier to detect and stop unscrupulous actors in international trade.

The new regulations also mean that the Tax Agency may, in certain situations, suspend payments of excess input VAT. This applies, for example, if there are indications that the information used as the basis for the payment is not correct.

"Overall, these changes aim to create healthier competition for businesses and a more secure system for everyone," says Johanna Arrhén.
