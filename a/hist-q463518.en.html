Knut Långe, also known as Knut Holmgersson, died in 1234. After the Battle of Olustra in either 1229 or 1230, he became King of Sweden and ruled until his death. He may have briefly co-ruled with Erik Eriksson, the last king of the Erician dynasty, and it has been suggested that Knut Långe himself belonged to the Erician lineage. He resided on the Skohalvön peninsula in Uppland and was sometimes referred to as "Knut of Sko." Politically, Knut is believed to have been associated with the Folkungar, a rebellious group that emerged several times during the 13th century.

== Coat of Arms == In 1230, Knut Långe had coins minted in Östra Aros. One of these coins features a coat of arms with two bands, which is similar to the coat of arms depicted on the surcoat of his son, Holmger Knutsson, which has three bands. The French heraldic organization, Heraldique Europeenne, displays the coat of arms for Canut Johanson (?-1234), King of Sweden, and refers to Knut Långe. The coat of arms is divided with three horizontal bars: a silver bar in the center, accompanied by two gold bars: D'azur, à une fasce d'argent, accompagnée de deux fasces d'or.

== Family == Information about the origins of Knut Långe is largely based on speculation. The only known patronymic comes from an unreliable source from the late 14th century, which suggests his name was "Joansson," leading to speculation that he was the son of Jarl Jon. However, it is more likely that Knut Långe was the same person as a Knut Holmgerson who witnessed a document issued by King Eric Eriksson in 1224 and was mentioned as a relative and member of the king's council during the following years. This possibility is strengthened by the fact that one of Knut's sons was also named "Holmger." He is then assumed to have been the son of a Holmger who is described as King Knut Eriksson's *nepos*. This term of kinship, used exclusively for people belonging to a younger generation, often means nephew or niece in Swedish medieval Latin. Therefore, Knut may have belonged to the Erik family.

In 1230, he had coins minted in Östra Aros. One of the coins features a coat of arms with two strings, which also appears on the coat of arms of his son, Holmger. Since the coat of arms also displays the arms of the Bjälbo family, it is likely that Holmger's mother belonged to this family. The claim that Knut Långe was married to the Danish noblewoman Helena Pedersdotter Strange is less probable.

Children:

  • Holmger Knutsson, died 1248 (executed after a rebellion by the Folkungar dynasty, which culminated in the Battle of Sparrsätra in 1247)
  • Filip Knutsson (Erik family), died 1251 (executed)

== Biography == Around 1220, Knut is said to have granted the land where the future Sko monastery would be located in Uppland to Dominican monks. These monks later left the site, and it became a Cistercian nunnery instead. According to an account from the 16th century, he was buried there. Knut Långe was likely a member of the council that ruled Sweden from 1222 to 1229 in place of the underage King Eric Eriksson. After an uprising, and following the Battle of Olustra in either 1229 or 1230, he received the Swedish crown when Eric fled to Denmark. His exact involvement in the uprising is unknown: he may have been a rebel leader or a compromise candidate proposed after Eric's flight. He was formally recognized as king by 1231 and reigned until his death in 1234. However, it is unclear exactly how Eric returned: according to the Lund Annals, it occurred as early as 1232, while the Chronicle of Eric claims it happened after Knut's death and further conflicts. The fact that Knut's jarl, Ulf Fase, continued in his position after the change of leadership suggests that the transition may have been peaceful. An obviously incorrect statement by Sturla Þórðarson claiming that Knut was a co-regent with Eric Knutsson has therefore been interpreted as meaning that he briefly ruled jointly with Eric Eriksson. Entries in the Östgötalagen (a Swedish law code) state that several Swedish laws were revised under a King Knut. Based on when similar revisions were made elsewhere in Europe, and because these changes were not incorporated into the Äldre Västgötalagen (another Swedish law code), it is believed that this refers to Knut Långe. These changes included provisions stating that creditors could no longer seize property from debtors without the involvement of authorities after a court judgment (a practice known as "nam," now called seizure), and that royal courts were to be held every three years.
