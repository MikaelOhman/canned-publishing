Full text

The Tjängvide stone is a runic and pictorial stone discovered embedded in a cellar at the Tjängvide farm in the parish of Alskog on Gotland in 1844. It dates from between the 7th and 9th centuries. The stone is now stored at the Historical Museum in Stockholm, which has also taken its logo from one of the images on the stone.

==The Stone== The stone is made of limestone and measures 170 cm high, 30 cm thick, and up to 120 cm wide. It is slightly damaged in its lower part, and the upper half is broken, but both parts are preserved. It is believed to be of pagan origin as it contains no crosses or other Christian symbols. Today, it is unknown whether there is a connection between the images and the people mentioned in the runes, as we can only interpret what the images depict, and the texts are very incomplete.

==Images== The front side of the Tjängvide stone consists of two fields with images, an upper one and a lower one. Around the entire stone and between the images runs a braided pattern that resembles Valknut symbols. In the upper field, there is a large eight-legged horse with a small rider, who receives a welcoming horn from a woman. There are also a couple of other human figures, a four-legged animal, and some less clear images. The lower field is almost entirely filled by a longship with high prows and stern, and a sail that is nearly as wide as the ship's length.

=== Interpretations === The eight-legged horse is believed to be Sleipner, and the rider is either Odin or a deceased warrior riding into Valhalla. The woman is thought to be a Valkyrie. Another interpretation suggests that the rider is Sigurd Fafnesbane, welcomed by Gudrun Gjuksdotter's mother upon his arrival at the court of the giants, riding on the horse Grane, a descendant of Sleipner. It is also possible that the horse's eight legs simply symbolize speed, and that the rider is alive and being welcomed home by his wife. In addition to the drinking horn, the woman carries something else, which may be a key she is handing over to the rider.

The male figure closest to the woman appears to be carrying a bow and may be a deceased hunter, while the four-legged animal behind the woman could be his dog. The figure above the rider might be a man hanged from a tree. The reason for the figure lying down is purely due to space constraints. Another interpretation is that it depicts two warriors fighting in Valhalla.

== Inscriptions == There are two runic inscriptions on the stone; one to the left in the upper field and one to the right in the lower field. The upper inscription is worn, and all that can be distinguished is the sequence of runes "fuÞorkhn". The runic inscription to the right of the lower field is damaged, and it is possible that a large portion of the text is missing in its lower part where the stone is broken. Both are written in short-twig runes without spaces or punctuation to separate the words.

Transcription of the runic inscription:

§A fuþorkhn... ...fuþr-... §B ... (r)aisti stainin aft iurulf bruþur sin ÷ sikuif(i)r(t)(u)(a)(n)k(i)sifil Normalization to Old Swedish:

§A fuþork ... §B ... ræisti stæininn aft Hiorulf/Iorulf, broður sinn ... Translation to modern Swedish:

§A fuþork ... §B ... raised the stone in memory of Hjorulf, his brother ... Sophus Bugge reads the last part as "sikuifartuirkunifil" and translates it as "Sigvif's wife died, she fell in battle," or "Sigvi died on a journey, her husband fell in battle." Brate interprets the verb "sveka" (to betray) instead of the name Sigvi and translates it as "The Vendians betrayed him on his journey, he fell in battle."

How certain are we about this?

Runic inscriptions are the only contemporary primary sources from the Viking Age – carved by the people themselves, not written down long afterwards. This makes names, family relationships, and mentioned journeys strong evidence. The limitation is that the texts are short and formulaic (they state what was erected, not the entire context), and that damaged or difficult-to-read runes mean that some readings are uncertain – such readings are marked with question marks or parentheses in the transliteration.
