Pontus De la Gardie, born as Ponce d'Escouperie in 1520 in Caunes-Minervois, Aude, France, and died on November 5, 1585 (drowned) in Narva, was a French military figure, baron, and member of the council. He was the son of the merchant Jacques Scoperier, but he invented an aristocratic title based on a property near his family home. He began his military career in France but eventually entered Danish service. During the Nordic Seven Years' War, he was captured by the Swedes in 1565. He joined the Swedish army and became a baron in 1571, after which he married Sofia Johansdotter Gyllenhielm in 1580.

== In the Service of Henry II == As a child, Pontus De la Gardie was sent to a monastic school and became a monk for six years, but in his teens, he began training in military affairs. His first campaign was under Marshal Brissac in Piedmont, and he subsequently participated in several internal conflicts between European states. During the Scottish civil war in 1559, he was sent by Henry II to Scotland to support the Catholics. The mission was unsuccessful, and Scotland was officially declared a Protestant country the following year. Subsequently, Pontus De la Gardie sailed to Denmark with his French and Scottish mercenaries and offered his services to the newly appointed Frederick II.

== In the Service of Frederick II == During the chaotic Nordic Seven Years' War, De la Gardie was tasked with defending the Danish fortress of Varberg, but little could be done in the face of the Swedish attacks in 1565. At that time, the fortress was built to withstand arrows, not cannons. The Swedish commander Nils Boije led the siege, which ended on August 28 with the defenders surrendering.

== In the Service of King Eric XIV == Thus, Pontus De la Gardie's brief period in Denmark was over. However, he was seen as a valuable asset for Sweden's expanding military machine and was offered a position within its circle of commanders. Given that the alternative likely would have been execution [source needed], he accepted the offer. King Eric XIV was fond of the Frenchman, but Jöran Persson warned against the professional soldier and believed it would be best to expel him. It turned out that Eric XIV's advisor was correct, as De la Gardie soon joined the side of the Dukes and became one of the engineers behind Eric's deposition.

== In the Service of John III == The versatile De la Gardie subsequently gained a favored position with John III and was rewarded by him with estates and honors. Among other things, John III granted Mäster Olofsgården to Pontus De la Gardie in 1576. King Sigismund likely resided in the house when he visited Stockholm. He served as chamberlain to Queen Catherine Jagiellonica from 1569, with whom he had a very good relationship, and who in 1576 granted him authority to negotiate regarding her mother's inheritance in Italy. Despite this, he is mentioned in connection with the plot of 1574. On July 27, 1571, he was elevated to baron and received Ekholmen, near Enköping, as his primary estate. Furthermore, he was used in several important missions to the Hanseatic cities, to France and Spain in 1571, to the Emperor in 1576, to the Netherlands, Naples, and Pope Gregory XIII, with whom he negotiated the terms for the Swedish Church's reunification with the Catholic Church. On January 14, 1580, he married the king's daughter, Sofia Johansdotter Gyllenhielm. The wedding took place in Vadstena monastery church on January 14, 1580. Present at the ceremony was Antonio Possevino, the high secretary of the Jesuit order. The ritual was Roman Catholic, and the accident that occurred during the marriage ceremony – when a balcony collapsed and killed someone – many believed to be an outburst of divine wrath, as God was thought to sympathize with the Protestants. As early as 1574, De la Gardie briefly commanded the Swedish troops in Finland, but without success. When war broke out against Russia, De la Gardie was reappointed field marshal in 1580 and sent, well-equipped, across to Finland. He captured the fortress of Kexholm on November 5, 1580, and then led his troops across the thick ice of the Gulf of Bothnia from Viborg to Ingria, capturing Wesenberg and Tolsburg in Estonia in 1581. In March, he returned to Finland, visited Sweden, arrived again in Estonia in August, and attacked Ingria once more. Narva was stormed on September 6. Soon, Ivangorod, Jama, Koporje, and all of Ingria were under Swedish control, effectively cutting off the Russians from the Baltic Sea. In 1582, De la Gardie became General Field Marshal and Supreme Commander of the armed forces. The Treaty of Plussa was signed in 1583, initially providing for a two-month ceasefire, but subsequent meetings extended the agreement in several stages. De la Gardie, who served as Swedish governor in Livonia (Estonia) and Ingria, participated in the negotiations. In 1585, De la Gardie became a member of the Swedish Council of State. On November 5, 1585, while leaving the location of the peace negotiations with the Russians and rowing in a boat down the Narova River towards Narva, the fragile vessel capsized.
