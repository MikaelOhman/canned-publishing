In plain language

The European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) is warning that there is a larger and more diverse range of drugs available on the market. This means that there are more types of substances to choose from, making it harder to know what products contain and increasing the risk of serious health problems.

In particular, cannabis products have become more varied and may contain dangerous additives. In Sweden, for example, e-cigarettes containing chemical substances that mimic cannabis have been found, which is dangerous because it is often difficult to know exactly what these products contain.

Other drugs, such as cocaine and synthetic substances (chemicals similar to amphetamines), are also increasing in use. There is also significant concern about opioids, which are a common cause of death related to drug use. Some new types of these substances are very potent and can lead to acute poisoning.

Despite the fact that the number of drug-related deaths in Sweden decreased by 37 percent between 2015 and 2024, the situation is still considered serious. Authorities emphasize the importance of collaboration to combat crime and ensure that those who need help receive care and support.

The European drug market is rapidly evolving and becoming increasingly complex. The widespread availability of an ever-increasing variety of substances poses increased health risks, according to the EU's narcotics agency. The increasing supply of cannabis products is also a cause for concern.

In its annual report "European Drug Report 2026: Trends and Developments," the EU's Narcotics Agency (EUDA) highlights the changes occurring in the European drug market, which present new challenges.

The report shows that approximately 24.9 million adult Europeans have used cannabis in the past year. New cannabis products, ranging from plant extracts to edibles and products mixed with synthetic cannabinoids, are becoming increasingly common. The wider availability of potent and diverse cannabis products increases health risks. In several EU countries, including Sweden, e-cigarettes containing synthetic or semi-synthetic cannabinoids have been seized.

"In Sweden, we are seeing an increase in the seizure of e-cigarettes that contain new cannabinoids," says Åsa Bertilsson Pfuhl, investigator at Folkhälsomyndigheten. "Sometimes it is unknown what these products contain, which can lead to unexpected and serious risks."

After cannabis, cocaine is the most prevalent drug in Europe. Globally, production in South America is higher than ever, and increased cocaine production is also observed within Europe. Analyses of wastewater show that usage is increasing in many European cities. At the same time, synthetic cathinones are establishing themselves as cheaper alternatives to amphetamines and cocaine. Ketamine has also become part of the illegal market, with increased risks for users.

Opioids, often in combination with other substances, remain the most common cause of drug-related deaths in Europe. In 2024, approximately 7,600 deaths in the EU were directly linked to drug use. Most cases involved multiple substances. New synthetic opioids, such as nitazenes and orfinerils, are causing particular concern. They have caused acute poisonings and deaths, and reports of orfineril-related incidents are increasing in Europe.

In Sweden, the number of drug-related deaths has decreased by 37 percent between 2015 and 2024. However, comparing statistics between different countries is difficult, partly due to variations in reporting.

"Drug mortality remains a serious problem, both in Sweden and other countries," says Åsa Bertilsson Pfuhl. "The rapid changes in the drug market require a focus on monitoring and collaboration to maintain preparedness. Preventative measures and law enforcement efforts are particularly important, as is access to healthcare, support services, and harm reduction strategies."
